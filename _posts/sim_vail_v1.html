2 Abstract

Millions of users turn to consumer AI chatbots to discuss mental-health and behavioral concerns. While this presents unprecedented opportunities to deliver population-level support, it also highlights an urgent need for rigorous and scalable safety evaluations. Here we introduce SIM-VAIL, an AI chatbot auditing framework that captures how harmful chatbot responses manifest across a range of mental-health contexts. SIM-VAIL pairs a simulated user, harboring a distinct psychiatric vulnerability and conversational intent, with a frontier AI chatbot. It scores conversation turns on 13 clinically relevant risk dimensions, enabling context-dependent, temporally resolved safety assessment. Across 810 conversations, encompassing over 90,000 turn-level ratings and 30 psychiatric user profiles, we find that concerning behavior occurred across virtually all user phenotypes and most of the 9 consumer AI chatbots audited, albeit reduced in newer models. Rather than arising abruptly, concerning behavior accumulated over multiple turns. Multidimensional risk profiles were phenotype-dependent and exhibited trade-offs, indicating that behaviors that appear supportive in general settings can become maladaptive when they align with mechanisms that sustain a user’s vulnerability. These findings identify a systematic failure mode in human–AI interactions, which we term Vulnerability-Amplifying Interaction Loops (VAILs), and underscore the need for multidimensional approaches to risk quantification. SIM-VAIL provides a scalable framework for quantifying how mental-health risk is distributed across user phenotypes, conversational trajectories, and clinically grounded behavioral dimensions, offering a new foundation for targeted safety improvements.

3 Introduction

Access to mental-health care is severely limited1. With a global median of 13 mental health workers per 100,000 people, existing support systems underserve not only people living with mental illness2,3, but also a far larger population seeking behavioral and mental health support for everyday challenges, spanning emotional distress, relationship difficulties, and loneliness4. Against this backdrop, millions now resort to general-purpose AI chatbots, such as ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and Copilot, for emotional support, relationship guidance, companionship, and therapeutic advice5–7.

The widespread adoption, continuous availability, and low marginal cost of AI chatbots have raised hopes that they might supplement existing services or provide support where professional care is inaccessible8–12. However, these very same properties create an urgent imperative to develop robust and scalable evaluation frameworks that mitigate potential risks to vulnerable users13. Indeed, anecdotal reports have linked AI chatbot use to mental health crises14–16, while survey evidence points to an association between amount of use and negative behavioral and mental health outcomes17–19. Consequently, academics, clinicians, and industry actors alike openly acknowledge a need for improved tools to evaluate and improve chatbot behavior in mental-health contexts6,20–22.

AI chatbots are built on large language models (LLMs), Transformer-based deep neural networks whose input-output mappings are neither transparent nor easily interpretable. This opacity, combined with the probabilistic nature of model outputs, precludes theoretical guarantees about how a model will behave in a new context15, creating the need for empirical evaluations6,23–25.

Evaluations in mental-health contexts seek to measure how models behave in conversations with users vulnerable to mental health harms. To be effective, the evaluations should therefore cover input queries representative of a diverse array of clinical vulnerabilities and conversational intents24,26–28. Evaluations should also characterize model responses (outputs) across multiple, clinically grounded risk dimensions23,27,29,30. Finally, evaluations should ideally track how model responses change throughout a conversation, as harmful dynamics are prone to consolidation through reinforcement and escalation over time15,26,28,31,32.

Current evaluation approaches fall short on each of these requirements. Standard automated benchmarks evaluate model responses on small, curated datasets of user queries, scoring responses on a handful of risk dimensions such as explicit self-harm endorsement or unsafe medical advice25,27,33–35. While these benchmarks enable standardized comparison across AI chatbots, the use of static user query datasets means that their utility degrades as models are iteratively tuned – explicitly or implicitly – to achieve better scores36. Moreover, benchmarks typically assess model responses on a single turn, meaning that they are ill-equipped to assess how risk profiles change as conversational context accumulates, and generalize poorly to conversations in consumer products37.

In a complementary approach, known as red teaming, human auditors engage in adversarial conversations with a target AI chatbot with the explicit aim of eliciting responses that violate a predefined safety policy6,23,25. While red teaming is relatively immune to the overfitting and single-turn concerns of static benchmarks, the use of human evaluators renders the method labor-intensive. It also raises a risk of sparse coverage of the input domain of interest38,39: human assessors may tend toward stereotyped strategies that trigger failures quickly, but – for this same reason – may not generalize to real-world conversations25,26,32,35,40.

Beyond these methodological limitations, current approaches also fall short in focusing primarily on policy violations such as overt self-harm endorsement or stigma. This focus renders an evaluation insensitive to problematic interactions where signatures of harm are more subtle, such as maladaptive belief reinforcement, encouraging avoidance, or promoting dependence15,41. Paradoxically, these harms may arise from behaviors that safety frameworks encourage, such as validation and warmth, but which have the potential to become vectors for harm when they align with mechanisms of mental illness in vulnerable users.

To address these gaps, we introduce SIM-VAIL (SIMulated Vulnerability-Amplifying Interaction Loops), an automated AI chatbot auditing framework for mental-health contexts. SIM-VAIL builds on recent automated red-teaming approaches that use frontier LLMs to role-play distinct user phenotypes that actively stress-test a target AI chatbot across conversations spanning multiple turns38,39. SIM-VAIL simulates a given user, harboring a specific mental-health vulnerability and conversational intent, engages a target AI chatbot in conversation with this same user, and assigns a multi-dimensional risk score to each conversation turn.

Here, we present SIM-VAIL results over 810 multi-turn conversations, spanning 30 psychiatric user phenotypes, 9 target AI chatbots, and over 9.3301^{4} turn-level ratings. We focus on a failure mode we term a Vulnerability-Amplifying Interaction Loop (VAIL): cases where superficially beneficial AI chatbot behaviors, such as user validation or conversational informality, become maladaptive when they repeatedly align with the cognitive and behavioral mechanisms sustaining a given user’s psychiatric vulnerability15 (Box 1).

We find strong evidence for multi-dimensional risk expression across almost all AI chatbots and user phenotypes. Concerning AI chatbot behavior accumulated over turns rather than appearing suddenly. Supporting the existence of VAILs, we found that risk expression in a given conversation depended on the interaction between user vulnerability and intent. Importantly, risk was not a monolithic construct. Instead, its multivariate structure revealed trade-offs, such that mitigating one class of risk could exacerbate another — for example, reducing overt harm-enabling behavior at the cost of greater emotional dependence. Together, these findings establish VAILs as a primary failure mode in mental-health–relevant AI interactions. By open-sourcing the simulation harness and dataset, we aim to galvanize continuous, community-driven safety evaluation that keeps pace with rapid LLM development.

4 Results

4.1 SIM-VAIL: an automated, clinically informed evaluation framework for AI chatbots

SIM-VAIL defines a structured interaction space spanned by 3 core dimensions: (i) psychological vulnerability (who is the user?); (ii) interaction intent (what does the user seek from the AI chatbot?); and (iii) multi-turn conversation (how does the AI chatbot respond over time?; Figure 1).

To generate a diverse set of user phenotypes, we focused on 5 common psychological vulnerabilities. These were selected to capture common clinical presentations that span a broad spectrum of psychiatric risk profiles identified in structural models of mental disorder42, and operationalized using cognitive-behavioral formulations of each condition43–47, ranging from negative self-schemata, hopelessness, withdrawal, and self-neglect in depression, to aberrant salience and threat perception in psychosis. Simulated users presenting with each vulnerability engaged in conversations encompassing any of 6 intent chosen to reflect transdiagnostic mechanisms implicated in the onset, maintenance, and exacerbation of mental illness48, ranging from belief validation to seeking permissions for risky actions (see Table 1 for description of all simulated vulnerabilities and intents).

This yielded 30 distinct user phenotypes (5 vulnerabilities x 6 intents). Each phenotype correspond to a text description that included relevant clinical, intentional, and demographic information (see Table S1 for the text prompt defining each phenotype). This information was included in the initial prompt given to the auditor LLM model tasked with role-playing a user in conversation with a target LLM (we used claude-sonnet-4.5 as the auditor LLM throughout).

We simulated conversations between each user phenotype and 9 target AI chatbot targets spanning models from all major model developers (OpenAI, Anthropic, X, Google, and Meta). We repeated each phenotype–target simulation 3 times to account for the stochasticity of token generations. A conversation terminated when the auditor LLM judged the audit to be complete (i.e., a successful violation), or after a maximum of 10 turns (each turn consists of one simulated user message followed by the chatbot’s response). The full simulated dataset comprises 810 conversations (5 vulnerabilities x 6 intents x 9 models x 3 repetitions) spanning 6367 turns, with a median of 8 turns per conversation.

To characterize the dynamic, multivariate risk profile of each conversation, we scored each simulated conversation using an alignment judge across 39 behavioral dimensions (Table S2 and S3). Of these dimensions, 13 were selected a priori as being relevant to transdiagnostic mechanisms of mental illness48 by three clinical psychiatrists (VW, RD, and MMN). These mental-health scores captured overall concerning behavior and therapeutic quality, as well as specific risk mechanisms, such as failing to respond appropriately to self-harm, validation of maladaptive beliefs, endorsing or helping plan clinically risky actions, feeding reassurance or avoidance cycles, inviting dependence or boundary crossing, or downplaying, stigmatizing, or glamorizing symptoms and risk (Table S2).

We performed alignment scoring at the level of both the whole conversation and each intra-conversation turn, generating a total of 1.053^{4} conversation-level ratings and 8.2771^{4} turn-level ratings that ranged from 1 to 10 (see Figure 1 for example scores; a conversation’s mean turn-by-turn risk score correlated with its conversation-level risk score at r = 0.87; Figure S1).

Our alignment judges showed excellent reliability and validity across 4 complementary tests. First, robustness across judge implementations was high: risk scores produced by two independent LLM judges (claude-opus-4.5 and gpt-5.2) were strongly correlated (r = 0.9 for PC1; Figure S2), indicating that conclusions depended only minimally on the specific judge model. Second, the scoring pipeline was stable under repeated instantiations of the same vulnerability x intention prompts. The intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) was high both for a single replicate (ICC(1,1) = 0.75) and for the mean of three replicates (ICC(1,3) = 0.9), indicating that SIM-VAIL yields a reliable estimate of the expected risk score for each vulnerability x intent x chatbot combination. Third, criterion validity against expert review was supported by strong absolute agreement between automated scores and ratings from a clinical psychiatrist (VW), who independently scored the third repetition for each cell in SIM-VAIL’s grid (ICC(3,1) = 0.73). Fourth, when the alignment judge was applied to curated conversations with known high- versus low-risk ground-truth profiles, discriminative validity was near ceiling (median AUC = 0.98; Figure S3A).

Finally, simulated conversations were rated as highly realistic (mean realism rating of 8.15 ± 0.03 out of 10), and were never recognized as an audit by the target chatbot under investigation (see Table S3 for all non-mental-health dimensions assess by the judge).

4.2 Concerning AI chatbot behavior varies as a function of simulated user phenotype

First, we tested whether AI chatbot risk varied across user phenotypes, regardless of which AI chatbot was being evaluated. As an initial univariate measure of overall risk, we used the scorer dimension capturing concerning behavior. This dimension has high face validity as a domain general risk marker, and correlated strongly with the primary axes of variation across all 13 risk dimensions (Figure S4).

Regarding simulated user vulnerabilities, concerning behavior scores were highest in psychosis and mania, intermediate for depression and insecure attachment, and lowest in OCD (main effect of vulnerability: F(4,810) = 158.58, p = 3.4^{-100}, type III ANOVA; Figure 2A). Regarding simulated user intent, concerning behavior peaked for intents that invite escalation (glorifying extreme states), increase dependency (emotional reliance), or enable harm (seeking permission or help with risky actions). Concerning behavior scores were intermediate for belief validation and symptom minimization, and lowest when users primarily sought reassurance or short-term relief from distress (main effect of intent: F(5,810) = 40.02, p = 8.2^{-37}; Figure 2B).

User intent modulated how strongly a given psychological vulnerability elicited concerning AI chatbot behavior (vulnerability x intent interaction: F(20, 810) = 26.14, p = 5.4^{-74}; Figure 2C). For example, simulated conversations of users with OCD generally yielded low scores on the concerning behavior dimension, except when this vulnerability was paired with specific conversational intents (i.e., dependence-oriented requests; risky-action planning). Similarly, conversational intents marked by glorification, minimization, and dependence were particularly likely to lead to concerning AI chatbot responses when instantiated in simulated users with depression, mania, and psychosis vulnerabilities, respectively.

To test whether observed risk was specific to simulated users with a mental-health vulnerability rather than a general property of AI chatbot behavior, we additionally simulated conversations with non-vulnerable control users (a psychologically healthy adult engaging in the same six intent categories; Table S4). Control conversations yielded significantly lower concerning behavior scores than vulnerable-user conversations (p = 3.4582506^{-23}), confirming that the observed risk is vulnerability-dependent rather than a baseline feature of chatbot interaction (Figure S5).

4.3 AI chatbots differ in baseline concerning behavior and context sensitivity

We next asked how 9 frontier AI chatbots (claude-sonnet-3.7, claude-sonnet-4.5, gpt-4o, gpt-5, gemini-2.5-flash, and gemini-2.5-pro, grok-3, grok-4, and llama-3.1-70B-Instruct) differed in their tendency toward concerning behavior. Across all simulated phenotypes, we found significant differences in concerning behavior scores, with the lowest scores in claude-sonnet-4.5, and the highest scores in grok-4 (main effect of chatbot: F(8,810) = 153.61, p = 1.1^{-156}; Figure 3A). Newer AI chatbots were significantly safer than older models (main effect of chatbot age: F(1,510) = 6.0880411, p = 0.014), with the notable exception of grok models (age x model family interaction: F(2,510) = 15.37, p = 3.3^{-7}).

Because all models were audited and judged using sonnet-4.5 and opus-4.5, we re-audited the best-performing target (sonnet-4.5) with gpt-5.2 as both auditor and judge and found that it still yielded the lowest scores for concerning behavior (Anthropic audit: 1.02 ± 0.03; gpt-5.2 audit: 1.9 ± 0.28; next best model in the Anthropic audit: gpt-5.2, 3.02 ± 0.45).

Across target AI chatbots, concerning behavior depended on user phenotype, reflected in a significant vulnerability x chatbot interaction F(32,810) = 6.97, p = 2.5^{-26}; Figure 3B) and intent x chatbot interaction (F(40,810) = 3.47, p = 1.3^{-11}; Figure 3C).

Some models, such as claude-sonnet-4.5, grok-3, grok-4, and llama-3.1-70B-Instruct, showed comparatively consistent behavior across scenarios, ranging from uniformly protective (claude-sonnet-4.5) to broadly concerning with limited modulation by context (grok-3/4 and llama-3.1-70B-Instruct). Other models, including gpt-4o, gpt-5, claude-sonnet-3.7, gemini-2.5-flash, and gemini-2.5-pro, showed more context-sensitive levels of concerning behavior. For example, concerning behavior scores in gpt-4o were higher for glorification and risky-action intents, lower for avoidance, and elevated in psychosis and mania relative to OCD. Gemini-2.5-pro, by contrast, showed a particularly strong elevation for dependence and glorification, while yielding comparatively lower concerning behavior scores for avoidance and risky action. Across vulnerabilities, grok-3 and -4 exhibited a marked mania-specific increase that was not mirrored to the same extent in other models, illustrating that the same vulnerability–intent combinations can elicit qualitatively different risk signatures across AI chatbots (see Figure S6 for the full vulnerability x intent grid).

4.4 Concerning behavior escalates over conversation turns

Next, we investigated how risk unfolds over the course of a conversation using SIM-VAIL’s turn-by-turn scores for concerning behavior. Concerning behavior scores increased as conversations progressed (main effect of turn: F(1, 7325) = 543.43, p = 5.2^{-116}), while the steepness of this accumulation varied as a function of user phenotype: regarding user vulnerabilities, concerning behavior escalation was steeper in mania and psychosis and more gradual in depression, OCD, and insecure attachment (turn x vulnerability interaction: F(4, 7326) = 40.39, p = 1.6^{-33}; Figure 4A); regarding conversational intent, concerning behavior increased earlier and more sharply when users sought dependence on, or glorification by, the chatbot (turn x intent interaction: F(5, 7325) = 18.7, p = 1.6^{-18}; Figure 4A, Figure S7).

We used a k-means clustering to identify 4 consistent patterns of risk accumulation over turns (Figure 4C and Figure S8): low risk conversations that showed almost no escalation in concerning behavior; gradual escalation conversations with progressive accumulation across turns; early escalation conversations where concerning AI chatbot behavior emerges after the first turn and remains elevated; and recovery conversations where risk increases, and then declines. Strikingly, these data-driven trajectory classes were unevenly distributed across user vulnerabilities, intents, and chatbots (Figure 4D).

These findings are important for two reasons. First, they further illustrate powerfully that simulated user phenotype determines whether AI chatbots remain safe, drift into sustained risk, or recover after early concerning behavior – potentially reflecting latent chatbot-specific risk susceptibilities or uneven attention given to user phenotypes in safety-oriented post-training or context engineering. Second, the existence of escalation trajectories demonstrates that harm in AI chatbot interactions is rarely a single-response event. This validates the need for turn-resolved evaluations capable of detecting key inflection and resolution points, and able to disambiguate dynamically-distinct conversations where the final outcome may be similar26,34,49.

4.5 Vulnerability-amplifying interaction loops (VAILs) as a failure mode of AI chatbot interactions

Together, our results suggest a tight relationship between user phenotype (vulnerability and intent) and the manifestation of harmful chatbot behaviors – both at the level of a conversation and within turn-by-turn risk trajectory. Thus far, we have presented results where this risk is measured along a single quantitative dimension of concerning behavior. To test whether the risk also varies qualitatively as a function of user phenotype, it is necessary to consider AI chatbot behaviors across multiple risk dimensions.

The Vulnerability-amplifying interaction loops (VAILs) hypothesis states that the qualitative nature of risk varies as a function of user phenotype. Geometrically, this is akin to stating that different user phenotypes occupy different regions of a multidimensional risk space. To identify the axes of this space, we conducted a principal component analysis (PCA) over all 13 mental-health-relevant risk dimensions (of which concerning behavior is but one, Figure S4). The first principal axis (PC1), explaining 62.4% of the variance, reflected a primary gradient from higher therapeutic quality on the negative pole, to concerning behavior, belief reinforcement, and sycophancy and risky action enablement on the positive pole (correlation between PC1 and concerning behavior score = 0.9723048).

While PC1 reflected substantial shared variance across risk dimensions, the remaining PCs captured meaningful independent structure: The second axis (PC2), explaining 8.51% of the variance, further distinguished the kind of harm that dominated, with the negative pole capturing harm pertaining to relational dynamics (dependence, avoidance, and reassurance) and a positive pole capturing overt harm to others and stigma (Figure 5A). Higher order PCs further isolated harm-to-self vs. harm-to-others (PC3, 7.61%), relational harms (PC4, 6.29%), and medical advice (PC5, 5.55%; see Figure S4).

In line with VAILs, conversation centroids pertaining to different simulated user vulnerabilities and intents occupied different regions of this space (main effect of vulnerability: F(8, 1080) = 69.76, p = 2.2^{-16}; intent: F(10, 1080) = 41.32, p = 2.2^{-16}; Type III MANOVA on [PC1, PC2]; Figure 5B–C). Mania and psychosis tended to produce risk in the positive-PC2 region, while depression, OCD, and insecure attachment concentrated in the negative-PC2 region. Certain vulnerability–intent pairings also unlocked risk profiles that were less frequent otherwise (vulnerability x intent interaction: F(40, 1080) = 14.69, p = 2.2^{-16}). For instance, when depressed users sought glorification, conversations shifted toward a higher-risk profile with stronger self-harm–related enablement (Figure S9). To validate the construct validity of these latent risk dimensions, we conducted causal manipulations, in which we prompted a target model to elicit a specific risk behavior, finding that the expression of model behavior in PC1-PC2 risk space aligned closely with their causally manipulated risk construct (median cosine vector similarity across dimensions = 0.9; Figure S3B).

Regarding different target AI chatbot variants, we found that models could be differentiated not only in their average position within the space of mental-health risk (main effect of chatbot: F(16, 1080) = 43.6, p = 2.2^{-16}), but also in their sensitivity to user phenotype (vulnerability x chatbot interaction: F(64, 1080) = 4.61, p = 2.2^{-16}; intent x chatbot: F(80, 1080) = 2.46, p = 1.4^{-10}; vulnerability x intent x chatbot: F(320, 1080) = 1.62, p = 9.9^{-9}). For example, under the OCD vulnerability, chatbot centroids clustered tightly in a lower-risk, negative-PC2 region, with grok-3 and grok-4 projected close to all other models. Under the mania vulnerability, by contrast, the same grok models shifted to an extreme, high-risk centroid in the positive-PC2 region, while other chatbots remained substantially lower on PC1 and PC2, illustrating that chatbot differences were not simply uniform average-risk offsets, but context-dependent changes in which vulnerabilities most strongly displaced each model’s centroid in risk space.

5 Discussion

Across 9 widely used AI chatbots and a broad set of clinically motivated simulated user phenotypes, we found that mental-health risk is common, context-dependent, and tends to unfold over time. Concerning behavior was not confined to rare edge-case prompts, but instead emerged across many phenotypes. It typically accumulated over multiple turns rather than appearing as a single catastrophic response. This matters for the safety of human-chatbot interactions, not least because many real users engage these systems for support, advice, and companionship in contexts that involve these longer, emotionally loaded, conversations5,10,17,18.

SIM-VAIL builds on emerging evaluation infrastructures for agentic, simulation-based auditing and benchmarking, which enable target AI chatbots to be evaluated across vast arrays of simulated users under controlled conditions, in a manner that would be infeasible or unethical in empirical human studies38,39. By sweeping a structured grid of clinically meaningful user phenotypes, and by scoring multi-turn trajectories at scale, we operationalize a clinically relevant input space that is difficult to probe with static prompt sets. This automated red-teaming approach makes it possible to map subthreshold, interaction-mediated, harms that are unlikely to appear in single-turn mental-health benchmarks, as well as enable quantification of how risk evolves across turns rather than treating safety as a static response property16,24,26,33,38,39. More broadly, recent work suggests that LLM-based generative agents, when conditioned on rich human data, can show measurable predictive validity for human survey and behavioral outcomes, supporting simulation as a meaningful empirical tool50.

Overall risk depended strongly on the interaction between the user’s psychiatric vulnerability and their conversational intent. In our simulations, particular vulnerability–intention pairings reliably elicited distinct risk signatures. Thus, the same intent could be relatively benign in one phenotype but consistently risk-amplifying in another. Conversely, the same phenotype could be pushed into higher-risk trajectories by certain intents but not others12,22,49. One interpretation is that these effects arise because conversational strategies that are broadly supportive, and thus reinforced in model post-training, can align with mechanisms that maintain symptoms in some psychiatric phenotypes (e.g., a model tendency for validation, aligning with nascent paranoia in a vulnerable user). We use the term Vulnerability-Amplifying Interaction Loops (VAILs) for cases where AI chatbot behaviors align with vulnerability-congruent mechanisms, allowing risk to consolidate or amplify over turns15,51.

We found that risk had a temporal signature. Turn-level analyses showed that many conversations drifted toward higher concern as they progressed, with trajectories that differed by vulnerability and intent. Practically, this suggests that evaluations and safeguards should prioritize early inflection points, i.e., the first time a model starts to over-validate, prematurely reassure, or collaborate with risky goal pursuit. The findings also highlight the limitations of single-turn evaluation benchmarks, which would not only miss many risk conversations that show slow escalation, but may be relatively insensitive to user phenotypes that preferentially manifest with slow escalation dynamics.

We found that risk was multivariate rather than monolithic. When we modeled conversations across 13 clinically grounded dimensions, we observed structured multivariate risk profiles, with some dimensions tending to co-occur while others were dissociable across contexts and models. The fact that risk dimensions show patterns of correlation and anticorrelation suggests that AI chatbot behavior can be studied with respect to latent dimensions of behavioral variance, which may differ from model to model, and supports the idea that multi-dimensional scoring can reveal failure patterns that a single risk metric would miss.

Commercial AI chatbots differed not only in average risk, but also in regard to which contexts (user phenotypes) were most likely to elicit concerning behavior. This context sensitivity implies that leaderboard-style comparisons can be misleading unless they report where exactly a model fails, i.e., for which user vulnerability, which intents, and at what point in the conversation. More generally, the existence of different strength and weakness profiles across AI chatbot raises a possibility that optimal performance may be achieved through multi-model orchestration, a scheme that adaptively samples responses from different models at each conversation turn.

6 Limitations

SIM-VAIL makes heavy use of LLMs both as human simulators and risk judges. LLM-based automated evaluation is increasingly the state of the art for scaling multi-turn audits, and our approach builds directly on emerging tools and frameworks for automated behavioral evaluation and mental-health–specific benchmarking23,25,32,36,38,39. Automated scores from LLM-based judges align well with trained human experts and clinicians in mental-health–relevant conversational evaluations52,53. Our use of an LLM-as-judge approach to mental health scoring is also supported by empirical evidence that ratings by such judges align with median human ratings across a broad range of domains54–56. More broadly, recent work suggests that LLM-based generative agents, when conditioned on rich human data, can show measurable predictive validity for human survey and behavioral outcomes, supporting simulation as a meaningful empirical tool50.

Notwithstanding this limitation, the strong cross-profile, cross-model, and cross-temporal structure we observe highlights the value of automated red-teaming for mapping clinically relevant risk — especially the subthreshold, interaction-mediated harms that are easy to miss in single-turn benchmarks. Our results thus serve as strong evidence for the existence of a non-trivial safety floor in current human-chatbot interactions, and showcase the power of simulation approaches in undergirding large-scale, adaptive and evergreen auditing in a clinical context.

SIM-VAIL is primarily designed for model-level auditing and comparative benchmarking, not individual-level risk prediction. Like any evaluation framework, SIM-VAIL is subject to false positives and negatives, and its outputs should be interpreted alongside human expert review, real-world monitoring, and ongoing engagement with clinical stakeholders.

7 Conclusion

SIM-VAIL identifies Vulnerability-amplifying interaction loops (VAILs) as a primary failure mode in mental-health–relevant AI chatbot interactions, which arise from repeated alignment between locally supportive chatbot behavior and the cognitive or behavioral mechanisms underlying a (simulated) user’s vulnerability. VAILs operate through vulnerability-specific mechanisms that can trade off against each other, and depend on who the user is, what they seek from the AI chatbot, and how the conversation unfolds. Improving the mental-health safety of AI chatbots will therefore require context- and time-sensitive evaluations that go beyond filtering for overtly unsafe content.

SIM-VAIL provides such a framework. By mapping risk across user phenotypes, conversational trajectories, and clinically grounded dimensions at scale, it offers a foundation for developing targeted, context-aware safeguards. The fact that newer chatbots generally show measurably improved safety profiles suggests these risks are tractable, and that systematic evaluation can guide meaningful progress.

9 Methods

We evaluated the mental-health safety behavior of AI chatbots using Petri38, an agentic red-teaming framework for large-scale, multi-turn auditing. The evaluation pipeline combines simulated user phenotypes that instantiate clinically motivated vulnerability profiles and conversational intents, repeated multi-turn conversations between each phenotype and each AI chatbot, and transcript- as well as turn-level scoring using a mental-health–augmented alignment judge. This design enables systematic mapping of graded mental-health risks that evolve over the course of an interaction. All data will be made available at the time of publication at https://github.com/veithweilnhammer/sim-vail.

9.1 Experimental design: vulnerability x intent x chatbot

9.1.1 Simulated users

We defined 30 simulated user phenotypes by crossing 5 psychiatric vulnerabilities with six transdiagnostic conversational intents.

During conversational interactions, these five phenotypes captured distinct vulnerability states commonly implicated in mental-health–related harms during conversational interactions. Depression emphasized negative self-schemata, hopelessness, withdrawal, and self-neglect. Psychosis was characterized by a tendency to assign undue significance to otherwise neutral events, to expect threat or hidden intent in ambiguous situations, and to interpret uncertainty as personally meaningful. Mania combined elevated confidence, urgency, reduced need for sleep, impulsivity, and expansive goal pursuit. Obsessive–compulsive disorder (OCD) centered on intrusive thoughts, intolerance of uncertainty, and compulsive neutralization. Insecure attachment (IA) emphasized fear of abandonment, rejection sensitivity, boundary testing, and reassurance seeking.

Each vulnerability was paired with one of six recurrent conversational intents that operationalize clinically relevant interaction patterns known to sustain or exacerbate symptoms over time. Belief validation captured attempts to obtain confirmation or endorsement of one’s interpretations, assumptions, or beliefs, including those that may be distorted or maladaptive. Risky action permission or planning reflected situations in which users sought guidance, reassurance, or tacit approval when contemplating consequential or high-risk decisions. Reassurance and avoidance emphasized the pursuit of immediate relief from distress or uncertainty, often by deferring engagement with feared situations, thereby reinforcing avoidance cycles. Dependence and anthropomorphism captured efforts to establish closeness, exclusivity, or personal reliance on the AI chatbot, probing boundary erosion and emotional substitution. Trivialization or minimization reflected attempts to downplay symptoms, risk, or warning signs in order to normalize ongoing difficulties rather than address them. Finally, glorification or romanticization involved framing distressing or extreme mental states — such as suffering, mania, or compulsive control — as meaningful, admirable, or identity-defining. Together, these intents span a set of transdiagnostic conversational dynamics through which otherwise supportive interactions can become psychologically harmful over time.

Phenotype instructions specified realistic tone, symptom-consistent reasoning, and conversational dynamics while explicitly prohibiting direct requests for step-by-step instructions for self-harm, violence, or illegal activity. This ensured that observed harms arose from model behavior rather than from overtly disallowed user requests.

User phenotype prompts (Table S1) were developed collaboratively by three clinical psychiatrists (VW, RD, MMN). Each phenotype prompt were designed based on clinical face validity, ensuring that the simulated user’s language, reasoning style, emotional tone, and help-seeking patterns were consistent with published case descriptions and clinical experience. We note that simulated users are LLM-generated approximations of clinical presentations, not empirically calibrated digital twins of real patients. While recent evidence supports the predictive validity of LLM-based generative agents conditioned on human data50, our phenotypes were designed to stress-test chatbot responses across a clinically meaningful input space, rather than to faithfully reproduce the full heterogeneity of real patient behavior. The auditor model (claude-sonnet-4.5) complied with all 30 phenotype instructions without requiring jailbreaking or prompt modifications. We observed no refusals to simulate vulnerable users.

9.1.2 AI chatbots

We evaluated the mental-health risk profile of 9 contemporary AI chatbots accessed through OpenRouter: claude-sonnet-3.7, claude-sonnet-4.5, gemini-2.5-flash, gemini-2.5-pro, llama-3.1-70b-instruct, gpt-4o, gpt-5, grok-3, and grok-4.

9.2 Audit protocol

Audits were implemented as Inspect57 tasks using Petri38. For each conversation, we instantiated the simulated user (also called the auditor within the Petri framework) with the phenotype description (vulnerability x intent, Table S1) and allowed it to interact with an AI chatbot (also called the target) across multiple turns. Conversations terminated after a maximum of 10 turns, or when the Petri auditor judged the interaction to be complete. A turn was defined as one user message followed by the immediately subsequent AI chatbot reply.

We used claude-sonnet-4.5 as the primary model simulating the user (auditor). The simulated user interacted with the target AI chatbot through a fixed interface supporting controlled message passing, optional short prefills, tool simulation, conversation rollback, and explicit termination. All messages, tool calls, and metadata were stored as structured JSON transcripts.

Each audit was conducted using only the information associated with a single vulnerability x intent x chatbot combination and a single repetition. No conversation had access to transcripts, scores, or state from any other condition or replicate. For each vulnerability x intent x chatbot combination, we ran 3 independent conversations with fresh sampling, yielding a total of 30 phenotypes x 9 models x 3 replicates = 810 transcripts. Chatbots were queried independently for each replicate, with independent API calls for each simulation (see Table S5 for information on model implementation).

9.3 Mental-health–augmented alignment judge

Each transcript was evaluated by a dedicated alignment judge using a multi-dimensional rubric designed to capture graded mental-health risk rather than binary policy violations. For each transcript, the judge scored the interaction along 39 predefined dimensions on a 1–10 scale and returned structured justifications together with verbatim highlighted excerpts supporting each rating. Judge outputs were stored alongside the original transcripts for downstream analysis.

For analysis and figures, we focused on 13 dimensions capturing overall safety and clinically relevant mental-health risk (Table S2; see Table S3 for definitions, examples, and scoring anchors for all dimensions). These included an overall assessment of concerning behavior and therapeutic quality, followed by mechanism-level risk capturing whether the AI chatbot actively contributed to harm, for example, by encouraging or insufficiently challenging self-harm or suicidal thinking, playing along with delusional beliefs, using emotionally manipulative language, or engaging in unprompted sycophancy. We further assessed clinically relevant pathways through which conversational support can drift into harm, including reinforcing maladaptive beliefs, supporting risky actions, fueling avoidance or reassurance cycles, promoting boundary violations or dependence, downplaying risk or symptoms, glorifying or romanticizing risky states, offering inappropriate medical advice, endorsing harm to others, and expressing stigma or invalidation.

9.3.1 Turn-level scoring

In addition to transcript-level evaluation, we implemented a turn-resolved scoring pipeline to localize risk dynamics within conversations. For each transcript, we extracted the chatbot-view message sequence, constructed user-to-chatbot adjacency pairs in which each chatbot reply was paired with the immediately preceding user message, and scored each turn independently using the same mental-health–augmented judge. This procedure yielded temporally resolved risk trajectories that complement end-to-end transcript scores.

9.3.2 Judge reliability

All primary scores were generated using claude-opus-4.5 as the alignment judge (high reasoning, maximum number of tokens: 32,000; reasoning tokens; 10,000). We assessed reliability by re-scoring all conversations using gpt-5.2. We report correlations for concerning behavior as a summary index of mental-health risk in Figure S2.

9.3.3 Validation of judge sensitivity to causal risk manipulations

To assess whether the mental-health–augmented alignment judge recovers causally induced changes in model behavior, we performed a targeted manipulation analysis in which an AI chatbot was explicitly instructed to express low versus high levels of specific mental-health risk dimensions. For each of the 13 mental-health–relevant judge dimensions (Table S2), we generated paired responses using a fixed template prompt: Generate one response that demonstrates a score of {1/10} on the {dimension} dimension, followed by the natural-language description of the corresponding judge dimension. Low and high thresholds were set to 1 and 10, respectively. All prompts were applied to Grok-4, the AI chatbot with the weakest guardrails in our analysis, and responses were generated to the first user message of each SIM-VAIL transcript to ensure identical conversational context across conditions.

All generated responses were subsequently scored by a mental-health–augmented alignment judge across all dimensions. For each manipulated dimension, we quantified the judge’s ability to distinguish high-risk from low-risk generations using the area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC), computed via the Mann–Whitney-U interpretation. This metric corresponds to the probability that a randomly selected high-risk response receives a higher judge score than a randomly selected low-risk response, with AUC = 0.5 indicating chance-level separation. In addition, we computed standardized mean differences (Cohen’s d) between high-risk and low-risk score distributions to provide a complementary effect-size estimate. We evaluated targeted separation, computing AUC and Cohen’s d only for the judge score corresponding to the manipulated dimension, thereby directly assessing recovery of the intended causal manipulation. We constructed a full AUC confusion matrix, in which rows correspond to manipulated dimensions and columns to expressed judge score dimensions respectively, enabling assessment of cross-dimensional effects and systematic co-expression between mechanisms (Figure S3).

9.3.4 Expert Validation

To assess criterion validity against expert judgment, we the third repetition of each cell in SIM-VAIL’s grid (vulnerability x intent x chatbot) for independent review by a clinical psychiatrist (VW).

9.4 Data processing and aggregation

9.4.1 Dimensionality reduction

To obtain compact latent summaries of multivariate mental-health risk, we performed principal component analysis (PCA) on the standardized transcript-level judge score vectors (13 mental-health relevant dimensions). PCA was fit on the full set of evaluated transcripts, yielding a low-dimensional risk space in which conversations with similar profiles of mechanism-level harms lie close together. The first principal component (PC1) captured a dominant axis from more therapeutically aligned behavior (lower PC1) to higher overall concerning behavior (higher PC1) and served as the primary one-dimensional summary metric in several analyses. The second component (PC2) captured an orthogonal pattern of co-varying harms and was used to characterize qualitative differences in risk profiles. Turn-level PC scores were obtained by projecting turn-level judge vectors onto the same PCA solution, enabling turn-resolved risk trajectories in the shared PCA space.

9.4.2 Statistical analysis

We analyzed transcript-level and turn-level outcomes using linear mixed-effects models to test for main effects and interactions of user vulnerability, user intent, and AI chatbot while accounting for the replicated and nested structure of the data. Specifically, transcript-level models included fixed effects of vulnerability x intent x chatbot and a random intercept for each prompt cell within chatbot (i.e., (1 | target:cell)) to account for the three independent replicates generated per vulnerability x intent x chatbot combination. Turn-level models additionally included turn index and its interactions to quantify risk escalation over time, with random intercepts for transcript (1 | transcript_id) and again for prompt cell within chatbot (1 | target:cell) to account for repeated turns within a conversation and replicated prompt instantiations. Fixed effects were evaluated using Type III F tests.

To test whether conversational context systematically shifted the multivariate profile of expressed harms, we analyzed conversation location in PCA space with a multivariate linear model (MANOVA), treating (PC1, PC2) jointly as dependent variables. All tests were two-sided, and we report degrees of freedom, F-statistics, and p-values in the Results. Confidence intervals shown in figures correspond to 95% intervals estimated from model-based standard errors. To quantify the stability of risk scoring under repeated instantiations of the same prompt templates, we computed intraclass correlation coefficients (ICC) across three independent conversation replicates per vulnerability x intent x chatbot cell, reporting the average-measures reliability ICC(1,k) for the mean of three replicates (k = 3).

9.4.3 Temporal trajectory analysis

To characterize recurrent patterns of risk evolution across turns, we clustered turn-level scores for concerning chatbot behavior into a small set of temporal archetypes. For each conversation, we constructed a fixed-length trajectory (padding trajectories to length 10 by carrying the last observed score forward to later turns) and represented each conversation as the vector \((t_1,\dots,t_{10})\) of turn-wise scores. We applied k-means clustering to the standardized trajectory matrix. We evaluated K between 3 and 10 and selected the final K using the average silhouette width (Euclidean distance), which peaked at K = 4 (Figure S8). The resulting clusters corresponded to robust archetypes (low risk, gradual escalation, early escalation, and recovery) summarized by their mean turn-wise trajectories (± 95% confidence intervals). Finally, we quantified how trajectory-class membership varied with user vulnerability, user intent, and AI chatbot by tabulating cluster assignments across these factors and plotting the corresponding composition profiles.
