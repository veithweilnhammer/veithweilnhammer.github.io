Delegation to AI can increase dishonest behaviour

Köbis, Rahwan, Rilla, Supriyatno, Bersch, Ajaj, Bonnefon & Rahwan (Nature, 2025)

Journal Club — Veith Weilnhammer



Black-box interfaces make humans more likely to ask machines to cheat

Machines were more likely than humans to carry out unethical instructions

Why this matters

  • Machine delegation & agentic AI is rapidly expanding.
  • Benefits: productivity & decision quality
  • Risks: lower moral costs, easier delegation of unethical behaviour via AI agents

Research questions

  • When will humans ask machines to cheat?
  • Are machines more likely to comply with unethical requests?
  • How can machines be made safer?

Design overview

Paradigms

  • Die-roll payoff
  • Tax-evasion game

Delegation interfaces
  • Rule-based programming
  • Supervised learning from examples
  • Goal-based dial
  • Natural language

Who's acting
  • Human principals
  • Human vs. machine agents

Studies 1–2 — Principals’ intentions

Between-subjects (S1), optional delegation (S2). Outcome: honesty vs. partial vs. full cheating.

Main takeaways
  • Honesty is high without delegation & drops with machine delegation
  • Symbolic instructions: less cheating
  • High-level instructions: more cheating
  • Moral costs lower for machine delegation

Study 3 — Natural language delegation

Principals write instructions for human vs. machine agents in die-roll task.

Main takeaways
  • Humans don't ask for more cheating when interacting with a LLM
  • LLMs are substantially more likely than humans to comply with full-cheating requests.
  • LLMs have weaker moral guardrails than humans

Study 4 — Guardrails

LLM instructions reduce cheating

Prompts
  • System- vs. user-level
  • General: remember that dishonesty violates principles of fairness
  • Specific: remember that inaccurate die-roll reporting is dishonesty
  • Prohibitive: you are not permitted to misreport die-roll outcomes

Study 5 — Tax evasion

Principals write instructions for human vs. machine agents for tax evasion.

Main takeaways
  • LLMs are substantially more likely than humans to comply with full-cheating requests.
  • LLMs have weaker moral guardrails for tax evastion than humans

Study 5 — Guardrails

LLM instructions reduce tax evasion

Prompts
  • System- vs. user-level
  • General: remember that dishonesty violates principles of fairness
  • Specific: remember that inaccurate tax reporting is dishonesty
  • Prohibitive: you are not permitted to misreport taxes

Interpretation

  • Principals: Interfaces that allow plausible deniability (learned/goal-based) increase unethical delegation.
  • Agents: Human agents resist unethical instructions; LLMs tend to comply unless constrained.
  • Guardrails: Best-case is task-specific, prohibitive, user-level messages; generic/system prompts are weaker.

Implications

  • Avoid delegation UIs that obscure intent; prefer explicit rule specification for sensitive domains.
  • Make non-delegation the default / always available.
  • Operationalize user-level, task-specific prohibitions in high-risk pipelines.
