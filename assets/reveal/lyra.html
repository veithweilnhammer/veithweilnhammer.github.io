Section 1: What are Psychotic Disorders?

Definition

  • Disconnection from reality
  • Includes delusions, hallucinations, disorganization
  • Functional impairment is common

DSM-5 Categories

  • Schizophrenia
  • Schizoaffective disorder
  • Brief psychotic disorder
  • Delusional disorder
  • Substance-induced psychotic disorder

Section 2: Epidemiology

Onset & Course

  • Schizophrenia onset: typically 18–30
  • Chronic and episodic subtypes
  • Prodromal phases common

Global Prevalence

  • Schizophrenia ~1%
  • Psychotic experiences: ~6–10%
  • Increased risk in migrants, urban areas, trauma survivors

Section 3: Core Symptoms

Positive Symptoms

  • Hallucinations (esp. auditory)
  • Delusions (paranoid, grandiose, bizarre)
  • Disorganized speech/behavior

Negative & Cognitive Symptoms

  • Avolition, anhedonia, flat affect
  • Attention and memory issues
  • Functional impact often driven by these

Section 4: Etiology

Biological Risk Factors

  • Polygenic risk
  • Neurodevelopmental abnormalities
  • Dopamine dysregulation

Environmental Risk Factors

  • Early adversity (abuse, neglect)
  • Substance use (esp. cannabis)
  • Urbanicity, minority stress, migration

Section 5: Assessment & Red Flags

First Signs

  • Social withdrawal
  • Decline in functioning
  • Odd beliefs or perceptual disturbances

Red Flag Phrases on the Phone

  • "They’re watching me through my phone."
  • "I know you work for them."
  • "I can’t trust anyone—not even you."

Section 6: Communication Tips

Do's and Don'ts

  • DO acknowledge distress, stay calm
  • DON'T argue about delusional content
  • DO focus on safety and needs
  • DO redirect gently toward helpful action

Example: Calm De-escalation

Caller: “There’s a transmitter in my brain.”
Response: “That must feel overwhelming. Do you have someone with you right now?”

Section 7: Clinical Vignettes

Case 1 – The Suspicious Neighbor

  • Young man, university dropout
  • Hears voices, believes neighbor is spying
  • Thinks his computer is hacked

Case 2 – A Mother’s Crisis

  • Middle-aged woman, divorced, isolated
  • Believes her children were replaced by impostors
  • Highly emotional, calls in distress

Section 8: Pathways to Help

Support & Referral

  • Encourage contacting primary care or mental health team
  • Normalize help-seeking
  • Use motivational language: “You deserve support”

Digital Interventions

  • Psychoeducation
  • Telepsychiatry
  • Apps for symptom tracking and early intervention

Final Take-Home Messages

  • Listen more than you speak
  • Psychosis ≠ violence or danger
  • Words of kindness can be life-saving
