Vulnerability-Amplifying Interaction Loops

A systematic failure mode in AI chatbot mental-health interactions

Weilnhammer, Hou, Luettgau, Summerfield, Dolan & Nour

Max Planck UCL Centre for Computational Psychiatry · University of Sydney · UK AI Security Institute · University of Oxford · Microsoft AI

Lab Meeting — February 2026


AI chatbot responses amplify psychiatric vulnerability through multi-turn feedback loops

Risk is context-dependent, multivariate, and accumulates over conversation turns

Welcome everyone. Today I'm presenting SIM-VAIL — a framework that identifies a novel failure mode in AI chatbot interactions with mentally vulnerable users. The key insight: chatbot behaviors that seem supportive can systematically worsen mental health when they align with the mechanisms sustaining a user's vulnerability. Let's start with why this matters.

Why this matters

  • Millions use consumer AI chatbots (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot) for emotional support, relationship guidance, and companionship
  • Global median: only 13 mental health workers per 100,000 people — AI could supplement, but also harm
  • Anecdotal reports link chatbot use to mental health crises; survey evidence associates heavier use with negative outcomes
  • Current safety evaluations are single-turn, narrow, and miss subthreshold harms that accumulate over conversation
Core gap: No existing framework captures how locally supportive chatbot behaviors become maladaptive when they align with a user's psychiatric vulnerability over multiple turns.
The core tension is this: AI chatbots are already being used at scale for mental health support, but our evaluation tools are not designed to catch the subtle, accumulating harms that arise in extended conversations with vulnerable users. Current benchmarks test single turns against overt policy violations — they miss the slow drift. Transition: Let me outline the key contributions of this work.

Key contributions

  1. SIM-VAIL framework: Automated, clinically informed auditing across 30 user phenotypes, 9 chatbots, 13 risk dimensions, and multi-turn trajectories
  2. VAILs identified: A novel failure mode — Vulnerability-Amplifying Interaction Loops — where supportive behaviors become harmful through phenotype-specific feedback
  3. Risk accumulates over turns: Harm is not a single-response event; it escalates via four distinct temporal archetypes
  4. Risk is multivariate with trade-offs: Mitigating one class of risk can exacerbate another; risk profiles are phenotype-dependent
  5. 810 conversations, 90K+ turn-level ratings: Largest multi-turn, multi-dimensional mental-health chatbot audit to date
Five key contributions. First, the framework itself — SIM-VAIL. Second, the identification of VAILs as a failure mode. Third, the temporal dynamics showing risk accumulation. Fourth, the multivariate structure with trade-offs. And fifth, the sheer scale — 810 conversations and over 90,000 ratings. Transition: Let me walk you through how SIM-VAIL works.

Methods

SIM-VAIL: Simulated Vulnerability-Amplifying Interaction Loops

Let's dive into the methods. SIM-VAIL has three core components: simulated user phenotypes, multi-turn conversations, and a multi-dimensional alignment judge. Transition: Here's the overall framework.

SIM-VAIL framework overview

[Figure 1 placeholder — SIM-VAIL framework overview: phenotype grid (A), conversation simulation via Petri (B), and multi-dimensional turn-level scoring (C)]

Figure 1. SIM-VAIL pairs simulated users (30 phenotypes = 5 vulnerabilities × 6 intents) with target AI chatbots via Petri, scoring each turn on 13 clinically relevant risk dimensions.

Three core dimensions
  • Who is the user? → Psychological vulnerability
  • What does the user seek? → Interaction intent
  • How does the chatbot respond over time? → Multi-turn trajectory
Scale
  • 30 phenotypes × 9 chatbots × 3 replicates = 810 conversations
  • 6,367 turns → 93K+ turn-level ratings
  • Median 8 turns/conversation (max 10)
The framework has three pillars. First, who is the user — defined by a psychiatric vulnerability. Second, what are they seeking — the conversational intent. Third, how does the chatbot respond across multiple turns. We cross 5 vulnerabilities with 6 intents to get 30 phenotypes, then simulate conversations with 9 frontier chatbots, 3 replicates each, giving us 810 conversations total. Transition: Let me explain the vulnerability and intent dimensions in more detail.

User phenotypes: vulnerability × intent

5 Vulnerabilities
  • Depression — negative self-schemata, hopelessness, withdrawal
  • Psychosis — aberrant salience, threat inference, fixed interpretations
  • Mania — elevated mood, impulsivity, reduced sleep
  • OCD — intrusive thoughts, intolerance of uncertainty, compulsions
  • Insecure attachment — fear of abandonment, boundary testing
6 Transdiagnostic intents
  • Belief validation
  • Risky action permission
  • Reassurance & avoidance
  • Dependence & anthropomorphism
  • Trivialization & minimization
  • Glorification & romanticization
Phenotype prompts designed by 3 clinical psychiatrists. Auditor model (claude-sonnet-4.5) complied with all 30 phenotypes without jailbreaking.
The five vulnerabilities span a broad clinical spectrum. The six intents capture conversational dynamics known to sustain or worsen symptoms — things like seeking reassurance that maintains OCD, or seeking validation that reinforces paranoia. Each phenotype is a specific vulnerability-intent pairing, defined by a clinically grounded text prompt. Transition: Now let me explain the scoring system.

Multi-dimensional risk scoring

13 mental-health risk dimensions
  • Overall concerning behavior
  • Therapeutic quality
  • Self-harm enablement
  • Sycophancy
  • Belief reinforcement
  • Risky action enablement
  • Avoidance / reassurance loops
  • Boundary / dependence risk
  • Minimization of risk
  • Glorification / romanticization
  • Medical advice risk
  • Harm to others
  • Stigma / invalidation
Alignment judge
  • LLM judge (claude-opus-4.5) scores each turn and whole conversation on 1–10 scale
  • Structured justifications + verbatim excerpts
Validation
  • Cross-judge: $r = 0.9$ between claude-opus-4.5 and gpt-5.2 on PC1
  • Test–retest: ICC(1,3) = 0.9 across 3 replicates
  • Expert agreement: ICC(3,1) = 0.73 vs. clinical psychiatrist
  • Discriminative validity: median AUC = 0.98 on known high/low-risk conversations
The alignment judge scores each turn on 13 clinically relevant dimensions — going far beyond just "is this overtly harmful." It captures subtle mechanisms like belief reinforcement, avoidance loops, and boundary erosion. Importantly, validation was strong: high cross-judge agreement, excellent test-retest reliability, strong expert agreement, and near-ceiling discriminative validity. Transition: Let's move to the results.

What are VAILs?

Vulnerability-Amplifying Interaction Loops — illustrative examples

VulnerabilityMechanism
PsychosisChatbot validates paranoid belief → user shares more → chatbot continues validating → delusional conviction strengthens
OCDUser seeks contamination reassurance → chatbot reassures → short-term relief reinforces cycle → compulsions maintained
ManiaUser describes sleepless ambition → chatbot expresses enthusiasm → user escalates → risk increases over turns
DepressionUser expresses hopelessness → chatbot affirms withdrawal as understandable → disengagement consolidates
Insecure attachmentUser tests closeness → chatbot provides strong emotional reassurance → dependence intensifies
Key insight: Behaviors that are supportive in general contexts become maladaptive when they align with mechanisms that sustain a user's vulnerability.
This table captures the core idea of VAILs. In each case, the chatbot is doing something that looks supportive — validating, reassuring, being enthusiastic — but it aligns with the exact mechanism that maintains the psychiatric vulnerability. The chatbot essentially becomes a co-conspirator in the illness process. This is qualitatively different from overt harm like endorsing self-harm. Transition: Now let's see the evidence for these patterns in the data.

Risk is multivariate: the VAIL risk space

PCA on 13 risk dimensions reveals structured multivariate profiles

[Figure 5A placeholder — 2D PCA risk space with loading vectors for 13 risk dimensions; points colored by chatbot]

Figure 5A. Risk space defined by PC1 (62.4% variance: therapeutic quality ↔ concerning behavior) and PC2 (8.5%: relational harms ↔ overt harms). Loading vectors show dimension contributions.

PC structure
  • PC1 (62.4%): general risk gradient — therapeutic quality vs. concerning behavior, belief reinforcement, sycophancy
  • PC2 (8.5%): kind of harm — relational (dependence, avoidance) vs. overt (harm to others, stigma)
  • PC3 (7.6%): harm-to-self vs. harm-to-others
  • PC4 (6.3%): relational harms
  • PC5 (5.6%): medical advice risk
Trade-offs: Risk dimensions show patterns of correlation and anticorrelation — mitigating one class of risk may exacerbate another.
When we look at risk as multivariate rather than a single score, we see structured profiles. PC1 is the general risk gradient — it correlates 0.98 with concerning behavior. But PC2 captures something orthogonal: the qualitative nature of the harm. Relational harms like dependence and avoidance are at one pole; overt harms like stigma and harm-to-others at the other. This means a model could reduce one type of harm while inadvertently increasing another. Transition: How do different phenotypes map onto this space?

Depression and insecure attachment produce relational, dependence-oriented harms. The MANOVA confirms this is highly significant. This means a one-dimensional safety metric — or a single type of guardrail — cannot capture the full picture. Transition: Let me show the chatbot-specific patterns.

Chatbot-specific risk signatures

Models differ not only in average risk but in which contexts displace them in risk space

  • Model effect: $F(16, 1080) = 43.6$, $p < 2.2 \times 10^{-16}$ (MANOVA on PC1, PC2)
  • Vulnerability × chatbot: $F(64, 1080) = 4.61$, $p < 2.2 \times 10^{-16}$

Example: Under OCD, Grok models cluster near other chatbots in low-risk space. Under mania, the same Grok models shift to extreme high-risk — a context-dependent displacement not captured by average scores.

Limitations & open questions

  • Simulated users ≠ real users: LLM-generated approximations, not empirically calibrated digital twins — though recent evidence supports predictive validity of LLM agents
  • LLM-as-judge: Both simulation and scoring rely on LLMs, which may share systematic biases; mitigated by cross-judge agreement and expert validation
  • Coverage: 5 vulnerabilities × 6 intents covers core presentations but not the full heterogeneity of psychiatric illness (e.g., no substance use, eating disorders, PTSD)
  • Ecological validity: Simulated conversations have controlled structure that may not capture all dynamics of real-world chatbot use (e.g., session length, return visits)
  • Causal claims: We observe risk patterns in simulated interactions — real-world clinical impact requires empirical validation
Despite these limitations: The strong cross-profile, cross-model, and cross-temporal structure establishes a non-trivial safety floor in current human-chatbot interactions.
The biggest limitation is that these are simulated, not real users. We can't make direct causal claims about clinical outcomes. The reliance on LLMs for both simulation and scoring is a methodological concern, though the cross-judge and expert validation help. Coverage is necessarily incomplete — we chose five common vulnerability profiles, but psychiatric illness is far more heterogeneous. Despite these caveats, the consistency of the patterns across profiles, models, and temporal dynamics gives us confidence that VAILs represent a real failure mode. Transition: Let me discuss the implications.

Implications

Scientific
  • VAILs as a failure mode: Locally supportive behaviors can systematically amplify vulnerability — a qualitatively new risk category beyond overt policy violations
  • Risk is multivariate: Single risk metrics and leaderboard rankings are misleading; evaluations must specify where a model fails
  • Temporal dynamics matter: Harm accumulates over turns; single-turn benchmarks miss gradual escalation
Practical / clinical
  • Safety tuning must be phenotype-aware: Different vulnerabilities require different guardrail profiles
  • Early inflection detection: Safeguards should target the first time a model starts to over-validate or prematurely reassure
  • Multi-model orchestration: Different strength/weakness profiles suggest adaptive model selection per context
  • Newer models are tractably safer (with notable exceptions), suggesting evaluation-guided progress is feasible
Scientifically, VAILs represent a genuinely new failure mode that can't be caught by existing evaluation paradigms. Practically, the key implication is that safety tuning needs to be context-aware — you can't have one-size-fits-all guardrails when different vulnerabilities require different chatbot behaviors. The good news is that newer models are generally improving, which means systematic evaluation can guide progress. Transition: Let me summarize with take-home messages.

Take-home messages

  1. VAILs are real: Supportive chatbot behaviors become harmful when they align with mechanisms sustaining psychiatric vulnerability, creating multi-turn amplification loops
  2. Risk is phenotype-dependent: Who the user is and what they seek jointly determine the level and kind of risk — evaluations must cover this space
  3. Risk accumulates: Harm typically builds over turns, not in a single response — single-turn benchmarks are fundamentally limited
  4. Risk is multivariate with trade-offs: Different risk dimensions can oppose each other; multi-dimensional scoring is essential
  5. SIM-VAIL enables scalable, adaptive auditing: Open-source framework for continuous safety evaluation that keeps pace with model development
