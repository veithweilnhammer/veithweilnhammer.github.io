Human-computer interactions predict mental health

MAILA — a MAchine learning framework for Inferring Latent mental states from digital Activity

Veith Weilnhammer, Jefferson Ortega & David Whitney

Helen Wills Neuroscience Institute & Department of Psychology, UC Berkeley · Max Planck UCL Centre for Computational Psychiatry · Vision Science Group, UC Berkeley

Lab Meeting — 2025


Welcome everyone. Today I'm presenting MAILA — a framework that decodes mental health from cursor and touchscreen activity, two signals generated by virtually every digital device at zero marginal cost. The key claim: routine human-computer interactions encode non-verbal signatures of psychological function with biomarker accuracy. Let me start with why this matters.

Why passive, scalable measurement matters

  • Mental illness is the leading cause of disability worldwide — yet symptoms often go undetected for years
  • Language-based tools (interviews, questionnaires) are limited by availability, introspection, stigma, and language barriers
  • Existing biomarkers (PRS, neuroimaging, wearables) typically yield $R < 0.2$ and $\text{AUC} \approx 0.55\text{–}0.75$ — expensive, static, low-throughput
  • Cursor & touchscreen activity are generated by every device, recorded continuously at zero cost, and independent of language or social expectations
Core gap: No single tool achieves sufficient accuracy, cost-efficiency, and ecological validity at the same time. Can routine digital behavior close this gap?
The motivation is straightforward. Mental illness is massively prevalent but underdetected. Language-based tools are the gold standard but they don't scale. Existing biomarkers are expensive and capture static traits, not dynamic states. Human-computer interactions are uniquely promising because they're already being generated by billions of people every day at zero cost. The question is: do they actually encode mental health? Core idea: map digital behavior to mental state

[Figure 1 placeholder — Schematic showing each participant represented in two spaces: the space of digital behavior $X$ (movement features $x_1$–$x_C$) and the space of mental health $Y$ (self-report dimensions $y_1$–$y_Q$). MAILA maps from $X$ to $Y$.]

Figure 1. MAILA predicts mental states from cursor and touchscreen activity. Each participant is represented in a space of digital behavior $X$ (left) and a space of mental health $Y$ (right). By mapping from $X$ to $Y$, MAILA predicts self-reported mental health from human–computer interactions.

The core idea is simple. We project each participant into two spaces: one defined by how they move the cursor or touchscreen, and one defined by their self-reported mental health. MAILA learns a mapping from the first to the second. Crucially, the movement features are derived from the dynamics of interaction — speed, curvature, pauses — not from what the person actually answered. Transition: Let me summarize the key contributions.

Key contributions

  1. Dataset: ~20,000 cursor & touchscreen recordings from 9,000 participants, labelled with 1.3 million mental-health self-reports across 13 dimensions
  2. Biomarker-level accuracy: Predicts distress ($R = 0.26$), wellbeing ($R = 0.18$), and classifies self-identified depression (AUC = 0.64) and OCD (AUC = 0.7) from movement alone
  3. Dynamic tracking: Detects within-person changes in mental health (AUC = 0.73), captures circadian fluctuations, and tracks moment-to-moment arousal and valence
  4. Generalization: Across devices, tasks (survey, game, drawing), time, demographics, and an external dataset of naturalistic computer use
  5. Beyond language: Cursor movement predicts unique variance in belief instability beyond self-reports; reveals 3 orthogonal dimensions of mental health
Five key contributions. First, the dataset — to our knowledge the largest collection of HCI data labelled with mental health. Second, prediction accuracy that matches or exceeds established psychiatric biomarkers. Third, dynamic tracking of within-person change and circadian patterns. Fourth, broad generalization across contexts. And fifth, evidence that movement encodes information about cognition beyond what language captures. Data structure: randomized response interface

[Figure S11 placeholder — Randomized response interface: response line at random position/orientation/length on each trial; screen coordinates orthogonal to self-report content]

Figure S11. Randomized response mapping ensures cursor/touch position is orthogonal to the mental-health rating. Screen coordinates carry no above-chance information about self-reports ($R = 0.01$).

Key design features
  • 67 items (53 distress + 14 wellbeing) on continuous 0–1 scale
  • Response line: random position, orientation, length per trial
  • Question order randomized per participant
  • MAILA receives entire trajectory — no response labels
Resulting matrices
  • $X$: $N \times C$ movement feature matrix ($C = 500$ motifs)
  • $Y$: $N \times Q$ mental health matrix (items, dimensions, global)
Psychometrics: Cronbach's $\alpha = 0.91$ overall; test–retest $R = 0.86$ (<1 week), declining to $R = 0.69$ after 8 weeks.
A critical design feature is the randomized response interface. On every trial, the response line appears at a random position, length, and orientation. This means the same mental health rating — say, "not at all distressed" — maps to different screen locations on different trials. MAILA never sees which question was answered or where the response was given. It only gets the full cursor trajectory. This decouples movement dynamics from response content. The psychometric properties of the questionnaire are strong. Transition: Now let me walk through the pipeline.

Here's the full pipeline. Raw trajectories are screen-normalized, then segmented into overlapping windows of 100 samples. Each segment is passed through a pretrained LSTM autoencoder that compresses it into a 50-dimensional embedding. All embeddings across all participants are pooled and clustered into 500 motifs via K-means. Each participant's fingerprint is a distribution over these 500 motifs — what fraction of their movements belong to each cluster. This gives us the N-by-500 feature matrix X. Finally, we train one SVR per mental health dimension to predict Y from X. Transition: Let me explain the autoencoder in more detail.

Representation learning: LSTM autoencoder

[Figure S4 placeholder — 25 example cursor trajectories: original (blue) vs. reconstructed (red) by the LSTM autoencoder. Reconstruction loss = $7.66 \times 10^{-5}$.]

Figure S4. Autoencoded cursor movements. Original (blue) and reconstructed (red) trajectories. Final validation loss: $5.2 \times 10^{-5}$.

Architecture
  • Encoder: LSTM ($H = 64$) → final hidden state → linear + sigmoid → 50-dim latent $z \in (0,1)^{50}$
  • Decoder: LSTM ($H = 64$) conditioned on $z$ → reconstructed 2D positions
  • Trained on external Boğaziçi dataset (100 epochs, MSE loss)
  • Frozen and applied to MAILA data
From embeddings to motifs
  • Pool all segment embeddings across $N$ participants
  • K-means → $C = 500$ clusters = movement motif library
  • Per participant: proportion of segments per cluster → fingerprint
The autoencoder is the core representation engine. The encoder LSTM processes each 100-sample trajectory and compresses it into a 50-dimensional latent vector. The decoder reconstructs the trajectory from this vector. Critically, it was pretrained on an independent naturalistic dataset and then frozen — no fine-tuning on MAILA data. This means the representations capture general movement dynamics, not task-specific patterns. We then cluster these embeddings into 500 motifs and compute each participant's distribution over them. Transition: Now the modeling and validation design.

Modeling & validation design

Prediction
  • One SVR (RBF kernel, $C = 1$, $\epsilon = 0.1$) per mental-health feature
  • 5-fold CV with non-overlapping participant IDs — no leakage
  • K-means centroids fit on training folds only; frozen for test
Generalization tests
  • Models trained on baseline → frozen for follow-up, survey, game, touch, external
  • No retraining or domain adaptation
Metrics
  • Spearman $\rho$ — rank-order correspondence
  • Normalized RMSE ($e$) — absolute error scaled by outcome range
  • AUC — binarized at 10th, 25th, 50th, 75th, 90th percentiles
Significance
  • Permutation tests ($n = 1000$ shuffles) for all primary metrics
  • LME models for repeated/hierarchical structures
The prediction model is deliberately simple: one SVR per mental-health dimension. The key safeguard is that participant IDs never leak between folds — all data from one person are in the same fold. K-means centroids are fit on training data only and frozen at test time. Generalization is tested by freezing the entire pipeline trained on baseline and applying it without any retraining to different datasets. We use three complementary metrics and permutation tests for significance. Transition: Now let's see the results.

Limitations & open questions

  • Effect sizes are modest at the individual level: $R \approx 0.15\text{–}0.26$; individual prediction errors of 17–25%. Group-level accuracy is high, but individual screening requires further improvement
  • Calibration context: Models trained during mental-health self-reflection. Whether this context enhances or constrains generalization to fully unconstrained use is an open question
  • Online sample: Diverse (96 nationalities, 18–85 years) but recruited via Prolific; not representative of all populations, including non-digital-native users
  • Self-report ground truth: Not clinical diagnosis — self-identified depression/OCD, not clinician-verified
  • Privacy & ethics: Passive decoding raises serious concerns about consent, autonomy, misuse in hiring, insurance, law enforcement. Regulatory frameworks essential
  • Not a clinical tool: Must not replace clinical judgment or personal narratives; risk of reducing mental health to quantifiable patterns
The authors also demonstrate that HCI data can be used for lie detection (AUC up to 0.89) and identity tracking (AUC = 0.58), underscoring the urgent need for safeguards.
Several important limitations. Individual-level correlations are modest — R around 0.2 means substantial uncertainty for any single person. The calibration paradigm involves self-reflection about mental health, which may create a specific cognitive context. The sample is online and self-identified, not clinically verified. And there are serious ethical concerns: the same signals that enable mental health prediction could be misused for profiling, discrimination, or surveillance. The authors themselves demonstrate that cursor data carry identity information and can detect fabricated self-reports. This dual-use challenge demands strong regulatory frameworks. Transition: What are the implications?

Implications

Scientific
  • New measurement modality: Non-verbal, data-driven signatures of mental health from routine digital behavior
  • Beyond the p-factor: 3 orthogonal PC dimensions show HCI encodes the quality, not just level, of distress
  • Behavioral phenotyping: Cursor embeddings predict cognitive traits (belief instability) beyond self-reports — convergent validity for computational psychiatry
  • Dynamic states, not static traits: Within-person change tracking and circadian dynamics
Practical
  • Population monitoring: Near-ceiling group-level accuracy at $N > 100$ — real-time public mental health surveillance at scale

  • Input: 100 × 2 screen-normalized positions
  • Encoder: LSTM ($H=64$) → $h_L$ → linear ($64 \rightarrow 50$) + sigmoid → $z \in (0,1)^{50}$

Appendix: Metric definitions

Spearman $\rho$ (R)

Rank-order correspondence between predicted and observed values. Non-parametric; robust to outliers. Captures whether the model preserves the relative ordering of mental health across participants.

Normalized RMSE ($e$)

$e = \sqrt{\text{MSE}} / \text{range}(y)$. Absolute error scaled by outcome range. Values of 17–25% indicate the model captures the majority of variance at the group level but has meaningful individual-level noise.

AUC-ROC

Continuous SVR outputs treated as ranking scores; ground truth binarized at the 10th, 25th, 50th, 75th, and 90th percentiles. Evaluates sensitivity across multiple thresholds along the mental-health continuum, not just a single clinical cutoff.

Permutation tests

$n = 1000$ iterations with randomly shuffled targets. $p$-value = proportion of permuted metrics ≥ observed. Provides non-parametric significance without distributional assumptions.

For reference, here are the formal definitions of the metrics used throughout. Spearman rho for rank-order, normalized RMSE for absolute error, and AUC computed at multiple percentile thresholds. All significance is assessed via permutation tests with 1,000 iterations.
