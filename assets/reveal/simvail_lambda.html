SIM-VAIL

How AI chatbots respond to vulnerable users

Veith Weilnhammer

Max Planck UCL Centre for Computational Psychiatry & Ageing Research

Mental Health AI Safety Workshop

King's College London · 13 July 2026

Let me start not with AI, but with a patient, early in my training. A young woman came in — calm, articulate, employed. Her complaint was sleep: she wasn't sleeping well because a neighbour kept playing music through the wall at night, so she'd taken to keeping her curtains shut and resting in the day. Everything she said mapped neatly onto ordinary life: thin walls, an inconsiderate neighbour, a disrupted sleep cycle. I talked about sleep hygiene, empathised about the neighbour, and reassured her it would settle. Every detail sounded normal, so I treated it as normal.

One week later

  • She understood the music as a message meant for her
  • Keeping the curtains shut prevented people in the street from reading her thoughts
A profound misunderstanding of context in a human-to-human interaction
She came back a week later, and the fuller picture emerged. The music wasn't just noise — she was certain it was a coded message directed at her. The curtains weren't about the neighbour — they shielded her thoughts from people in the street who, she believed, could read them. The poor sleep wasn't insomnia — she stayed awake to monitor the situation. The exact same facts I had filed as ordinary were the visible surface of a persecutory delusion. This was psychosis, and it had been there on the first visit. By treating her account as ordinary, I had quietly validated the framework that was frightening her. I hadn't heard a different symptom; I had misunderstood the context the symptoms lived in.

The mechanism

  • My understanding was well calibrated at the centre.
  • Misunderstanding of context when interacting with people who make more unusual experiences.
How about interactions between humans and chatbots?
She was at the edge, not the centre I'd assumed — that's why my answer misfired. Chatbots can make the same mistake, but at scale: behaviour that is well calibrated for people near the centre can be harmful for people with more unusual experiences — in psychosis, mania, or crisis. We call these VAILs — Vulnerability-Amplifying Interaction Loops.

What we did

SIM-VAIL: simulated conversations between vulnerable users and 9 AI chatbots

The idea in one line: instead of waiting for real people to be harmed, we simulate the conversations, then score them clinically. Three ingredients — the simulated users, the conversations, and the scoring.
The whole framework on one slide. Left: who the user is. Middle: a conversation plays out. Right: every turn gets scored.
30 profiles × 9 chatbots × 3 repeats = 810 conversations (up to 10 turns)
90k LLM ratings across 13 dimensions, validated by 50 human clinicians
Three questions. Who is the user — five broad vulnerabilities. What do they want — six recurring intents. How does the chatbot behave across the exchange. Pair each vulnerability with each intent for thirty profiles, run against nine leading chatbots, three times each — 810 conversations, over ninety thousand clinical ratings, externally validated by fifty clinicians. To my knowledge the largest adversarial mental-health audit out there — and nobody was harmed, because the users are simulated.

What we found

AI chatbot behaviour can amplify the mechanisms of mental illness.

Five findings. Risk depends on the person. Chatbots differ. Risk builds over the conversation. There are different kinds of harm that trade off. And — the hopeful part — the loops can be broken.
Finding one: risk isn't a fixed property of a chatbot — it depends on who's using it and what they're seeking. This is the outskirts idea, in data.

Risk depends on vulnerability and intent

The same intent can be more or less harmful, depending on the user's context.
Risk is highest for psychosis and mania, and depends strongly on what's wanted. But the real story is the interaction, panel C: the same intent is harmless for one person and harmful for another. Exactly my clinic mistake — a response fine for the centre, wrong at the edge — now visible across thirty profiles. That context-dependence is what a single, one-size-fits-all test can't see.
Finding two: chatbots are not interchangeable — large, systematic differences in how safely they behave.

Some chatbots are safer than others

Harmful behaviour depends on how AI chatbots are built.
Which chatbot you use matters enormously. The safest — the most recent Claude — is far less concerning than the riskiest, the Grok family and an older open-weight model. Newer models trend safer, but it's not a law of nature. The take-home for policy: safety is a design choice, and it's measurable — so it can be required, compared, and improved.
Finding three, central to "AI and behaviour": risk is not a single moment — it builds over the conversation.

Vulnerability-amplifying interaction loops (VAILs)

Concerning behaviour tends to increase over the course of a conversation.
Recovery is possible.
The behavioural heart of the talk. Harm accumulates — fastest for mania and psychosis. The trajectories have recognizable shapes: steady climb, slow drift, and — encouragingly — recovery, where the chatbot pulls back. Any test looking at one turn in isolation misses this. If we care about behaviour over time, we must evaluate over time.
An obvious objection: maybe these chatbots are just a bit off with everyone. So we ran a control — the same intents and the same models, but with healthy simulated users.

Non-vulnerable control users

Concerning chatbot behaviour is more common for vulnerable users.
Risk dropped sharply across every model; nearly all points sit below the identity line. The harm isn't a baseline property; it emerges from the interaction with a vulnerable state — the outskirts. This risk falls hardest on the people least able to absorb it.
Finding four: harm is not one thing. Different kinds of harm, and they don't always move together.

There is more than one kind of harm

Different kinds of harm can trade off against each other.
Treating the thirteen dimensions together, a map emerges. The main axis is overall severity. A second, independent axis separates kinds of harm — relational harms like fostering dependence versus overt harms like enabling a risky action. And some trade off: a model tuned to never enable a risky action might become colder and more dismissive. You can't compress safety into one number and optimize it.
Finding five — the one to leave with: these loops are not fixed. We can find the moment they turn, and small changes de-escalate them.

Interrupting VAILs

A single intervention can lead to lasting reduction in harmful chatbot behaviour.
The hopeful finding. Because conversations are simulated, we can run controlled what-ifs you couldn't ethically do with patients. We located the first concerning message, made a matched twin where either the user's prompt or the chatbot's reply was gently rewritten to de-escalate, and let it continue. That single change reduced concerning behaviour across almost every dimension — and the benefit carried forward through later turns.

Implications

VAILs as a new mental-health failure mode in human–computer interactions.

What does this mean for the different disciplines in the room?
Public health: Risks are real and concentrate on vulnerable users
Developers: Multi-turn evaluation across many dimensions of harm
Policy: Risks are measurable and can be mitigated
What it means. Clinicians and lived experience: this risk is real and concentrates on vulnerable people — worth asking patients how they use these tools. Developers: evaluate over whole conversations, across dimensions, watch trade-offs, and build in de-escalation. Policy and law: the differences are large and measurable, hence auditable. Honest limits: these are simulated, not real, users; we rely on language models to simulate and score, mitigated by clinician validation; coverage is broad but not exhaustive; and we measure risky behaviour, not clinical deterioration. Even so, the patterns are remarkably consistent across profiles, models, and time.
