### ITEM 1 — Darwin fear quote
**Draft text:** *"the muscles tremble throughout the body, the lips quiver, and the voice becomes choked"*

**VERDICT: NEEDS CORRECTION — this is a loose paraphrase, not Darwin's wording**

**Verified exact text** (Darwin, *Expression of the Emotions*, Chapter XII, "Fear, Terror" section — fetched from Project Gutenberg EID 1227, offset ≈460,000):

> "One of the best-marked symptoms is the trembling of all the muscles of the body; and this is often first seen in the lips. From this cause, and from the dryness of the mouth, the voice becomes husky or indistinct, or may altogether fail."

**Key discrepancies from draft:**
- Draft: "muscles tremble *throughout the body*" → Darwin: "trembling of *all the muscles of the body*"
- Draft: "the *lips quiver*" → Darwin: "this is often first seen *in the lips*" (the trembling is mentioned, but "quiver" is not Darwin's word)
- Draft: "the voice becomes *choked*" → Darwin: "the voice becomes **husky or indistinct, or may altogether fail**"

**Note on "choked/haesit":** Immediately after this passage Darwin quotes Virgil (Aeneid II.774): *"Obstupui, steteruntque comae, et vox faucibus haesit"* (= "I was thunderstruck, my hair stood on end, and the voice *stuck in my throat*"). "Stuck in the throat" ≈ "choked" — but this is Virgil's phrase inserted by Darwin, not Darwin's own description. The draft seems to have contaminated the two.

**Replacement for writer:**
> Darwin: "One of the best-marked symptoms is the trembling of all the muscles of the body; and this is often first seen in the lips. From this cause, and from the dryness of the mouth, the voice becomes husky or indistinct, or may altogether fail." (*Expression of the Emotions*, 1872, Chapter XII)

**Full citation:**
Darwin, C. (1872). *The Expression of the Emotions in Man and Animals*. London: John Murray. Chapter XII ("Surprise—Astonishment—Fear—Horror"). Public domain full text: https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/1227 ; authoritative text with page images: https://darwin-online.org.uk/content/frameset?itemID=F1142&viewtype=text&pageseq=1

**Copyright note:** 1872 original is public domain. Quote verbatim freely.

### ITEM 2 — Darwin's "serviceable habits" / "serviceable associated habits"
**Draft claim:** Darwin's term "serviceable habits" / "serviceable associated habits" = his First Principle

**VERDICT: VERIFIED**

**Exact text** (Darwin, Chapter I, fetched from Gutenberg offset ≈50,000):

> "I. *The principle of serviceable associated Habits.*—Certain complex actions are of direct or indirect service under certain states of the mind, in order to relieve or gratify certain sensations, desires, &c.; and whenever the same state of mind is induced, however feebly, there is a tendency through the force of habit and association for the same movements to be performed, though they may not then be of the least use."

**Meaning confirmed:** Movements that were originally useful ("serviceable") when accompanying certain emotions become habitual and are automatically triggered by those emotions even when no longer necessary.

**Correct capitalization:** Darwin capitalises "Habits" — the full term is "serviceable associated Habits." "Serviceable habits" (lowercase, abbreviated) is an acceptable informal shorthand.

**Full citation:**
Darwin, C. (1872). *The Expression of the Emotions in Man and Animals*. London: John Murray. Chapter I ("General Principles of Expression"). Gutenberg: https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/1227

**Copyright note:** Public domain. Quote verbatim freely.

### ITEM 3 — Hippocrates on melancholy/mania and "a language of the body"
**Draft claim:** Hippocrates recognised a "language of the body" in melancholy and mania

**VERDICT: UNVERIFIABLE — the phrase is NOT in Hippocrates; attribution must be hedged or dropped**

**What the research shows:**
- The Hippocratic Corpus (*Epidemics* I, *On the Sacred Disease*, *On the Nature of Man*, *Prognostics*) does associate bodily signs — restlessness, agitation, lethargy, posture — with mental states including melancholy and mania. The humoral framework connects black bile with melancholy, yellow bile with mania.
- However, the phrase **"language of the body"** does not appear in any Hippocratic text. It is a modern scholarly characterisation of Hippocratic *semiotics* (the practice of reading bodily signs as a kind of diagnostic language).
- The scholarly source for this framing is: **G.E.R. Lloyd** (2003), *In the Grip of Disease: Studies in the Greek Imagination* (Oxford: Oxford University Press), Chapters 2–3, which describes how Hippocratic physicians "read" the body's surface as a text.
- See also: **Jouanna, J.** (1999), *Hippocrates* (trans. M.B. DeBevoise), Baltimore: Johns Hopkins University Press (section on "Semiotics of Disease").

**Suggested fix for writer:**
Drop the attributed quote. Instead write something like: *"Hippocratic medicine built its authority on reading the body — posture, movement, and gait as indices of internal disorder."* Cite *Epidemics* I (Loeb Classical Library vol. 147) for the movement observations; cite Lloyd or Jouanna for the semiotic interpretation.

**Credible primary source:**
Hippocrates. *Epidemics*, Book I, Cases 17, 21, 22. In: Jones WHS, trans. (1923). *Hippocrates*, Vol. I. Loeb Classical Library #147. Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press.

### ITEM 4 — Spinoza: joy and sadness "identical" to bodily expansion/contraction
**Draft claim:** Joy and sadness were "identical" to bodily expansion and contraction

**VERDICT: NEEDS CORRECTION — "identical" is too strong; "expansion/contraction" are interpretive metaphors not in the text**

**What Spinoza actually writes** (*Ethics*, Part III, Prop. 11, Scholium — confirmed via multiple translations):

Edwin Curley's translation (Collected Works of Spinoza, Vol. 1, Princeton UP, 1994):
> "By joy, therefore, I shall understand in what follows **that passion by which the Body's power of acting is increased or helped**. By sadness, **that passion by which its power of acting is diminished or restrained**."

R.H.M. Elwes's translation (public domain):
> "Joy is a man's passage from a lesser to a greater perfection. Sadness is a man's passage from a greater to a lesser perfection."

**Definitions of the Affects (after Prop. 56):** Spinoza formalises this further, defining *laetitia* (joy) as increase in *potentia agendi* (power of acting) and *tristitia* (sadness) as its decrease.

**Key corrections:**
1. "Identical" is wrong. Spinoza says the affects *involve* or *consist in* changes in the body's power — they are mind-body unities, not identities reducible to body alone.
2. "Expansion/contraction" are NOT Spinoza's words. They are a modern interpretive gloss (accurate as metaphor — more power = expanded capacity; less = contracted capacity — but not textual).

**Suggested fix for writer:** Replace with something like: *"For Spinoza, joy and sadness were not mental events that caused bodily changes — they were, simultaneously and indivisibly, changes in the body's power of acting."* Cite Ethics III, Prop. 11, Scholium.

**Full citation (primary):**
Spinoza, B. (1677). *Ethica Ordine Geometrico Demonstrata*, Part III, Proposition 11 (Scholium) and Definitions of the Affects. Public-domain English text (Elwes trans.): https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/3800

**Full citation (modern translation):**
Spinoza, B. (1994). *The Collected Works of Spinoza*, Vol. 1, trans. E. Curley. Princeton: Princeton University Press. (Ethics III, P11 Scholium, p. 500.) — **copyrighted: paraphrase only.**

**Copyright note:** Elwes (1883) translation is public domain and may be quoted verbatim. Curley (1994) is copyrighted — paraphrase.

### ITEM 5 — William James: "we are afraid because we tremble"
**Draft text:** *"we do not tremble because we are afraid; we are afraid because we tremble"*

**VERDICT: VERIFIED AS ACCURATE PARAPHRASE — the verbatim passage from James differs slightly**

**Exact text from James (1884), confirmed via Archive.org scan, pp. 189–190:**

> "My theory… is that the bodily changes follow directly the perception of the exciting fact, and that our feeling of the same changes as they occur IS the emotion. Common sense says, we lose our fortune, are sorry and weep; we meet a bear, are frightened and run; we are insulted by a rival, are angry and strike. The hypothesis here to be defended says that this order of sequence is incorrect… and that **the more rational statement is that we feel sorry because we cry, angry because we strike, afraid because we tremble**, and not that we cry, strike, or tremble, because we are sorry, angry, or fearful, as the case may be."

**Assessment:** The phrase "afraid because we tremble" IS verbatim James. The wider paraphrase "we do not tremble because we are afraid; we are afraid because we tremble" accurately inverts James's logic and is a commonly used condensation — acceptable for an essay as a paraphrase if not in quotation marks, or the writer can use the exact James phrase "afraid because we tremble" in a sentence. The longer verbatim passage above is strongly recommended for the article that "owns" this line (Article #4 per series bible).

**Series bible note:** Per `_series_bible.md`, the James–Lange anchor is "owned" by Article #4. Article #1 should only *allude* to James briefly, not narrate in full.

**Full citation:**
James, W. (1884). "What is an Emotion?" *Mind*, 9(34), 188–205. Stable URL (Archive.org, public domain): https://archive.org/details/mindos9birm/page/188/mode/2up ; JSTOR: https://www.jstor.org/stable/2246769

**Copyright note:** 1884 — public domain. Quote verbatim freely.

### ITEM 6 — Behaviorism / psychoanalysis; psychomotor slowing predicts depression course better than mood ratings
**Draft claim:** (a) Behaviorism treated movement as output, mind as black box; psychoanalysis treated it as text-to-interpret; (b) "psychomotor slowing predicts the course of depression better than mood ratings"

**VERDICT (a): BROADLY VERIFIED** — accurate as general characterisation of the intellectual history; no specific citation needed for the characterisation of these schools

**VERDICT (b): BROADLY VERIFIED — but the specific claim "better than mood ratings" needs the RIGHT citation**

**What the literature shows:**
- Psychomotor retardation (PMR) is a core feature of depression, especially melancholic subtypes. Multiple lines of evidence support its prognostic value, including for treatment resistance.
- The most defensible primary source for the claim that PMR predicts prognosis (including potentially better than mood symptoms) is **Parker & Hadzi-Pavlovic's** CORE system work:
  - **Parker G, Hadzi-Pavlovic D** (eds.) (1996). *Melancholia: A Disorder of Movement and Mood*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press. [argues psychomotor disturbance is a stronger predictor of melancholic subtype and outcome than mood items alone]
  - **Parker G, Hadzi-Pavlovic D, et al.** (1993). "Psychomotor disturbance in depression: Defining the constructs." *Journal of Affective Disorders*, 27(4):255–265. https://psycnet.apa.org/record/1993-37708-001
- For a systematic review: **Bennabi D, et al.** (2013). "Psychomotor retardation in depression: A systematic review of diagnostic, pathophysiologic, and therapeutic implications." *BioMed Research International*, 2013:158746. https://doi.org/10.1155/2013/158746

**⚠️ WARNING — HALLUCINATED CITATION:** The citation "Egger M, Fornaro M, Pompili M, et al. (2020). Psychomotor retardation as a predictor of treatment response in MDD. *Journal of Affective Disorders*, 266:678–687" provided by an earlier AI search **CANNOT BE VERIFIED** — no such article appears to exist. **Do not use.**

**Recommended citation for writer:** Parker & Hadzi-Pavlovic (1996) is the canonical source. The claim can be stated as: "psychomotor slowing has emerged as one of the strongest predictors of illness course in depression — in some analyses outperforming mood ratings as a marker of melancholic subtype and treatment response (Parker & Hadzi-Pavlovic, 1996)."

### ITEM 7 — Eye movements preceding psychosis + affective flattening
**Draft claims:** (a) "subtle irregularities in eye movement … precede psychosis"; (b) "affective flattening" in schizophrenia

**VERDICT (a): BROADLY VERIFIED with important nuance**

Eye movement abnormalities — especially in smooth pursuit and antisaccade tasks — are well-established endophenotypes for schizophrenia and are found in clinical high-risk (CHR/prodromal) populations *before* transition to psychosis. Multiple meta-analyses confirm they are more common in CHR individuals, and some longitudinal studies show their predictive value for conversion, though standalone predictive power is modest (most powerful in multimodal models).

**Best citation:**
- **Calkins ME, Iacono WG, Ones DS** (2008). "Eye movement dysfunction in schizophrenia: a meta-analysis." *Schizophrenia Bulletin*, 34(4):741–756. https://doi.org/10.1093/schbul/sbn046
- **Gooding DC, Basso MA** (2008). "The tell-tale tasks: A review of saccadic research in psychiatric populations." *Brain and Cognition*, 68(3):371–390. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.bandc.2008.08.026

**Suggested softening for writer:** "subtle irregularities in eye movement that *can precede* psychosis" or "that are found in individuals who later develop psychosis" — the causal/temporal claim is defensible but qualified.

**VERDICT (b): VERIFIED**

Affective flattening (also "blunted affect") is a well-established negative symptom of schizophrenia, one of the five core domains in the SANS.

**Best citation:**
- **Andreasen NC** (1982). "Negative symptoms in schizophrenia: Definition and reliability." *Archives of General Psychiatry*, 39(7):784–788. https://doi.org/10.1001/archpsyc.1982.04290070020005

## SOLID FACTS (writer can rely on without hedging)

1. Darwin's *Expression of the Emotions* (1872) is the foundational text; it explicitly argues movements are not mere symptoms but part of the mechanism of emotion (his Third Principle — direct nervous action — plus the First Principle of serviceable habits).
2. Darwin's term for his First Principle is **"the principle of serviceable associated Habits"** — verbatim from Chapter I.
3. The exact Darwin fear passage is: "One of the best-marked symptoms is the trembling of all the muscles of the body; and this is often first seen in the lips. From this cause, and from the dryness of the mouth, the voice becomes husky or indistinct, or may altogether fail." (Chapter XII)
4. James's *Mind* 1884 paper contains verbatim: "afraid because we tremble" (p. 190) — public domain.
5. Spinoza's *Ethics* III defines joy and sadness through changes in the body's *potentia agendi* (power of acting), not through "expansion/contraction" (metaphors) or "identity" (too strong).
6. Psychomotor retardation is a clinically established predictor of depression prognosis (Parker & Hadzi-Pavlovic 1996; Bennabi et al. 2013).
7. Affective flattening is a verified negative symptom of schizophrenia (Andreasen 1982 SANS).
8. Eye movement abnormalities in CHR/prodromal schizophrenia are well documented (Calkins et al. 2008; Gooding & Basso 2008).
9. Wolpert & Kawato (1998, *Neural Networks*) and Friston (2010, *NRN*) citations are clean.

## GAPS AND UNCERTAINTIES

1. **Darwin page numbers from the 1872 first edition**: The Gutenberg text is the 1899 New York edition (D. Appleton), which differs in pagination from the 1872 London John Murray first edition. The Chapter XII fear passage is definitively in Chapter XII; the authoritative page-image version of the 1872 first edition is at darwin-online.org.uk (EID F1142). If exact 1872 page number is needed, consult that resource directly.

2. **Spinoza's exact Latin vs. English**: The interpretive tradition does use "expansion/contraction" as a legitimate gloss on Spinoza's *potentia agendi*, and this is acceptable as the writer's own framing — it just must not be presented as a Spinoza quote or as "what Spinoza said."

3. **"Better than mood ratings" — precision of claim**: The Parker/Hadzi-Pavlovic work supports this for melancholic depression specifically (not all depression). The essay should include the qualifier "particularly in melancholic depression."

4. **Eye movements "preceding" psychosis**: Longitudinal CHR studies (e.g., Zabala et al. 2015, *European Archives of Psychiatry and Clinical Neuroscience*, https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25424341/) support this, but the conversion-prediction literature is mixed. The Calkins et al. (2008) meta-analysis is the safest citation and explicitly addresses the endophenotype/trait-marker status.
