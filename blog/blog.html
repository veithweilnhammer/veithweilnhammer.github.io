  • Audience: curious non-specialists + peers; explain jargon in one clause.
  • Voice: clear, calm, personal.
  • Length: whatever the idea needs.
  • Structure: free-form. Write a normal post — prose, whatever headings (if any) fit the piece. Nothing is enforced. If you do use ## headings, they become the post’s sidebar table of contents automatically (a ## Resources or ## References heading is left out of the TOC).

Substack playbook (make posts more visible)

  • subtitle — one line that doubles as the SEO/preview hook. This is the single biggest lever for opens and search.
  • tldr — one sentence placed at the very top so skimmers stay.
  • tags — 3–6 topic tags for Substack’s discovery/recommendations.
  • Cross-linking — the generator auto-appends a canonical link back to the matching blog post; add 1–2 inline links to related posts as you write.
  • CTA — a subscribe/share line is auto-appended; keep it.
  • Extra levers to apply by hand in Substack: a strong first image, publish at a consistent time, enable Recommendations, and cross-post to Notes.

THEME: The Moving Mind — reading mental life from movement

Brief (LLM context). This series treats movement as the visible surface of mental life. Its throughline: long before psychiatry measured molecules or parsed words, it read the mind from motion — Darwin’s trembling lip, the poker player’s tell, the slowed gait of depression, the flat gaze of schizophrenia. The series traces that idea across history, philosophy, and neuroscience and makes one argument in stages: that action and perception are a single inferential loop (predictive coding / active inference); that psychiatry’s reliance on self-report leaves most of the mind unmeasured; and that new tools — digital phenotyping, human–computer interaction — could recover a science of movement and meaning, if we resist mistaking data for empathy.

Voice. Essayistic and historically grounded, one foot in the humanities (Darwin, James, Merleau-Ponty, Goffman) and one in computational neuroscience (Wolpert, Friston). Each piece opens with a concrete scene — a tell, a trembling hand, a waiting-room posture — before turning to the argument. Audience: curious general readers and researchers alike. Connects directly to my own work on inference in perception and on decoding mental states from digital behavior (MAILA).

Recurring motifs (weave through every post, don’t state them mechanically):

  • Movement as symptom (measurable, mute) vs movement as expression (meaningful, relational) — every post lives in this tension.
  • Inference under uncertainty — the clinician, the poker player, and the brain are all Bayesian readers of partial evidence.
  • The inherited mind–body dualism science claims to reject but reproduces in practice (reaction times as output, voxels as cause).
  • Attunement / synchrony — the coordination of movement and meaning that makes social life possible, and what its breakdown reveals.
  • Leakage vs concealment — the body as both instrument of expression and mask (Goffman’s stage).
  • Data ≠ empathy — finer instruments, thinner phenomena; measurement must coexist with interpretation.

The arc

A ~14-part series in four movements. Order is a spine, not a straitjacket.

Part I — Foundations: the old intuition

# Working title Angle Anchors
1 The Oldest Form of Mind-Reading Movement as a window into inner life, from facial expression to everyday gesture; why science lost the thread. Darwin (1872); James (1884); Spinoza
2 The Tell Leakage and inference under uncertainty — the mind breaking into public space, from poker to the clinic. Ekman; Bernstein; Goffman (1959); Polanyi (tacit knowledge)
3 When Machines Learned to See Emotion The dream of quantifying affect through motion — what affective computing got right and wrong. Ekman & Friesen (FACS); Picard (1997)
4 The Body in the Machine A philosophical turn: perception and action as one continuous loop; thinking as an embodied act. Merleau-Ponty (1945); embodied cognition

Part II — Mechanism: the brain that moves to know

# Working title Angle Anchors
5 Motor Control as Inference The brain as a predictive engine; every movement, even a blink, as probabilistic inference. Wolpert & Kawato (1998); Shadmehr & Krakauer (2008); Friston (2010)
6 Thinking Is a Motor Act How movement doesn’t just reflect cognition but partly constitutes it. embodied cognition; active inference
7 The Non-Verbal Turn in Psychiatry How psychiatry recognized that motion can diagnose before words do — and then forgot. Kraepelin; Bleuler (1911, affective flattening); Andreasen (1979, psychomotor slowing)
8 When Attunement Breaks Catatonia, Parkinson’s, schizophrenia, autism — the loss of coordination between movement and meaning. phenomenology of synchrony; catatonia literature

Part III — Measurement: the present crisis

# Working title Angle Anchors
9 The Limits of Self-Report Why we still measure mind from what people say, not what they do; how rating scales flatten. Insel et al. (2010, RDoC)
10 The Rise of Digital Phenotyping What digital behavior promised — and why it hasn’t yet delivered individual understanding. Onnela & Rauch (2016)
11 The Mind in the Cursor Human–computer interaction as a non-verbal channel; my own work decoding mental states from digital behavior. MAILA (Weilnhammer et al.); digital phenotyping
12 Data Without Theory The recurring temptation to measure movement without meaning — from phrenology to the polygraph to micro-expression training. history of pseudo-quantification

Part IV — Ethics & future: movement and meaning

# Working title Angle Anchors
13 The Ethics of Invisible Measurement Turning ordinary interaction into data — the line between empathy and surveillance. Mittelstadt et al. (2016)
14 Toward a New Language of Behavior Imagining a shared, interpretable vocabulary of movement; recovery as the return of synchrony. Saxe (theory of mind); synthesis
