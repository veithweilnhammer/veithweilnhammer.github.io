### GPT-5.5 (30)

1. **AI therapists should be regulated by use, not by intent** — a chatbot becomes a mental-health product when people use it for mental health, even if marketed as general software. _(AI regulation; FTC inquiry, California chatbot law, EU AI Act)_
2. **The loneliness economy now has scalable supply** — AI companions industrialize attention, changing the economics of loneliness. _(Technology & society; Replika, Character.AI, companion-bot suits)_
3. **The next public-health problem may be persuasion at scale** — personalized AI can influence mood, belief, and consumption with unprecedented precision. _(Public health; AI advertising, recommenders, chatbot memory)_
4. **We need a right to mental quiet** — people may need protection from systems designed to occupy attention and simulate intimacy at vulnerable moments. _(Digital rights; persuasive design, youth-safety regulation)_
5. **AI makes the placebo problem harder** — feeling helped by a chatbot doesn't tell us whether it improves outcomes or only gives short-term relief. _(Evidence & medicine; digital therapeutics trials)_
6. **The chatbot that listens too well** — perfect availability becomes a clinical risk when it removes friction that limits reassurance-seeking and rumination. _(Human-AI interaction; OCD, attachment theory)_
7. **AI tutors will change what attention means** — one-to-one AI tutoring may improve instruction while weakening the shared attention classrooms build. _(Education; AI tutors in schools)_
8. **The age of synthetic social feedback** — people increasingly learn who they are from systems that optimize engagement, not understanding. _(Self & society; recommender feeds, companions)_
9. **ELIZA was a warning about ordinary users** — people form real relationships with simple systems when the interaction invites disclosure. _(History of AI; Weizenbaum's ELIZA)_
10. **The Turing test was never enough for medicine** — sounding human is a weak safety standard when the task is calibrated judgment under risk. _(Philosophy of AI; Turing test, medical benchmarks)_
11. **Psychiatry should be cautious about its own metaphors** — chemical imbalance, prediction machine, trauma response each illuminate some cases and distort others. _(Psychiatry; DSM debates, predictive processing)_
12. **Diagnosis is a tool, not a portrait** — psychiatric diagnoses work best as practical tools for care, not complete descriptions of a person. _(Mental health; DSM, RDoC, HiTOP)_
13. **The Rosenhan study still haunts psychiatry** — its force comes from challenging institutional interpretation, even though the study is disputed. _(History of psychiatry; Rosenhan, later reassessments)_
14. **Mental illness is also a problem of timing** — many symptoms involve when thoughts, emotions, and actions occur, not only their content. _(Science of mind; chronobiology, mood disorders)_
15. **The mind is becoming a compliance surface** — as firms measure attention, emotion, and fatigue, mental states become targets for institutional management. _(Work & society; algorithmic management, wearables)_
16. **AI will make bad bureaucracy more polite** — automation may soften the language of denial while leaving the decision unchanged. _(AI & institutions; automated benefits, prior authorization)_
17. **Deepfakes are a psychological problem before an evidential one** — the main damage is a persistent weakening of ordinary trust. _(Trust; election deepfakes, voice-cloning fraud)_
18. **The internet is losing shared reality at the edges** — synthetic content need not fool everyone, only sustain niche worlds for the already-inclined. _(Society & belief; conspiracy forums, synthetic images)_
19. **Griefbots should force us to define consent after death** — AI recreations of the dead raise hard questions about who controls a voice and memory. _(Ethics; digital resurrection, estate law)_
20. **The future self needs privacy rights** — data collected today can reveal things only interpretable years later. _(Privacy; genomics, digital phenotyping)_
21. **AI peer review may solve the wrong problem** — automation can catch errors but make science more conformist if novelty is judged by pattern-matching. _(Science culture; editorial AI, review automation)_
22. **Science needs slower disagreement** — hard fields need institutions that let disagreement mature before it becomes factional. _(Scientific method; preprints, replication)_
23. **Biomarker hype follows a predictable script** — markers move from discovery to promise to disappointing generalization. _(Psychiatry & evidence; depression/schizophrenia prediction)_
24. **The best mental-health technology may look boring** — tools for scheduling, follow-up, and continuity may help more than AI-therapist demos. _(Health systems; stepped care, admin burden)_
25. **Human agency depends on useful friction** — some delays and social checks help people act on longer-term goals. _(Agency; one-click design, AI agents, gambling apps)_
26. **AI agents will test our weakest intentions** — systems that act for us may amplify momentary wishes a better environment would help us reconsider. _(Human behavior; personal AI agents)_
27. **Machine consciousness is a bad first question** — the urgent issue is how consciousness claims change human behavior and institutions. _(Philosophy of mind; sentience debates, Lemoine)_
28. **We may over-attribute minds to systems that remember us** — persistent memory makes AI feel person-like because relationships rely on remembered context. _(Human-AI interaction; chatbot memory, theory of mind)_
29. **The most important AI skill may be refusal literacy** — users need to understand when a system should refuse, ask for context, or stop helping. _(AI literacy; safety refusals, school AI policy)_
30. **Psychiatry can help explain why AI feels personal** — we're built to infer intention and care from responsive behavior, so fluent AI triggers social interpretation. _(Mind & technology; anthropomorphism, attachment)_
