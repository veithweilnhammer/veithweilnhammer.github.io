# Writing style

The reference for how blog posts should read. Built by contrasting a **negative
example** (the first draft of *The Oldest Form of Mind-Reading*, in
`drafts/oldest-form-of-mind-reading.md`) with **positive examples** — my own
manuscripts in `writing_context/` (`cursor_mind`, `manuscript_2`,
`sim_psych_triage`, `sim_vail_v1`).

The positive examples are academic papers. For the blog, keep their **sentence
discipline and grounded thinking** but change the register: this is a **personal
blog**, written for the **widest possible audience**. Drop the paper machinery
(inline citations, figures, tables), drop jargon, and aim for low-key, intuitive
prose that a curious non-specialist can follow from start to finish. The point is
the *thinking style*, not the paper format.

## The one-line summary

Write like a clear science explainer, not an essayist. State findings and
mechanisms plainly, in the order a reader needs them, using plain words. Aim
for the register of a good popular-science article or a well-written methods
section: direct, concrete, evidence-first. Every sentence should carry
information, and no sentence should need a specialist to decode it. If a line
would sound at home in a literary essay, rewrite it. The closest model for
cadence is the author's own manuscripts in `writing_context/` — measured,
information-dense sentences that carry content, not short dramatic beats.

## Voice

- **Declarative and information-dense.** State the claim, then support it. Each
  sentence advances the argument. If a sentence only sets a tone, cut it.
- **Grounded.** Tie claims to a mechanism, an observation, a number, a named
  idea, or a concrete example. Prefer "chatbots under-triaged 40% of
  emergencies" over "the results were striking."
- **Precise nouns and verbs, few adjectives.** The positive examples almost
  never reach for a decorative adjective. Let the specifics do the work.
- **Calm, low-key authority.** Report what is true plainly. Don't build to a
  crescendo, don't perform depth. The insight should land because it's correct,
  not because the prose swells. Understatement beats overstatement.
- **Widest possible audience.** Assume a curious, intelligent reader with no
  background in the field. Anything a non-specialist couldn't follow needs to be
  explained or cut. This is the default lens for every sentence.
- **Active and intuitive language.** Prefer active verbs and plain, concrete
  phrasing over passive or abstract constructions. "The eye moves to sample the
  scene" beats "the scene is sampled by ocular movement." Use everyday words
  where they'll do.
- **Avoid jargon; define it when unavoidable.** If a technical term genuinely
  earns its place, define it in one plain clause the first time, then use it.
  Otherwise reach for the ordinary word. Never use jargon as shorthand for
  sounding authoritative.
- **Logical connective tissue.** Make the logical role of each sentence clear.
  Full academic connectives ("However," "Consequently," "Therefore," "Taken
  together") are fine and match the author's manuscripts; so are plainer ones
  ("But," "So"). What matters is that transitions sit inside real sentences, not
  used as dramatic one-word beats.
- **Cadence: measured, not punchy.** Match the sentence rhythm of the author's
  manuscripts in `writing_context/`: mostly medium-to-long sentences that carry
  several clauses of real content. Avoid strings of short, dramatic sentences,
  and avoid sentence fragments used for emphasis. A paragraph should read like a
  careful explanation, not a series of punches.
- **Use "I" and "we" the way a normal blog does — sparingly.** It's a personal
  blog, so "I" is fine when there's a genuine personal point, and "we" is fine
  for the shared human "we" ("we read each other through movement"). Just don't
  foreground the *act of writing*. Keep the focus on the subject, not on the
  author narrating themselves:
  - ✗ "Here, I argue that movement matters." / "In this post I want to show…"
  - ✓ "Movement matters because…" / "I first noticed this in the clinic, where…"
    (when the personal frame is the real point)
  - Default to writing about the subject; reach for "I" when it genuinely adds
    something, not as filler.

## One self-contained argument per post

The hard rule: **each post makes one self-contained argument.** It develops a
single claim (or poses a single question, or summarizes a single thing) from
start to finish. Five loosely related topics do not belong in one post. State the
thesis in one sentence to yourself before writing; if a paragraph doesn't advance
it, cut the paragraph. The post must stand on its own — a reader needs no other
post to follow it, and no other post is required to finish the thought.

A softer preference, not a hard constraint: where it helps, ground the argument in
a concrete real thing — a named study and its numbers, an actual case, a
documented event, a specific measurement — rather than staying fully abstract. Use
it when it sharpens the point; don't force it.

## Structure

Follow the shape of the manuscripts, loosened for a blog:

1. **Open on the real problem, stated flatly** — the concrete gap or question,
   ideally grounded in something specific (a study, case, or measurement).
   (Manuscripts open with "Access to mental-health care is severely limited" /
   "People rarely state the full clinical picture at once.") *Not* a slow
   atmospheric scene, and *not* an abstract generality if a concrete opening is
   available.
2. **Establish what's known and where it falls short** — the "However / Current
   approaches fall short" move.
3. **State the single argument** plainly, without "here I argue" / "in this post
   I'll show" framing, and without framing it as part of a series.
4. **Develop that one argument** in clear steps, each earning the next. Don't
   branch into a second thesis or a survey of loosely related points.
5. **Close on the substance** — land the one argument, plainly stated. No poetic
   coda, no "next time" teaser, and no call to subscribe.

## Each post stands alone

Posts are standalone essays, not episodes in a series. Do not add "this series,"
"in this series," next-post or previous-post links, or subscribe/follow calls to
action. A reader landing on one post should get a complete piece that never
refers to other posts or asks them to continue elsewhere. (Shared background in
`drafts/_series_bible.md` is only used to avoid repeating the same material
across posts — never to cross-link them.)

## Hard bans (these are what killed the first draft)

- **No antithesis / negation-definition constructions.** This is the biggest
  one. Ban every variant of "not merely X, it is Y" / "This is not A. It is B."
  / "not just … but …".
  - ✗ "This is not mind-reading in the supernatural sense. It is older and more
    ordinary than that."
  - ✗ "Thought does not merely produce action after the fact."
  - ✗ "A gesture is not only a report on thought but part of how thought takes
    shape."
  - Say the positive claim directly instead.
- **No pseudo-poetic scene-setting or imagery.**
  - ✗ "Before the first sentence in a clinic, there is already evidence. Someone
    pauses at the door a fraction too long. Their shoulders stay lifted after
    the coat comes off."
  - ✗ "Fear is not announced; it interferes. It enters the lips, the mouth…"
- **No evocative sensory fragment-enumeration.** Listing images to build a mood
  is the most common poetic tell. Replace it with a plain statement of what the
  cues are and what they indicate.
  - ✗ "A pause, a turned face, a change in pace signals something inward before
    a word is said."
  - ✓ "Gaze direction, walking speed, and hand movement all carry measurable
    information about a person's attention and mood."
- **No rhetorical crescendos or abstract flourishes at the end.**
  - ✗ "that mental life has a surface; that the surface moves; that another
    person is never fully transparent and never fully hidden."
- **No triadic/piled-up lists for rhythm.**
  - ✗ "posture, gesture, gaze, breath, rhythm, and hesitation." Pick the two
    that matter and move on.
- **No "the claim is simple, though its consequences are not" throat-clearing.**
  Just make the claim.
- **No punchy "setup" or announcement sentences.** Don't open a paragraph with a
  short dramatic line that advertises the point instead of stating it.
  - ✗ "Point-light experiments show how little of the body observers need."
  - ✗ "This reading is inference, and it works under real uncertainty."
  - ✓ "In 1973 Gunnar Johansson filmed people in the dark with lights on their
    joints; observers still recognized a walking figure from the moving dots
    alone, which shows how little of the body the perception requires."
- **No sentence fragments for emphasis.**
  - ✗ "And the visible signal is real." / "The cost was high."
  - State it inside a full sentence instead.
- **No antithesis / parallel "trade" constructions used for rhythm.** These read
  as clever but hollow.
  - ✗ "Behaviorism kept the movement and set the mind aside."
  - ✗ "Psychoanalysis made the opposite trade. It kept the inner life … and
    drifted away from measurement."
  - ✗ "Movement stayed on stage; meaning left."
  - Describe what each thing actually did, plainly and separately.
- **No rhetorical understatement openers.**
  - ✗ "Measurement is not the whole problem, though." / "The tension is not
    academic."
  - Say the positive point: "Measurement alone does not solve the problem,
    because…"
- **No dramatic em-dash asides or one-sentence paragraphs for effect.** Em-dashes
  are fine when they carry a real clause of content, not a one-word flourish.
- **No grand unsupported abstractions** ("cognition becomes public,"
  "the visible surface of mental life") unless immediately cashed out in
  something concrete.
- **Don't over-cite.** It's a blog. Name a source when it genuinely anchors a
  claim or a reader would want it; otherwise skip the citation. A short
  "Further reading" list at the end beats a dozen inline references.

## Before / after

Each pair shows a poetic draft line and a scientific-blog rewrite. The rewrites
lead with the claim, name the concrete thing, and drop the mood-building.

**1. Opening.**

*Draft (poetic):*
> Before the first sentence in a clinic, there is already evidence. Someone
> pauses at the door a fraction too long. Their shoulders stay lifted after the
> coat comes off. The hand offered in greeting is warm, cold, hesitant.

*Rewrite (scientific):*
> In a psychiatric interview, a lot of information arrives before the patient
> says anything. How fast someone sits down, how still they hold their body, how
> much they gesture — these all track features of their mental state. Clinicians
> read these cues constantly, but almost never measure them.

**2. The core claim.**

*Draft (poetic):*
> This is not mind-reading in the supernatural sense. It is older and more
> ordinary than that. Human beings are animals who infer one another through
> movement.

*Rewrite (scientific):*
> People infer each other's inner states from movement. Gaze, walking speed, and
> gesture carry reliable cues about attention, mood, and intention, and
> observers pick them up automatically, without training. This everyday reading
> of other people is where the harder question about measuring movement begins.

**3. A historical figure.**

*Draft (poetic):*
> Darwin's fear is not an idea locked behind the eyes. It trembles. It enters the
> lips, the mouth, the muscles of phonation. It makes speech unreliable.

*Rewrite (scientific):*
> Darwin argued that emotional expression is part of the biology of emotion, not
> a decoration added on top. In his account, fear shows up in the body —
> trembling muscles, a dry mouth, an unsteady voice — because these responses
> descend from actions that once helped animals survive. Expression, on this
> view, is a physical event you can observe, not a private feeling that stays
> hidden.

**4. The closing move.**

*Draft (poetic):*
> The promise is not a machine that finally sees through people. It is a science
> that learns to describe what clinicians, artists, parents, and lovers have
> always depended on: that mental life has a surface; that the surface moves.

*Rewrite (scientific):*
> The goal is not a machine that reads minds. It is a way to measure the
> movement-based cues clinicians already rely on, so they can be studied,
> compared across people, and checked against outcomes. That is a smaller claim
> than mind-reading, and a more testable one.

**5. Describing what a field did (the antithesis trap).**

*Draft (AI-sounding):*
> Behaviorism kept the movement and set the mind aside. Psychoanalysis made the
> opposite trade. It kept the inner life — it made hidden mental life the whole
> subject — and drifted away from measurement.

*Rewrite (plain):*
> Behaviorism studied observable movement and treated inner mental states as out
> of bounds for science. Psychoanalysis did close to the reverse: it put hidden
> mental life at the center of its account but rarely tied its claims to anything
> a second observer could measure.

**What changed in every case:** the claim comes first; abstractions are replaced
by named, concrete things (gaze, gait, gesture, a dry mouth); the negation
scaffolding and image-lists are gone; and the tone is flat and factual rather
than building toward a feeling.

## Quick checklist before publishing

- [ ] No "not X, it is Y" / "not merely" / "not just … but" anywhere.
- [ ] "I"/"we" used sparingly and naturally, never to narrate the act of writing.
- [ ] A non-specialist could follow every sentence; jargon is cut or defined.
- [ ] Language is active and concrete, not passive or abstract.
- [ ] No evocative image-lists or mood-building; cues are named plainly.
- [ ] No punchy setup sentences, fragments-for-effect, or antithesis "trade" pairs.
- [ ] Cadence matches the manuscripts: measured, information-dense sentences.
- [ ] Post stands alone: no series framing, no next/prev links, no subscribe CTA.
- [ ] Opening sentence states a real problem or claim, not a mood.
- [ ] Every paragraph adds information, not atmosphere.
- [ ] Claims are anchored (mechanism, number, example, named idea).
- [ ] Adjectives and metaphors earn their place; cut the decorative ones.
- [ ] Ending states the substantive point plainly — no poetic coda, no teaser.
- [ ] Citations are light and load-bearing; extras moved to "Further reading."
