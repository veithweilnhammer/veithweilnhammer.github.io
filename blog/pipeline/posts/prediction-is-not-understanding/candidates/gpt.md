# Evidence

Thesis: Predictive success is evidence that a system has captured some regularity in its inputs, but understanding requires additional evidence that the system represents and can use the causal or structural reasons those regularities hold.

1. Large language models can be trained as autoregressive predictors and then display broad task performance from text prompts. Source: Brown et al., 2020, “Language Models are Few-Shot Learners.” Link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.14165
2. The “stochastic parrots” critique argues that large language models trained on text form can produce fluent language while raising unresolved questions about meaning, grounding, bias, and social harm. Source: Bender, Gebru, McMillan-Major, and Shmitchell, 2021, “On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1145/3442188.3445922
3. A GPT-style model trained to predict legal Othello moves developed an internal nonlinear representation of board state, and interventions on that representation could change outputs. Source: Li et al., 2023, “Emergent World Representations: Exploring a Sequence Model Trained on a Synthetic Task.” Link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.13382
4. Analyses of Llama-2 representations found linear encodings of spatial and temporal information across several datasets, which the authors interpret as evidence for ingredients of world models. Source: Gurnee and Tegmark, 2023, “Language Models Represent Space and Time.” Link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.02207
5. Ptolemy’s geocentric system used eccentric circles, epicycles, deferents, and the equant to predict planetary positions, and it persisted until displaced by Copernican and Keplerian astronomy. Source: Encyclopaedia Britannica, “Ptolemaic system.” Link: https://www.britannica.com/science/Ptolemaic-system
6. Ptolemy’s Almagest contained geometrical models and tables for predicting the positions of the Sun, Moon, planets, and stars, and served as a basic astronomical guide for Islamic and European astronomers until the early seventeenth century. Source: Encyclopaedia Britannica, “Almagest.” Link: https://www.britannica.com/topic/Almagest
7. Predictive-processing accounts describe perception as a process in which higher-level systems predict lower-level input and update when prediction error occurs. 8. The free-energy principle frames the brain as using generative models to minimize prediction error or free energy, offering one influential version of the predictive-brain view. Source: Friston, 2010, “The free-energy principle: a unified brain theory?” Link: https://doi.org/10.1038/nrn2787

## Do not overclaim

- Do not claim that language models cannot have any internal models; Othello-GPT and representation-probing studies provide evidence that predictive training can induce structured internal representations.
- Do not claim that Othello-GPT proves ordinary chatbots understand the real world; it is a synthetic, fully specified game environment.
- Do not treat “world model” as a settled term. It can mean a useful internal representation, a causal model, a simulation engine, or a broader capacity for grounded reasoning.
- Do not claim that predictive processing says the brain is only a next-token predictor. Predictive-processing theories concern embodied perception, action, and hierarchical generative models.
- Do not imply that Ptolemy’s system matched modern astronomical accuracy. It was predictively useful in its historical context while resting on a false geocentric cosmology.
- Do not equate causal understanding with verbal explanation alone; a system may have useful structural knowledge without being able to describe it in human terms.

## Flags

- The exact boundary between prediction, representation, causal modeling, and understanding is philosophical and operational rather than settled by one experiment.
- Probing studies can show that information is present in model activations, but the interpretation depends on whether the model uses that information in the relevant behavior.
- The Ptolemaic example is a simplified historical illustration; scholarly accounts differ on how to separate Ptolemy’s mathematical astronomy from physical cosmology.
- Predictive-processing theories are influential but contested, and they should be used here only to make the cautionary brain-side point.

# Draft

Ptolemy’s astronomy is a useful warning about prediction. In the Almagest, written in the second century, he described a geocentric system in which the Sun, Moon, planets, and stars moved around a stationary Earth. The model used circles, epicycles, eccentric centers, and an equant point to match the irregular paths that planets appear to trace in the sky. For many practical purposes, it worked. Astronomers used Ptolemaic tables for centuries to predict positions, eclipses, and other celestial events, even though the physical picture behind the calculations placed Earth at the center of a universe that later astronomy displaced.

That example separates two things that often travel together. Prediction means using past and present information to forecast the next observation. Understanding means grasping enough of the structure that generates those observations to explain why they occur, how they would change under intervention, and where the pattern should transfer. A system can succeed at the first task while failing at the second. Ptolemaic astronomy captured many regularities in the apparent motion of planets, but its central physical story was false.

The same distinction matters now because predictive success has become one of the main public signals by which people judge artificial intelligence. A chatbot answers fluently, solves a programming problem, summarizes a document, or continues a conversation in the expected tone. The output fits the context, so it is tempting to infer that the system understands the subject in the way a competent person would. That inference skips the main question. Fluency shows that the model has learned regularities linking contexts to continuations. It leaves open what kind of structure the model has learned, how robust that structure is, and whether it supports causal reasoning beyond the observed pattern.

Large language models make this issue unusually clear because their core training signal is predictive. GPT-3, for example, was trained as an autoregressive language model: given previous text, it learned to continue the sequence. Modern chatbots add instruction tuning, reinforcement learning, tool use, retrieval, and safety layers, but the central capability still grows out of a system optimized on vast amounts of text to make good continuations. The result is real competence. A model that predicts text well across enough contexts must learn grammar, facts, styles, task formats, and many relations among concepts. The open question concerns the status of those learned relations.

The “stochastic parrots” debate is one version of that question. Bender, Gebru, McMillan-Major, and Shmitchell argued that large language models trained on form alone can produce fluent text without grounded meaning, while also reproducing biases and harms present in their data. The force of the critique comes from the gap between linguistic surface and worldly cause. Text contains traces of the world while remaining an indirect record of human language. A model can learn that certain words, arguments, and examples tend to appear together without learning the conditions that make those statements true.

The strongest reply to a shallow version of that critique comes from work showing that prediction can induce internal structure. In Othello-GPT, Li and colleagues trained a GPT-style model to predict legal moves in the board game Othello from move sequences. The model had no built-in board or rules, yet probes found an internal representation of board state. Interventions on that representation changed the model’s outputs in ways that matched the altered board. This is important because it shows that a predictive objective can lead a system to recover something like the hidden state that generates the sequence. The model predicted legal moves partly by encoding the board.

That result should narrow the argument rather than end it. Othello is a small, closed world with exact rules, complete state, and a clear notion of legal continuation. Natural language is generated by a messier process involving perception, bodies, institutions, social practices, private intentions, and a physical world that text only samples indirectly. Evidence that a model encodes a board in Othello supports the possibility of internal structure. It does not show that a general chatbot has the right causal model for medicine, law, physics, or another person’s life.

Other representation studies point in the same direction. Gurnee and Tegmark reported that Llama-2 models contain representations of space and time across datasets involving places, historical figures, artworks, and news. Findings like these make a simple “surface statistics only” story less plausible. They also leave the harder interpretation problem in place, because a discovered representation is still only one piece of evidence. A map-like encoding of cities can help predict text about geography, but understanding a city also involves transport systems, political borders, economic relations, and the many ways those structures change when something intervenes.

The useful conclusion is modest and demanding: prediction can produce representations, and predictive accuracy alone does not tell us which representations are present. The evidence has to come from more targeted tests. Does the system generalize when the surface form changes but the structure remains? Does it fail when a spurious correlation disappears? Can an intervention on an internal variable change behavior in the predicted direction? Can the system transfer the same structure to a new case where memorized continuations are unavailable? These questions measure the relation between prediction and mechanism, rather than treating prediction as its own explanation.

The brain case requires the same care. Predictive-processing theories describe the brain as a system that continually predicts sensory input and updates when prediction error occurs. In Andy Clark’s account, higher-level systems try to predict lower-level signals, and the remaining error drives revision. In Karl Friston’s free-energy framework, perception and action are organized around generative models that reduce expected surprise. These theories have been influential because they explain why perception is active and context-sensitive. The brain uses prior structure to interpret incoming signals, which makes perception an active process.

Yet the phrase “prediction engine” can mislead when it becomes a theory of understanding by itself. Predictive success in the brain can be local, automatic, and narrow. The visual system predicts the continuation of edges, the timing of motion, and the likely causes of sensory input. Motor control predicts the sensory consequences of action. These processes can work well without the person being able to explain the underlying cause. They can also fail systematically, as illusions and expectation effects show. A predictive brain may support understanding, but its predictions are evidence about processing, not proof that any particular process understands what it predicts.

This matters because people sometimes use the brain analogy to lower the bar for machines. If brains predict and language models predict, then perhaps prediction is enough. The analogy leaves out the features that make human understanding broader than successful forecasting. Human prediction is embedded in perception, action, bodily need, social correction, and causal intervention. A child who understands that a glass breaks when dropped can do more than complete sentences about glasses. The child can avoid dropping it, test what happens on carpet, infer why a heavier object matters, and use the same structure when thinking about a plate. The prediction sits inside a world-directed loop of action and correction.

A language model may acquire fragments of that structure from text, especially when text records many human interactions with the world. The right position is empirical caution. Some predictive systems learn useful internal models because those models are the best way to compress and forecast their data. Some predictive systems exploit correlations that collapse outside the training distribution. Many real systems do both at once, learning genuine structure in one part of the task and shallow shortcuts in another. The practical problem is to distinguish these cases before people rely on the output.

Causal testing gives a clearer standard. Judea Pearl’s work on causal inference emphasizes that association, intervention, and counterfactual reasoning answer different questions. Ordinary prediction asks what tends to happen next given what has happened before. Causal understanding asks what would happen if something were changed, what would have happened under a different condition, and which variables mediate the effect. In artificial systems, this means that benchmark accuracy should be supplemented by tests of intervention, transfer, and counterfactual stability. In people, the same idea explains why rote performance can look competent until the problem is changed.

The stakes are highest where fluent prediction is mistaken for calibrated judgment. In medicine, law, education, hiring, and mental-health support, a system may produce text that matches expert language while missing the causal structure that makes the expert judgment safe. A model can recommend a plausible next step because similar cases in text led to that answer, while failing to track the hidden variable that changes the decision. Prediction is useful evidence, and it becomes risky when a smooth output is treated as comprehension before the harder tests have been run.

A better response is to treat predictive performance as an empirical clue. It tells us that the system has captured something about the data-generating process. It does not settle whether that something is a surface regularity, a useful internal representation, a causal model, or a mixture whose reliability changes by context. The same caution applies to brains and machines. Forecasting what comes next can be part of understanding, and in many cases it is one route by which understanding is built. The proof comes when the system handles changed conditions, causal interventions, and new settings in ways that show it has grasped more than the next observation.
