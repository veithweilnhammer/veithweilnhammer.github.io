### Exact Quotes
- "The predictive brain, if these accounts are correct, is constantly trying to guess what is out there in the world: continually trying to predict the incoming sensory signal." (Andy Clark, paraphrased/quoted from *Surfing Uncertainty*, 2016)
- "Interventional experiments indicate this representation can be used to control the output of the network and create 'latent saliency maps' that help explain predictions." (Li et al., 2022, *Emergent World Representations*)

### Do Not Overclaim
- Do not claim that large language models are entirely devoid of world models; research like Othello-GPT shows they are capable of forming them under certain conditions.
- Do not claim that predictive processing proves the brain is just a statistical parrot; human predictive models are grounded in a physical environment and tested through action.
- Do not claim that prediction is a useless metric; it is highly effective for forecasting, just not synonymous with causal comprehension.

### Flags
- Determining exactly when a neural network transitions from shallow pattern matching to building an internal world model remains an active area of research. The Othello-GPT results hold for a synthetic task, but the extent to which these findings generalize to models trained on vast, messy natural language datasets is still heavily debated.

# Draft

In recent years, artificial intelligence has produced systems capable of generating highly fluent, coherent text across a vast range of topics. When a user asks a large language model a question, the model reliably outputs a sensible and often factually correct answer. It can sustain a conversation, summarize complex legal documents, and write working software code. Observers naturally react to this performance by assuming the system understands what it is saying. We intuitively conclude that a machine capable of explaining quantum mechanics or debugging a script possesses a mental model of those concepts.

This conclusion rests on a specific assumption about how intelligence works. The assumption is that producing accurate, context-appropriate language requires a genuine grasp of underlying meaning. In this view, prediction and comprehension are tightly linked. If a system can predict exactly which words should follow a given prompt in almost any scenario, we assume it must have built an internal representation of the physical or logical world those words describe.

The primary mechanism driving these models is next-token prediction. During training, the system processes massive sequences of text—billions of words scraped from books, articles, and websites. The network analyzes a string of text and calculates a probability distribution for the next word. Its internal weights are then adjusted so that it assigns higher probability to the word that actually appeared in the source text. The model minimizes the error between its forecast and the training data. Over iterations, using vast amounts of computing power, it becomes extremely good at this specific task. It learns grammatical rules, genre conventions, and factual associations simply by tracking which words co-occur most frequently across different contexts.

However, moving from predictive accuracy to the attribution of understanding involves a logical leap. Accurate prediction and genuine understanding are separable: a system can forecast what comes next extremely well while having no grasp of the underlying causes, so a model's predictive success — like the brain's — cannot by itself be taken as evidence that it understands.

History provides clear examples of accurate prediction divorced from causal reality. For centuries, astronomers relied on the Ptolemaic model of the universe to forecast planetary movements. This system placed the Earth at the center of the cosmos and explained the complex, seemingly erratic paths of planets using epicycles—smaller circles moving along larger orbital circles. When observations diverged from predictions, astronomers added more epicycles to correct the math. The mathematics of epicycles successfully predicted eclipses, guided navigation, and matched observational data with remarkable precision over hundreds of years. The system was highly effective at forecasting the next state of the night sky. 

Yet the underlying model of reality driving those predictions was entirely wrong. The planetary orbits were not circular, and the Earth was not stationary at the center. The predictive success of the Ptolemaic system did not mean its creators had captured the physical structure of the solar system. It simply meant they had found a mathematical method to map the observable patterns from a specific vantage point on Earth. Prediction worked perfectly well without causal understanding.

We see the same separation in everyday human behavior. A person can learn to operate a complex piece of machinery by memorizing a sequence of steps. They know that pressing a specific sequence of buttons causes a specific motor to start, and they can predict the machine's behavior perfectly under normal conditions. If asked what happens when a particular lever is pulled, they provide the correct answer based on past observation. But if a gear breaks or the machine is moved to a different operating environment, their ability to operate it collapses. They have acquired a set of accurate predictive rules, but they lack a structural model of how the machine's components interact. They forecast the next state successfully, but they do not understand the mechanism.

This distinction sits at the center of the current debate over large language models. On one side, researchers argue that these systems are essentially "stochastic parrots," a concept introduced by Emily Bender, Timnit Gebru, and their colleagues. In this view, language models are sophisticated statistical engines that stitch together linguistic forms based on the immense volume of text they have processed. They learn the probability distribution of human language, mapping how words relate to other words. When they generate a sentence, they reproduce patterns of co-occurrence found in their training data, without any grounding in the physical or social world. Their fluency reflects the structural regularities of the data, not evidence of internal comprehension. A model writing about a dropped apple does not know gravity exists, nor does it have a concept of an apple's physical weight; it only knows that the word "falls" often follows the word "apple" in physics texts. The system operates entirely on form, with no access to meaning.

On the other side of the debate, some researchers point out that minimizing prediction error on a sufficiently complex dataset might actually force a neural network to develop internal models of the world. A purely statistical lookup table cannot easily compress the vast, combinatorial complexity of human language. To achieve high predictive accuracy across diverse contexts, a network might need to infer the latent variables generating the text. It might be more efficient for the network to build a functional representation of gravity than to memorize every instance of objects falling.

This possibility was tested in a study by Kenneth Li and colleagues, who trained a sequence model to predict the next legal move in the board game Othello. The network received no information about the rules of the game or the two-dimensional spatial layout of the board; it only processed text transcripts of moves, similar to how a language model processes text. The model learned to predict valid moves with high accuracy. The researchers then probed the internal activations of the network and found evidence of a nonlinear internal representation of the board state. They could intervene in this internal representation—flipping the activation corresponding to a specific piece on the board—to change the network's output in predictable ways, proving the network was using this emergent model to make its predictions.

The Othello experiment demonstrates that a system trained purely on sequence prediction can induce a functional world model. But the necessity of the experiment highlights the core problem. The researchers could not determine whether the model understood the board simply by watching it predict moves accurately. The external behavior—accurate prediction—was consistent with both shallow pattern matching and the existence of a deep internal model. To resolve the question, they had to open the network, map its internal state to human concepts, and run causal interventions. The predictive success alone did not serve as proof of understanding.

The relationship between prediction and understanding is also a central theme in modern cognitive science. Influential theories of the brain, often grouped under the predictive processing framework, describe the human mind as a prediction engine. Theorists like Andy Clark argue that the brain does not passively receive sensory information and build a model from the bottom up. Instead, it constantly generates top-down hypotheses about what is out there in the world. It predicts incoming sensory signals and compares its guesses against the actual input arriving through the eyes and ears. The difference between the prediction and the sensory data—the prediction error—is sent back up the neural hierarchy to refine the internal models. In this view, perception and learning are driven by the continuous effort to guess the state of the world and minimize prediction error.

Because the brain can be modeled as a prediction machine, it is tempting to conclude that any artificial system that minimizes prediction error is doing what the brain does, and therefore must possess understanding. But the analogy is incomplete. The predictive success of the human brain is anchored in a physical body and a physical environment. Human beings do not just observe static sequences of data; we act in the world. We move our heads to change our visual perspective, we grasp objects to test their weight, and we manipulate our environment to see what happens. Our internal models are continuously tested and refined through causal intervention, linking sensory prediction to physical consequences.

Furthermore, pointing out that the brain minimizes prediction error does not mean that prediction error minimization is the definition of understanding. The brain uses prediction as a mechanism to update its models, but human comprehension involves capabilities that go beyond forecasting the next sensory input.

If accurate prediction is insufficient, what kind of evidence actually points to genuine understanding? True comprehension reveals itself when a system handles situations outside its normal operating conditions.

One primary marker of understanding is the ability to handle causal interventions. A system that understands a process knows not just what usually happens next, but what would happen if a specific variable were artificially changed. It can answer counterfactual questions. If the Ptolemaic astronomers understood orbital mechanics, they could have predicted the trajectory of an object moving through the solar system on a novel path. A statistical model can forecast the weather based on historical data, but a causal model can predict how the climate will change if atmospheric carbon is doubled, a scenario with no exact historical precedent in the training data.

Another marker is robust transfer learning. A person who understands the mathematical concept of a limit can apply it to physics, economics, or geometry. The underlying structure is abstracted away from the specific symbols used to teach it. When a system relies on shallow correlations, it typically fails when the superficial features of a problem change, even if the underlying logic remains the same. Genuine understanding allows a system to generalize across domains and recognize structural similarities in entirely new contexts.

The distinction between prediction and understanding has immediate practical consequences. As artificial intelligence models are integrated into healthcare, law, and autonomous navigation, we rely on them to make decisions that affect human lives. When a model produces a highly accurate medical diagnosis based on patient data, or writes a coherent legal brief analyzing case law, it is easy to assume the model understands human physiology or jurisprudence. We see competence in the output and assume comprehension in the process.

This assumption leads to dangerous over-reliance. If a system is merely matching statistical patterns, its reliability is bounded strictly by the distribution of its training data. It will fail silently and confidently when presented with a rare disease or an unusual combination of legal facts that alters the statistical landscape. The model does not know why a specific symptom indicates a specific disease, nor does it grasp the biological mechanism linking the two; it only knows that the symptom and disease appear together frequently in the medical texts it processed. It operates effectively within the boundaries of the familiar but breaks down unpredictably when required to reason from first principles.

Treating prediction as comprehension causes us to trust models in scenarios where they lack the structural grounding to succeed. We expect them to reason through unprecedented situations, to adapt to novel constraints, and to recognize obvious physical absurdities. When they fail, the failures often seem bizarre to human observers, precisely because we projected a human-like world model onto a statistical engine. We assume that a system capable of explaining orbital mechanics must know that a heavy rock falls faster than a feather in a vacuum, but a statistical model might assert the opposite if its training data contains enough figurative language suggesting otherwise.

Accurate prediction is a powerful engineering achievement. Forecasting the next word, the next frame of video, or the next market movement enables technologies that are genuinely useful. But mapping the correlations of an environment is a distinct property from grasping the mechanisms that govern it. A model that perfectly predicts a sequence has solved the problem of forecasting. Whether it understands the reality generating that sequence remains a separate question, and its accuracy alone can never answer it.
