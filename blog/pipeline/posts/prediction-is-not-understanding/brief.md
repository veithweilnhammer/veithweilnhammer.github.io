# Brief: Prediction Is Not Understanding, in Brains or in Machines

## Slug
prediction-is-not-understanding

## Working title
Prediction Is Not Understanding, in Brains or in Machines

## One-line angle (also the Substack subtitle / SEO hook)
A system can forecast what comes next with great accuracy while grasping nothing about why it comes next — which is why accurate prediction, in a brain or a model, is no proof of comprehension.

## Single argument (thesis)
Accurate prediction and genuine understanding are separable: a system can forecast what comes next extremely well while having no grasp of the underlying causes, so a model's predictive success — like the brain's — cannot by itself be taken as evidence that it understands.

## Concrete anchor (optional — a real example to ground the argument)
The "stochastic parrots" versus "world models" debate over large language models: one side reads their fluent next-token prediction as shallow pattern-matching, the other as evidence of internal models of the world — a dispute that turns entirely on whether prediction implies understanding.

## What this post must cover
- The core distinction: prediction is forecasting the next observation; understanding is grasping the causal or structural reasons behind it, and the two can come apart.
- Everyday and scientific examples of prediction without understanding: Ptolemaic epicycles predicted planetary positions well with a false model; a person can predict a system's behavior by rote without knowing why.
- The machine case: LLMs are trained to predict the next token, and their fluency is real, but fluency is consistent with either shallow correlation or a genuine internal model — the debate (stochastic parrots vs. world models) is exactly about which.
- The brain case, used carefully: predictive-processing accounts describe the brain as a prediction engine, but predictive success there also does not settle whether any given process "understands" — the same caution applies.
- Why the inference "predicts well, therefore understands" is invalid, and what evidence would actually bear on understanding (e.g., generalization, causal intervention, transfer).
- The stakes: over-reading predictive performance as comprehension drives both hype and misplaced trust in high-stakes settings.
