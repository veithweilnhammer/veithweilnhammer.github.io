# Brief: The Turing Test Was Never a Test of Intelligence

## Slug
turing-test-not-intelligence

## Working title
The Turing Test Was Never a Test of Intelligence

## One-line angle (also the Substack subtitle / SEO hook)
Turing proposed a test of whether a machine could pass for human in conversation — a social question — and seventy years of treating it as a benchmark for intelligence has confused the field.

## Single argument (thesis)
Turing's 1950 imitation game measured indistinguishability from a human in conversation, a social and behavioral question, not the presence of intelligence or understanding, so recent claims that large language models "pass the Turing test" settle nothing about whether they think.

## Concrete anchor (optional — a real example to ground the argument)
Alan Turing's 1950 paper "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," which explicitly replaced the question "Can machines think?" with the operational imitation game — and recent 2024–2025 studies claiming LLMs now pass it.

## What this post must cover
- What Turing actually proposed: in the 1950 paper he set aside "Can machines think?" as too ill-defined and substituted the imitation game — can an interrogator reliably tell machine from human by text alone.
- Why this is a social/behavioral criterion, not a mechanistic one: it measures an observer's ability to discriminate, which depends as much on human judgment and gullibility as on the machine.
- How the test got mythologized into an intelligence benchmark, and why that reading imports assumptions Turing was careful to avoid.
- The modern claims: recent studies reporting that LLMs pass in controlled settings, and what those results do and do not show.
- The key move: passing tells us a system can produce human-like conversation, which is a fact about output and about human perception, not about internal understanding.
- Why the confusion matters practically: using indistinguishability as a proxy for competence is dangerous where the real task requires calibrated judgment (e.g., medicine, safety).
