# Evidence

Thesis: Turing's imitation game measures whether an interrogator can distinguish a machine from a human in text conversation; that social discrimination result can be important without settling whether the machine understands, reasons, or should be trusted in high-stakes work.

## Key claims

1. Turing opened “Computing Machinery and Intelligence” by treating “Can machines think?” as a poorly defined question and replacing it with the imitation game. Source: Alan M. ## Do not overclaim

- Passing a Turing-style game does not prove consciousness, understanding, reasoning, agency, or general competence.
- Failing the game does not prove absence of intelligence, because the game privileges humanlike text conversation.
- A reported pass is always conditional on the protocol: number of parties, time limit, participant pool, base rates, prompt, model version, and scoring rule.
- Turing did make claims about machine thinking, but the imitation game itself remains an operational substitution for an ill-defined question.
- Human judges can improve, learn artifacts, or be misled by style; judge behavior is part of the measurement.
- Clinical or safety competence should be evaluated with domain outcomes, uncertainty calibration, and error costs, not with perceived humanness.

## Flags

- The 2025 Jones and Bergen result is currently an arXiv preprint; the 2024 GPT-4 paper is peer-reviewed in NAACL proceedings.
- The accessible UMBC Turing PDF header says “Mind 49,” but the journal citation is *Mind*, New Series, volume 59, issue 236, pages 433–460.
- The exact punctuation in online Turing transcripts varies slightly; the quotes above were verified against the accessible UMBC PDF, not the OUP page, which was not accessible through web fetch.
- The phrase “the Turing test” is a later shorthand; Turing's paper mainly uses “imitation game.”

# Draft

Recent claims that language models have passed the Turing test sound like claims about machine intelligence, because that is how the test is usually remembered. The strongest recent evidence is more specific. In a 2025 study by Cameron Jones and Benjamin Bergen, participants had five-minute text conversations with one human and one system, then chose which partner was human. GPT-4.5, when given a prompt that instructed it to adopt a humanlike persona, was selected as the human 73% of the time. LLaMA-3.1-405B reached 56% under the same kind of prompt, while ELIZA and GPT-4o were selected much less often. Those results show that under some controlled conversational conditions, people can fail badly at telling a language model from a person.

That finding matters, especially for online trust. It also leaves the central mistake intact if it is reported as evidence that a model thinks. Turing's test measures indistinguishability in a particular social setting. The observation is made at the boundary between a transcript and a judge. It records what a system says, what a person expects, and how well the person can detect the difference under the rules of the game. It gives no direct measurement of the system's internal mechanism, its grasp of the world, or its ability to make reliable decisions outside the conversation.

Turing's 1950 paper is usually cited as the origin of the question, “Can machines think?” The paper begins by stepping away from that question. Turing wrote that defining “machine” and “think” by ordinary usage would lead toward something like a poll about language, which he called absurd. He then made the operational move that gave the paper its influence: “Instead of attempting such a definition I shall replace the question by another, which is closely related to it and is expressed in relatively unambiguous words.” The replacement was the imitation game.

The first version of the game involved three people: a man, a woman, and an interrogator. The interrogator could communicate only through written questions and answers, and had to decide which hidden participant was which. Turing then changed one role: “What will happen when a machine takes the part of A in this game?” The machine's task was to make the interrogator misidentify it under text-only questioning. The setup deliberately removed voice, appearance, and touch, so the judgment had to rest on the written exchange.

This structure matters because the measured event is the interrogator's decision. A machine may be fluent, evasive, funny, dull, accurate, or strategically imperfect, and all of these features matter only through their effect on a human judge. The judge brings assumptions about what people sound like, what machines sound like, which mistakes count as human, and which answers seem too polished. The result therefore depends on both sides of the interaction. A better model can raise its apparent humanness, and a different judge, time limit, prompt, or comparison human can change the outcome.

Turing knew he was proposing a criterion with parameters rather than a microscope for intelligence. His well-known forecast was that, after about fifty years, computers might play the imitation game so well that “an average interrogator will not have more than 70 per cent chance of making the right identification after five minutes of questioning.” The sentence is often remembered as a threshold for thinking. In the paper, it is a prediction about a five-minute identification game. The time limit, the error rate, and the average interrogator are all part of the claim.

The mythologized version of the test compresses two different questions into one. The first question is empirical: can a machine produce conversation that makes human judges misclassify it under stated conditions? The second question is conceptual: what should we infer about intelligence or understanding if it does? Turing tied these questions together because he thought the imitation game was a useful way to make progress on a vague debate. He did not give a biological, computational, or psychological account of thinking. He proposed a public test that could be run without first settling the meaning of the word.

The reverse problem is also easy to miss. A system could be useful, reliable, or even highly capable while failing a short human-imitation game, because the game rewards one narrow form of social resemblance. A diagnostic model that speaks in constrained templates, a theorem prover that refuses small talk, or a machine that truthfully identifies itself at the start of every exchange would all perform poorly by the ordinary rules. Their failure would show a mismatch with the game, not a global absence of competence.

The recent language-model studies make the separation between the empirical game and the conceptual inference easier to see. In the 2024 NAACL study by Jones and Bergen, the best GPT-4 prompt was judged human in 49.7% of games. That was much higher than ELIZA and GPT-3.5, but still below the 66% rate for actual human participants. The same paper reported that participants mainly relied on linguistic style and socioemotional traits when deciding who was human. Their judgments drew heavily on conversational texture, including how answers were phrased and how socially natural the witness seemed.

The 2025 study sharpened the same point. GPT-4.5 performed best when prompted to present a humanlike persona. That is an important experimental fact. A persona prompt changes the model's social presentation, including its degree of confidence, informality, self-disclosure, and willingness to make ordinary-seeming remarks. If this changes the pass rate, then the pass rate is partly a measure of social packaging. It shows that humanness in the test is something an output can perform for a judge.

The result is still substantial because text conversation is a demanding human activity, and fooling informed participants in a three-party comparison is harder than producing a plausible answer in isolation. A system that can do this has crossed a practical threshold for deception, impersonation, and social presence. The result should change how we think about online identity. It should also make us cautious about any interface that relies on ordinary users noticing whether the other side is human.

The same result remains limited as evidence about mind. Many different internal processes can lead to the same transcript. A person may answer a question through memory, perception, inference, habit, politeness, or deliberate deception. A model may answer through patterns learned during training, instructions in the prompt, retrieval from context, or tool use added by a surrounding system. The imitation game observes the final conversational behavior and the judge's classification. It leaves these causes pooled together.

This gap is especially visible when the criterion moves from conversation to competence. A model can sound like a careful medical assistant while failing at the parts of medicine that matter most: asking for missing information, expressing uncertainty, following safety constraints, and weighing the cost of different errors. Medical evaluations now test models on clinical decision-making tasks because a fluent answer measures a different property from a safe decision. The same distinction applies in law, education, finance, and emergency response, where the important question is usually whether the system can do a constrained job with known risks.

This is why indistinguishability is a weak proxy for high-stakes deployment. Humanlike conversation can increase trust before competence has been established. A model that hesitates, apologizes, uses ordinary phrasing, and remembers personal details may feel more reliable than a model that gives a dry answer. Those cues are useful in human interaction because they often correlate with attention and care. In a generated transcript, they can be optimized separately from accuracy. The Turing-style setting rewards the cues that make a judge say “human,” while many real tasks require the cues that make an expert say “safe enough for this use.”

The original imitation game also contains a built-in incentive to deceive. The machine wins by causing the interrogator to make the wrong identification. That is acceptable for a thought experiment about humanlike conversation. It becomes a poor standard for systems that should disclose uncertainty, reveal limitations, and avoid false authority. If the goal is trustworthy assistance, a system that announces its machine status, refuses to guess, and asks for context may be better than a system that passes as a person for five minutes.

Keeping the Turing test in its proper place makes the recent results more useful. They show that human detection is fragile under realistic text conditions. They show that model presentation strongly shapes whether people attribute humanness. They show that the social boundary between human and machine has become harder to police by intuition alone. These are substantial findings, and they deserve better than being folded into a vague announcement that artificial intelligence has finally arrived.

The practical lesson is to match the evaluation to the claim. If the claim is that a system can pass for human in short conversation, a Turing-style experiment is directly relevant. If the claim is that a system understands a disease, can advise a distressed user, or can make a safe plan, the test has the wrong target. Those claims need benchmarks that measure the task itself, including uncertainty, error severity, robustness, and performance across cases that differ from the examples a model has seen.

Turing's contribution was to replace an unworkable question with a public procedure. The procedure remains useful when interpreted as a test of human-machine indistinguishability in conversation. Recent language models now perform strongly on that procedure under some conditions, which tells us that conversational humanness has become easy to generate and hard to detect. It settles the social question posed by the game only within the tested setting. The harder questions about understanding, competence, and safety require different instruments.
