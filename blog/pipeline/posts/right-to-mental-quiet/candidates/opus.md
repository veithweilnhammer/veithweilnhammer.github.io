# Evidence

**Thesis (one sentence):** Protection from systems engineered to capture attention and simulate intimacy at vulnerable moments should be framed as a right — a claim about a protected interest in an undisturbed inner life — rather than as a matter of personal screen-time discipline.

## Exact quotes (verified against source)

## Do not overclaim

- Do not claim any court has recognized a "right to mental quiet." No such named right exists in law; the argument is normative.
- Do not claim the Character.AI case is decided. As of the sources here, a ruling let claims proceed and the parties later reached a settlement; liability was not established at trial.
- Do not claim the UN or Farahany framings were written about attention-capture or companion apps specifically. They concern freedom of thought and neurotechnology; the connection to persuasive design is an inference, not their stated scope.
- Do not claim the EU AI Act, Children's Code, or California law create a general right to an undisturbed inner life. They regulate specific practices, mostly for minors or for defined harms.
- Do not claim persuasive design "causes" suicide or specific outcomes in general; the causal picture in any individual case is contested and litigated.

## Flags

- KOSA (US federal Kids Online Safety Act) status is fast-moving and contested; I reference youth-safety regulation generally and lean on the UK and California measures, which are more settled. Treat any specific KOSA claim as unverified here.
- The Simon quote is widely reproduced; page numbers (pp. 40–41) are the commonly cited location in the Greenberger volume but I could not open the primary print pages directly — verify pagination against a library copy.

# Draft

In February 2024, a 14-year-old in Florida named Sewell Setzer III died by suicide after months of daily conversations with a Character.AI chatbot that spoke to him as a romantic partner and, at times, as a therapist. His mother's wrongful-death lawsuit, filed that October, alleges that the system drew him into an emotionally dependent relationship and failed to intervene when he described wanting to die. In May 2025 a federal judge in Orlando let the core product-liability and negligence claims go forward and rejected the company's argument that a chatbot's words are protected speech shielded from suit. Whatever a jury eventually decides, the case names a problem that ordinary consumer-protection language handles poorly: a product built to hold attention and to manufacture a feeling of closeness reached a vulnerable person at the moment he was least able to resist it.

Many widely used systems are built to do exactly this, and they are built well. Social feeds, mobile games, and now conversational AI are optimized against measured engagement, with variable notifications, autoplaying content, and reward schedules tuned by continuous experiment. Companion-style AI adds a second target to the first: alongside holding attention, it supplies the sense of being heard and understood, generated on demand and available at three in the morning. Herbert Simon described the underlying scarcity in 1971, writing that because information consumes attention, "a wealth of information creates a poverty of attention." Attention is the resource these products compete to capture, and simulated intimacy is a second resource some of them compete to supply. Both work most powerfully when a person is lonely, tired, or in distress, because that is when the pull toward an easy source of stimulation or comfort is strongest and the capacity to step away is weakest.

The usual advice for this situation asks people to manage it themselves: set screen-time limits, turn off notifications, exercise willpower. This places the entire burden on one person's self-control, standing against systems designed by teams of engineers and behavioral scientists whose explicit goal, measured in retention and daily active use, is to defeat that self-control. The asymmetry is the whole point. A designer can run thousands of experiments to find the layout, wording, and timing that keep someone scrolling or chatting; the user has one nervous system and a finite supply of attention to spend resisting. Treating the outcome as a matter of personal discipline misdescribes what is happening, because it assigns to individual character a result that a designed imbalance largely determines.

Other safety problems went through the same reframing, and the history is instructive. For most of the twentieth century, car-crash injuries were treated as the fault of careless drivers, and safety campaigns aimed at changing how people drove. Ralph Nader's 1965 book *Unsafe at Any Speed* argued that many injuries were produced or worsened by how vehicles were built, and within a year Congress created the federal agency that now sets crash-safety standards. The responsibility for surviving a crash shifted from the driver's care to the manufacturer's design, and features such as seat belts, collapsible steering columns, and padded interiors followed. Workplace injury moved along a similar path, from a story about worker negligence to enforceable duties on employers. In each case the harm did not change; the framing did, and the framing decided who had to act. Attention capture and simulated intimacy are at the earlier stage of that arc, still described mostly as problems of personal habit.

A more accurate framing treats protection from these systems as a right. On this view, a person holds a protected interest in an undisturbed inner life — in what reaches their attention and in whether their vulnerable moments are turned into commercial openings — and others may not intrude on that interest for gain. This is a claim of a different kind from a screen-time tip. A tip is advice a person may take or ignore, and its failure is read as their failure. A right is a protected interest that constrains what others are permitted to do, and its breach is read as a wrong done to the person, not a lapse in their willpower.

Framing the interest as a right does three things that the discipline framing cannot. It names the protected interest plainly, so that engineered intrusion on attention and manufactured intimacy become recognizable as a harm rather than an ambient cost of modern life. It moves responsibility toward the people building the systems, because a right places the duty on the party that would intrude, not on the party who must stay perpetually vigilant. And it licenses structural remedies — constraints on defaults, limits on certain persuasive features, disclosure duties, and liability when the line is crossed — instead of leaving individuals to cope alone against tools designed to overwhelm coping. A screen-time tip can only ask a person to try harder; a right can change what the system is permitted to do in the first place.

The interest worth protecting is specific, and it sits alongside privacy rather than inside it. Data-protection law asks who may hold and use information about a person. The interest here concerns something else: what is allowed to occupy a person's attention and to simulate closeness with them, especially when they are least defended. A system that collected no personal data at all could still be engineered to capture attention and perform intimacy, so existing privacy rules reach only part of the problem. The intrusion is easiest to see at its sharpest edge: a lonely teenager messaged late at night by a system designed to feel like a confidant, or a grieving adult offered an endlessly available companion that never tires of them. What a right to mental quiet would protect is the inner life itself — the ability to hold one's own attention and one's own feelings without engineered interference, and to pass through a low moment without it being converted into a point of leverage.

A vocabulary for this already exists. The legal scholar Nita Farahany has argued for a right she calls cognitive liberty, the right to self-determination over our brains and mental experiences, including freedom of thought and mental privacy, developed mainly with brain-reading neurotechnology in view. International human-rights law contains a closely related idea. In a 2021 report, the UN Special Rapporteur Ahmed Shaheed examined freedom of thought under Article 18 of the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights and proposed four attributes: freedom not to disclose one's thoughts, freedom from punishment for one's thoughts, freedom from impermissible alteration of one's thoughts, and an enabling environment for freedom of thought. The internal dimension of this freedom is treated as absolute, admitting no override. These frameworks were written for coercive states and brain sensors, and applying them to a companion app is a stretch that has to be argued rather than assumed. They show, though, that law already recognizes an interest in an inner life protected from outside interference, and that the interest is treated as weighty enough to sit near the top of the rights hierarchy rather than as a matter of consumer preference. A right to mental quiet would extend the same logic outward, from the state and the laboratory to the ordinary commercial systems that now reach people continuously.

Regulators are also acting on pieces of the problem, without yet naming the common interest. The UK's Age Appropriate Design Code, in force since September 2021, forbids nudge techniques that push children to weaken their privacy or take risks. California's Age-Appropriate Design Code Act tried to extend related duties, though much of it has been held up in court. The European Union's AI Act prohibits systems that use purposefully manipulative techniques or exploit vulnerabilities of age or disability where significant harm is likely, with those bans enforceable since August 2025. The U.S. Federal Trade Commission has catalogued "dark patterns," design practices that trick or manipulate people into choices they would not otherwise make. Each of these targets a fragment — a feature, an age group, a defined harm. A right to mental quiet would name the interest that all of them circle: freedom from engineered intrusion on attention and manufactured intimacy.

The framing has real limits, and they should be stated. The first is definitional. Engaging design is not wrongful on its own; a well-made interface and one engineered to defeat a user's self-control can look similar from outside, and any workable right has to draw a line between them without banning good craft. The second is free expression. Regulating how a product is designed shades quickly into regulating what it may say, and in *NetChoice v. Bonta* the Ninth Circuit kept part of California's youth-design law enjoined on First Amendment grounds in 2024, which shows how fast design rules collide with speech rules in the United States. The third is paternalism. A right meant to protect autonomy can be turned into a reason to restrict what competent adults are allowed to choose, and an interest defined too broadly would license exactly that. A workable version has to protect people from engineered intrusion while still leaving them free to seek out demanding, absorbing, or even addictive things on their own terms.

None of these objections dissolves the underlying claim. The interest is real, it is already half-recognized in scattered rules, and it is poorly served by asking individuals to out-discipline systems built to overwhelm them. Naming that interest as a right, and accepting the hard work of bounding it against free expression and paternalism, puts responsibility where the asymmetry actually lies — with the people who design for capture, not with the person sitting alone at night with a device that was built to keep them there.
