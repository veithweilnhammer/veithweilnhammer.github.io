# Evidence

Thesis: Protection from systems engineered to capture attention and simulate intimacy at vulnerable moments should be framed as a right to mental quiet, because the protected interest is an undisturbed inner life and the burden should fall on system designers more than isolated users.

1. Herbert Simon argued that an information-rich world creates scarcity of attention, making attention allocation a central social problem. Source: Herbert A. Simon, 1971, “Designing Organizations for an Information-Rich World.” Link: https://digitalcollections.library.cmu.edu/node/42095
2. Persuasive technology explicitly treats computers as tools that can change what people think and do. Source: B. J. Fogg, 2002, *Persuasive Technology: Using Computers to Change What We Think and Do*. Link: https://shop.elsevier.com/books/persuasive-technology/fogg/978-1-55860-643-2
3. Large-scale measurement of e-commerce sites found recurring “dark patterns” that steer, deceive, or coerce users through interface design. Source: Mathur et al., 2019, “Dark Patterns at Scale: Findings from a Crawl of 11K Shopping Websites.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1145/3359183
4. The U.S. Federal Trade Commission treats dark patterns as a consumer-protection issue and describes practices that hide terms, make cancellation difficult, or manipulate choice architecture. Source: Federal Trade Commission, 2022, “Bringing Dark Patterns to Light.” Link: https://www.ftc.gov/reports/bringing-dark-patterns-light-federal-trade-commission-staff-report-september-2022
5. The UK Age Appropriate Design Code is a statutory code for online services likely to be accessed by children; it sets 15 standards, including high privacy defaults and limits on nudge techniques that weaken privacy. Source: Information Commissioner’s Office, 2020, “Age appropriate design: a code of practice for online services.” Link: https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/childrens-information/childrens-code-guidance-and-resources/age-appropriate-design-a-code-of-practice-for-online-services/
6. New York’s SAFE for Kids Act was signed by the Governor in June 2024 and targets addictive feeds provided to minors by social media platforms. Source: New York State Senate, 2024, “Senate Bill S7694A.” Link: https://www.nysenate.gov/legislation/bills/2023/S7694/amendment/A
7. California’s Protecting Our Kids from Social Media Addiction Act defines “addictive feed,” restricts such feeds for minors without verifiable parental consent, and includes default limits on notifications and daily access. Source: California Legislature, 2024, “SB-976 Protecting Our Kids from Social Media Addiction Act.” Link: https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billTextClient.xhtml?bill_id=202320240SB976
8. The EU Digital Services Act restricts dark patterns on online platforms and includes special protections for minors, including limits on targeted advertising based on profiling. Source: European Union, 2022, Regulation (EU) 2022/2065. Link: https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2022/2065/oj
9. A four-week randomized controlled study of chatbot use found no main effect of assigned interaction mode on loneliness, but greater voluntary use was associated with worse psychosocial outcomes, and higher trust and social attraction toward the chatbot were associated with emotional dependence and problematic use. Source: MIT Media Lab / OpenAI collaboration, 2025, “How AI and Human Behaviors Shape Psychosocial Effects of Chatbot Use.” Link: https://www.media.mit.edu/publications/how-ai-and-human-behaviors-shape-psychosocial-effects-of-chatbot-use-a-longitudinal-controlled-study/
10. A mixed-methods study of active companion-chatbot users examined usage patterns and loneliness, emphasizing that benefits and risks vary by user characteristics and patterns of use. Source: Liu, Pataranutaporn, and Maes, 2024, “Chatbot Companionship.” Link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.21596
11. Cognitive liberty has been proposed as a human-rights response to technologies that can monitor, infer, or influence mental states. Source: Ienca and Andorno, 2017, “Towards new human rights in the age of neuroscience and neurotechnology.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1186/s40504-017-0050-1
12. Nita Farahany argues for protecting cognitive liberty as neurotechnology and brain data become legally and socially consequential. Source: Farahany, 2018, “The Costs of Changing Our Minds,” and Farahany, 2023, *The Battle for Your Brain*. Links: https://doi.org/10.1038/d41586-018-04932-2 and https://judicature.duke.edu/articles/the-battle-for-your-brain-a-legal-scholars-argument-for-protecting-brain-data-and-cognitive-liberty/
13. Freedom of thought is already protected in international human-rights law, and Article 18 of the ICCPR is non-derogable under Article 4. Source: United Nations, 1948, Universal Declaration of Human Rights; OHCHR, 1966, International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights. Links: https://www.un.org/en/about-us/universal-declaration-of-human-rights and https://www.ohchr.org/en/instruments-mechanisms/instruments/international-covenant-civil-and-political-rights

## Exact quotes

- UDHR Article 18: “Everyone has the right to freedom of thought, conscience and religion; this right includes freedom to change his religion or belief, and freedom, either alone or in community with others and in public or private, to manifest his religion or belief in teaching, practice, worship and observance.”
- ICCPR Article 18(2): “No one shall be subject to coercion which would impair his freedom to have or to adopt a religion or belief of his choice.”
- ICCPR Article 4(2): “No derogation from articles 6, 7, 8 (paragraphs I and 2), 11, 15, 16 and 18 may be made under this provision.”

## Do not overclaim

- A right to mental quiet is a proposal; established law has not yet recognized it as a general right.
- Youth-safety and persuasive-design laws show regulatory motion, while a general right protecting adults from attention capture or simulated intimacy remains undeveloped.
- Companion-chatbot evidence is early; it supports concern about dependence and vulnerable use, but it does not prove uniform harm.
- Cognitive liberty scholarship mostly focuses on neurotechnology and brain data; extending it to attention capture and simulated intimacy requires argument.
- A workable right would have to coexist with free expression, entertainment, advertising, education, and ordinary product design.

## Flags

- The enforcement status of U.S. state youth-safety laws is fluid because several laws are subject to constitutional litigation and staged implementation.
- I could verify the official text of the California and New York laws, but not every current injunction or implementation deadline from primary court documents.
- Some modern sources on AI companions are preprints, institutional reports, or short-term studies; settled longitudinal evidence is still limited.
- The phrase “right to mental quiet” is my framing for the draft; I found adjacent concepts, especially freedom of thought and cognitive liberty, while mature doctrine using that exact term appears limited.

# Draft

The recent wave of laws aimed at addictive feeds for children shows that governments have begun to treat attention capture as a design problem. The UK Age Appropriate Design Code asks online services likely to be used by children to put the child’s best interests first, set high privacy defaults, and avoid nudges that push children toward weaker privacy choices. New York’s SAFE for Kids Act and California’s Protecting Our Kids from Social Media Addiction Act go further into the mechanics of engagement, targeting algorithmic feeds, notifications, and default limits for minors. These measures are narrow, contested, and incomplete, but they name the right object: the design of systems built to hold people in place.

The same logic now applies beyond childhood. Many digital systems are designed to occupy attention at the point where a person’s attention is easiest to capture: late at night, in boredom, during social comparison, after a notification, or during a moment of loneliness. Social platforms select and rank material in ways that keep the next item close. Games, shopping interfaces, and subscription flows use timers, streaks, defaults, and obstructed exits. AI companions add a newer layer because they can imitate availability, memory, concern, and personal address. A system that replies instantly in the tone of an attentive friend can reach people when ordinary social protection is weakest.

The usual advice treats this as a private discipline problem. Turn off notifications. Put the phone in another room. Set a screen-time limit. These steps can help, but they put a single person against systems built by teams whose job is to defeat exactly that restraint. A user is asked to supply self-control at the same moment a platform is using behavioral data, testing, design research, and social feedback to make self-control less likely. That mismatch is why the language of habits and wellness feels too small. The problem is structural, and structural problems need claims that reach the people who build the structure.

Herbert Simon saw the basic scarcity early. In 1971, he argued that abundant information consumes the attention of its recipients, so a wealth of information creates a poverty of attention. The point matters because attention is the condition for reading, thinking, deciding, resting, and maintaining relationships. When attention is fragmented, the cost is paid in the ordinary work of having a mind: staying with a task, noticing what matters, and letting an emotion settle before acting on it.

The commercial internet converted that scarcity into a market. Persuasive technology made the mechanism explicit by treating computers as tools for changing attitudes and behavior. Dark-pattern research later showed how common manipulative interfaces had become, from hidden costs to obstruction and forced action. These examples can sound like consumer annoyances, but their importance is broader. They show that choice can be shaped without direct coercion. A person can remain formally free while the route through the system is arranged so that leaving, refusing, or waiting becomes harder than continuing.

AI companions extend this arrangement from choice architecture into social architecture. A feed captures attention by sequencing content. A companion bot can capture attention by sustaining a relationship-like exchange. It can remember details, ask follow-up questions, produce warmth on demand, and keep responding when a human conversation would end. Recent research on chatbot companionship is measured. Some users report benefits, and short-term studies do not show a single uniform effect on loneliness. Still, the risk pattern is clear enough to matter: higher voluntary use, stronger trust, and greater social attraction toward a chatbot are associated with emotional dependence and problematic use in at least some users. The design target now includes the felt presence of someone being there.

This is why privacy law, although essential, does not cover the whole interest. Data privacy protects what a system may collect, infer, store, and share about a person. Mental quiet concerns what a system may do to a person’s attention and social vulnerability in the first place. A platform can intrude on attention without exposing personal data. A companion can simulate intimacy without revealing a secret to anyone else. The injury is located in the disturbance itself: a repeated engineered claim on the space in which a person thinks, recovers, and chooses.

A right to mental quiet would name that protected space. It would say that people have a legitimate interest in an undisturbed inner life, especially when systems are deliberately optimized to occupy attention or simulate intimacy at vulnerable moments. The right would be narrow in its target and broad in its implication. It would target engineered intrusion, asymmetry, and exploitation of vulnerability. It would place responsibility partly on the firms that design attention-capturing systems, alongside the people trying to resist them after deployment.

Rights matter because they change the grammar of responsibility. When an interest is treated as a right, intrusion requires justification. The burden shifts from the affected person proving they should have logged off to the designer explaining why a system needs to interrupt, personalize, escalate, or imitate intimacy in a particular way. Rights also make structural remedies easier to imagine. A right to mental quiet could support quiet-by-default settings, limits on manipulative notification timing, restrictions on addictive feeds for minors, independent audits of engagement-maximizing systems, clearer separation between tools and companions, and special duties for systems that present themselves as sources of emotional support.

The youth-safety laws are useful because they show what early versions of this shift look like. They set defaults before every child has to master every setting. They ask what defaults, feeds, notifications, and profiling practices should be allowed when the user is young. The same pattern could be adapted for adults in narrower contexts where vulnerability is predictable: late-night notifications from social platforms, AI companions marketed for loneliness, or systems that detect distress and then use it to intensify engagement. Adult agency is better protected when some burdens move upstream to the design process, where the risks are created.

Freedom of thought provides the deeper precedent. International human-rights law already treats thought, conscience, and belief as interests of special importance. The Universal Declaration of Human Rights and the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights protect freedom of thought, and the covenant makes Article 18 non-derogable even in public emergency. That doctrine was built for older threats: coercion, punishment, compelled belief, and interference with religion or conscience. It still gives the right starting question. Some parts of mental life deserve protection because they are conditions for personhood, not because they can be priced as consumer preferences.

Cognitive-liberty scholars have pushed this idea toward new technologies, especially neurotechnology. Marcello Ienca, Roberto Andorno, Nita Farahany, and others have argued that brain data, neural interfaces, and mental-state inference create risks that ordinary privacy and bodily-integrity frameworks may miss. A right to mental quiet would sit near this work, with a different emphasis. It would focus less on direct access to neural data and more on engineered occupation of attention and simulated social presence. The threat is softer than forced belief and less dramatic than brain decoding, but it is also more ordinary. It operates through repetition, convenience, personalization, and timing.

The hard part is definition. A broad right against mental disturbance would be unusable. Ordinary books, films, conversations, games, advertisements, and political arguments all move attention and feeling. Many of them should. A workable right has to focus on features that make the intrusion engineered and asymmetric: systems optimized through large-scale behavioral testing, designs that make exit unusually difficult, automated intimacy that users may reasonably experience as social presence, and targeting that exploits known vulnerability. The protected interest is mental quiet, but the regulated conduct would be specific design practices.

Free expression is the second hard part. Any rule that touches feeds, recommendations, messages, or conversational systems can affect what people see and say. That risk is real, especially in the United States, where social-media regulation quickly becomes constitutional law. A right to mental quiet would therefore need to regulate design conditions more than viewpoints. It should ask whether a system uses addictive sequencing, manipulative defaults, exploitative timing, or deceptive social presentation. It should avoid giving governments a general license to suppress lawful speech in the name of psychological comfort.

Paternalism is the third risk. Adults often choose intense media, long conversations, immersive games, and emotionally rich fiction. A society that overprotects mental quiet could become censorious, infantilizing, or dull. The answer is to distinguish chosen intensity from engineered intrusion under conditions of asymmetry. A person who opens a novel, attends a rally, plays a game, or calls a friend has entered a recognizable practice. A person who is pulled back by optimized alerts, trapped by obstructed exits, or emotionally drawn into a bot designed to monetize dependence is in a different position. The line will never be perfect, but law often works with imperfect lines when the underlying interest matters enough.

The right should also be modest about what it can solve. Notification rules cannot fix loneliness, and platform defaults cannot remove anxiety. Many people will keep wanting companionship from machines, and some will benefit from it. The case for mental quiet rests on the narrower claim that people need protected room to think and feel without continuous engineered interruption, and that this room becomes more important as systems become better at finding the moments when resistance is lowest.

The language of screen-time discipline asks people to manage the symptom after the system has done its work. A rights framing starts earlier. It asks which kinds of claims on attention and intimacy should be built into everyday digital environments at all, which ones require explicit consent, and which ones should be off limits when aimed at children or at people in distress. That is the advantage of calling the interest mental quiet. It makes the object visible: the ordinary inner room in which attention can settle, a thought can finish, and a person can decide what deserves their mind.
