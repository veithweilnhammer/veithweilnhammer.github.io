# Brief: We May Need a Right to Mental Quiet

## Slug
right-to-mental-quiet

## Working title
We May Need a Right to Mental Quiet

## One-line angle (also the Substack subtitle / SEO hook)
As systems are built to occupy our attention and simulate intimacy at our most vulnerable moments, protection from them may belong in the language of rights, not screen-time tips.

## Single argument (thesis)
Protection from systems engineered to capture attention and simulate intimacy at vulnerable moments should be framed as a right — a claim about a protected interest in an undisturbed inner life — rather than as a matter of personal screen-time discipline.

## Concrete anchor (optional — a real example to ground the argument)
The rise of persuasive-design regulation aimed at minors — laws and proposals targeting engagement-maximizing features and requiring youth-safety protections — as an early, piecemeal recognition of an interest that a right to mental quiet would name directly.

## What this post must cover
- The problem stated plainly: many systems are deliberately built to occupy attention and manufacture a feeling of intimacy, and they increasingly do so at moments of loneliness, fatigue, or distress.
- Why the "just use willpower / set screen limits" framing fails: it puts the whole burden on individuals against systems engineered by teams to defeat exactly that self-control.
- What a rights framing adds: rights name a protected interest that others may not intrude on, shift responsibility toward those building the systems, and license structural remedies rather than personal coping.
- The specific interest to protect: an undisturbed inner life — freedom from engineered intrusion on attention and simulated intimacy — distinct from privacy of data.
- Precedent and current motion: persuasive-design and youth-safety regulation, and adjacent concepts like "cognitive liberty" and freedom of thought.
- The honest limits and tensions: defining the right without banning ordinary engaging design, reconciling it with free expression, and the risk of paternalism.
