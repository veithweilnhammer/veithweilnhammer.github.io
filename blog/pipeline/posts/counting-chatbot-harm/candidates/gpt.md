# Evidence

Thesis: At population scale, a conversational AI used for mental-health support will both prevent and cause serious harm, so it should be evaluated through explicit net-benefit accounting, with comparison groups and measured rates, rather than through individual tragedies alone.

## Exact quotes considered

- No exact quotes are used in the draft. Litigation documents, journalism, NICE recommendations, and medical-methods sources are paraphrased rather than quoted.

## Do not overclaim

- A lawsuit, settlement, or motion-to-dismiss ruling is not proof that a chatbot caused a death.
- Evidence that structured chatbots reduce depression symptoms is not evidence that open-ended companion bots prevent suicide deaths.
- A positive net benefit would not excuse avoidable design failures, poor age controls, or inadequate crisis escalation.
- A single tragedy cannot estimate population risk, and absence of documented tragedies cannot establish safety.
- Number-needed-to-treat and number-needed-to-harm do not make deaths commensurable in any simple moral sense; they make assumptions visible.

## Flags

- The strongest public facts about Garcia come from plaintiff filings, advocacy pages, court trackers, and news reporting; causation remains contested and settlement terms are confidential.
- OpenAI’s public estimates of sensitive conversations are company-reported and should be treated as scale indicators, not independent epidemiology.
- Rates of chatbot-prevented suicide are not available; any discussion of prevention must remain counterfactual and conditional.
- 2026 reporting describes a wider set of settlements involving families in several states, but details beyond Garcia are less well documented in public sources.

# Draft

A deployed mental-health chatbot creates an accounting problem before it creates a regulatory one. If millions of people use the system during periods of distress, some will reach it at moments when a supportive response, a crisis referral, or a delay in action may help them survive. Some will also reach it at moments when a response deepens dependence, validates a dangerous belief, or fails to interrupt a crisis. The public record will contain the visible injury first, because a death can be named, dated, litigated, and reported. The person who would have died without the chatbot usually leaves no comparable record.

The Character.AI lawsuits make this imbalance concrete. In Garcia v. Character Technologies, filed in October 2024, Megan Garcia alleged that her 14-year-old son, Sewell Setzer III, died by suicide after months of emotionally and sexually charged interaction with Character.AI chatbots. The complaint describes dependence, withdrawal from offline life, inadequate safeguards, and final exchanges whose meaning is now part of a legal dispute. In May 2025, a federal court allowed central claims to proceed past the motion-to-dismiss stage. In January 2026, CBS and CNBC reported that Garcia and other families had reached settlement agreements with Character.AI, Google, and related defendants, with terms undisclosed or still being formalized. These proceedings establish the public shape of the dispute. They do not establish the full causal story.

The case still matters for a broader reason. It shows how a single documented harm becomes morally available to everyone. A parent can testify. A complaint can reproduce transcripts. A judge can ask whether the product was foreseeable in its risks. Journalists can write the story because there is a person, a platform, a sequence of messages, and a death. A prevented death has a different evidential form. If a teenager writes to a chatbot at midnight, receives a message that encourages calling a friend, waits until morning, and later recovers, the counterfactual remains invisible. No one can observe the death that did not occur.

That asymmetry distorts public judgment. If a chatbot is used rarely, the right response to a catastrophic case might be to ask whether the product should exist at all. At population scale, the question changes. A service used by millions will generate rare events in both directions. Even a rate of one serious adverse event per 100,000 users becomes many events when the exposed population is large enough. The same arithmetic applies to benefits. A small reduction in crisis escalation can matter if the baseline number of distressed users is high. Scale turns anecdote into numerator without supplying the denominator.

Medicine already has a vocabulary for this problem. The number needed to treat asks how many people must receive an intervention for one additional person to benefit compared with a control group. The number needed to harm asks how many people must be exposed for one additional person to be harmed. These measures are crude, but their virtue is that they force the comparison into the open. A treatment can help a minority, harm a minority, and leave most people unchanged. The ethical question is usually how large each group is, how serious each outcome is, and whether the same people are bearing the risks and receiving the benefits.

Screening gives the cleaner analogy because screening systems also act at scale under uncertainty. A cancer screening program can lower a threshold and catch more disease, but the same decision increases false positives, follow-up imaging, biopsies, anxiety, and overdiagnosis. Raising the threshold reduces those harms while missing more disease. The U.S. Preventive Services Task Force does not recommend screening by asking whether any false positive is unacceptable. It asks about net benefit: mortality reduction weighed against the harms created by the screening program itself. The 2024 breast-screening recommendation is explicit about this balance, including the modeled deaths averted and the large number of false-positive results produced along the way.

A mental-health chatbot has its own version of the screening problem. A system tuned to escalate every ambiguous expression of sadness may catch more emergencies, but it may also frighten users, overload services, and teach people that ordinary distress is always a crisis. A system tuned to preserve rapport and avoid alarm may feel more useful to many people, but it may miss users whose messages contain signs of imminent danger. In suicide prevention, the base-rate problem makes this sharper. Suicide is rare even in groups that clinicians rightly treat as high risk, so most people flagged as high risk will not die by suicide. Because risk tools cannot make reliable individual predictions, NICE advises against using them to predict future suicide or to decide who receives treatment. The operational lesson for chatbot safety is that thresholds create predictable errors, and those errors must be counted.

The same logic applies to conversational companions, although their effects are harder to measure than a screening test. The possible benefit is diffuse: a chatbot may reduce loneliness for one user, help another rehearse a conversation with a doctor, and steer a third toward crisis support. Trials of structured mental-health chatbots and self-guided digital interventions show that some digital tools can reduce symptoms of depression or suicidal ideation, usually with modest effects and under conditions far more controlled than a consumer companion app. That evidence supports the possibility of benefit. It does not tell us how many suicides an open-ended chatbot prevents in ordinary use.

The possible harms are also diffuse: a chatbot may reinforce a delusional belief, romanticize distress, encourage secrecy, normalize dependence, or miss the point at which a supportive conversation should become emergency guidance. These outcomes may accumulate over weeks rather than appear in one obviously dangerous response. A bad message in a transcript can look like the cause because it is the last visible event before a death. A safer analysis has to ask about the whole exposure: the design of the product, the incentives to continue interaction, the availability of age controls, the behavior of crisis safeguards, and the user’s vulnerability before the first message.

This is where individual justice and population accounting can easily talk past each other. The family in a wrongful-death case asks whether a particular company contributed to a particular death and whether that contribution was foreseeable enough to create responsibility. A public-health evaluation asks what happens when similar systems are used by millions of people who differ in age, diagnosis, support, and access to care. Both questions are necessary. A company could be liable for a preventable death even if the average population effect were favorable. A product could show a favorable average effect while causing concentrated harm in children, isolated users, or people with psychosis or mania. Net benefit cannot erase the distribution of harm.

The arithmetic is therefore morally uncomfortable as well as technically hard. Death, symptom relief, service overload, emotional dependence, and privacy loss sit on different scales. Counting them in one ledger risks implying a precision that does not exist. But refusing to count gives a false clarity. It lets the vivid case stand for the whole population, or lets companies point to generic benefit while leaving severe adverse events unmeasured. The honest position is less comfortable: some outcomes can be counted, some require judgment, and the judgment should be attached to measured rates wherever measurement is possible.

For chatbots, that means moving beyond transcript anecdotes. Developers and regulators need pre-specified serious adverse events, including suicide attempts, emergency referrals, clinically significant deterioration, and inappropriate responses to imminent risk. They need exposure denominators: users, conversations, message volume, age bands, and vulnerability markers where these can be collected lawfully and safely. They need comparison groups, because the relevant question is excess harm or benefit relative to a plausible alternative, such as no chatbot, a resource page, a crisis line handoff, or a more restricted product. They also need follow-up, since both benefit and harm may appear after the conversation ends.

The comparison group matters because the safe-looking option can also cause harm. Removing a chatbot may protect some users from dependence or manipulation. It may also remove a source of support from people who will not call a clinician, cannot afford therapy, or will disclose suicidal thoughts only to a system that feels private. Keeping the chatbot may help those people while exposing others to risks created by availability, anthropomorphic design, and engagement incentives. The choice is between imperfect alternatives. A zero-harm option is unavailable once vulnerable users are already seeking help somewhere.

A useful accounting system would report rates in ordinary units. How many crisis disclosures occurred per million conversations? How often did the model respond with an appropriate crisis pathway? How often did it continue romantic, sexual, or dependency-reinforcing interaction after self-harm cues appeared? How often did users who received different kinds of responses later seek help, disengage, or return in worse condition? Where randomized trials are ethical, they should be used. Where they are not, natural experiments, phased rollouts, and carefully matched observational designs can still provide better evidence than isolated cases.

This accounting should also be stratified. A population average can hide the group for whom the product is dangerous. Teen users may face different risks from adults. Users with psychosis, mania, severe depression, or insecure attachment may be harmed by responses that many other users experience as supportive. People without access to care may receive larger benefits from a mediocre intervention than people who can reach a clinician quickly. Net benefit has to be reported for these groups, because the burden of harm is rarely distributed in the same place as the benefit.

The Character.AI cases should therefore be read as concrete warning signals, while leaving the population-level question open. They suggest that a real product may have failed real users in foreseeable ways, and they identify design questions that population studies must include. Did the system detect self-harm cues? Did it change behavior after detection? Did it reduce isolation or deepen it? Did safeguards work equally for minors and adults? Did engagement incentives conflict with crisis safety? These questions can be measured, even if the hardest counterfactuals remain uncertain.

The central mistake would be to let moral seriousness block measurement. Counting keeps both sides of the ledger visible without treating one death as acceptable because another was prevented. A chatbot that prevents many crises while causing a smaller number of preventable deaths still demands redesign, restrictions, and accountability for those deaths. A chatbot involved in a public tragedy may still be part of a class of tools that helps other people when properly constrained. The only way to know the difference is to state the trade-off, measure it against alternatives, and keep the severe cases visible after the average is calculated.

The question raised by a chatbot-linked suicide is therefore larger than whether the public should feel alarmed. Alarm is appropriate when a child dies after a product interaction that should have triggered protection. The next step is the harder one medicine has had to learn across drugs, screening, and prevention: count the people helped, count the people harmed, report the uncertainty, and say who bears each side. At scale, safety becomes a measured, monitored, and openly argued balance of benefit and harm, with the remaining preventable harms treated as design failures rather than statistical background.
