# Evidence

Thesis: In many supportive interactions, the useful part of empathy is an observable sequence of accurate understanding, attentive response, and appropriate care; simulated empathy becomes troubling in the narrower cases where the missing inner state changes accountability, reciprocity, commitment, or truthfulness.

1. Empathy bundles several capacities; contemporary psychology commonly separates affective sharing from cognitive understanding or perspective-taking. Source: Davis, 1983, “Measuring individual differences in empathy: Evidence for a multidimensional approach.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.44.1.113
2. Empathy research often distinguishes experience sharing from mentalizing, meaning explicit consideration of another person’s mental state. Source: Zaki & Ochsner, 2012, “The neuroscience of empathy: progress, pitfalls and promise.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1038/nn.3085
3. Empathic accuracy means correctly inferring the specific content of another person’s thoughts and feelings, which is closer to understanding than to shared feeling. Source: Ickes, 1993, “Empathic accuracy.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.100.4.586
4. Hume treated sympathy as a mechanism in which signs of another person’s feeling produce an idea that can become a passion in the observer. Source: Hume, 1739–40, *A Treatise of Human Nature*. Link: https://www.gutenberg.org/files/4705/4705-0.txt
5. Adam Smith treated sympathy as imaginative perspective-taking: we form an idea of another person’s state by conceiving what we would feel in that situation. Source: Smith, 1759, *The Theory of Moral Sentiments*. Link: https://www.gutenberg.org/files/67363/67363-0.txt
6. Therapist empathy predicts psychotherapy outcomes in meta-analysis, with Elliott et al. reporting an average association around r = .31. Source: Elliott, Bohart, Watson & Greenberg, 2011, “Empathy.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1037/a0022187
7. General-practice empathy is associated with patient satisfaction, adherence, reduced anxiety and distress, diagnostic information gathering, and clinical outcomes, although the evidence base is heterogeneous. Source: Derksen, Bensing & Lagro-Janssen, 2013, “Effectiveness of empathy in general practice: a systematic review.” Link: https://doi.org/10.3399/bjgp13X660814
8. Rogers’s classic account made communicated empathic understanding and the client’s perception of it part of the conditions for therapeutic change, alongside genuineness and unconditional positive regard. Source: Rogers, 1957, “The necessary and sufficient conditions of therapeutic personality change.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1037/h0045357
9. In a blinded comparison of 195 Reddit AskDocs questions, licensed healthcare professionals rated ChatGPT answers as empathetic or very empathetic far more often than physician answers, 45.1% versus 4.6%; the study tested perceived response quality, not real clinical outcomes. Source: Ayers et al., 2023, “Comparing physician and artificial intelligence chatbot responses to patient questions posted to a public social media forum.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1001/jamainternmed.2023.1838
10. People may disclose more to computer-mediated or virtual-human interviewers when they believe a human is not judging them, which supports a limited role for artificial listeners in low-stakes disclosure. Source: Lucas, Gratch, King & Morency, 2014, “‘It’s only a computer’: Virtual humans increase willingness to disclose.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.chb.2014.04.043
11. Empathic concern can motivate costly helping in laboratory studies, which matters for cases where care requires sacrifice rather than a well-formed response. Source: Batson, Duncan, Ackerman, Buckley & Birch, 1981, “Is empathic emotion a source of altruistic motivation?” Link: https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.40.2.290
12. Ethicists of human-robot and human-AI relationships have long treated simulated care as a possible source of deception, attachment, and dependence, especially for vulnerable users. Source: Sharkey & Sharkey, 2011, “Children, the elderly, and interactive robots.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1109/MRA.2010.940150
13. IEEE’s 2024 white paper on emulated empathy frames deception in AI partners as contested rather than automatically settled, and recommends careful analysis of transparency, harm, and context. Source: IEEE Standards Association, 2024, “Is Deception in Emulated Empathy Innately Bad?” Link: https://standards.ieee.org/ieee/White_Paper/11856/

## Do not overclaim

- Do not claim that an AI system has empathy in the affective or conscious sense.
- Do not infer clinical safety from high ratings of empathetic wording in short written answers.
- Do not treat user satisfaction, perceived warmth, or disclosure as evidence of long-term benefit.
- Do not imply that Hume or Smith anticipated machines; use them only to show that sympathy can be described as a process with inputs, transformations, and functions.
- Do not flatten therapy into reassurance. Rogers’s account also requires genuineness, acceptance, and the client’s perception of the relationship.
- Do not suggest that inner feeling never matters. It matters when care requires responsibility, mutuality, moral risk, or sacrifice.
- Do not make this a general essay about consciousness, intelligence, anthropomorphism, or the Turing test.

## Flags

- Hume and Smith used “sympathy” in a broader eighteenth-century sense; it overlaps with modern empathy but does not map perfectly onto affective empathy, cognitive empathy, or compassion.
- The Ayers et al. JAMA study used expert ratings of written responses to public forum questions. It does not show that chatbots improve health outcomes or handle risky conversations safely.
- The evidence for therapist and physician empathy mostly concerns human helpers. It supports the importance of perceived and communicated empathy, but it does not prove that artificial empathy has equivalent effects.
- The ethics literature disagrees about whether simulated care is inherently deceptive or conditionally acceptable with transparency and safeguards.
- The strongest empirical evidence that feeling itself motivates costly help comes from human experiments; applying it to AI supports a conceptual boundary rather than a direct machine comparison.

# Draft

In 2023, a JAMA Internal Medicine study compared answers from physicians and ChatGPT to 195 patient questions posted on Reddit’s AskDocs forum. Licensed healthcare professionals, blinded to the source of each answer, rated the chatbot answers as empathetic or very empathetic much more often than the physician answers, 45.1% versus 4.6%. The study leaves clinical safety, diagnostic accuracy, and long-term outcomes unresolved. Its narrower result still matters: in a written exchange, a system with no feelings can produce language that trained human raters experience as more empathic than the language written by busy clinicians.

That result irritates a common moral intuition about artificial empathy. Many people respond that the system does not really care, and the statement is true. A language model has no sorrow, concern, loyalty, or stake in the user’s life. The problem is that this true statement is too broad to settle the practical question. Supportive interactions contain several separable parts: the helper has to understand what is being said, respond in a way that shows attention, offer useful next steps, and convey enough care that the other person can continue. Private feeling may support those tasks in humans, but the recipient usually encounters the tasks through behavior.

The word empathy hides this distinction because it covers at least two different things. One is affective empathy, the experience of sharing or resonating with another person’s state. The other is cognitive empathy, or accurate understanding of what another person is going through. Psychologists also study empathic accuracy, the ability to infer the specific content of someone else’s thoughts and feelings. These capacities often travel together in human life, which makes the distinction easy to miss. A friend who understands why you are upset may also feel upset on your behalf. A clinician who reads distress accurately may also feel concern. The pairing is familiar, but it is still a pairing.

Once the distinction is visible, a less dramatic claim becomes available. In many supportive interactions, the useful part of empathy is the performance: the other person is understood accurately, answered carefully, and helped appropriately. The recipient cannot directly inspect the helper’s inner state. They infer it from timing, wording, memory, attention, and action. If the practical benefit comes from being heard, oriented, and reassured, then a system that performs these functions well can deliver a real benefit despite lacking the private feeling that a human helper might have.

This way of thinking has older roots than the current debate about AI. Hume described sympathy as a process that begins with signs and moves inward. “When any affection is infused by sympathy,” he wrote, “it is at first known only by its effects, and by those external signs in the countenance and conversation, which convey an idea of it.” He was describing human minds, and the structure still matters for the present question. Sympathy has inputs that can be perceived, transformations that can be described, and effects that can be evaluated. It is a mechanism as well as a feeling.

Adam Smith made a related point in *The Theory of Moral Sentiments*. We have no direct access to another person’s suffering, he argued, so we understand it “by conceiving what we ourselves should feel in the like situation.” Sympathy, in this account, depends on imaginative reconstruction. We place ourselves in another person’s circumstances and form an idea of what their state would be like. Smith’s account still involves feeling in the observer, but it also treats sympathy as a procedure with a function: it helps one person respond to another person’s condition from the outside.

The relevance for AI is limited but useful. Hume and Smith give no support to loose analogies about machine inner life. Their accounts show that empathy has always had an outward-facing, reconstructive side. We know other people through signs, context, inference, and response. Human sympathy may culminate in a shared feeling, but much of the work happens in the interpretable middle: noticing what matters, forming a model of the other person’s situation, and acting on it in a way the other person can recognize.

This is why simulated empathy can be enough in some ordinary settings. A person who asks whether a frightening symptom is common may need calm information and a recommendation to seek medical advice if certain warning signs appear. A student who feels humiliated after failing an exam may need a response that names the disappointment, separates one failure from a global judgment, and suggests a concrete next step. A lonely person may benefit from writing down what happened and receiving a response that tracks the details without dismissing them. In these cases, the benefit depends heavily on accurate uptake and appropriate response.

Human support often works through the same channel. In psychotherapy research, therapist empathy predicts outcomes, and in general practice, empathic communication is associated with satisfaction, adherence, reduced anxiety, better information gathering, and sometimes clinical outcomes. The important point for this argument is that much of the evidence concerns empathy as perceived and communicated. Rogers’s classic conditions for therapeutic change include the client perceiving the therapist’s empathic understanding. Therapy still involves genuineness, boundaries, and responsibility. The point here is narrower: empathy has to arrive as something the recipient can detect.

Artificial listeners may even have advantages in narrow circumstances. In research on virtual humans, people were more willing to disclose when they believed they were interacting with a computer and had less reason to fear human judgment. This finding supports a narrower point about disclosure. Low-stakes disclosure sometimes benefits from availability, patience, and the absence of social embarrassment. A system can offer those features without feeling anything. The useful property is the interactional setting it creates, not an inner warmth hidden behind the screen.

The boundary appears when the missing inner state changes what the interaction means. Accountability is the first case. A human helper can be answerable for carelessness, betrayal, neglect, or cowardice. Institutions can discipline clinicians, friends can be confronted, and family members can owe repair. A chatbot can be shut down, patched, or audited, but it cannot take responsibility in the first-person sense. Responsibility moves to designers, deployers, clinicians, or institutions. In low-stakes reassurance, this may be enough. In moments of danger, the location of responsibility becomes central.

Reciprocity is the second boundary. Many supportive conversations are embedded in relationships that continue over time. A friend remembers what they said last week, alters their plans, worries when you go quiet, and accepts that your needs can inconvenience them. A system can store context and generate continuity, but it has no life that can be interrupted by yours. This matters because some forms of care are mutual. They involve obligations and vulnerability on both sides. Simulated empathy can imitate the language of reciprocity while leaving the user in a one-sided arrangement.

Commitment is the third boundary. Some caring acts require cost. A person may drive across town at midnight, risk an awkward confrontation, stay present through anger, or give up something they wanted because another person needs them. Human empathic concern can motivate this kind of costly help. A chatbot can recommend that someone call emergency services, contact a trusted person, or make a plan, and that may be valuable. The system itself cannot bear the cost of care. When the morally important act is sacrifice rather than interpretation, simulated empathy reaches its limit.

Deception is the fourth boundary. Users can benefit from simulated empathy while also being misled by it. The ethical difference often depends on what the system claims. A tool that says, in effect, “I can help you think this through, but I am software,” gives the user a frame for the interaction. A product that encourages the user to believe it loves them, needs them, or suffers when ignored creates a different moral situation. The ethical problem comes from false claims about attachment, dependence, and care, especially when warm wording makes those claims plausible.

These boundaries make the usual objection more precise. The sentence “it does not really care” has force when the user needs someone answerable, when the relationship is supposed to be mutual, when care requires costly commitment, or when the system presents simulated concern as real attachment. The same sentence has less force when the task is routine explanation, low-stakes reassurance, reflective listening, or help organizing a confusing situation. A weather app does not care whether you get wet, but it can still help you bring an umbrella. Emotional support is more intimate than weather forecasting, so the analogy only goes so far. Still, the useful question concerns which part of care the interaction requires.

This also changes how AI systems should be judged. The evaluation should ask whether the system understood the user accurately, whether it responded in a way that helped and avoided intensifying the problem, whether it escalated appropriately when risk appeared, and whether it represented its own limitations honestly. A system that sounds warm while missing danger performs empathy badly. A system that gives accurate, bounded, transparent support in a low-stakes moment may perform the relevant part well enough.

The defensible position is narrow and practical. Simulated empathy is possible because empathy has an observable, functional side. That fact should reduce one broad worry and sharpen several narrower ones. The absence of feeling leaves many supportive exchanges intact, then becomes morally decisive when the situation requires responsibility, reciprocity, commitment, or truthfulness about care. Treating those as the real fault lines gives us a better standard than asking a machine to possess a feeling it plainly lacks.
