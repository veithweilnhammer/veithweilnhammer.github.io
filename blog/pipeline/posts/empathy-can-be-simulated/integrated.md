# Evidence

**Thesis:** In most supportive interactions the useful part of empathy is an
observable sequence — accurate understanding, attentive response, conveyed care —
so simulated empathy is often genuinely enough, and the real task is to locate the
specific conditions under which the absence of real feeling causes harm:
accountability, reciprocity, commitment or sacrifice, and deception.

## Key claims and sources

1. **Empathy is standardly split into an affective component (sharing another's
   emotional state) and a cognitive component (understanding another's state and
   perspective).** The distinction is routine in contemporary psychology and
   neuroscience.
   - Davis, M. H. (1983). "Measuring individual differences in empathy: Evidence
     for a multidimensional approach." *Journal of Personality and Social
     Psychology* 44(1): 113–126. https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.44.1.113
   - Decety, J. & Jackson, P. L. (2004). "The functional architecture of human
     empathy." *Behavioral and Cognitive Neuroscience Reviews* 3(2): 71–100.
     https://doi.org/10.1177/1534582304267187
   - Zaki, J. & Ochsner, K. (2012). "The neuroscience of empathy: progress,
     pitfalls and promise." *Nature Neuroscience* 15: 675–680.
     https://doi.org/10.1038/nn.3085
   - Shamay-Tsoory, S. G. (2011). "The neural bases for empathy." *The
     Neuroscientist* 17(1): 18–24. https://doi.org/10.1177/1073858410379268

2. **The affective and cognitive components can dissociate: they depend on partly
   separate brain systems.** In a lesion study, damage to the inferior frontal
   gyrus impaired emotional empathy while damage to the ventromedial prefrontal
   cortex impaired cognitive empathy, a double dissociation.
   - Shamay-Tsoory, S. G., Aharon-Peretz, J. & Perry, D. (2009). "Two systems for
     empathy: a double dissociation between emotional and cognitive empathy in
     inferior frontal gyrus versus ventromedial prefrontal lesions." *Brain*
     132(3): 617–627. https://doi.org/10.1093/brain/awn279

3. **"Empathic accuracy" — correctly inferring the specific content of another
   person's thoughts and feelings — is closer to understanding than to shared
   feeling.**
   - Ickes, W. (1993). "Empathic accuracy." *Journal of Personality* / related
     account. https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.100.4.586

4. **What makes people feel supported is perceived responsiveness: the belief
   that the other party understands them, values them, and cares about their
   needs — beliefs inferred from observable behavior.**
   - Reis, H. T., Clark, M. S. & Holmes, J. G. (2004). "Perceived partner
     responsiveness as an organizing construct in the study of intimacy and
     closeness." In *Handbook of Closeness and Intimacy*.
   - Reis, H. T. & Gable, S. L. (2015). "Responsiveness." *Current Opinion in
     Psychology* 1: 67–71. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.copsyc.2015.01.001

5. **David Hume (1739–40) treated sympathy as a mechanism: signs of another
   person's feeling produce an idea in the observer that can become a feeling
   itself.**
   - Hume, D. *A Treatise of Human Nature* (1739–40), Book II, Part I, Section XI.
     https://www.gutenberg.org/files/4705/4705-0.txt

6. **Adam Smith (1759) treated sympathy as imaginative perspective-taking: we
   form an idea of another's state by conceiving what we would feel in their
   situation — a describable process with inputs and outputs.**
   - Smith, A. *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* (1759), Part I, Section I,
     Chapter I. https://www.gutenberg.org/files/67363/67363-0.txt

7. **Carl Rogers (1957) named accurate empathy as a core condition of effective
   psychotherapy and defined it in observable terms — sensing the client's inner
   world and communicating that understanding back accurately enough that the
   client perceives it — alongside genuineness and unconditional positive
   regard.** Rogers held the empathy had to be genuine.
   - Rogers, C. R. (1957). "The necessary and sufficient conditions of
     therapeutic personality change." *Journal of Consulting Psychology* 21(2):
     95–103. https://doi.org/10.1037/h0045357

8. **Therapist empathy predicts psychotherapy outcomes in meta-analysis, with an
   average association around r = .31.**
   - Elliott, R., Bohart, A. C., Watson, J. C. & Greenberg, L. S. (2011).
     "Empathy." *Psychotherapy* 48(1): 43–49. https://doi.org/10.1037/a0022187

9. **General-practice empathy is associated with patient satisfaction, adherence,
   reduced anxiety and distress, better information gathering, and some clinical
   outcomes, though the evidence base is heterogeneous.**
   - Derksen, F., Bensing, J. & Lagro-Janssen, A. (2013). "Effectiveness of
     empathy in general practice: a systematic review." *British Journal of
     General Practice* 63(606): e76–e84. https://doi.org/10.3399/bjgp13X660814

10. **In a blinded comparison of 195 patient questions from Reddit's r/AskDocs,
    licensed healthcare professionals rated ChatGPT answers as empathetic or very
    empathetic far more often than physician answers, 45.1% versus 4.6%.** The
    study tested perceived response quality of written answers, not clinical
    outcomes, diagnostic accuracy, or safety in risky conversations.
    - Ayers, J. W. et al. (2023). "Comparing physician and artificial
      intelligence chatbot responses to patient questions posted to a public
      social media forum." *JAMA Internal Medicine* 183(6): 589–596.
      https://doi.org/10.1001/jamainternmed.2023.1838

11. **People may disclose more to computer-mediated or virtual-human interviewers
    when they believe a human is not judging them, supporting a limited role for
    artificial listeners in low-stakes disclosure.**
    - Lucas, G. M., Gratch, J., King, A. & Morency, L.-P. (2014). "'It's only a
      computer': Virtual humans increase willingness to disclose." *Computers in
      Human Behavior* 37: 94–100. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.chb.2014.04.043

12. **Empathic concern can motivate costly helping in laboratory studies, which
    matters for cases where care requires sacrifice rather than a well-formed
    response.**
    - Batson, C. D., Duncan, B. D., Ackerman, P., Buckley, T. & Birch, K. (1981).
      "Is empathic emotion a source of altruistic motivation?" *Journal of
      Personality and Social Psychology* 40(2): 290–302.
      https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.40.2.290

13. **Ethicists of human–robot and human–AI relationships treat simulated care as
    a possible source of deception, attachment, and dependence, especially for
    vulnerable users; whether it is inherently deceptive is contested.**
    - Sharkey, A. & Sharkey, N. (2011). "Children, the elderly, and interactive
      robots." *IEEE Robotics & Automation Magazine* 18(1): 32–38.
      https://doi.org/10.1109/MRA.2010.940150
    - IEEE Standards Association (2024). "Is Deception in Emulated Empathy
      Innately Bad?" White paper.
      https://standards.ieee.org/ieee/White_Paper/11856/

## Exact quotes (public domain, verified wording)

- Hume: "No quality of human nature is more remarkable, both in itself and in its
  consequences, than that propensity we have to sympathize with others, and to
  receive by communication their inclinations and sentiments, however different
  from, or even contrary to our own." (*Treatise*, 2.1.11)
- Hume: "When any affection is infused by sympathy, it is at first known only by
  its effects, and by those external signs in the countenance and conversation,
  which convey an idea of it." (*Treatise*)
- Hume: "When I see the effects of passion in the voice and gesture of any person,
  my mind immediately passes from these effects to their causes, and forms such a
  lively idea of the passion, as is presently converted into the passion itself."
  (*Treatise*)
- Smith: "As we have no immediate experience of what other men feel, we can form
  no idea of the manner in which they are affected, but by conceiving what we
  ourselves should feel in the like situation." (*Theory of Moral Sentiments*,
  I.I.I)
- Smith: "By the imagination we place ourselves in his situation, we conceive
  ourselves enduring all the same torments, we enter as it were into his body, and
  become in some measure the same person with him, and thence form some idea of
  his sensations, and even feel something which, though weaker in degree, is not
  altogether unlike them." (*Theory of Moral Sentiments*, I.I.I)

## Do not overclaim

- Do not claim an AI system has empathy in the affective or conscious sense; it
  reproduces the observable outputs.
- Do not infer clinical safety, diagnostic accuracy, or long-term benefit from
  high ratings of empathetic wording in short written answers (the Ayers limit),
  or from satisfaction, perceived warmth, or disclosure.
- Do not claim the affective and cognitive components are cleanly separable or
  fully independent; the dissociation is partial and the distinction is a working
  one, contested at the margins (some add motivational/compassionate components).
- Do not claim cognitive empathy replaces affective empathy in human life; they
  usually operate together.
- Do not imply Hume or Smith anticipated machines. They described a mechanism in
  humans; the inference that such a process could in principle run in something
  that does not feel is ours, licensed only by their treating sympathy as a
  process at all.
- Do not present Rogers as endorsing simulated empathy; he required it to be real
  and named genuineness and acceptance as separate conditions. He is cited only
  because his operational description is of observable conduct.
- Do not suggest inner feeling never matters. It matters where care requires
  responsibility, mutuality, moral risk, or sacrifice.

## Flags

- Hume and Smith used "sympathy" in a broad eighteenth-century sense that overlaps
  with, but does not map exactly onto, modern affective empathy, cognitive
  empathy, or compassion.
- The therapist/physician empathy evidence concerns human helpers; it establishes
  that perceived and communicated empathy matters, not that artificial empathy has
  equivalent effects.
- The responsiveness construct (Reis/Gable) comes from human relationships; its
  extension to human–machine interaction is an inference.
- The ethics literature disagrees about whether simulated care is inherently
  deceptive or conditionally acceptable with transparency and safeguards.
- Batson's costly-helping evidence is from human experiments; applied to AI it
  supports a conceptual boundary, not a direct machine comparison.

# Draft

In 2023 a *JAMA Internal Medicine* study compared answers from physicians and
ChatGPT to 195 patient questions posted on Reddit's AskDocs forum. Licensed
healthcare professionals, blinded to the source of each answer, rated the chatbot
answers as empathetic or very empathetic far more often than the physician
answers, 45.1 percent against 4.6 percent. The study leaves clinical safety,
diagnostic accuracy, and long-term outcomes unresolved, and it should. Its
narrower result still matters: in a written exchange, a system that feels nothing
produced language that trained human raters experienced as more empathic than the
language written by busy clinicians.

The usual response to this is that the machine does not really care, and the
statement is true. A language model has no sorrow, no concern, no loyalty, and no
stake in the user's life. The trouble is that this true statement is too broad to
settle the practical question. A supportive interaction has several separable
parts. The helper has to understand what is being said, respond in a way that
shows attention, offer a useful next step, and convey enough care that the other
person can carry on. Private feeling may drive those tasks in a human helper, but
the person being helped meets the tasks through behavior.

The word "empathy" hides this because it covers at least two things that come
apart under inspection. One is affective empathy, the experience of sharing or
resonating with another person's state — feeling a version of someone's grief when
you attend closely to them. The other is cognitive empathy, the accurate
understanding of what another person is going through, which psychologists also
study as empathic accuracy: correctly inferring the specific content of someone's
thoughts and feelings. These capacities usually travel together in human life,
which is why the distinction is easy to miss. A friend who grasps why you are
upset often feels upset on your behalf. But they can separate. In a lesion study,
damage to one prefrontal region left people able to understand another's state
while blunting their felt response, and damage to another region did the reverse.
Affective empathy happens inside the helper. Cognitive empathy is something the
helper does, and the person being helped can see it.

So which one does the recipient benefit from? When researchers ask what makes
people feel supported, the answer centers on perceived responsiveness: a person
feels helped when they believe the other party understands them, values what they
are going through, and cares about their needs. Each of those beliefs is formed by
reading behavior. Understanding is inferred from accurate reflection of what you
said and sensible follow-up questions. Being valued is inferred from attention
that stays on you. Care is inferred from warmth and appropriately calibrated
concern. Someone leaves a good supportive conversation feeling understood and
answered, and every input to that feeling arrived through what the other party
said and did.

Clinical psychology reached the same operational description from its own
direction. Carl Rogers, setting out the conditions for effective psychotherapy in
1957, listed accurate empathy among them and defined it in terms a second person
could check: sensing the client's inner world and communicating that understanding
back accurately enough that the client recognizes it. Rogers held that the
therapist's empathy had to be genuine, so this is no license for pretending. The
narrower point is what he reached for when he needed to describe empathy well
enough to teach and measure it. The description was of observable conduct — what
the therapist reflects, checks, and conveys. Even in the tradition that placed the
most weight on empathy, the part that could be specified was the part the other
person perceives.

The philosophical tradition that took empathy most seriously already treated it as
a mechanism with a describable function. Writing in 1739, David Hume observed that
sympathy begins with signs and moves inward: "When any affection is infused by
sympathy, it is at first known only by its effects, and by those external signs in
the countenance and conversation, which convey an idea of it." He was describing
human minds, and the structure still matters here. Sympathy has inputs that can be
perceived, transformations that can be described, and effects that can be
evaluated. Adam Smith made a related point twenty years later. Because we have no
direct access to another person's suffering, he argued, we reach it by imaginative
reconstruction: "By the imagination we place ourselves in his situation, we
conceive ourselves enduring all the same torments... and thence form some idea of
his sensations." Both men described sympathy as something with inputs, steps, and
outputs rather than as an inner glow that is simply present or absent. Once you
describe empathy as a process, you have described the kind of thing that could, in
principle, be run by a system that does not feel — a stronger claim than either of
them made, but one their framing invites.

This is why simulated empathy is often enough. Someone anxious at three in the
morning wants to be heard and steadied. Someone confused by a medical result wants
it explained in plain terms with appropriate concern. Someone who made a small
mistake wants a response that names the disappointment, separates one failure from
a verdict on their whole life, and points to a concrete next step. In each case the
benefit depends on accurate uptake and a fitting response, and a competent
performance delivers exactly that. These make up most of everyday support, and they
are the interactions people most often bring to a chatbot, at hours and in volumes
where no human helper is available. Human support works through the same channel:
therapist empathy predicts psychotherapy outcomes, with an average association
around r = .31, and in general practice empathic communication tracks
satisfaction, adherence, reduced anxiety, better information gathering, and
sometimes clinical outcomes. Much of that evidence concerns empathy as perceived
and communicated, which is the part that can arrive without a matching feeling
behind it. An artificial listener can even hold a narrow advantage: people
sometimes disclose more when they believe a human is not judging them, so
availability, patience, and the absence of social embarrassment can help on their
own. Insisting these exchanges are worthless because no one behind them felt
anything mistakes where their value sits, which is in the recipient's experience of
being understood.

The absence of feeling does start to bite, but in specific conditions rather than
across the board. The first is accountability. When a supportive exchange carries
real moral stakes — advice that could steer someone toward harm, a crisis where the
wrong response has consequences — part of what a person relies on is that someone
is answerable for the response. A human helper can be held responsible because they
understood the stakes and chose how to act. A system that feels nothing carries no
responsibility of its own; it can be shut down, patched, or audited, and the
answerability moves to designers, deployers, and institutions. In low-stakes
reassurance that relocation may be fine. In a moment of danger, where being
answerable is part of what the situation requires, a flawless performance does not
supply it.

The second condition is reciprocity. Some of what people want from support is a
mutual relationship, in which their state genuinely registers with the other party
and changes what that party feels and does over time. A friend who is relieved
when you recover and troubled when you relapse is in a two-way arrangement where
your life has weight on their side. Simulated care runs in one direction. The
response can be accurate and warm and still leave the encounter one-sided, because
there is no second party whose inner state your situation moves. This is why a
well-built companion can leave heavy users feeling emptier over time even as each
individual exchange goes well: the interactions supply the form of being cared
about while the mutual weighting that the form usually signals is absent.

The third condition is commitment that costs something. Real help sometimes
requires the helper to bear a cost — to give up time, to take on risk, to stay
present through a stretch that is unpleasant with no benefit to themselves. In
human beings that willingness draws on actually caring about the outcome; empathic
concern can motivate costly help in experiments precisely when a cheaper option is
available. A system with nothing at stake can express commitment in words and
recommend that someone call emergency services or reach a trusted person, which may
be valuable. It cannot act when acting would require a sacrifice, because it has no
interest of its own to weigh against the cost. Where the morally important act is
showing up and bearing a cost rather than interpreting well, the performance and
the reality separate.

The fourth condition is deception. A user can benefit from simulated empathy while
being misled by it, and the difference usually turns on what the system claims. A
tool that says, in effect, "I can help you think this through, but I am software,"
gives the user a true frame for the interaction. A product that encourages the user
to believe it loves them, needs them, or suffers when ignored builds a false belief
about the relationship they are in. The comfort may be real; the harm is separate,
and it consists in the person arranging their expectations, their disclosures, and
sometimes their decisions around a bond that has no second side. This is the
condition most under the control of design, and the one where "it does not really
care" carries its sharpest force.

Naming these four conditions changes how the systems should be judged. The
evaluation worth running sets aside whether a chatbot feels anything and asks
whether it understood the user accurately, responded in a way that helped rather
than intensifying the problem, escalated appropriately when risk appeared, and
represented its own limits honestly. A system that sounds warm while missing danger
performs empathy badly. A system that gives accurate, bounded, transparent support
in a low-stakes moment performs the relevant part well enough.

The defensible position is narrow and practical. Simulated empathy is possible
because empathy has always had an observable, functional side, and that fact should
retire one broad worry while sharpening several precise ones. The absence of
feeling leaves most supportive exchanges intact, then becomes decisive when the
situation requires responsibility, reciprocity, costly commitment, or truthfulness
about care. Treating those four as the real fault lines gives a better standard than
asking a machine to hold a feeling it plainly lacks, and it tells us which
interactions these systems can be trusted with and which ones they should be kept
honest about.
