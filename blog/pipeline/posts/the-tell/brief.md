# Brief: The Tell: Reading Mental Health From Human-Computer Interaction

## Slug
the-tell

## Working title
The Tell: Reading Mental Health From Human-Computer Interaction

## One-line angle (also the Substack subtitle / SEO hook)
A poker player's involuntary tell leaks the hand they are hiding; the way we type, scroll, and move a cursor leaks mental health that words conceal or never reach.

## Single argument (thesis)
The way a person interacts with a device involuntarily leaks information about their mental state that self-report routinely misses, because stigma, limited insight, and the difficulty of putting feelings into words all distort what people say but not how they behave.

## Concrete anchor (optional — a real example to ground the argument)
Open on the poker "tell": a practiced player controls their words and face but still leaks the strength of their hand through involuntary movement — a change in breathing, a hesitation, a shift in how they place a chip. The same principle applies to how people handle their phones and keyboards.

## What this post must cover
- The poker tell as a clean example of a hidden internal state leaking through involuntary behavior that the person is not trying, or able, to control.
- Why words are an unreliable readout of mental state: stigma makes people underreport, alexithymia leaves some unable to name feelings, and limited insight means people often cannot accurately observe their own state.
- What "how" someone interacts carries that "what" they say does not — timing, hesitation, typing and cursor dynamics, scrolling — as measurable behavioral traces (the general idea of digital phenotyping).
- The historical lineage: Darwin's *The Expression of the Emotions in Man and Animals* argued expression is part of the biology of emotion, observable from the outside; reading interaction traces continues that tradition with new instruments.
- The honest limits: these signals are noisy, correlational, easy to over-claim, and raise consent questions because the data is leaked rather than volunteered.
- Why this matters: a readout that does not depend on a person's willingness or ability to describe their state could reach people whom self-report scales miss.
