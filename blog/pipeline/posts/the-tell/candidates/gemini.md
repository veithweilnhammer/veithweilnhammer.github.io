# Evidence

1. **Thesis**: The way a person interacts with a device involuntarily leaks information about their mental state that self-report routinely misses, because stigma, limited insight, and the difficulty of putting feelings into words all distort what people say but not how they behave.
2. **Biological expression**: Involuntary emotional expression is biologically rooted in the nervous system and operates independently of conscious will. Source: Darwin, C. (1872). *The Expression of the Emotions in Man and Animals*. Link: https://darwin-online.org.uk/content/frameset?itemID=F1142&viewtype=text&pageseq=1
3. **Alexithymia limits**: Alexithymia, defined as the inability to recognize or describe one's own emotions, affects roughly 10% of the general population, fundamentally limiting the efficacy of linguistic self-report. Source: Taylor, G. J., Bagby, R. M., & Parker, J. D. (1997). *Disorders of Affect Regulation: Alexithymia in Medical and Psychiatric Illness*. Link: https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511526831
4. **Digital phenotyping**: Digital phenotyping extracts physiological and behavioral markers, such as keystroke dynamics and cursor kinematics, to infer mental health states directly from device interactions. Source: Onnela, J. P., & Rauch, S. L. (2016). *Harnessing Smartphone-Based Digital Phenotyping to Enhance Behavioral and Mental Health*. Link: https://doi.org/10.1038/npp.2016.7
5. **MAILA capabilities**: Machine learning models applied to routine human-computer interactions can track dynamic mental states along continuous clinical dimensions with high accuracy. Source: Author's MAILA dataset and associated research in `cursor_mind.qmd`.

**Exact quotes:**
- "The study of Expression is difficult, owing to the movements being often extremely slight, and of a fleeting nature; but two points are clear: firstly, the same state of mind is expressed throughout the world with remarkable uniformity; and secondly, the chief expressive actions are innate, or depend on the nervous system, and are not acquired by the individual." (Darwin, 1872, Introduction)
- "Such expressions are often quite involuntary, and are governed by the nervous system, independently of the will." (Darwin, 1872, Chapter I)

**Do not overclaim:**
- Keystroke dynamics and cursor movements do not directly "diagnose" psychiatric disorders; they measure behavioral correlations like psychomotor slowing that track with certain states.
- Interaction data cannot completely replace clinical interviews; it provides a complementary, continuous behavioral readout.
- A slow typing speed or erratic cursor trajectory is not specific to depression or anxiety; fatigue, cold hands, or motor conditions can produce indistinguishable signals at the individual keystroke level.

**Flags:**
- While Darwin's observation that expression is rooted in biology remains accurate, some of his evolutionary extrapolations on emotion have been heavily modified by modern affective science.
- Digital phenotyping validation varies across studies because much of the data processing relies on proprietary algorithms, making exact cross-study validation difficult in some commercial applications.

# Draft

Professional poker players spend their careers attempting to suppress the outward signs of their internal mental states. They systematically relax their facial muscles to maintain a neutral expression and regulate their speech to avoid revealing the strength of their hand. Despite this conscious suppression, their actual physiological state frequently leaks through involuntary physical movements. A player holding a strong hand experiences an autonomic nervous system response that alters their baseline breathing rate. They exhibit micro-hesitations before pushing chips into the center of the table, or their fingers tremble due to a sudden increase in adrenaline. These unscripted motor actions transmit information that the player actively attempts to hide. The physical tell works because the body's motor and physiological responses to an emotional state operate faster than conscious control, making them extremely difficult to suppress completely. The same biological mechanism applies to how people handle their keyboards and smartphones in daily life. The physical way a person interacts with a digital device involuntarily leaks information about their mental state that self-report questionnaires routinely miss, because stigma, limited insight, and the difficulty of putting feelings into words all distort what people choose to say but not how they behave.

Mental health care relies almost entirely on language to detect and measure psychological distress. To evaluate a patient, clinicians ask them how they feel, or deploy standardized questionnaires like the PHQ-9 for depression or the GAD-7 for anxiety. These tools require the patient to rate the frequency of their symptoms on numerical scales over a specific period, usually the preceding two weeks. This linguistic approach assumes that individuals can accurately observe their own internal states, average those experiences correctly over time, map the result onto standard clinical definitions, and communicate them without distortion. This mechanism fails for several structural reasons.

Societal stigma causes individuals to actively conceal their symptoms. Patients frequently underreport feelings of hopelessness, severe anxiety, or paranoia to avoid social judgment, occupational consequences, or unwanted clinical interventions. Similar to the poker player masking a hand, the patient uses conscious control to filter the words they produce, effectively severing the link between their internal state and their external report.

Accurate self-report also requires a high degree of clinical insight, which many individuals lack even in the absence of stigma. Psychological distress often develops gradually over months or years, altering a person's cognitive baseline so slowly that they fail to recognize the severity of their own decline. Retrospective questionnaires introduce massive recall bias; a patient experiencing a good day often underestimates the severity of their symptoms from the previous week. A patient may know they feel unwell but remain unable to pinpoint the onset, duration, or intensity of their symptoms compared to a baseline they no longer clearly remember.

The biological limits of self-awareness prevent many people from translating their emotional states into language. The psychiatric construct of alexithymia describes a condition where individuals experience a profound inability to identify, process, and describe their own emotions. Epidemiological studies estimate that alexithymia affects approximately ten percent of the general population, and the prevalence is substantially higher among individuals with neurodevelopmental differences or severe psychiatric disorders. For these patients, the inability to articulate distress is a genuine cognitive limitation rather than a refusal to disclose information. Relying entirely on words systematically fails the populations who cannot easily produce them.

The concept that internal mental states leak through observable physical behavior rests on foundational biology. In his 1872 work *The Expression of the Emotions in Man and Animals*, Charles Darwin established that emotional expression operates as an innate physiological reflex. Darwin argued that expressions are governed directly by the nervous system and occur automatically, "independently of the will." He observed that humans display distinct, involuntary motor patterns in response to specific emotional stimuli, such as the contraction of the corrugator muscle in the brow during distress or the widening of the eyes in fear. In Darwin's framework, an emotion is a physical event that alters the body's mechanical operation. Fear or anxiety triggers the autonomic nervous system, changing muscle tension, respiration rates, and fine motor control. These physiological shifts make the internal state continuously visible from the outside. Because these motor responses evolved as survival mechanisms, they operate at a biological level that resists deliberate suppression.

Modern motor-control theory supports this biological link between state and movement. The brain plans and executes physical actions using internal models that are continuously updated by ongoing affective and cognitive processes. The neural circuits responsible for planning movement, such as the basal ganglia, also process reward, motivation, and emotional valence. A change in mood or arousal necessarily alters the parameters of these internal models, modifying the execution of routine physical tasks. Emotional distress changes the physical geometry of movement.

Researchers now capture this involuntary motor leakage through ubiquitous digital instruments. The physical mechanics of device interaction generate measurable behavioral traces that operate independently of the linguistic content a user produces. The specific timing of a keystroke, the duration of a hesitation before clicking a link, and the speed and trajectory of a scrolling motion all contain continuous physiological signals. This approach, known as digital phenotyping, treats the smartphone or computer as a passive sensor for motor behavior. When an individual types a message, the content of the text represents their conscious, filtered output. The typing dynamics—the milliseconds between key presses, the pressure applied to the screen, the frequency of backspace usage, and the overall rhythm of the interaction—represent the unfiltered motor execution.

These dynamics break down into specific, quantifiable kinematic features. In keystroke analysis, researchers measure dwell time, the exact duration a finger holds down a key, and flight time, the latency between releasing one key and striking the next. Cursor tracking measures acceleration profiles, straight-line deviation, and target acquisition time. Clinical conditions systematically alter these motor patterns. Major depressive disorder frequently causes psychomotor retardation, a physical slowing down of thought and movement. In digital phenotyping data, psychomotor slowing manifests as increased flight time between keystrokes, longer dwell times as motor execution degrades, slower overall cursor trajectories, and longer pauses between active scrolling bursts. Conversely, generalized anxiety disorder can induce psychomotor agitation, which appears in interaction data as erratic cursor movements with high straight-line deviation, rapid shifting between applications, and uneven, high-velocity typing bursts. By measuring the physical interaction traces, digital phenotyping tracks the physiological symptoms of mental health conditions directly.

Frameworks designed to analyze these interaction traces map dynamic mental states with high accuracy. Machine-learning models trained on massive datasets of cursor and touchscreen recordings successfully link fine-grained movement motifs to continuous dimensions of psychological distress and wellbeing. By evaluating the geometry and timing of thousands of individual mouse movements or screen taps, these models extract clinically relevant information that standardized self-reports miss.

The way a user drags a digital slider or navigates a dropdown menu contains hundreds of discrete data points regarding acceleration, deceleration, and trajectory correction. When aggregated, these micro-movements form a behavioral signature that correlates strongly with the user's overall psychological function. Unlike a retrospective questionnaire that measures a mental state once every two weeks, interaction traces provide a continuous, longitudinal stream of data. The movement itself encodes the mental state, providing a high-dimensional, real-time readout that does not require language or introspection. This continuous data stream captures the natural circadian fluctuations and subtle daily shifts in mood that discrete self-report mechanisms inherently smooth over.

These behavioral signals remain noisy and correlational. A subtle change in typing dynamics might reflect the psychomotor slowing of a depressive episode, or it might simply indicate that the user is physically tired, distracted by their environment, or operating a device with a damaged touchscreen. Isolating the clinical signal from the vast noise of everyday variance requires massive datasets and standardized contextual baselines. Researchers must account for hardware differences, varying levels of digital literacy, and the specific ergonomic constraints of different devices before they can reliably infer mental health status.

Because users leak this data involuntarily, tracking these metrics raises significant questions regarding consent and data governance. Using behavioral traces to infer clinical states requires collecting and analyzing sensitive physiological information that users rarely realize they are generating. A person typing a mundane email does not explicitly consent to an algorithmic evaluation of their psychomotor speed. The capacity to read mental health continuously from background interaction data introduces profound ethical risks if deployed outside of strictly regulated clinical environments. The data is leaked rather than volunteered, shifting the balance of power from the patient to the observer.

Interaction traces provide a continuous, objective readout that does not depend on a person's capacity to describe their internal state. A clinical measurement based on involuntary physical behavior identifies psychological distress in populations that traditional self-report scales consistently fail to reach. By analyzing the unconscious mechanics of how people handle their devices, digital phenotyping offers a scalable, scientifically grounded method to detect mental health conditions that words conceal. The involuntary movements that give away the poker player's hand provide a mechanism to deliver care to individuals who cannot ask for it.
