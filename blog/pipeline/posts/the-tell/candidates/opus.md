# Evidence

**Thesis (one sentence):** The way a person interacts with a device involuntarily
leaks information about their mental state that self-report routinely misses,
because stigma, limited insight, and the difficulty of putting feelings into
words all distort what people say while leaving how they behave largely intact.

## Key claims and sources

1. **Observers judged the strength of professional poker players' hands better
   than chance from arm motions alone, and worse than chance from faces alone.**
   78 undergraduates rated two-second silent World Series of Poker clips showing
   only faces, only arms, or the upper body, without seeing the cards; face-only
   ratings were below chance, arm-only ratings above chance, upper-body at chance.
   The authors linked the arm signal to the smoothness/confidence of the betting
   motion. — Slepian, Young, Rutchick & Ambady (2013), *Psychological Science*,
   24(11), 2335–2338. DOI: 10.1177/0956797613487384.
   Secondary summary: https://www.psychologicalscience.org/publications/observer/obsonline/to-call-a-players-poker-hand-look-to-the-arms-not-the-face.html

2. **Darwin argued that the outward signs of emotion are part of the biology of
   emotion, observable from the outside, and more reliable than words.** — Charles
   Darwin (1872), *The Expression of the Emotions in Man and Animals*, concluding
   chapter. Full text: https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/1227/pg1227.txt

3. **Alexithymia — marked difficulty identifying and describing one's own
   emotions — was named by Peter Sifneos in 1973 and affects roughly one in ten
   people in the general population.** — Sifneos, P.E. (1973), "The prevalence of
   'alexithymic' characteristics in psychosomatic patients," *Psychotherapy and
   Psychosomatics*, 22(2), 255–262. DOI: 10.1159/000286529. Prevalence figure
   (~10%): population studies e.g. Salminen et al. (1999), *J. Psychosomatic
   Research* 46(1):75–82, DOI 10.1016/S0022-3999(98)00053-1; Franz et al. (2008),
   *Soc. Psychiatry Psychiatr. Epidemiol.* 43:54–62, DOI 10.1007/s00127-007-0265-1.

4. **Stigma and shame are among the most common reasons people delay or avoid
   disclosing mental-health problems.** — Clement, S. et al. (2015), "What is the
   impact of mental health-related stigma on help-seeking? A systematic review,"
   *Psychological Medicine*, 45(1), 11–27. DOI: 10.1017/S0033291714000129.

5. **"Digital phenotyping" is the inference of individual health states from
   moment-to-moment data produced by personal devices.** — Insel, T.R. & Jain,
   S.H. (2015), "Psychiatry and the smartphone," *Nature Biotechnology*, 33(5),
   462–464. DOI: 10.1038/nbt.3223. (Term also associated with Onnela & Torous.)

6. **Passively collected smartphone keystroke metadata (typing speed, error/
   correction patterns, timing — not message content) tracks the severity of
   depressive and manic symptoms over time.** — Zulueta, J. et al. (2018),
   "Predicting Mood Disturbance Severity with Mobile Phone Keystroke Metadata: A
   BiAffect Digital Phenotyping Study," *JMIR mHealth and uHealth*, 6(7):e241.
   DOI: 10.2196/mhealth.9775. URL: https://mhealth.jmir.org/2018/7/e241/

7. **Deliberate movement is continuously planned and corrected against internal
   predictions, on neural systems that also carry affect and attention — so
   mental state is expected to leave signatures in motor behavior.** — Wolpert &
   Ghahramani / Shadmehr et al., motor-control theory; and the mind–movement
   tradition (Darwin 1872; Ekman; de Gelder). General grounding used in the
   author's own MAILA manuscript (`writing_context/cursor_mind.qmd`).

8. **Author's own MAILA work (light tie-in only, no numbers):** cursor and
   touchscreen activity recorded during ordinary computer/phone use carries
   information about mental health, including signal only partially reflected in
   verbal self-report. — `writing_context/cursor_mind.qmd` (unpublished; used as
   a general illustration, not summarized).

## Do not overclaim

- The poker study used 78 undergraduate raters and short WSOP clips; it shows a
  real above-chance signal in arm motion, not that anyone can reliably "read" an
  individual player in real time. Do not inflate it into mind-reading.
- Digital-phenotyping accuracies are often group-level averages; separating two
  groups on average is not the same as a reliable readout for one person on one
  day. Avoid implying clinical-grade individual diagnosis.
- Keystroke/cursor signals are correlational and noisy; a slow, error-filled
  typing bout has many innocent causes. Do not present the signal as specific.
- Alexithymia is a dimensional trait, not a binary "can't feel"; ~10% is an
  approximate population figure that varies by instrument and sample.
- MAILA must be a general illustration only — do NOT cite its numbers, sample
  sizes, or accuracy figures.

## Flags

- Alexithymia prevalence "~10%" is a reasonable consensus figure but varies
  (roughly 8–13%) by cutoff and population; stated as "about one in ten."
- The Slepian "worse than chance for faces" direction is well documented in the
  APS write-up and abstract; exact effect sizes not transcribed from the primary
  PDF here.
- Insel & Jain's exact definitional phrasing is copyrighted modern text;
  paraphrased in the draft rather than quoted.
- Zulueta et al. DOI listed as 10.2196/mhealth.9775 (JMIR mHealth uHealth
  2018;6(7):e241); one aggregator rendered it as jmir.9775 — the stable URL
  https://mhealth.jmir.org/2018/7/e241/ is authoritative.
- BiAffect reported modest predictive accuracy (AUC ≈ 0.6 range in some analyses);
  the draft states the direction of effect, not accuracy numbers, to avoid
  overclaiming.

# Draft

In 2013 a group of psychologists showed undergraduates two-second silent clips
from the World Series of Poker and asked them to rate the strength of each hand
without ever seeing the cards. When the clips showed only the players' faces, the
ratings came out worse than chance, because professionals have trained their
faces to give nothing away and the little that does leak tends to mislead. When
the same clips showed only the players' arms as they pushed chips into the pot,
the ratings came out better than chance. The strength of a concealed hand escaped
through the smoothness of a betting motion that the player was not trying, and
probably could not manage, to control.

The gap between what a person controls and what escapes anyway is the whole point
of a tell. A poker player can choose what to say, arrange the face, and hold the
shoulders still, but the confidence that comes with a strong hand travels down
into the arm and changes how a chip gets placed. The internal state is hidden on
purpose, and it comes out through a channel the person is not watching. The
question worth asking is how far this reaches past the card table, into the
ordinary behavior that now runs through keyboards, touchscreens, and cursors.

This works because of an asymmetry in what people practice controlling. We
rehearse and monitor the channels that others watch and hold us to, the words we
choose and the expression we wear, and a professional poker player spends years
training exactly those, which is why the face turned out to be the worst place to
look and produced guesses below chance. Almost nobody rehearses the manner of a
movement. There is no practised version of how quickly a hand reaches for a chip,
or how a cursor decelerates as it nears its target, because these are channels we
were never taught to perform. The effort we spend managing the front-facing
signals is what leaves the back channels honest.

Most of what we know about a person's mental state, we get by asking. A
questionnaire, a clinical interview, or the plain question of how someone is
doing all assume that the person can observe their own state, is willing to
report it, and can find the words for it. Each of these assumptions fails often
enough to matter, and in mental health they tend to fail together. Shame and the
fear of being labeled keep people from disclosing; a systematic review of why
people delay or avoid mental-health care found stigma among the most common
reasons. About one person in ten has marked difficulty identifying and describing
their own emotions, a trait the psychiatrist Peter Sifneos named alexithymia in
1973, and for them the words are genuinely hard to locate rather than
deliberately withheld. Even setting stigma and vocabulary aside, people are unreliable observers
of their own minds, because the state being judged distorts the faculty doing the
judging: someone sliding into a depressive episode may report feeling fine, or
fail to register the change until it is well advanced.

What these failures share is that they corrupt what a person says while leaving
much of what a person does intact. Stigma changes the answer to "how are you,"
not the rhythm with which the answer is typed. A person who cannot name their low
mood still moves through a screen differently while low. The manner of an
action — its timing, its hesitations, its corrections — sits mostly outside the
reach of the judgment and willingness that shape speech, which is what makes it
worth measuring in the first place. A patient may describe a steady week to a
clinician while the record of their typing shows the opposite. Neither account is
a lie; the two are simply sampling different things, since one asks what the
person concluded about themselves and the other captures how they were actually
moving while they did it.

There is a mechanism behind this, and it is not mysterious. Every deliberate
movement is planned and then corrected on the fly against an internal prediction
of how it should go, the loop that lets you steer a cursor onto a small button or
catch a typo before your finger finishes the word. That planning and correction
runs on neural systems that also carry arousal, mood, and attention, so when
those states shift, the fine structure of a movement shifts with them. Anxiety
tends to speed and tighten motor output; low mood slows it and drains its vigor;
a lapse in attention leaves a longer pause or a clumsier correction. The movement
still reaches its target, which is why the person notices nothing, but the path
it took to get there has changed shape.

Everyday interaction with a device records that manner continuously. How long
someone pauses before starting to type, how fast and how evenly the keys fall,
how often a sentence is deleted and rewritten, how a cursor accelerates and
overshoots and corrects, how a thumb scrolls and stops, how long the hesitation
runs before a written message is finally sent — all of it is generated for free,
every time a person picks up a phone or sits at a computer. Researchers call the
inference of health states from these traces digital phenotyping, and its appeal
is that the raw material comes from normal use and costs nothing extra to gather.
The record also arrives at a scale and in a setting that a clinic cannot match:
it accumulates continuously, in the middle of a person's real day, rather than in
the fifteen minutes of an appointment that a person first has to recognize they
need and then go and attend. The BiAffect study offers a concrete case: it recorded the metadata of
smartphone typing, timing and error patterns rather than the content of any
message, and found that slower typing, more corrections, and altered rhythms
tracked the severity of depressive and manic symptoms as they rose and fell over
time. In my own work, cursor and touchscreen movements recorded while people used
a computer normally carried information about their mental health, including some
that the same people's questionnaire answers did not fully capture.

The idea that inner states show up in movement, and that movement can be read
from the outside, is much older than any device. Charles Darwin gave a whole book
to it in 1872, *The Expression of the Emotions in Man and Animals*, arguing that
the outward signs of emotion — a trembling muscle, a dry mouth, a change in the
set of the face — belong to the biology of the emotion itself, inherited
responses you can observe rather than a coating laid over some private feeling.
Near the end of that book he made a claim that reads almost as a definition of a
tell: the movements of expression, he wrote, "reveal the thoughts and intentions
of others more truly than do words, which may be falsified." Darwin had only his
eyes and careful notes to work with. Reading the timing of a keystroke or the
path of a cursor pursues the same project with instruments that record what an
observer's eye is far too slow to catch.

None of this turns behavior into a clear window. The signals are noisy, and a
slow, error-filled bout of typing can mean low mood, or a cheap phone, or a
message thumbed out while walking down stairs. They are correlational, tracking
states without explaining them, and a correlation that holds across thousands of
people can still be wrong for one person on one afternoon. The accuracy claimed
for these methods is easy to inflate, because a model that separates two groups
on average may be close to useless at the level where a decision about a single
person actually gets made, and a model trained on one group of people using one
kind of device can quietly lose its footing on another. And the property that
makes a tell valuable is exactly
what makes it fraught: a tell is leaked rather than offered. Someone who fills in a
questionnaire knows what they have disclosed, while someone whose cursor is being
read has volunteered nothing and may not even know the reading is possible. A
measurement that works because it bypasses a person's control also bypasses their
consent, and that has to be treated as a first-order problem rather than a
footnote.

The reason to take these signals seriously in spite of the noise and the risk is
the population they might reach. Self-report scales work well for people who
already sense that something is wrong, can name it, and are willing to say so, and
those are roughly the people who already find their way to care. The people such
scales miss are the ones who cannot yet see the change in themselves, who lack
the words for it, or who have good reason to stay quiet, and they are missed
precisely at the point where noticing would help most, before a problem has
grown obvious enough to name. A readout that leans on
none of those abilities — that draws instead on how a person already moves through
an ordinary day of typing and scrolling — could register a shift in some of them
earlier than any question would. The poker player's arm gives away a hand the
face has kept hidden, and the same principle, applied carefully and with the
consent problem taken seriously, could let us notice distress that a person cannot
yet see, cannot name, or has decided not to speak.
