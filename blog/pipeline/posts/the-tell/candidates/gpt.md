# Evidence

Thesis: Device interaction can reveal mental-health information that self-report misses, because stigma, limited insight, and difficulty naming emotions distort words more than they distort timing, hesitation, and movement.

1. Professional poker players leak hand strength through movement despite the culture of the “poker face.” Source: Slepian, Young, Rutchick, & Ambady (2013), “Quality of Professional Players’ Poker Hands Is Perceived Accurately From Arm Motions.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1177/0956797613487384
2. Darwin treated emotional expression as an observable biological part of emotion, including involuntary bodily changes in fear. Source: Darwin (1872), *The Expression of the Emotions in Man and Animals*. Link: https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/1227
3. Mental-health stigma deters disclosure and help-seeking; Clement et al. identified 144 studies with 90,189 participants, with disclosure concerns as the most commonly reported stigma barrier. Source: Clement et al. (2015), “What is the impact of mental health-related stigma on help-seeking?” Link: https://doi.org/10.1017/S0033291714000129
4. Alexithymia names a documented difficulty identifying and describing feelings, which limits self-report even when the person is cooperative. Sources: Sifneos (1973), “The prevalence of ‘alexithymic’ characteristics in psychosomatic patients,” https://doi.org/10.1159/000286529; Bagby, Parker, & Taylor (1994), “The Twenty-Item Toronto Alexithymia Scale—I,” https://doi.org/10.1016/0022-3999(94)90005-1
5. Limited insight is a recognized feature of psychosis and other psychiatric states, making first-person report an incomplete source of clinical information. Sources: David (1990), “Insight and Psychosis,” https://doi.org/10.1192/bjp.156.6.798; Amador et al. (1993), “The assessment of insight in psychosis,” https://doi.org/10.1176/ajp.150.6.873
6. Digital phenotyping uses data from personal digital devices to quantify behavior in everyday life and has been proposed for behavioral and mental-health monitoring. Source: Onnela & Rauch (2016), “Harnessing smartphone-based digital phenotyping to enhance behavioral and mental health.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1038/npp.2016.7
7. Smartphone keyboard dynamics can be linked to affective states in clinical samples; in one suicidality sample, less phone movement while typing was associated with more anhedonia. Source: Knol et al. (2024), “Smartphone keyboard dynamics predict affect in suicidal ideation.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1038/s41746-024-01048-1
8. Keyboard correction behavior has been associated with mood-disorder symptoms, including depression and mania ratings. Source: Liu et al. (2024), “Digital Phenotypes of Mobile Keyboard Backspace Rates and Their Associations With Symptoms of Mood Disorder.” Link: https://doi.org/10.2196/51269
9. Mouse and cursor traces can measure cognitive and affective processes, but effects can be small and task-dependent; a large stress study found little evidence for reliable stress detection from mouse usage. Source: Zimmermann et al. (2021), “Tracking stress via the computer mouse? Promises and challenges.” Link: https://doi.org/10.3758/s13428-021-01568-8
10. Passive mental-health data raise privacy, consent, and misuse risks because sensitive inferences can be made from behavioral traces that users may experience as incidental. Sources: Torous et al. (2016), “New Tools for New Research in Psychiatry,” https://doi.org/10.2196/mental.5165; Huckvale, Torous, & Larsen (2019), “Assessment of the Data Sharing and Privacy Practices of Smartphone Apps for Depression and Smoking Cessation,” https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2019.2542

Do not overclaim:

- Do not claim device traces diagnose mental illness on their own.
- Do not claim a single cursor movement, pause, or scroll pattern has a fixed psychological meaning.
- Do not claim behavioral data are free of bias; device type, task, culture, disability, age, and context can all change interaction patterns.
- Do not claim these signals are always involuntary; people can intentionally change some behavior after measurement is disclosed.
- Do not treat digital phenotyping as a replacement for consent, clinical judgment, or care pathways.

Flags:

- I verified Darwin quotes against the Project Gutenberg text, which is an 1899 Appleton edition of the 1872 work; wording can vary by edition.
- The poker evidence supports leakage from arm motion in professional play; the exact physiological mechanism behind the leakage remains uncertain.
- The HCI mental-health literature is heterogeneous. Keyboard studies are stronger examples for mood-related signal than general mouse-stress studies, where evidence is mixed.
- Scrolling-specific mental-health evidence is less mature than keyboard and cursor evidence, so scrolling is treated as a plausible interaction trace rather than a validated stand-alone marker.

# Draft

A poker player can choose every word they say and keep their face still, yet a bet still contains information about the hand. In a study of professional poker footage, Michael Slepian and colleagues showed silent clips of players placing bets while the cards were hidden. Viewers judged hand quality more accurately from the players’ arm motions than from their faces. The result matters because professional poker is a setting built around concealment. The player knows what they want to hide, has practiced hiding it, and still moves in a way that carries information.

The tell is useful as an example because it separates two channels that everyday life usually mixes together. A person can manage the channel they intend to use for communication, such as speech or facial display, while another channel carries information through timing, tension, and movement. The second channel may be weak, noisy, and easy to misread, yet it can still add information because it is tied to the body’s preparation for action. A strong hand, a bluff, fear, fatigue, or indecision changes how movement is planned and executed. The outside observer sees only the movement, but the movement has already been shaped by the internal state.

Mental-health assessment has the same structure, although the stakes and ethics are different. The formal channel is usually language. A patient describes low mood, anxiety, sleep, appetite, hallucinations, intrusive thoughts, risk, and functioning. A questionnaire turns those descriptions into scores. A clinician asks follow-up questions and watches whether the answers fit the person’s behavior. This language-based system remains essential because mental suffering is partly first-person experience. Still, words are a narrow readout of mental state, and the reasons are familiar to anyone who has tried to ask a direct question at the wrong moment.

Stigma changes what people are willing to say. In the systematic review by Sarah Clement and colleagues, mental-health-related stigma had a small-to-moderate negative association with help-seeking, and disclosure concerns were the most common stigma-related barrier. The distortion can be subtle. A person may soften symptoms for a GP, avoid mentioning suicidal thoughts on an intake form, or describe panic as stress because a psychiatric label feels costly. The resulting sentence can be true in a limited sense and still understate the clinical problem.

Some people also lack the words they would need for an accurate report. Alexithymia, a term introduced by Peter Sifneos, refers to difficulty identifying and describing feelings. Someone can experience bodily arousal, irritability, emptiness, or dread without being able to classify it as fear, sadness, shame, or anger. Questionnaires assume that people can inspect their inner state and map it onto a response option. That assumption often works well enough for research and care, but it fails for the people whose distress is least available to language.

Limited insight creates a further problem. In psychosis, mania, severe depression, and some neurocognitive states, the condition can alter the person’s ability to recognize that anything is wrong. Anthony David’s work on insight in psychosis made this a clinical topic rather than a moral judgment. A person may report accurately from inside their current model of the world while that model has already been changed by illness. In such cases, asking for a self-report is still valuable, but it gives a partial view because the observer and the observed system are the same person.

These limits make behavior clinically important because clinicians already use it during ordinary assessment. They notice whether someone speaks more slowly than usual, answers after long pauses, appears driven by pressure, or struggles to complete a form that should be easy. Those observations rarely count as separate measurements, partly because they are hard to standardize. Human-computer interaction changes the measurement problem because devices record behavior at high temporal resolution as part of ordinary use. A keyboard records the delay between key presses and the rate of corrections. A cursor records speed, curvature, stops, and reversals. A touchscreen records pressure, swipes, and scrolling rhythm. The content of the message says what the person chose to enter; the interaction record shows how the action unfolded.

Digital phenotyping is the research term for using data from personal digital devices to measure behavior in everyday life. The phrase can make the problem sound more technical than it is. A phone is a sensor for movement, timing, location, sleep-wake rhythm, social contact, and language. A laptop is a sensor for attention, hesitation, motor control, and task switching. These signals were designed for interface control, billing, personalization, or analytics, yet they also describe how a person is acting in the world. Mental state influences action, so some part of mental state should be recoverable from those traces.

Keyboard studies show how an ordinary action can become a mental-health measurement. In a clinical sample with suicidal ideation, Loran Knol and colleagues combined daily self-reports with smartphone keyboard dynamics from the BiAffect system. One finding was that less phone movement while typing was associated with more anhedonia, the reduced capacity for interest or pleasure. Another line of work has linked mobile keyboard backspace behavior with symptoms of depression and mania. These are modest claims about statistical association. They are still important because they show that the trace left by typing contains information beyond the literal words typed on the screen.

Studies of cursor and mouse data make the limits visible. Mouse tracking has a long history in cognitive science because trajectories reveal indecision and competition between choices before a person clicks. It is tempting to extend that logic directly to stress and mood. A large study by Johannes Zimmermann and colleagues tested stress detection from mouse usage across several tasks and found little evidence for a reliable signal; classification was close to guessing, and observed effects were small. That result should stay inside the argument. Device behavior can carry mental-health information, but the mapping from trace to state depends on the task, the person, the device, and the timescale.

The poker analogy helps because every tell is interpreted in context. A hesitation can mean uncertainty, strategy, fatigue, distraction, or an internet connection problem. A slow scroll can mean low motivation, careful reading, boredom, visual impairment, or a cracked screen. A high backspace rate can mean agitation, perfectionism, small keys, unfamiliar language, or a train ride. The signal becomes meaningful when it is repeated, compared with the person’s own baseline, linked to outcomes, and tested in data held out from model-building. The scientific question asks whether many weak features, measured repeatedly, improve prediction enough to help.

Darwin’s *The Expression of the Emotions in Man and Animals* gives this modern work an older lineage because it treated emotional expression as part of the biology of emotion. Fear, in his description, changes the body: “The frightened man at first stands like a statue motionless and breathless, or crouches down as if instinctively to escape observation.” He also wrote that actions associated with states of mind can be partly repressed, while “the muscles which are least under the separate control of the will are the most liable still to act.” Darwin was studying faces, posture, voice, hair, skin, and muscles. The underlying claim was that emotion has an observable motor and bodily signature.

Human-computer interaction continues that tradition with different instruments. Darwin had observation, correspondence, photographs, and comparative anatomy. A phone has timestamps and sensors. A browser has scroll events. A keyboard has millisecond-level timing. The object of measurement has changed from face and limb to finger and cursor, but the biological premise is continuous: mental states alter perception, arousal, attention, motivation, and motor planning, and those changes affect action before they become words.

The ethical problem follows directly from the same fact. A self-report is volunteered in a recognizable form. A behavioral trace is often produced as a by-product of doing something else. People understand that they typed a sentence; they may have a much weaker understanding that the timing of their typing, the smoothness of their cursor path, or the rhythm of their scrolling could be used to infer depression, anxiety, mania, or cognitive change. This makes consent central. Sensitive mental-health information inferred from incidental behavior can harm people through discrimination, stigma, unwanted intervention, or simple loss of control over intimate facts about themselves.

Responsible use therefore requires a narrower ambition than the language of mind-reading suggests. These systems should be opt-in, transparent, limited to clear purposes, and evaluated against outcomes that matter to users and clinicians. They should report uncertainty rather than labels, and they should be tested for bias across devices, disabilities, languages, age groups, and clinical populations. A model that works in a university sample on one phone can fail in a clinic, in another country, or on a cheaper device. A signal that predicts group averages can still be too unreliable for an individual decision.

Even with those limits, the value is substantial. Many people who need help will never complete a screening questionnaire at the right time, and many who complete one will give answers shaped by shame, poor insight, or the limits of language. Device interaction offers a complementary readout that depends less on deliberate disclosure. Used well, it could identify changes that invite a careful question: your behavior has shifted; are you sleeping, functioning, or coping differently? That question is less glamorous than a machine that diagnoses from a swipe, and it is more clinically plausible.

The tell is therefore a useful model for the future of mental-health measurement. It points to information that leaks through behavior when the intended message is incomplete. In poker, that leakage may cost a hand. In mental health, it could help notice suffering that words miss, provided the measurement stays humble about what it can infer and strict about who controls the data. The goal is a better route into care for people whose distress is hidden from others, hidden from language, or partly hidden from themselves.
