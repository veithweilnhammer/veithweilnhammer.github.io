# Review — opus

Thesis (one sentence): How a person interacts with a device involuntarily leaks
mental-state information that self-report misses, because stigma, limited
insight, and difficulty naming feelings distort what people say but not how they
move. The draft develops this one argument start to finish, covers every
must-cover item, stands alone (no series framing/CTA), and re-tells none of the
forbidden material. Facts spot-checked below are sound. Issues are mostly local
voice tics plus one long list and some redundancy.

## Priority fixes (highest impact first)

1. **Piled-up "how… how… how…" enumeration (worst style hit).** Para on
   everyday interaction:
   > "How long someone pauses before starting to type, how fast and how evenly
   > the keys fall, how often a sentence is deleted and rewritten, how a cursor
   > accelerates and overshoots and corrects, how a thumb scrolls and stops, and
   > how long the hesitation runs before a message is finally sent are all
   > generated as a by-product of normal use."
   Six-clause anaphoric list built for rhythm, and hard to parse. Cut to the two
   or three cues that matter in a plain sentence, e.g. "Ordinary use records the
   pause before typing starts, the evenness of the keystrokes, and how often a
   sentence is deleted and rewritten — all as a by-product."

2. **"X, not Y" antithesis.** Para 4:
   > "Stigma changes the answer to \"how are you,\" not the rhythm with which the
   > answer is typed."
   Banned negation-contrast for effect. Say it positively: "Stigma changes the
   spoken answer while the typing rhythm behind it stays the same."

3. **"produce the opposite" — banned opposite-trade parallelism.** Mechanism
   para:
   > "Anxiety can produce the opposite, a psychomotor agitation that speeds and
   > roughens motor output."
   Describe it directly: "Anxiety can instead speed and roughen motor output, a
   psychomotor agitation."

4. **Punchy setup/announcement sentences opening paragraphs.** Two short lines
   that advertise the point instead of stating it:
   > "The mechanism behind this is well understood." → fold into the next
   > sentence: "The mechanism is a well-understood loop: every deliberate
   > movement is planned and then corrected…"
   > "The BiAffect study is a concrete case." → merge into what follows: "One
   > concrete case, the BiAffect study, recorded the metadata of smartphone
   > typing…"
   Also consider trimming the topic sentence "That gap between what a person
   controls and what escapes anyway is the whole point of a tell." — borderline
   announcement; it survives if kept, but tighten.

5. **Redundancy in the "says vs does" paragraph (para 4).** The same point is
   restated ~4 times: "corrupt what a person says while leaving much of what a
   person does intact" / the stigma line / "still moves through a screen
   differently while low" / "sits mostly outside the reach of the judgment and
   willingness" / the patient-typing example. Cut to two beats: the general
   claim plus the patient example. Tightens pacing without losing content.

6. **Two long requirement/bias lists — trim for cadence.**
   > "responsible use has to be opt-in, transparent, limited to clear purposes,
   > evaluated against outcomes that matter, and tested for bias across devices,
   > disabilities, languages, and clinical groups."
   Five requirements + four bias axes piled in one sentence. Keep the load-
   bearing ones (opt-in, transparent, tested for bias) and drop the rest.

## Minor / optional

- "None of this turns behavior into a clear window." — mild metaphor opener but
  acceptable as a plain claim; keep if you like it.
- "The property that makes a tell valuable is also what makes it fraught" —
  slightly aphoristic antithesis, but it earns its place; leave or plain it out.
- Closing triad "cannot yet see, cannot name, or has decided not to speak" maps
  onto the three failure modes, so it reads as substance, not crescendo — fine.
