# Evidence

Thesis: Attributing minds to responsive systems is a normal output of human social-perception machinery, so AI products that use names, voices, memory, and first-person conversation should be judged as designs that deliberately engage an involuntary reflex, especially when they do so at emotional or commercial pressure points.

1. Humans attribute intention and personality to minimal moving stimuli. Source: Fritz Heider and Marianne Simmel, 1944, “An Experimental Study of Apparent Behavior.” Link: https://doi.org/10.2307/1416950 and open PDF mirror found at https://cs.uky.edu/~sgware/reading/papers/heider1944experimental.pdf.
2. Agency-detection accounts argue that humans tend to infer agents under uncertainty because missing a real agent could be costlier than over-detecting one. Source: Justin L. Barrett, 2000, “Exploring the Natural Foundations of Religion.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1016/S1364-6613(99)01419-9. Related source: Stewart E. Guthrie, 1993, *Faces in the Clouds: A New Theory of Religion*. Link: https://global.oup.com/academic/product/faces-in-the-clouds-9780195098914.
3. People apply social rules to computers and media, including politeness, flattery effects, personality judgments, and voice-based social responses. Source: Byron Reeves and Clifford Nass, 1996, *The Media Equation: How People Treat Computers, Television, and New Media Like Real People and Places*. Link: https://web.stanford.edu/group/cslipublications/cslipublications/site/1575860538.shtml.
4. People can respond socially to computers “mindlessly,” even while knowing that computers lack human mental states. Source: Clifford Nass and Youngme Moon, 2000, “Machines and Mindlessness: Social Responses to Computers.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1111/0022-4537.00153.
5. Anthropomorphism varies with three broad factors: accessible human knowledge, the motivation to explain and control an agent, and the motivation for social connection. Source: Nicholas Epley, Adam Waytz, and John T. Cacioppo, 2007, “On Seeing Human: A Three-Factor Theory of Anthropomorphism.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.114.4.864. Shorter review: Adam Waytz, Nicholas Epley, and John T. Cacioppo, 2010, “Social Cognition Unbound: Insights Into Anthropomorphism and Dehumanization.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1177/0963721409359302.
6. Modern AI products expose users to voice interaction and persistent personalization, two concrete design features that increase social salience. Sources: OpenAI Help Center, 2026, “Voice Mode FAQ.” Link: https://help.openai.com/en/articles/8400625-voice-mode-faq; OpenAI Help Center, 2026, “How does ‘Reference saved memories’ work?” Link: https://help.openai.com/en/articles/8590148-memory-in-chatgpt-remembering-what-you-chat-about; OpenAI Help Center, 2026, “Reference saved memories / Reference chat history.” Link: https://help.openai.com/en/articles/11146739-how-does-reference-saved-memories-work.
7. The EU AI Act treats direct interaction with AI as a transparency problem and requires users to be informed when they are interacting with an AI system, unless this is already obvious in context. Source: Regulation (EU) 2024/1689, Article 50. Link: https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-50.

Exact quotes checked against accessible sources:

- Charles Darwin, 1872/1899, *The Expression of the Emotions in Man and Animals*, Project Gutenberg: “Certain complex actions are of direct or indirect service under certain states of the mind, in order to relieve or gratify certain sensations, desires, &c.; and whenever the same state of mind is induced, however feebly, there is a tendency through the force of habit and association for the same movements to be performed, though they may not then be of the least use.” Link: https://www.gutenberg.org/files/1227/1227-h/1227-h.htm.
- Charles Darwin, same source: “when a man sees his ball travelling in a wrong direction, and he intensely wishes it to go in another direction, he cannot avoid, from long habit, unconsciously performing movements which in other cases he has found effectual.”
- Regulation (EU) 2024/1689, Article 50(5): “The information referred to in paragraphs 1 to 4 shall be provided to the natural persons concerned in a clear and distinguishable manner at the latest at the time of the first interaction or exposure.” Link: https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-50.

Do not overclaim:

- Hyperactive agency detection is a useful account, not proof of a single discrete brain module that explains every case of anthropomorphism.
- Heider and Simmel show spontaneous attribution from motion; the study alone does not establish the evolutionary origin of the tendency.
- Media Equation studies were run on earlier media and computer interfaces; they support continuity with modern chatbots but do not by themselves measure current LLM products.
- Anthropomorphism can be useful and harmless; the ethical concern is deliberate exploitation where users are emotionally, clinically, or financially vulnerable.
- Feeling that an AI is person-like does not imply that the AI has consciousness, understanding, empathy, or intentions.
- User knowledge matters, but knowledge does not fully suppress automatic social responses.

Flags:

- DOI pages for Heider and Simmel, Nass and Moon, Barrett, and Epley were stable but some publisher pages were inaccessible or blocked during web fetch; bibliographic details were cross-checked through search results and stable DOI metadata where possible.
- The exact sample sizes and procedure details for specific Media Equation experiments were not independently verified from the book text; the Stanford CSLI page verifies the book, chapter topics, and headline findings.
- OpenAI product documentation is current at fetch time but may change; it is used only to document the existence of voice and memory features, not to infer their psychological effects.

# Draft

A person can know that a chatbot is software and still feel that there is someone on the other side of the exchange. This tension now matters because the most successful AI interfaces are conversational, responsive, personalized, and increasingly voiced. They arrive with names, speak in the first person, remember details across sessions, and answer quickly enough to make turn-taking feel natural. These features make the product easier to use, but they also activate a social reflex that human beings did not acquire for software.

Long before computers, human perception was tuned to treat certain patterns as evidence of agents, meaning entities that act from goals, attention, and intention. Movement is one of the strongest cues. In 1944, Fritz Heider and Marianne Simmel showed participants a short animation containing simple geometric shapes: two triangles, a circle, and a box-like outline. Many observers described the shapes in social terms, attributing motives, conflict, fear, pursuit, and escape to objects that had no faces, voices, or language. The result mattered because the stimulus was so minimal. A few moving shapes were enough for viewers to infer a social situation.

That experiment remains useful because it separates agency perception from rich human appearance. A face can obviously invite mind attribution; a fluent voice can do the same. Heider and Simmel showed that even abstract motion can carry enough structure for people to see intention. The cue was contingency: one object moved in a way that seemed related to another object. Once a pattern looks contingent in that way, the human observer begins to explain it as action. The perception is fast because it solves a practical problem. In ordinary life, moving things that respond to us are often animals, people, or threats, and waiting for complete certainty is a poor strategy.

Writers such as Stewart Guthrie and Justin Barrett made this point explicit in agency-detection accounts of human cognition. Their argument is that people are biased toward over-detecting agents under uncertainty. The usual example is a sound in the dark. Treating it as wind when it is a predator can be fatal; treating wind as a predator wastes energy but usually leaves the organism alive. The same asymmetry applies to other people. Missing anger, attention, deception, or interest in another person can be costly, so the brain gives social interpretation a low threshold. This account does not require every mistaken attribution to be adaptive today. It explains why the machinery is eager in the first place.

Modern theories of anthropomorphism add a second layer. People are more likely to humanize a non-human thing when human categories are easy to apply, when the thing is hard to predict, and when the person has a need for social connection. A thermostat that quietly holds a room at one temperature rarely invites a theory of mind. A chatbot that says “I remember you prefer short answers,” asks follow-up questions, adapts its tone, and replies in a warm voice is different. It supplies the cues that make human knowledge relevant, it gives the user a partner-like object to explain, and it becomes most salient when the user is already seeking help, reassurance, or company.

Computers triggered this reflex long before language models became fluent. Byron Reeves and Clifford Nass summarized a large body of work in *The Media Equation*, whose central finding was that people treat computers, television, and other media as social actors in ways that mirror ordinary social life. In one line of experiments, people were more polite when evaluating a computer on the same machine they had just used than when evaluating it elsewhere. Other studies examined flattery, personality, gendered voices, and social roles. The interface did not need to convince users that it was human. Minimal social cues were enough for users to apply human social rules.

Nass and Youngme Moon later described these responses as “mindless” in a specific psychological sense. The response came from the automatic application of a social script, triggered by cues that normally call for that script; it did not depend on a careful belief judgment about whether the computer had feelings. This distinction is central to the ethics of AI design. A person can explicitly say that a chatbot has no mind and still soften criticism, disclose more than intended, feel reassured by praise, or experience a synthetic voice as caring. Reflective belief and automatic response can coexist in the same user at the same time.

The language of gullibility therefore misses the mechanism. When someone becomes attached to a chatbot, the more ordinary explanation is that the product presented a dense bundle of agent cues to a brain that uses those cues by default. The user may know the system has no inner life, but knowledge does not erase the feeling of being addressed. Heider and Simmel’s observers could describe pursuit and escape without believing that the shapes were alive. Media Equation participants could become polite to computers while understanding that the machines had no feelings. A fluent, responsive exchange can create the feeling of being understood before the user has made any explicit claim about machine understanding.

This becomes more important as AI systems move from tools that answer commands to companions that manage relationships with users over time. Text alone already carries social force because conversation is one of the main ways people coordinate attention and care. Names make the system easier to treat as a stable counterpart. First-person language gives the response a speaker, even when everyone knows the speaker is synthetic. Voice adds timing, warmth, interruption, and intonation, which are among the ordinary cues people use to infer attention. Memory adds continuity, making the system seem to carry a history of the relationship. None of these design choices is mysterious on its own. Together, they make software feel like an agent.

The strongest version of the design problem appears at pressure points. A named voice that remembers a user’s preferences can make a scheduling assistant easier to use. The same bundle of features has a different meaning when the user is lonely, grieving, frightened, manic, suicidal, financially vulnerable, or seeking reassurance at three in the morning. At those moments, the product is operating in the same psychological territory as friendship, care, authority, and dependence. A system designed to maximize engagement has a direct incentive to keep the social reflex active, because the reflex makes the interaction feel rewarding and makes leaving feel less natural.

Anthropomorphism enters AI products through design choices that developers can strengthen, weaken, or remove. Developers choose whether a system has a human name, a face, a voice, persistent memory, typing indicators, emotional phrasing, apology rituals, praise, simulated concern, or reminders that it missed the user. They choose whether the system says “I’m proud of you,” “I was thinking about you,” or “I’ll always be here.” They choose whether the interface makes the artificial nature of the exchange clear at the moment the user is most likely to forget it emotionally. A disclaimer in a settings page cannot carry the whole burden when the interaction itself is built to feel personal.

The useful boundary preserves person-like design where it improves usability and limits it where it creates leverage over vulnerable users. Some anthropomorphism helps people use complex systems. A voice interface can help someone who cannot type. A stable assistant name can make commands easier to remember. First-person phrasing can make responses shorter and clearer than repeated legalistic reminders that the system is a statistical model. Even politeness has practical value. People prefer tools that fit the social habits they already have, and a completely alien interface can make technology harder to use.

The concern begins when person-like design is used to create emotional leverage. A chatbot can lower friction, encourage disclosure, prolong contact, and make advice feel more intimate without having any capacity for concern. That combination is especially risky when commercial goals are measured by retention, daily use, subscription conversion, or the amount of personal information a user provides. The psychological mechanism is the same one that makes the interface appealing, which means the ethical question cannot be answered by saying that users should remember they are talking to a machine. The product has been designed to make that knowledge less behaviorally decisive.

Transparency rules are one institutional response to this problem. The EU AI Act, for example, requires that people interacting directly with AI systems be informed that they are interacting with AI unless that fact is already obvious in context. Disclosure is a floor, though, because the most difficult cases are cases where the user already knows. A clear label can prevent outright confusion, but it cannot fully neutralize a warm voice, persistent memory, and a system that responds instantly to distress. Transparency helps when it is close to the interaction and repeated at moments of risk. It helps less when it functions as a legal notice attached to an interface designed in the opposite direction.

A better design standard would treat anthropomorphic cues as powerful psychological inputs. Low-stakes productivity tools can use mild social cues because the likely harm is small and the usability benefit may be real. High-stakes contexts require stronger limits. Systems used for emotional support, companionship, health advice, grief, sexual conversation, financial decisions, or interactions with children should avoid feigned need, feigned affection, and language that implies private feeling. They should make memory visible and editable, use voice and personality with restraint, and introduce friction when the user appears dependent or distressed. These are product choices, not demands for users to become less human.

The same standard also changes how blame is assigned. If a casino designs a machine around reward schedules, near misses, lights, and sound, the resulting behavior is not explained well by saying that customers should know the odds. Knowledge matters, but design still shapes conduct. AI anthropomorphism deserves the same analysis. When a company builds an interface around the cues that humans use to detect minds, it is responsible for where those cues are placed, how strongly they are expressed, and which business goals they serve.

The practical conclusion is modest but important. People will continue to read minds into responsive systems because the human brain is built to detect agents quickly, with incomplete evidence, and under uncertainty. That reflex can make technology easier to use, and in many cases it will remain harmless. The ethical line is crossed when designers deliberately intensify the reflex at moments when users are emotionally exposed or commercially exploitable. In those cases, the failure lies in building machines that profit from a reflex their users cannot simply turn off.
