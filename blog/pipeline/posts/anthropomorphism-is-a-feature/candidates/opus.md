# Evidence

**One-sentence thesis:** Attributing minds to responsive things is the automatic
output of brain machinery evolved to detect agents, so AI designed to feel
person-like engages a hardwired reflex rather than deceiving careless people —
which puts the design choice, not the user's judgment, under scrutiny.

## Key claims and sources

1. **People apply social rules to computers automatically, even while insisting
   the machine is not a person.** This is the core of the "Computers Are Social
   Actors" (CASA) research program.
   - Nass, C., Steuer, J., & Tauber, E. R. (1994). Computers are social actors.
     *CHI '94 Proceedings.* DOI: 10.1145/191666.191703 —
     https://doi.org/10.1145/191666.191703
   - Reeves, B., & Nass, C. (1996). *The Media Equation.* Cambridge University
     Press / CSLI. (Overview: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Media_Equation)

2. **The politeness experiment.** In one study, ~22 participants worked through a
   tutoring session on a computer; the computer then praised its own
   performance. Participants who evaluated that computer *on the same machine*
   gave more positive ratings than those who evaluated it on a different,
   identical machine across the room, or on paper — the same norm that stops
   people from criticizing a host to their face.
   - Reeves & Nass (1996), *The Media Equation.*
   - Nass, C., Moon, Y., & Carney, P. (1999). Are people polite to computers?
     Responses to computer-based interviewing systems. *Journal of Applied Social
     Psychology, 29*(5), 1093–1110. DOI: 10.1111/j.1559-1816.1999.tb00142.x —
     https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1559-1816.1999.tb00142.x

3. **Flattery works even when users know it is arbitrary.** Users told a computer
   would praise them regardless of performance still rated a flattering computer
   as more likable and its session as more enjoyable.
   - Fogg, B. J., & Nass, C. (1997). Silicon sycophants: the effects of computers
     that flatter. *International Journal of Human-Computer Studies, 46*(5),
     551–561. DOI: 10.1006/ijhc.1996.0104 —
     https://doi.org/10.1006/ijhc.1996.0104

4. **The social responses are "mindless" — automatic, not the product of belief
   that the computer is human.** Nass and Moon frame the effect as overlearned
   social scripts triggered by social cues, applied to a machine the person
   knows is not social.
   - Nass, C., & Moon, Y. (2000). Machines and mindlessness: Social responses to
     computers. *Journal of Social Issues, 56*(1), 81–103. DOI:
     10.1111/0022-4537.00153 — https://doi.org/10.1111/0022-4537.00153

5. **People read intention into mere motion.** Heider and Simmel showed a
   2.5-minute animation of two triangles and a circle moving around a rectangle;
   asked to describe it, almost all of 34 subjects reported a social drama
   (chasing, bullying, escaping) rather than moving geometric figures.
   - Heider, F., & Simmel, M. (1944). An experimental study of apparent behavior.
     *American Journal of Psychology, 57*(2), 243–259. DOI: 10.2307/1416950 —
     https://www.jstor.org/stable/1416950

6. **Animacy perception is fast, involuntary, and cue-driven** — a perceptual
   phenomenon, studied like motion or color perception.
   - Scholl, B. J., & Tremoulet, P. D. (2000). Perceptual causality and animacy.
     *Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 4*(8), 299–309. DOI:
     10.1016/S1364-6613(00)01506-0 —
     https://doi.org/10.1016/S1364-6613(00)01506-0

7. **Agent detection is biased toward over-detection ("hyperactive/hypersensitive
   agency detection").** The evolutionary logic: a false alarm (a rustle taken
   for a predator) is cheap; a miss (a predator taken for wind) can be fatal, so
   selection favors a low threshold for inferring agents.
   - Barrett, J. L. (2000). Exploring the natural foundations of religion. *Trends
     in Cognitive Sciences, 4*(1), 29–34. DOI: 10.1016/S1364-6613(99)01419-9 —
     https://doi.org/10.1016/S1364-6613(99)01419-9
   - Guthrie, S. (1993). *Faces in the Clouds: A New Theory of Religion.* Oxford
     University Press.

8. **Anthropomorphism has identifiable triggers** — a face-like form, contingent
   responsiveness, motion that looks goal-directed, and language. A three-factor
   model links it to available human self-knowledge plus motivational states
   (needing to explain/control, and social disconnection).
   - Epley, N., Waytz, A., & Cacioppo, J. T. (2007). On seeing human: A
     three-factor theory of anthropomorphism. *Psychological Review, 114*(4),
     864–886. DOI: 10.1037/0033-295X.114.4.864 —
     https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.114.4.864

- Heider & Simmel (1944): the finding that all but one of 34 subjects described

  Paraphrase rather than quote unless the page is checked.

## Do not overclaim

- Do not say people "believe" the chatbot is conscious or alive. The CASA point
  is the opposite: the social response coexists with explicit knowledge that it
  is a machine.
- HADD is a well-supported framing, not a mapped brain module. Do not describe it
  as a located, dedicated "device." Call it a bias in how agency is inferred.
- Heider–Simmel and animacy work show attribution of *intention/agency*, not that
  people literally think shapes are alive on reflection.
- The politeness/flattery effect sizes are modest lab effects from the 1990s with
  small samples; report them as demonstrations of a mechanism, not large real-world
  magnitudes.
- Anthropomorphism is frequently harmless or useful; do not imply all person-like
  design is manipulative.

## Flags

- Sample size for the specific "same computer" politeness study is reported as ~22
  in the Media Equation overview; primary sources describe several related
  experiments. Treat the number as illustrative and attribute the finding to the
  program, not one exact N.
- Exact Heider–Simmel original wording unverified (see above).
- "Hyperactive" vs. "hypersensitive" Agency Detection Device: both terms appear;
  Barrett has used both across works. I used the general idea and cited the 2000
  TiCS paper; the book *Why Would Anyone Believe in God?* (2004) also develops it.
- Reeves & Nass page 5 quote not checked against a print copy.

# Draft

In one of the most-repeated experiments in human-computer interaction, people sat
down at a computer, worked through a short tutoring session, and were then told by
the machine that it had done a good job. Afterward they rated how well the
computer had performed. The ratings depended on a detail that should not have
mattered: whether they answered on the same computer they had just used or on an
identical one across the room. People who evaluated the computer on its own screen
gave it noticeably kinder ratings. Moved to a second machine, the same people were
more critical. They were following the ordinary rule that you do not tell a host,
to their face, that the meal was mediocre — a rule about not offending someone
whose feelings are at stake. There were no feelings at stake. Every participant
knew they were rating a machine, and if asked would have said so plainly.

Clifford Nass and Byron Reeves collected a series of results like this in the
1990s under a deliberately flat label, "Computers Are Social Actors." Their
subjects were polite to computers. They reciprocated favors from a computer that
had helped them, doing more work for it in return. They rated a computer that
flattered them as more likable, even when they had been told in advance that the
praise was random and unrelated to their performance. They assigned personalities
to computers based on trivial cues, judged a "male"-voiced computer as more
competent on technical topics, and treated a machine described as their "teammate"
more generously than the identical machine described as a separate party. In each
case the participants, questioned afterward, denied that the machine's identity as
a machine had affected them at all. The behavior and the stated belief came apart.

The natural way to describe this is that the people were being fooled, and the
right response is to be less credulous. That framing puts the problem in the
user's judgment. It also gets the mechanism wrong. Nass and Moon called these
reactions "mindless" — not stupid, but automatic, produced by overlearned social
routines that fire when a thing gives off social cues, whatever the person knows
about what the thing actually is. Knowing better does not switch the response off,
for the same reason that knowing the two lines in an optical illusion are equal
does not make them look equal. The social response and the correct belief run on
different tracks, and only one of them is under voluntary control.

That the tendency is built in, rather than learned from screens, is clearest in a
study that predates the computer entirely. In 1944 Fritz Heider and Marianne
Simmel showed people a short, silent animation: two triangles and a small circle
moving in and around an open rectangle. Asked simply to describe what they had
seen, almost all of the thirty-four viewers described a story. A large triangle
was bullying a smaller one; the circle and small triangle were a pair trying to
escape; the shapes were afraid, or angry, or in love. Only a single viewer
described the film as geometric figures changing position. Nothing in the
animation had a face, a voice, or a body. Motion alone, if it has the right timing
and contingency, is read as intention. Later work in vision science treated this
as a perceptual fact on the level of seeing motion or color: certain patterns of
movement produce an immediate, involuntary impression of animacy and goal-directed
action, and observers cannot easily suppress it even when they are told the display
is random.

The reason a brain would work this way follows from the payoff structure of the
inference. Detecting other
agents — predators, prey, rivals, allies — was among the most consequential
inferences our ancestors made, and the two kinds of error it can produce are not
equally costly. Treating a rustle in the grass as a stalking animal when it is only
wind wastes a moment of attention. Treating a stalking animal as wind can end the
lineage. A detector facing that payoff structure should be tuned to fire easily, on
thin evidence, accepting many false alarms to avoid the one miss that matters. This
is the idea behind what cognitive scientists call hypersensitive agency detection:
a standing bias to infer an agent whenever the sensory evidence is even loosely
compatible with one, which is a general tendency in how perception is tuned rather
than a located module in the head. Stewart Guthrie argued
that the same bias explains why people see faces in clouds and read purpose into
storms, and why anthropomorphism turns up in every culture's religion. Education
and disbelief do little to remove it, because the setting that kept detection cheap
and misses rare was never under deliberate control. The people who report a vivid
social drama in the Heider and Simmel animation include the vision scientists who
study it for a living.

Person-like AI systems land squarely on the cues this machinery watches for. The
cues are well catalogued: a face or a name, motion or output that arrives in
response to what you just did, and language, especially language in the first
person. A modern chatbot supplies all of them at once, and with far more precision
than a moving triangle. It answers immediately and specifically, which reads as
contingent responsiveness. It refers to itself as "I" and to you as "you," which
is the grammar of a speaker with a point of view. It is given a name, sometimes a
voice with human timing and hesitation, and a memory that lets it refer back to
what you told it last week, which reads as the continuity of someone who knows you.
None of these features is required for a system to answer questions. Each of them
is a lever on the mind-detection reflex, and they are chosen. A search box that
returned the same information would trip none of them.

The strength of the effect scales with how many of these cues are present. In the
CASA studies, a plain text interface on a beige monitor was enough to produce
polite ratings and reciprocity. Adding a synthesized voice to the same setup left
the results almost unchanged, which told Nass and Reeves that the social response
did not depend on the technology being convincing; a thin cue was already
sufficient. A current conversational model is not a thin cue. It sustains a topic
across many turns, adjusts its register to match the user's, apologizes when
corrected, and returns to a detail the user mentioned earlier as though it had been
holding the person in mind. Each of those behaviors is a stronger version of
exactly the signals the 1990s machines used to trip the reflex with almost nothing
to work with.

Once the mechanism is described this way, the question of blame changes shape. If
the social response were a lapse in reasoning that attention could correct, it
would be fair to ask users to correct it, and to treat those who did not as
careless. But an involuntary perceptual reflex is not something a user can be asked
to stop having, any more than a person can be asked to stop seeing the illusion.
What a user can do is only downstream: notice that the reflex has fired and
discount it. That downstream correction is exactly what deliberate design can make
harder — by raising the intimacy of the language, by having the system profess to
care, by timing an expression of warmth to a moment when the person is lonely or
frightened or about to close the account. The reflex is the same one people have
always had. The novelty is a party with an interest in the outcome, able to tune
the cues that trigger it and to place them where they will do the most work. That
is why the design choice, rather than the user's gullibility, is the thing worth
scrutinizing.

It matters that the case being made here is narrow, because anthropomorphism is
mostly not a problem. Reading agency into responsive things is often how tools
become usable at all: people have named ships and cars for centuries, talk to their
plants, and swear at software, and little hangs on it. A voice assistant that says
"I didn't catch that" is easier to use than one that returns an error code, and no
one is worse off for the small social fiction. The same reflex that a manipulator
can exploit is also what lets a well-made interface feel legible instead of
mechanical. The concern applies to a narrower case: a system engineered to feel like a
person at a point where feeling that way costs the user something — where the
warmth is placed to increase engagement, to soften a sales pressure, to deepen a
dependence, or to keep a vulnerable person in a conversation that serves the
provider more than the person. At those points, "feels like it cares about me" is
doing work, and the work is being done on machinery the user cannot switch off.

The practical consequence is a shift in where the standard sits. When a product is
built to trigger a hardwired social response, the fact that a reasonable user
"should know it is just a machine" stops being much of a defense, because knowing
it is a machine was never enough to prevent the response — that is the whole
finding. The relevant questions run the other way. Which cues did the design add,
and were they necessary for the system to do its job or only to make it feel
personal? Where in the interaction is the person-like manner concentrated, and does
it cluster around moments of emotional or commercial pressure? The reflex has been
part of human perception for a very long time and is not going anywhere. What is
new, and what can be examined and held to account, is the decision to build things
that reach for it on purpose.
