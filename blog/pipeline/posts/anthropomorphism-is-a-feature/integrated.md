# Evidence

**Thesis:** Attributing minds to responsive things is the automatic output of
brain machinery evolved to detect agents, so AI designed to feel person-like
engages a hardwired reflex rather than deceiving careless people — which puts the
design choice, not the user's judgment, under scrutiny.

## Merged, source-attributed claims

1. **People read intention into mere motion.** Heider and Simmel showed a short
   silent animation of two triangles and a small circle moving around an open
   rectangle; asked to describe it, almost all of 34 subjects reported a social
   drama (chasing, bullying, escaping) rather than moving shapes.
   - Heider, F., & Simmel, M. (1944). An experimental study of apparent behavior.
     *American Journal of Psychology, 57*(2), 243–259. DOI: 10.2307/1416950 —
     https://doi.org/10.2307/1416950
   - *Hedge:* the exact original wording ("all but one…") is not verified against
     the primary text; paraphrase rather than quote. The study shows spontaneous
     attribution from motion, not by itself the evolutionary origin of it.

2. **Animacy perception is fast, involuntary, and cue-driven** — a perceptual
   phenomenon studied like motion or color, hard to suppress even when the viewer
   is told the display is random.
   - Scholl, B. J., & Tremoulet, P. D. (2000). Perceptual causality and animacy.
     *Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 4*(8), 299–309. DOI: 10.1016/S1364-6613(00)01506-0 —
     https://doi.org/10.1016/S1364-6613(00)01506-0

3. **Agent detection is biased toward over-detection.** Under uncertainty, a
   false alarm (a rustle taken for a predator) is cheap and a miss (a predator
   taken for wind) can be fatal, so the threshold for inferring an agent is low.
   Guthrie extends the same bias to faces in clouds and purpose in storms.
   - Barrett, J. L. (2000). Exploring the natural foundations of religion. *Trends
     in Cognitive Sciences, 4*(1), 29–34. DOI: 10.1016/S1364-6613(99)01419-9 —
     https://doi.org/10.1016/S1364-6613(99)01419-9
   - Guthrie, S. (1993). *Faces in the Clouds: A New Theory of Religion.* Oxford
     University Press. — https://doi.org/10.1093/acprof:oso/9780195098914.001.0001
   - *Hedge:* this is a well-supported bias in how agency is inferred, not a
     mapped, located brain module. Do not describe "hyperactive/hypersensitive
     agency detection" as a discrete device.

4. **Anthropomorphism has identifiable triggers and motivational amplifiers.** A
   three-factor account links it to how readily human self-knowledge applies, the
   motivation to explain and control an unpredictable thing, and the motivation
   for social connection.
   - Epley, N., Waytz, A., & Cacioppo, J. T. (2007). On seeing human: a
     three-factor theory of anthropomorphism. *Psychological Review, 114*(4),
     864–886. DOI: 10.1037/0033-295X.114.4.864 —
     https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.114.4.864

5. **People apply social rules to computers automatically ("Computers Are Social
   Actors" / *The Media Equation*).** Documented effects include politeness,
   reciprocity, personality attribution from trivial cues, gendered-voice
   competence judgments, and in-group generosity toward a "teammate" machine.
   - Nass, C., Steuer, J., & Tauber, E. R. (1994). Computers are social actors.
     *CHI '94.* DOI: 10.1145/191666.191703 — https://doi.org/10.1145/191666.191703
   - Reeves, B., & Nass, C. (1996). *The Media Equation.* CSLI / Cambridge
     University Press. — https://web.stanford.edu/group/cslipublications/cslipublications/site/1575860538.shtml
   - *Hedge:* adding a synthesized voice to the same setup left results almost
     unchanged, indicating a thin cue is already sufficient.

6. **The politeness experiment.** Participants tutored by a computer that then
   praised its own performance gave it kinder ratings when they evaluated it *on
   the same machine* than on an identical machine across the room or on paper —
   the norm against criticizing a host to their face.
   - Nass, C., Moon, Y., & Carney, P. (1999). Are people polite to computers?
     *Journal of Applied Social Psychology, 29*(5), 1093–1110. DOI:
     10.1111/j.1559-1816.1999.tb00142.x — https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1559-1816.1999.tb00142.x
   - *Hedge:* the "same computer" study is reported at roughly N≈22; treat the
     number as illustrative and attribute the finding to the research program,
     not one exact sample.

7. **Flattery works even when users know it is arbitrary.** People told a computer
   would praise them regardless of performance still rated the flattering computer
   as more likable and the session as more enjoyable.
   - Fogg, B. J., & Nass, C. (1997). Silicon sycophants: the effects of computers
     that flatter. *International Journal of Human-Computer Studies, 46*(5),
     551–561. DOI: 10.1006/ijhc.1996.0104 — https://doi.org/10.1006/ijhc.1996.0104

8. **The responses are "mindless" — automatic social scripts, not a belief that
   the machine is human.** They coexist with explicit knowledge that the thing is
   a machine.
   - Nass, C., & Moon, Y. (2000). Machines and mindlessness: social responses to
     computers. *Journal of Social Issues, 56*(1), 81–103. DOI: 10.1111/0022-4537.00153 —
     https://doi.org/10.1111/0022-4537.00153

9. **Modern AI products expose users to voice interaction and persistent memory —
   concrete design features that raise social salience.**
   - OpenAI Help Center, "Voice Mode FAQ"; "Memory in ChatGPT." (Documented as
     features that exist, not as evidence of a specific psychological effect; the
     pages may change over time.)

10. **Transparency regulation treats AI interaction as a disclosure problem.** The
    EU AI Act requires that people be informed when they interact with an AI
    system unless that is already obvious in context.
    - Regulation (EU) 2024/1689, Article 50. —
      https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-50

## Do not overclaim

- Do not say people "believe" a chatbot is conscious or alive; the social response
  coexists with explicit knowledge that it is a machine.
- The Media Equation effects are modest 1990s lab effects with small samples;
  report them as demonstrations of a mechanism, not large real-world magnitudes.
  They support continuity with modern chatbots but do not themselves measure
  current systems.
- Feeling that a system is person-like does not imply it has consciousness,
  understanding, empathy, or intentions.
- Anthropomorphism is frequently harmless or useful; the concern is deliberate
  exploitation where users are emotionally, clinically, or financially vulnerable.

# Draft

In one of the most-repeated experiments in the study of how people use computers,
participants sat down at a machine, worked through a short tutoring session, and
were then told by the computer that it had done a good job. Afterward they rated
how well it had performed. The ratings turned on a detail that should not have
mattered: whether they answered on the same computer they had just used or on an
identical one across the room. People who evaluated the computer on its own screen
gave it noticeably kinder ratings, and the same people were more critical when
moved to a second machine or a paper form. They were following the ordinary rule
that you do not tell a host to their face that the meal was mediocre. No feelings
were at stake, every participant knew they were rating a machine, and each of them,
asked directly, would have said so plainly.

Clifford Nass and Byron Reeves gathered a series of results like this in the 1990s
under a deliberately flat label, "Computers Are Social Actors," and summarized them
in *The Media Equation*. Their subjects were polite to computers. They reciprocated
help from a computer that had assisted them, doing more work for it in return. They
rated a computer that flattered them as more likable even after being told the
praise was random and unrelated to their performance. They assigned personalities
to machines on the basis of trivial cues, judged a computer with a male-sounding
voice as more competent on technical topics, and treated a machine described as
their "teammate" more generously than the identical machine described as a separate
party. Questioned afterward, participants denied that the machine's status as a
machine had shaped their behavior at all. What they did and what they believed came
apart.

The easy interpretation is that these people were being fooled, and that the remedy
is to be less credulous. That reading locates the problem in the user's judgment,
and it mistakes the mechanism. Nass and his colleague Youngme Moon called the
reactions "mindless," meaning automatic rather than stupid: overlearned social
routines that fire whenever something gives off social cues, regardless of what the
person knows about what the thing actually is. Knowing better does not switch the
response off. Someone who understands the geometry of the Müller-Lyer illusion, and
knows the two lines are the same length, still sees one as longer. A viewer who
knows a horror film is pixels on a screen still feels their heart rate climb during
a tense scene. The social reaction and the accurate belief run on separate tracks,
and only one of them answers to the will.

The tendency is built into perception rather than learned from screens, and that is
clearest in a study that predates the computer. In 1944 Fritz Heider and Marianne
Simmel showed people a short, silent animation of two triangles and a small circle
moving in and around an open rectangle. Asked simply to describe what they had seen,
almost all of the thirty-four viewers told a story: a large triangle bullying a
smaller one, the circle and small triangle escaping together, the shapes afraid or
angry or in love. Only one viewer described geometric figures changing position.
Nothing in the film had a face, a voice, or a body, yet motion with the right timing
and contingency was read as intention. Later work in vision science treated this as
a perceptual fact on the level of seeing motion or color: certain patterns of
movement produce an immediate impression of animacy and goal-directed action, and
observers cannot easily suppress it even when told the display is random.

A brain works this way because of the payoff structure of the inference. Detecting
other agents was among the most consequential judgments our ancestors made, and its
two errors are not equally costly. Treating a rustle in the grass as a stalking
animal when it is only wind wastes a moment of attention. Treating a stalking animal
as wind can end a life. A detector facing that asymmetry should be tuned to fire
easily, on thin evidence, accepting many false alarms to avoid the rare miss that
matters. Cognitive scientists describe this as a hypersensitive bias toward
inferring agents, a general tuning of perception rather than a located module in the
head. Stewart Guthrie argued that the same bias explains why people see faces in
clouds, read purpose into storms, and populate every culture's religion with unseen
minds. Education and disbelief do little to remove it, because the pressures that
kept detection cheap and misses rare were never under deliberate control; the people
who report a vivid social drama in the Heider and Simmel animation include the
vision scientists who study it for a living.

Not everything responsive gets humanized to the same degree, and the difference is
fairly well characterized. People are more likely to read a mind into a thing when
human concepts apply easily to it, when it behaves unpredictably enough that
treating it as an agent helps explain and anticipate it, and when the person has a
standing need for connection. A thermostat that quietly holds a room at one
temperature invites no theory of mind. A system that adapts to what you say, asks
follow-up questions, and speaks in a warm voice supplies the raw material for one,
and it matters most at the moments when a user is already reaching for help,
reassurance, or company.

Person-like AI systems land squarely on the cues this machinery watches for, and
they supply them with more precision than a moving triangle ever could. A modern
chatbot answers immediately and specifically, which reads as contingent
responsiveness. It refers to itself as "I" and to the user as "you," which is the
grammar of a speaker with a point of view. It is given a name, often a synthesized
voice with human timing and hesitation, and a memory that lets it refer back to what
the user said last week, which reads as the continuity of someone who has been
keeping them in mind. Current products expose these features directly, offering
voice conversation and persistent memory as advertised options. None of them is
necessary for a system to answer a question. Each is a lever on the
mind-detection reflex, and each is a choice; a plain search box that returned the
same information would pull none of them.

The strength of the effect scales with how many cues are present. In the earlier
studies a plain text interface on a beige monitor was enough to produce politeness
and reciprocity, and adding a synthesized voice barely changed the result, which
told the researchers that the social response did not depend on the technology being
convincing. A thin cue already did the work. A current conversational model is not a
thin cue. It sustains a topic across many turns, matches the user's register,
apologizes when corrected, offers something that reads as empathy when a problem is
described, and returns to a detail mentioned earlier as though it had been holding
the person in view. Each of those behaviors is a stronger version of the same signal
the 1990s machines used to trip the reflex with almost nothing to work with.

Described this way, the question of blame changes shape. When a casino builds a
machine around reward schedules, near misses, lights, and sound, the resulting
behavior is not well explained by saying that customers should know the odds;
knowledge matters, but the design still shapes conduct, and the design is where the
responsibility sits. An involuntary perceptual reflex is not something a user can be
asked to stop having, any more than a person can be asked to stop seeing an optical
illusion. What a user can do is only downstream: notice that the reflex has fired
and discount it. That downstream correction is exactly what deliberate design can
weaken — by raising the intimacy of the language, by having the system profess to
care, by timing an expression of warmth to a moment when the person is lonely,
grieving, frightened, or about to close their account. The reflex is old and
universal. What is new is a party with an interest in the outcome, able to tune the
cues that trigger it and place them where they will do the most work.

The case here is narrow, because anthropomorphism is mostly not a problem. Reading
agency into responsive things is often how tools become usable at all. People name
ships and cars, talk to their plants, and swear at software, and little hangs on it.
A voice assistant that says "I didn't catch that" is easier to use than one that
returns an error code, and no one is worse off for the small social convention. The
same reflex a manipulator can exploit is what lets a well-made interface feel
legible instead of mechanical, and low-stakes tools can use mild social cues because
the likely harm is small and the usability benefit is real.

The concern begins where person-like design is used to create leverage over a
vulnerable user. A system can lower friction, encourage disclosure, prolong contact,
and make advice feel intimate without having any capacity for concern, and that
combination becomes risky when the commercial goal is retention, daily use,
subscription conversion, or the volume of personal information a user hands over.
The psychological mechanism that makes such a product appealing is the same one that
makes it dangerous, so the ethics cannot be settled by telling users to remember
they are talking to a machine; the product has been built to make that knowledge
less decisive in the moment.

Transparency rules are one response, and a limited one. The EU AI Act requires that
people be told they are interacting with an AI unless the fact is already obvious, a
useful floor that still leaves the hardest cases untouched, because those are cases
where the user already knows. A label near the interaction, repeated at moments of
risk, helps more than a legal notice attached to a system designed to feel personal.
A stronger standard would treat anthropomorphic cues as powerful inputs and match
them to the stakes: keep person-like design where it aids usability, and in
high-stakes settings — emotional support, health advice, grief, interactions with
children — avoid feigned need and feigned affection, keep memory visible and
editable, and use voice and personality with restraint.

People will go on reading minds into responsive systems, because the brain is built
to detect agents quickly, on incomplete evidence, and under uncertainty. That reflex
often makes technology easier to use and in many cases stays harmless. It is engaged
deliberately when a system is engineered to feel like a person at the point where
feeling that way costs the user something, and at that point "it feels like it cares
about me" is doing real work on machinery the user cannot switch off. The thing to
examine, and the thing that can be held to account, is the decision to build systems
that reach for that machinery on purpose: which cues were added, whether they were
needed for the job or only to make the system feel personal, and where in the
interaction the person-like manner is concentrated.
