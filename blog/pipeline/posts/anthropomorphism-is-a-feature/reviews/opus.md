# Review — `anthropomorphism-is-a-feature` (integrated draft)

Reviewer: opus. Scope: the `# Draft` prose in `integrated.md` (the `# Evidence`
block above it is working material, not the post). Overall this is a strong,
measured draft that largely matches the manuscript cadence. The problems are a
handful of recurring AI-tells (parallel "trade" pairs, punchy setup openers,
short negation beats), a thesis that surfaces too late, and one redundant
paragraph. All are line-level fixes.

---

## 1. Style-guide violations

### 1a. Antithesis / parallel "trade" constructions (hard ban — highest priority)

**The rustle/wind pair.** The clearest violation, a textbook two-sentence
antithesis built for rhythm:
> "Treating a rustle in the grass as a stalking animal when it is only wind
> wastes a moment of attention. Treating a stalking animal as wind can end a
> life."

This is exactly the banned "opposite trade" parallel ("Movement stayed on stage;
meaning left."), and "can end a life" is a dramatic short clause. Rewrite as one
plain sentence:
> "The two errors are not equally costly: mistaking wind for a predator wastes a
> little attention, while mistaking a predator for wind can be fatal, so a
> detector facing that asymmetry should fire on thin evidence."

**Old / new antithesis near the end:**
> "The reflex is old and universal. What is new is a party with an interest in
> the outcome, able to tune the cues that trigger it and place them where they
> will do the most work."

"The reflex is old and universal." is a short punchy beat, and "What is new
is…" completes an old/new antithesis. Rewrite:
> "What has changed is that a party with an interest in the outcome can now tune
> the cues that trigger this old, universal reflex and place them where they do
> the most work."

**Thermostat vs. adaptive system.** A contrast pair used for rhythm:
> "A thermostat that quietly holds a room at one temperature invites no theory
> of mind. A system that adapts to what you say, asks follow-up questions, and
> speaks in a warm voice supplies the raw material for one…"

The contrast is illustrative and mostly earns its place, but it leans on the
banned symmetry. Consider merging so the two examples sit inside one explanatory
sentence rather than mirrored clauses. Also "theory of mind" is undefined jargon
(see 1e).

**Appealing / dangerous:**
> "The psychological mechanism that makes such a product appealing is the same
> one that makes it dangerous…"

A "same X that makes Y" antithesis. It carries a real point, so this is a softer
flag, but consider: "The same mechanism that makes such a product appealing is
what makes it risky when the goal is retention or disclosure."

### 1b. Punchy setup / announcement sentences opening paragraphs (hard ban)

Three paragraphs open with a short line that advertises the point instead of
stating it:
> "A brain works this way because of the payoff structure of the inference."
> "The strength of the effect scales with how many cues are present."
> "Described this way, the question of blame changes shape."

Each should fold its claim into the substantive sentence that follows. E.g. for
the first: "The bias exists because detecting other agents was among the most
consequential judgments our ancestors made, and its two errors are not equally
costly." "changes shape" is also mild figuration.

### 1c. Short negation beats / fragments-for-effect

> "A thin cue already did the work. A current conversational model is not a thin
> cue."

"A current conversational model is not a thin cue" is a negation-for-effect
beat, and the two short sentences form a punchy pair against the measured
cadence. Rewrite: "A plain 1990s text interface was already a thin cue that
sufficed; a current conversational model supplies far more."

> "What they did and what they believed came apart."

A short dramatic closing beat on paragraph 2. State it plainly inside a sentence:
"Their behavior and their stated beliefs diverged."

### 1d. Rhetorical understatement opener

> "Transparency rules are one response, and a limited one."

"and a limited one" is the banned understatement tack-on ("Measurement is not
the whole problem, though."). Rewrite: "Transparency rules are one response, but
a limited one, because…" — and go straight to the reason.

### 1e. Jargon not defined for the widest audience

- **"theory of mind"** ("invites no theory of mind") — used twice-ish
  implicitly; never defined. A non-specialist may not know the term. Either
  define in a clause or use "invites no attribution of a mind."
- **"Müller-Lyer illusion"** — named, but the surrounding clause ("knows the two
  lines are the same length, still sees one as longer") does define it well
  enough. Acceptable; borderline.
- **"contingent responsiveness" / "contingency"** — "which reads as contingent
  responsiveness" uses a term a lay reader won't parse. Say "which reads as
  responding to what you just did."

### 1f. Piled-up lists for rhythm (softer flags)

Several enumerations run to 4–5 items where the guide asks for the two that
matter:
> "It sustains a topic across many turns, matches the user's register, apologizes
> when corrected, offers something that reads as empathy when a problem is
> described, and returns to a detail mentioned earlier…"
> "…lonely, grieving, frightened, or about to close their account."
> "…the shapes afraid or angry or in love."

These are content-bearing (not pure mood), so lower severity, but trimming the
5-item chains to the two strongest would match the manuscript rhythm. The
"which reads as… / which is the grammar of… / which reads as…" anaphora in the
"Person-like AI systems land squarely…" paragraph is also a mild parallel
crescendo — vary the sentence shapes.

### 1g. Minor figuration

> "The social reaction and the accurate belief run on separate tracks, and only
> one of them answers to the will."

"run on separate tracks" and "answers to the will" are lightly literary. Not a
hard ban, but "only one of them answers to the will" is the kind of abstract
flourish the guide flags. Consider: "…and only the belief is under voluntary
control."

**Net:** no "not X, it is Y" copula violations in the body (good), but enough
parallel pairs, punchy openers, and negation beats that the piece currently reads
more AI-cadenced than the manuscripts in a handful of spots. All fixable by line
edit.

## 2. Factual grounding

Overall well-grounded; every substantive claim traces to the evidence block and
the citations are real. Specific notes:

- **Heider & Simmel "only one" claim.** > "almost all of the thirty-four viewers
  told a story… Only one viewer described geometric figures changing position."
  Verified against secondary consensus (34 subjects; all but one described the
  animation in animate/intentional terms) via web search. The evidence block's
  own hedge flags that the exact primary-text wording is unverified. Low risk —
  the "all but one of 34" figure is the standard reported result — but keep the
  phrasing as a paraphrase (it currently is) and do not add a direct quote.

- **Opening superlative — mild overclaim.** > "In one of the most-repeated
  experiments in the study of how people use computers…" Nothing in the evidence
  supports "most-repeated." Soften to "In a much-cited experiment…" or drop the
  superlative.

- **Politeness experiment.** Same-machine vs. identical-machine-across-the-room
  vs. paper form, kinder ratings on the same machine — supported (Nass, Moon &
  Carney 1999). Good that the draft omits the N≈22 figure the evidence flagged as
  illustrative.

- **Flattery "told the praise was random."** Supported (Fogg & Nass 1997). Good.

- **Male-voice competence, "teammate" generosity, reciprocity.** All map to
  evidence claim 5 (CASA / Media Equation). Accurate, including the male-voice /
  technical-topic direction.

- **"adding a synthesized voice barely changed the result."** Matches evidence
  hedge (claim 5). Good.

- **EU AI Act Article 50.** "requires that people be told they are interacting
  with an AI unless the fact is already obvious" — accurate characterization of
  Art. 50 transparency obligations. Fine.

No unsupported claims of the machine being "believed" conscious — the draft
correctly keeps the response and the belief separate, honoring the "do not
overclaim" list.

## 3. Structure & clarity

- **Thesis surfaces too late.** The post opens with ~two full paragraphs of the
  politeness/Media-Equation anecdote before the actual claim appears, and even
  then only implicitly: > "That reading locates the problem in the user's
  judgment, and it mistakes the mechanism." The brief's thesis — the *design
  choice, not the user's judgment*, is what to scrutinize — is never stated as
  cleanly in the body as in the brief. The style guide's structure step 3 wants
  the single argument stated plainly and early. Recommend surfacing a one-line
  version of the thesis by the end of paragraph 1 or start of paragraph 2, then
  using the anecdote as support. The narrative lede is grounded (a real study),
  so it can stay, but the claim should arrive sooner.

- **Redundancy between paragraphs 8 and 9.** Paragraph 8 ("Person-like AI
  systems land squarely…") already lists the chatbot cues (immediate specific
  answers, "I"/"you," name, voice, memory). Paragraph 9 ("The strength of the
  effect scales…") re-lists a very similar set (sustains a topic, matches
  register, apologizes, empathy, returns to a detail). The two paragraphs overlap
  heavily. Merge: keep the "scaling with number of cues" logic and the 1990s
  "thin cue" contrast, but cut the second cue enumeration so the reader isn't
  told the same list twice.

- **Otherwise the arc is clear and followable by a non-specialist:** study →
  "you're being fooled" is the wrong frame → mechanism is perceptual (Heider &
  Simmel) → evolutionary why → what raises/lowers it → AI supplies those cues →
  blame shifts to design (casino analogy) → honest boundary → the real concern →
  regulation → close. Good logical order.

## 4. One self-contained argument

**Thesis in one sentence:** Attributing minds to responsive things is the
automatic output of evolved agent-detection machinery, so when AI is designed to
feel person-like the responsibility sits with the designers who deliberately
engage that involuntary reflex, not with users who "should know better."

The post develops this one argument start to finish and does **not** branch. It
resists the brief's warned-against drift into a generic "is AI conscious /
thinking" essay — good. The empathy mention ("offers something that reads as
empathy") is a single cue in a list, not a re-argument of the empathy thesis, so
it stays in its lane.

One paragraph to pressure-test: the moderators paragraph ("Not everything
responsive gets humanized to the same degree…"). It does serve the argument (it
explains *why AI in particular* trips the reflex), so keep it — but it is the
one place the piece could be accused of a mini-detour into Epley's three-factor
theory. It earns its place because it sets up "it matters most at the moments
when a user is already reaching for help." No cut needed.
