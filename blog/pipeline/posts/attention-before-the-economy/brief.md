# Brief: Attention Was a Scientific Concept Before It Was an Economy

## Slug

## Working title
Attention Was a Scientific Concept Before It Was an Economy

## One-line angle (also the Substack subtitle / SEO hook)
Long before "the attention economy," a century of psychology studied attention as a limited, effortful faculty — and that science names costs the business model quietly ignores.

## Single argument (thesis)
Attention was a well-studied scientific concept — a limited, selective, effortful faculty — for a century before it became an economic commodity, and that older science reveals real costs of the attention economy that its business logic treats as external.

## Concrete anchor (optional — a real example to ground the argument)
William James's 1890 treatment of attention in *The Principles of Psychology* ("Everyone knows what attention is...") set beside Herbert Simon's 1971 observation that a wealth of information creates a poverty of attention — the moment the concept crossed from psychology into economics.

## What this post must cover
- Attention as a scientific object: from William James (1890) onward, psychology studied attention as selective, limited in capacity, and effortful to sustain — a faculty with measurable properties and costs.
- What the science establishes: attention is a finite resource, switching between tasks carries costs, sustained attention fatigues, and interruption degrades performance — findings with real bite.
- The pivot to economics: Herbert Simon's 1971 point that information consumes attention, so an abundance of information creates scarcity of attention, which later hardened into "the attention economy."
- The mismatch: the business model treats captured attention as a resource to be maximized and sold, while the psychology treats attention as a limited faculty whose overuse and fragmentation carry costs to the person.
- The costs the model externalizes: fragmentation, chronic interruption, depletion, and the crowding out of sustained thought — harms borne by users, not priced by advertisers.
- The conclusion: taking the older science seriously reframes attention capture not as neutral engagement but as drawing down a limited human faculty.
