# Evidence

Thesis: Attention was a well-studied scientific concept — a limited, selective, effortful faculty — for a century before it became an economic commodity, and that older science reveals costs of the attention economy that its business logic leaves outside the price.

1. William James treated attention as selective possession of one object among several possible objects, involving concentration and withdrawal from competing objects. Source: William James, 1890, *The Principles of Psychology*, chapter XI, “Attention.” Link: https://www.gutenberg.org/files/57628/57628-h/57628-h.htm
2. James also described experience as shaped by selective interest rather than by all sensory input that reaches a person. Source: William James, 1890, *The Principles of Psychology*, chapter XI, “Attention.” Link: https://www.gutenberg.org/files/57628/57628-h/57628-h.htm
3. Selective attention was later studied experimentally through competing speech streams and dichotic listening. Source: E. Colin Cherry, 1953, “Some Experiments on the Recognition of Speech, with One and with Two Ears.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1121/1.1907229
4. Mid-century psychology treated information processing as capacity-limited, including limits in immediate memory and judgment. Source: George A. Miller, 1956, “The magical number seven, plus or minus two: Some limits on our capacity for processing information.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1037/h0043158
5. Sustained attention has a measurable vigilance decrement and requires mental work. Source: Joel S. Warm, Raja Parasuraman, and Gerald Matthews, 2008, “Vigilance Requires Hard Mental Work and Is Stressful.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1518/001872008X312152
6. Switching between even simple tasks produces measurable costs. Source: Robert D. Rogers and Stephen Monsell, 1995, “Costs of a predictible switch between simple cognitive tasks.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1037/0096-3445.124.2.207
7. Task switching remained a central finding across cognitive psychology, with costs partly reflecting control processes and carry-over from the previous task. Source: Stephen Monsell, 2003, “Task switching.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1016/S1364-6613(03)00028-7
8. Interruptions disrupt computing tasks and require recovery. Source: Shamsi T. Iqbal and Eric Horvitz, 2007, “Disruption and recovery of computing tasks.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1145/1240624.1240730
9. Interrupted work can be completed under greater time pressure and stress even when people compensate by working faster. Source: Gloria Mark, Daniela Gudith, and Ulrich Klocke, 2008, “The cost of interrupted work: More speed and stress.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1145/1357054.1357072
10. Herbert Simon moved the concept toward economics by arguing that information consumes attention and that information abundance creates attention scarcity. Source: Herbert A. Simon, 1971, “Designing Organizations for an Information-Rich World,” in Martin Greenberger, ed., *Computers, Communications, and the Public Interest*. Catalog link for the volume: https://openlibrary.org/works/OL22507855W/Computers_communications_and_the_public_interest
11. The phrase “attention economy” later became explicit in writing about the web. Source: Michael H. Goldhaber, 1997, “The attention economy and the Net.” Link: https://doi.org/10.5210/fm.v2i4.519
12. Heavy media multitasking is associated with differences in cognitive control in laboratory tasks, but this evidence should not be treated as proof that media multitasking alone causes broad cognitive decline. Source: Eyal Ophir, Clifford Nass, and Anthony D. Wagner, 2009, “Cognitive control in media multitaskers.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.0903620106

Exact quotes verified against public-domain sources:

- William James, *The Principles of Psychology*: “My experience is what I agree to attend to.”
- William James, *The Principles of Psychology*: “Every one knows what attention is. It is the taking possession by the mind, in clear and vivid form, of one out of what seem several simultaneously possible objects or trains of thought.”
- William James, *The Principles of Psychology*: “Focalization, concentration, of consciousness are of its essence. It implies withdrawal from some things in order to deal effectively with others.”

Do not overclaim:

- Do not say psychology proved attention is a literal fuel or single substance; “resource” is a useful model with limits.
- Do not claim task switching is impossible; the evidence shows measurable costs, not incapacity.
- Do not claim every interruption is harmful; the cost depends on timing, task complexity, and recovery conditions.
- Do not claim the attention economy invented attention scarcity; it monetized and intensified a constraint psychology had already studied.
- Do not turn the post into a rights argument about mental quiet, a boredom essay, or a general indictment of all digital media.
- Do not infer broad causal brain damage from media multitasking studies.

Flags:

- I verified the bibliographic existence of Simon’s 1971 chapter through catalog sources and found stable references to the edited volume, but I did not find an open primary full text from a durable institutional source during this run. I therefore paraphrase Simon rather than quote him.
- Some classic sources on attention, especially Broadbent’s *Perception and Communication* and Kahneman’s *Attention and Effort*, are books without convenient open DOI landing pages; I relied more heavily on primary articles and review articles with stable DOI links.
- The post uses “attention economy” in its broad contemporary sense, while the term’s later development includes several partially distinct traditions in advertising, web culture, and information economics.

# Draft

Attention entered modern psychology as a problem of selection long before it became a measure of engagement. William James gave the classic statement in 1890, when he described attention as the mind taking possession of one object among several possible objects. His definition already contained the central problem. To attend to one thing is to withdraw from others, so attention has a cost built into its operation from the beginning.

James was writing before smartphones, programmatic advertising, notification systems, and infinite feeds, but his account is immediately recognizable. A person can look at a page and fail to read it because the mind has been captured by another concern. A room can contain many sounds, but only one conversation becomes clear enough to follow. The world offers more possible objects than consciousness can hold at once, and attention is the faculty that selects one path through that excess.

This older scientific meaning matters because the public language around attention has shifted. Today, attention is often described as something platforms capture, retain, and sell. That economic language is useful because it names a real market. Advertisers pay for impressions, clicks, watch time, and conversions, and digital systems are designed to increase the probability that a user stays with one more item. Yet the market description starts from the side of the buyer and seller. It describes attention as inventory. Psychology starts from the side of the person. It describes attention as a limited human capacity whose use changes what else the person can perceive, think, and do.

The scientific tradition after James made this limit measurable. In the 1950s, Colin Cherry studied how people recognize speech when two messages compete, a problem later known through the “cocktail party” example. The point was practical and precise: when two speech streams arrive at once, listeners can follow one more effectively than the other, and unattended material is processed in a reduced way. Around the same period, George Miller reviewed limits in immediate memory and judgment, giving psychology one of its most famous examples of finite information-processing capacity. These studies differed in method, but they shared a premise that is now easy to forget. Human beings are selective systems because they have to be.

Selection is valuable because it makes thought possible. A mind that gave equal weight to every incoming signal would be flooded by detail. James saw this clearly when he wrote that experience is what a person agrees to attend to. That sentence can sound like philosophy, but it also states a practical fact about cognition. The contents of awareness are filtered by interest, task, habit, and effort. The filtered-out material may still exist in the environment, but it does not shape the person in the same way as what they notice, rehearse, and act on.

The limit also appears over time. Sustained attention is work. Vigilance research, including the long line of studies following radar and signal-detection tasks, showed that people become worse at monitoring for rare events as time passes. Warm, Parasuraman, and Matthews summarized the point bluntly in their 2008 review: vigilance requires hard mental work and is stressful. This finding is important because sustained attention often looks passive from the outside. A person watching a display, reading a dense document, or listening carefully in a meeting may appear to be doing very little. The mental demand lies in maintaining readiness while resisting drift.

The same logic applies when attention is moved too often. Task-switching experiments show that changing from one simple task to another carries a measurable cost, even when the participant knows a switch is coming. Rogers and Monsell demonstrated this in a controlled setting with simple cognitive tasks; later reviews treated switching costs as a robust window into cognitive control. The cost does not mean people cannot switch tasks. It means switching consumes time and control, and the previous task leaves a residue that has to be overcome before the next one can be performed cleanly.

Interruption studies bring these laboratory findings closer to everyday digital life. Iqbal and Horvitz studied disruption and recovery in computing tasks, while Mark, Gudith, and Klocke found that interrupted work can lead people to compensate by working faster while experiencing greater stress and time pressure. This is the kind of result the attention economy tends to miss. From a system’s point of view, an interruption may count as a successful re-engagement event. From the worker’s point of view, it may create a recovery problem, increase strain, and fragment a task that required continuity.

Herbert Simon’s 1971 observation sits exactly at the hinge between these two ways of thinking. Simon argued that information consumes attention, so a wealth of information produces a scarcity of attention. This was an economic statement, but it rested on a psychological fact. Information has a recipient, and the recipient has finite capacity. More messages, documents, choices, alerts, and recommendations increase the demands on the selection system that turns available information into usable thought.

The later “attention economy” developed from this insight, especially as the web made attention easier to measure and trade. Michael Goldhaber’s 1997 essay gave the term an explicit role in thinking about online life, and the phrase has since become ordinary. The basic idea is now familiar: when information is abundant, attention becomes scarce, and systems compete for it. In digital advertising, this competition becomes a business model. The more time and action a product can elicit, the more opportunities it has to sell access to the user’s mind.

The problem is that the business model inherits Simon’s scarcity claim while dropping much of the psychology that makes the scarcity matter. If attention is treated as a commodity, the obvious goal is to capture more of it. If attention is treated as a limited faculty, the obvious question is what repeated capture does to the person who must keep supplying it. The same minute can therefore appear in two accounts at once. In the platform account, it is an additional unit of engagement. In the psychological account, it is a minute taken from a finite pool of concentration, recovery, and self-directed thought.

This mismatch is easiest to see with interruptions. A notification has low production cost for the sender and high contextual cost for the receiver when it arrives during a difficult task. The sender does not pay for the time needed to recover the previous train of thought. The advertiser does not pay for the stress created by repeated fragmentation. The platform can count the click, while the user absorbs the switching cost. Economists call this kind of displaced burden an externality: a cost generated by an activity but carried by someone outside the transaction.

Attention capture has several such externalities, beginning with fragmentation. Work that requires a stable mental set becomes harder when attention is repeatedly pulled toward unrelated material. The person may still complete the work, but completion can require more effort, more time pressure, and more recovery. Chronic interruption adds a second cost. A single alert can be useful; a steady environment of alerts changes the background conditions under which thought happens. The person learns to expect disturbance, and expectation itself becomes part of the attentional load.

Depletion is the third cost. The word should be used carefully, because psychology has debated simple resource models of willpower and mental effort. Still, the broader point is well supported: sustained attention and monitoring require effort, and effortful control does not remain cost-free across long periods. A person who has spent the day filtering messages, resisting temptations, resolving task switches, and returning to unfinished work has used cognitive control that could have supported other tasks.

The fourth cost is the crowding out of sustained thought. This does not require a nostalgic story about books, childhood, or a lost age of concentration. It follows from the structure of attention itself. Some forms of thinking need uninterrupted time because the relevant object is larger than a single stimulus. Writing, planning, mathematical reasoning, careful reading, and difficult conversation all require the mind to keep a problem active while parts of it are inspected in sequence. Systems that repeatedly win brief captures of attention make this kind of continuity harder to maintain.

None of this means information should be scarce or that digital systems only harm attention. Search engines, messaging tools, maps, and recommendation systems often reduce cognitive load by finding relevant information quickly. The older psychology does not support a simple rejection of technology. It supports a more exact accounting. A system that directs attention can help when it serves a user’s goal, arrives at the right moment, and reduces irrelevant search. The same system imposes a cost when it redirects attention toward goals selected by someone else.

That distinction is often obscured by the word “engagement.” Engagement sounds neutral, and in some contexts it is a useful measure. A student engaged with a lesson, a patient engaged with treatment, or a citizen engaged with public information may be doing something valuable. In advertising-funded systems, however, engagement often measures successful capture rather than benefit to the person captured. The metric can rise when the user is learning, but it can also rise when the user is anxious, distracted, provoked, or unable to disengage.

The older science of attention gives us a better vocabulary. It lets us ask whether a design supports selection or overwhelms it, whether it protects sustained attention or breaks it into recoverable fragments, whether it reduces the cost of finding what matters or increases the number of things that must be resisted. These are empirical questions as much as moral ones. Psychology has methods for studying them because it has spent more than a century treating attention as a measurable feature of human cognition.

Taking that history seriously changes the accounting of the attention economy. The scarce object is the person’s capacity to select, sustain, switch, and recover. Those operations have limits, and the limits show up as slower performance, stress, fatigue, and lost continuity. A business can sell captured attention without pricing those costs, but the costs still occur in the human system that supplies the commodity. The science that began with James names the missing side of the transaction: attention capture draws down a limited faculty, and the bill is paid in the conditions under which people think.
