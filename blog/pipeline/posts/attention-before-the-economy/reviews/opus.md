# Review — Attention Was a Scientific Concept Before It Was an Economy (opus)

Overall this is a strong, well-argued draft: it leads with the claim, develops a
single thesis start to finish, and closes on substance. The main work is a
handful of clear style-guide violations (two of them the banned "not X, it is Y"
construction), a couple of punchy fragments, one grammar error, and one uncited
factual aside. None of these require a rewrite.

## 1. Style-guide violations

**(a) Banned negation/antithesis construction — "not X but Y." Highest priority.**

- End of the Cherry/Broadbent paragraph:
  > "…because the binding resource is not the eyes but the attention behind them."

  This is the exact "not X but Y" pattern the guide calls "the biggest one."
  Rewrite to state the positive claim: *"…because what limits them is attention
  rather than eyesight — the eyes can point at the hazard while attention is spent
  elsewhere."*

**(b) Banned "not X. It is Y" antithesis — the "settled profile" paragraph:**

  > "These are not soft observations about feeling distracted. They are repeatable
  > laboratory results with error bars."

  Two-sentence negation-then-assertion, explicitly banned. Say the positive point
  only: *"These are repeatable laboratory results with error bars, measured under
  controlled conditions."*

**(c) Punchy fragment / announcement sentence — "Attention became inventory."**

  In the "attention economy" paragraph:
  > "Attention became inventory."

  A three-word dramatic beat, exactly the "punchy setup sentence / fragment for
  effect" the guide bans. Fold it into a full sentence: *"In this framing a
  person's attention becomes inventory that a firm holds and resells to
  advertisers."*

**(d) Flourish sentence used as a beat — the James paragraph:**

  > "Attend to different things and you live in a different world."

  This is a short rhetorical crescendo tacked onto the quotation. It adds tone more
  than information. Either cut it or make it carry content: *"Which objects a
  person attends to therefore shapes what their world consists of."*

**(e) Milder setup/signpost sentences to tighten (not hard bans, but they read as
scaffolding):**

  - "Two further findings sharpen what 'limited' means in practice…" followed by
    "The first concerns switching." … "The second finding concerns endurance."
  - "The externalized costs can be stated concretely." then "The first is
    switching." … "The second is the strain of interruption." … "The third is the
    crowding out of sustained thought."

  These enumerated signposts are functional and the guide doesn't ban plain
  connectives, but there are two stacked enumerations back-to-back. Consider
  letting at least one set of costs flow inside sentences rather than announcing
  "The first is…". Not blocking.

**(f) Piled-up lists for rhythm (minor, recurring).** The triad "limited,
selective, and costly to sustain" is fine once as the thesis descriptor, but the
closing paragraph stacks two four-item lists in one sentence:
  > "…the person's capacity to select, sustain, switch, and recover, and that
  > capacity has limits that show up as slower performance, stress, fatigue, and
  > lost continuity."

  Readable, but on the edge of the "triadic/piled-up lists" caution. Trimming one
  list would sharpen it.

**Cadence overall:** Good. Sentences are measured and information-dense, matching
the manuscripts. It does not read as AI-generated apart from the specific beats
flagged in (a)–(d).

## 2. Factual grounding

- **Quotes check out.** James ("Every one knows what attention is…", "My
  experience is what I agree to attend to") and Simon ("What information consumes
  is rather obvious… a poverty of attention") match the Evidence file and I
  confirmed the Simon wording via web search (widely reproduced from the 1971
  essay).
- **Minor quote-fidelity issue (Simon).** The original is one sentence with a
  colon: *"What information consumes is rather obvious: it consumes the attention
  of its recipients."* The draft splits it into two sentences and capitalizes
  "It": `"What information consumes is rather obvious," he wrote. "It consumes the
  attention of its recipients."` Meaning is preserved, but for a direct quotation
  restore the colon form to avoid altering punctuation inside quote marks.
- **Uncited factual aside — the driving example.**
  > "A driver deep in a phone conversation can look straight at a hazard and fail
  > to register it, because the binding resource is not the eyes but the attention
  > behind them."

  This is factually sound (inattentional blindness / distracted-driving research),
  but it is **not supported by any source in the Evidence file** — it appears only
  in the opus candidate with no citation. Either add a load-bearing source (e.g.
  Strayer & Johnston on phone use and driving, or Simons & Chabris) or soften to
  present it as illustration rather than established result. (It also contains the
  banned "not X but Y" flagged in 1(a).)
- **Dates and attributions are accurate:** Cherry 1953 (experiment), Broadbent
  1958 (filter model), Miller 1956, Mackworth 1948, Rogers & Monsell 1995, Simon
  1971, Goldhaber 1997 / Davenport & Beck 2001, Mark et al. 2008, and the "about
  twenty-three minutes" figure correctly credited to the *earlier* (2005) field
  study. The draft correctly avoids attributing "the attention economy" phrase to
  Simon and avoids the fuel-tank/ego-depletion overclaim. Good compliance with the
  "Do not overclaim" list.

## 3. Structure & clarity

Strong. It opens flatly on the real claim (attention as a measured object before
1971), establishes the science, states the pivot (Simon), then the mismatch, then
the concrete externalized costs, then caveats, then a plain closing. A
non-specialist can follow it throughout; jargon ("shadowing," "vigilance
decrement," "externality") is each defined in plain words on first use. One small
grammar error:

  > "The standard examples are a factory that earns a profit while the surrounding
  > community absorbs the health costs of its polluted air."

  Number mismatch — "examples are a factory." Fix to *"The standard example is a
  factory that earns a profit while the surrounding community absorbs the health
  cost of its polluted air."*

## 4. One self-contained argument

**Thesis in one sentence:** Attention was studied as a limited, selective,
effortful faculty with measurable costs for ~80 years before Simon (1971) recast
it as economic scarcity, and that older science names concrete costs (switching,
interruption, crowded-out sustained thought) that the attention economy treats as
externalities.

The post develops exactly this one argument from start to finish; every paragraph
serves it. The externality framing is introduced and cashed out, not left as an
abstraction. No branching into a second thesis. This criterion passes cleanly.

## 5. Brief compliance & standalone

All "must cover" items are present: attention as a scientific object (James
onward), what the science establishes (limit, switch cost, vigilance, interruption
cost), the Simon pivot, the mismatch, the externalized costs, and the reframing
conclusion. Length is ~1,800 words, inside the 1,600–2,000 target.

Standalone: **passes.** No series framing, no next/previous references, no
"next time" teaser, no subscribe/follow CTA. It also correctly stays off the
material other posts own — it does **not** drift into the "right to mental quiet"
rights argument, the engineered-disfluency mechanism, or a boredom/mind-wandering
essay. The externality it names is economic, not rights-based, which is the right
lane for this post.

## 6. Top 3 priorities

1. **Remove the two banned negation constructions:** "the binding resource is not
   the eyes but the attention behind them" (1a) and "These are not soft
   observations about feeling distracted. They are repeatable laboratory results…"
   (1b). These are the guide's single biggest ban.
2. **Ground or soften the uncited driving example** (2), and fix the "examples
   are a factory" grammar error (3).
3. **Cut/rewrite the two dramatic beats** — "Attention became inventory." and
   "Attend to different things and you live in a different world." (1c, 1d) — into
   full, content-carrying sentences.

## Rating

`ship-with-minor-edits` — the argument, structure, sourcing, and length are all
sound; the fixes are a small set of localized line edits (two banned negations,
two punchy beats, one grammar slip, one citation) that don't touch the substance.
