# Review: gpt

## 1. Style-guide violations

- Opening is accurate in intent but too compressed and slightly theatrical: "Only in 1971 did the concept cross into economics, where it was recast as a scarce resource that firms could capture and sell. The older science did not disappear when this happened." Rewrite: "Herbert Simon later described attention as the scarce counterpart to abundant information. That economic framing should be read against the older psychological account, which treated attention as selective, limited, and effortful."

- The James paragraph adds essayistic asides instead of explaining the mechanism: "'Every one knows what attention is,' he wrote, before making clear that most people did not." James is not really saying most people do not know what attention is. Rewrite: "James used the familiar experience of attention to define a selective process."

- Avoid foregrounding the post's machinery: "The line that matters most for what follows is the one about cost". Rewrite: "For James, selection required withdrawal: attention 'implies withdrawal from some things in order to deal effectively with others.'"

- This line is too polished and unsupported: "Attend to different things and you live in a different world." Rewrite: "Different patterns of attention change which parts of the environment enter experience and guide action."

- The transition "James described attention; the generation after him measured it" has the antithetical rhythm the style guide warns against. Rewrite: "After James, experimental psychologists began measuring selective attention and capacity limits."

- "The limit generalizes well beyond the laboratory" is a punchy setup sentence, and "not the eyes but the attention behind them" is a banned negation/antithesis construction. Rewrite: "Cell-phone driving studies make the same point in a practical setting: drivers can look toward a traffic signal and fail to encode it when a conversation consumes attention."

- The task-switching paragraph uses announcement fragments: "The first concerns switching" / "The second finding concerns endurance." Rewrite by integrating the topic into the claim: "Task-switching studies attach numbers to one cost of divided attention: people respond more slowly and make more errors after a predictable switch than after a repeated task."

- "Sustained attention is not something a person can simply hold at a constant level" violates the hard ban on negation-definition phrasing. Rewrite: "Sustained attention declines with time on task, especially in monitoring tasks with rare targets."

- "These are not soft observations about feeling distracted. They are repeatable laboratory results with error bars." is defensive and punchy. Rewrite: "These results turn distraction from a vague feeling into measurable changes in detection, response time, and accuracy."

- "Attention became inventory" is a one-sentence dramatic beat. Rewrite: "In the business framing, attention is treated as inventory: something to aggregate, price, and sell to advertisers."

- The ledger metaphor is useful but overextended: "The same minute appears in two ledgers at once" / "drawn from a finite pool" / "the bill is charged to the person's account". Keep one concrete accounting sentence and drop the flourish. Rewrite: "The platform records the minute as engagement, while the user bears any recovery time, stress, or lost continuity that follows."

- "The externalized costs can be stated concretely. The first is switching" repeats the punchy setup pattern. Rewrite: "Switching is the clearest externalized cost, because a notification that pulls someone away from work can impose recovery costs on return."

- "None of this requires believing... Nor does it turn..." is a rhetorical caveat built from negation. Rewrite: "The evidence supports a narrower claim: attention is selective, switching is costly, and sustained monitoring declines over time. Some tools reduce search costs and support the user's own goals."

- "What the accounting mostly hides sits inside a single word, 'engagement'" reads like an essay flourish. Rewrite: "The word 'engagement' hides this distinction because it can describe either useful involvement or successful capture."

## 2. Factual grounding

- The draft's strongest facts are supported by the candidate evidence: James's definition and "My experience is what I agree to attend to" are verified in the York University public text of *Principles of Psychology*; Goldhaber's 1997 *First Monday* article is a real early web-era use of "attention economy"; Rogers and Monsell's residual switch-cost claim and Mackworth's vigilance-decrement claim match standard summaries; Mark, Gudith, and Klocke's 2008 paper says interrupted work was completed faster with more stress, frustration, time pressure, and effort.

- "Attention became a measured scientific object in the late nineteenth century" overstates James. The candidates explicitly warn not to say James experimentally measured attention's limits. Rewrite: "Attention entered modern psychology as a scientific problem in the late nineteenth century, and later experiments measured its limits."

- "Only in 1971 did the concept cross into economics" is too categorical. Simon is the right hinge for this post, and a quick web check verifies the standard "wealth of information creates a poverty of attention" quotation as Simon 1971, but "only" invites objections from older advertising and information-economics uses. Rewrite: "Simon gave the post's key economic formulation in 1971."

- "A driver deep in a phone conversation can look straight at a hazard and fail to register it" is not in the candidates' evidence. It is quickly verifiable via Strayer, Drews, and Johnston (2003), *Cell Phone-Induced Failures of Visual Attention During Simulated Driving*, which found drivers on cell phones could look at signals and fail to remember them. Add that source to Further reading or cut the example.

- "how quickly it gave out under load" risks the depleted-fuel model the evidence warns against. Rewrite: "how selection, switching, and sustained monitoring fail under specific task demands."

- "Every notification that succeeds in pulling a person away from a task imposes a switch cost of exactly the kind Rogers and Monsell measured" overgeneralizes from predictable laboratory task switches to real notifications. Rewrite: "A notification that pulls someone away from demanding work can impose a switch and recovery cost of the same broad type studied in task-switching and interruption research."

- "A design that maximizes sessions is, mechanically, a design that maximizes switches" is too strong. More sessions often mean more transitions, but session count is not identical to task switching. Rewrite: "Designs that optimize for frequent returns can increase the number of transitions users must manage."

- "the faculty James called focalization is precisely what a maximally engaging feed pulls apart" is plausible but not directly evidenced. Better: "Frequent unrelated prompts make it harder to maintain the continuous train of thought that difficult reading, writing, and planning require."

## 3. Structure & clarity

The structure is strong: psychology first, Simon as hinge, later attention economy, then externalized costs. A non-specialist can follow the main arc. The main clarity problem is density: the first half stacks James, Cherry, Broadbent, Miller, Rogers/Monsell, Mackworth, Simon, Goldhaber, Davenport/Beck, and Mark before the reader gets much relief. Consider cutting Miller or the driving example unless one is needed for the single argument. The post also waits until lines 253-266 to define externality; that definition should arrive earlier, when the thesis first says the business model leaves costs outside the price.

## 4. One self-contained argument

Single thesis: Psychology had already shown attention to be selective, limited, effortful, and costly before the attention economy turned captured attention into a commercial resource, so the business model externalizes costs that the older science can name.

The draft mostly develops this one argument. The Goldhaber/Davenport paragraph serves the thesis, but the sentence "This is a coherent commercial logic, and it has produced some of the most valuable companies in history" does not add much and introduces a side claim about market value. The driving example also branches into an uncited adjacent literature; keep it only if sourced briefly. Otherwise, every paragraph serves the main argument.

## 5. Brief compliance & standalone

The draft covers all must-cover items: James and the psychology tradition; finite capacity, switching costs, vigilance decrement, interruption costs; Simon's 1971 scarcity argument; the later business framing; externalized costs; and the conclusion that engagement is not neutral capture. It avoids the rights-based mental-quiet argument, boredom/mind-wandering, and engineered-disfluency material. It stands alone: no series framing, previous/next references, subscribe CTA, or dependence on another post.

One compliance issue: the file still contains a long `# Evidence` section before `# Draft`. If this integrated file is ever used as the publication source, that section must be stripped. If it is only pipeline context for reviewers, no issue.

## 6. Top 3 priorities

1. Remove the style-guide violations built around negation, punchy setup sentences, and accounting metaphors, especially in the opening, task-switching section, caveat paragraph, and conclusion.
2. Soften overclaims that turn laboratory findings into exact claims about every notification, every session, or attention "giving out" under load.
3. Cut or source the driving example, and reduce one or two secondary historical references so the post feels less like a survey and more like one continuous argument.

Rating: `ship-with-minor-edits` — the argument, structure, and evidence base are solid, but the draft needs a careful style pass and a few factual softeners before publication.
