# Review: Attention Was a Scientific Concept Before It Was an Economy

## 1. Style-guide violations

The draft is strong and substantive, but it relies heavily on "antithesis" patterns, negation definitions, and punchy setups to create rhythm.

**"Not X, it is Y" / Negation definitions**
- *Quote:* "These are not soft observations about feeling distracted. They are repeatable laboratory results with error bars."
  *Rewrite:* "These findings are grounded in repeatable laboratory results with measured error bars."
- *Quote:* "Sustained attention is not something a person can simply hold at a constant level; it degrades with time on task."
  *Rewrite:* "Sustained attention requires continuous cognitive effort and degrades reliably with time on task."

**Parallel "trade" constructions / Antithesis for rhythm**
- *Quote:* "The same minute appears in two ledgers at once. In the platform's ledger it is an additional unit of engagement. In the psychological ledger it is a minute drawn from a finite pool of concentration, recovery, and self-directed thought."
  *Rewrite:* "A minute spent on a platform is counted by the business as an additional unit of engagement, while for the user, it is a minute drawn from a finite capacity for concentration and self-directed thought."
- *Quote:* "James described attention; the generation after him measured it, and every major result pointed toward limits."
  *Rewrite:* "Following James's descriptive work, the next generation of psychologists measured attention and found strict limits."

**Punchy setup sentences and throat-clearing**
- *Quote:* "The externalized costs can be stated concretely. The first is switching."
  *Rewrite:* Cut the setup sentence entirely. "The first externalized cost is the cognitive penalty of switching."
- *Quote:* "The mismatch appears when the two descriptions of attention are laid side by side."
  *Rewrite:* Cut this sentence and start the paragraph directly with: "While the science treats attention as a limited faculty... the business model treats it as a resource to be maximized."

**Rhetorical / Poetic flourishes**
- *Quote:* "Attend to different things and you live in a different world."
  *Rewrite:* Cut this line. It acts as a poetic coda to the paragraph rather than delivering concrete information.

**Understatement / Negation openers**
- *Quote:* "None of this requires believing that attention is a fuel tank that empties on a fixed schedule, a stronger claim the evidence does not support."
  *Rewrite:* "The evidence supports these specific limits without requiring the stronger, unsupported claim that attention is a fuel tank that empties on a fixed schedule."

## 2. Factual grounding

- **Overclaim on Simon (1971):** The opening paragraph states: *"Only in 1971 did the concept cross into economics, where it was recast as a scarce resource that firms could capture and sell."* This compresses the history inaccurately. Simon (1971) observed that information consumes attention and creates a scarcity, but he did *not* frame it as a commodity for firms to "capture and sell." That business model framing came decades later (with Goldhaber in 1997 and Davenport in 2001), which the draft itself acknowledges later. The intro needs to correctly characterize Simon's contribution without anachronistically applying the 1990s business model to it.
- **Misrepresenting James:** The draft says, *" 'Every one knows what attention is,' he wrote, before making clear that most people did not."* A quick check of *The Principles of Psychology* reveals James literally meant it was a common-sense concept ("It is the taking possession by the mind..."). He did not imply that people misunderstood it. Cut "before making clear that most people did not."
- All other facts (Cherry, Broadbent, Mackworth, Rogers & Monsell, Mark) are accurately grounded in the provided evidence.

## 3. Structure & clarity

The chronological structure works beautifully. Tracing the scientific history (James to Cherry to Broadbent) directly into the economic crossover (Simon) and finally into the "mismatch" of the modern business model is an effective, logical progression. It is highly readable for a non-specialist. 

However, the opening sentence (*"Attention became a measured scientific object in the late nineteenth century..."*) violates the style guide's preference to "open on the real problem, stated flatly... not an abstract generality if a concrete opening is available." The post would hit harder if it opened immediately on the friction between the modern business model's view of attention and the older scientific reality of its limits.

## 4. One self-contained argument

*Thesis:* The scientific understanding of attention as a limited, effortful faculty reveals concrete human costs that the modern attention economy treats as unpriced externalities.

The post is extremely disciplined. It develops this single thesis from start to finish. There are no distracting digressions into the right to mental quiet, boredom, or moral panics. Every paragraph serves to establish either the scientific limits of attention or how the business model externalizes those limits.

## 5. Brief compliance & standalone

- **Must-cover items:** All points in the brief are fully addressed, including the scientific history, Simon's pivot, the mismatch, and the specific externalized costs (switching, fatigue, crowding out thought).
- **Standalone:** Perfect. There are no "subscribe" calls to action, no references to previous posts, and no series framing.
- **Do not re-tell:** The draft successfully avoids the "right to mental quiet" framing and the engineered disfluency mechanisms.

## 6. Top 3 priorities

1. **Scrub the "antithesis" and parallel structures:** Rewrite the "not X, it is Y" sentences and the "two ledgers" parallelism to conform to the style guide's demand for plain, declarative prose.
2. **Fix the historical compression in the intro:** Correct the first paragraph so it doesn't falsely attribute the "capture and sell" business model to Simon's 1971 scarcity observation.
3. **Remove the editorializing of James and poetic codas:** Delete the claim that James thought "most people did not" understand attention, and cut the poetic flourish "Attend to different things and you live in a different world."

**Rating:** `ship-with-minor-edits`  
*Justification:* The draft has an excellent, focused structure that successfully executes the brief's thesis and integrates the evidence well, requiring only targeted line-edits to fix a factual compression in the intro and scrub a few lingering rhetorical "antithesis" structures.
