# Evidence

**Thesis:** Psychiatry's most influential research programs have stopped treating DSM diagnostic categories as natural kinds and instead treat them as convenient administrative fictions, which means the manual that governs clinical practice no longer matches how the science describes mental illness.

1. **Insel's 2013 NIMH announcement:** Thomas Insel, then NIMH director, announced a shift away from DSM categories toward the Research Domain Criteria (RDoC) because DSM categories lack biological validity.
   - *Source:* Insel, T. (2013). Transforming Diagnosis. *National Institute of Mental Health Blog*.
   - *Link:* https://www.nimh.nih.gov/about/director/2013/transforming-diagnosis
2. **Heterogeneity in MDD:** The DSM-5 criteria allow for 227 unique symptom combinations that qualify for a diagnosis of major depressive disorder.
   - *Source:* Fried, E. I., & Nesse, R. M. (2015). Depression is not a consistent syndrome: An investigation of unique symptom patterns in the STAR*D study. *Journal of Affective Disorders*, 172, 96-102.
   - *Link:* https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jad.2014.10.010
3. **Heterogeneity in PTSD:** Under DSM-5 rules, there are 636,120 possible symptom combinations that meet the diagnostic criteria for posttraumatic stress disorder.
   - *Source:* Galatzer-Levy, I. R., & Bryant, R. A. (2013). 636,120 Ways to Have Posttraumatic Stress Disorder. *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, 8(6), 651-662.
   - *Link:* https://doi.org/10.1177/1745691613504116
4. **HiTOP Framework:** The Hierarchical Taxonomy of Psychopathology organizes symptoms into continuous dimensions and hierarchical spectra based on empirical co-occurrence rather than discrete categories.
   - *Source:* Kotov, R., Krueger, R. F., Watson, D., et al. (2017). The Hierarchical Taxonomy of Psychopathology (HiTOP): A dimensional alternative to traditional nosologies. *Journal of Abnormal Psychology*, 126(4), 454–477.
   - *Link:* https://doi.org/10.1037/abn0000258
5. **Shared genetic risk across disorders:** Large genome-wide association studies show significant genetic overlap across major psychiatric disorders, contradicting the idea of discrete categories.
   - *Source:* Cross-Disorder Group of the Psychiatric Genomics Consortium (2013). Identification of risk loci with shared effects on five major psychiatric disorders: a genome-wide analysis. *The Lancet*, 381(9875), 1371-1379.
   - *Link:* https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(12)62129-1

**Quotes:**
- "Patients with mental disorders deserve better. NIMH has launched the Research Domain Criteria (RDoC) project to transform diagnosis by incorporating genetics, imaging, cognitive science, and other levels of information to lay the foundation for a new classification system." (Insel, 2013)
- "The weakness is its lack of validity. Unlike our definitions of ischemic heart disease, lymphoma, or AIDS, the DSM diagnoses are based on a consensus about clusters of clinical symptoms, not any objective laboratory measure." (Insel, 2013)

**Do not overclaim:**
- Do not claim that RDoC has successfully produced biological blood tests or definitive brain scans for mental illness. It is a research framework, and progress in identifying definitive biomarkers remains slow.
- Do not state that the DSM is useless or being actively replaced in hospitals today. It remains highly reliable for clinical communication and absolutely required for the administration of healthcare, billing, and legal thresholds.
- Avoid suggesting that dimensional models have solved the problem of psychiatric diagnosis; they have simply shifted the problem from drawing boundaries between diseases to drawing boundaries on continuous spectra for clinical action.

**Flags:**
- The Fried (2015) paper finds 227 combinations, while some other papers calculate larger numbers depending on whether sub-threshold symptoms are included. The 227 figure is specifically tied to strict, core DSM-5 criteria.
- The shift toward RDoC caused significant friction between NIMH and the American Psychiatric Association (APA) in 2013, though both sides later issued joint statements smoothing over the conflict. The fundamental scientific divergence remains accurate.

# Draft

The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders governs clinical psychiatry, dictating treatment guidelines, drug approvals, and insurance reimbursement. A clinician must apply a label from the manual for a patient to access care. Yet the field's leading research programs have quietly abandoned the manual as a scientific map of mental illness. In 2013, Thomas Insel, then the director of the U.S. National Institute of Mental Health (NIMH), announced that the agency would orient its research funding away from DSM categories. Insel stated that the manual lacked biological validity. Its diagnostic categories rely entirely on clinical consensus about symptom clusters, lacking the objective laboratory measures that define diseases in the rest of medicine. To find those mechanisms, researchers needed to stop organizing their work around the manual's labels. Insel's statement formalized a growing consensus in the scientific community: psychiatry's administrative categories no longer match how the science describes human psychological suffering.

The dominance of the categorical approach originated in 1980 with the publication of the DSM-III. Driven by the psychiatrist Robert Spitzer, the manual shifted the field away from psychoanalytic theories of illness and toward descriptive checklists. The goal was to solve a massive reliability problem: before 1980, two psychiatrists evaluating the same patient frequently arrived at entirely different diagnoses. By providing explicit criteria and symptom thresholds, the DSM-III ensured that clinicians across the world could reliably agree on the label for a given presentation. This reliability allowed psychiatry to integrate seamlessly with the rest of evidence-based medicine, facilitating clinical trials, epidemiological surveys, and standardized billing. The system was highly reliable, meaning clinicians applied the rules consistently. The assumption, however, was that this reliability would eventually lead to validity—that the consistent clinical descriptions would eventually map onto distinct biological diseases.

The DSM operates as a categorical system, treating mental disorders as discrete entities that a patient either has or does not have. This approach structures the clinic efficiently. A patient meets the criteria for major depressive disorder, receives a corresponding diagnostic code, and qualifies for a specific treatment protocol. Historically, this categorical structure rested on the assumption that psychiatric diagnoses represent natural kinds. In this view, conditions like schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, and panic disorder exist as distinct biological diseases with specific causes, clear boundaries, and predictable courses, much like tuberculosis or diabetes. This framework traces back to the late nineteenth century, when Emil Kraepelin separated what he called dementia praecox from manic depression based on their distinct clinical trajectories. The ultimate goal of modern psychiatric research, working under this natural-kinds assumption, was to uncover the specific genetic and neurobiological mechanisms responsible for each discrete disorder on the list. If major depressive disorder represents a distinct disease, it should possess a distinct biological signature.

Decades of genetic and neuroscience research failed to find those specific signatures. The accumulating data revealed that mental illness rarely respects the borders drawn by the DSM. The first major strain on the categorical model is comorbidity. Patients frequently meet criteria for multiple diagnoses simultaneously, suggesting that the underlying pathology cuts across the manual's divisions. Large-scale genome-wide association studies confirm this overlap at the biological level. The Cross-Disorder Group of the Psychiatric Genomics Consortium demonstrated significant shared genetic risk across five major psychiatric disorders, including schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, and major depressive disorder. A genetic vulnerability to one condition heavily overlaps with the vulnerability to the others, undermining the idea that they represent fundamentally separate diseases.

The second major strain is the immense heterogeneity within the categories themselves. Because the DSM relies on polythetic criteria—requiring a certain number of symptoms from a larger list—patients with the same diagnosis present with entirely different clinical profiles. Eiko Fried and Randolph Nesse documented that the DSM-5 criteria allow for 227 unique symptom combinations that qualify for a diagnosis of major depressive disorder. Two patients can both receive the diagnosis while sharing barely a single symptom. For posttraumatic stress disorder, Isaac Galatzer-Levy and Richard Bryant calculated that there are 636,120 possible ways to meet the diagnostic criteria. This mathematical heterogeneity means the clinical label describes a vast array of different psychological and biological states. Expecting a single underlying mechanism to account for hundreds of thousands of different clinical presentations became scientifically untenable.

To address this lack of validity, the research community developed new frameworks that organize mental illness around continuous dimensions rather than discrete categories. The NIMH launched the Research Domain Criteria (RDoC) project to rebuild the study of mental illness from the ground up, based on observable behavior and neurobiology. RDoC explicitly requires researchers to ignore DSM categories when designing studies. Instead, the framework organizes research around continuous dimensions of human functioning, divided into fundamental systems: Negative Valence Systems, Positive Valence Systems, Cognitive Systems, Systems for Social Processes, and Arousal/Regulatory Systems. Each system is studied across a matrix of different measurement units, from genes and molecules to neural circuits, physiology, and self-reported behavior. Under this framework, a study investigating the Positive Valence System might focus on reward valuation—how the brain calculates the effort required to obtain a reward. The researchers measure this specific cognitive process directly, recruiting participants based on their measurable deficits in reward processing rather than a clinical diagnosis. The sample might include individuals holding traditional diagnoses of depression, schizophrenia, or substance use disorder, alongside individuals with no diagnosis at all. The underlying biological system becomes the target of research, treating the administrative boundaries of the DSM as irrelevant to the underlying mechanism.

In parallel, a consortium of clinical psychologists and psychiatrists developed the Hierarchical Taxonomy of Psychopathology (HiTOP) to reorganize the clinical symptoms themselves using continuous spectra. Developed by evaluating empirical statistical data about how symptoms co-occur in the general population, the framework treats psychopathology as a set of continuous dimensions rather than discrete disorders. The model builds a hierarchy from the ground up. At the lowest level are specific symptom components, which group into traditional syndromes, which then cluster into subfactors. These subfactors combine into broad spectra, such as the internalizing spectrum, encompassing distress, fear, and eating pathology, and the externalizing spectrum, covering disinhibited and antagonistic behaviors. At the very top sits a general factor of psychopathology, representing a generalized vulnerability to psychiatric distress. Because HiTOP uses continuous dimensions at every level, it eliminates the problem of arbitrary diagnostic thresholds. It naturally accounts for the high rates of symptom overlap, as patients can elevate on multiple spectra simultaneously. A patient is assessed and receives a dimensional profile, providing a much higher-resolution picture of their specific struggles than a binary diagnosis allows.

These dimensional frameworks treat the DSM categories as convenient administrative fictions. The labels remain necessary for the mechanics of modern healthcare, providing the discrete codes required for billing, legal thresholds, and epidemiological tracking. Researchers and clinicians still use the terms to communicate efficiently about broad clinical patterns. However, the scientific community treats them as heuristics, having abandoned the expectation that these labels carve nature at its joints. The manual organizes the healthcare system, but it no longer organizes the search for the underlying causes of mental illness. The concepts of major depressive disorder and generalized anxiety disorder function much like the concept of height—useful for everyday communication, but inadequate for the precise measurement required by genetic and biological research.

This divergence between science and administration places clinical practice in a difficult position. Dimensional models like HiTOP and RDoC map more accurately onto the biological and psychological reality of human suffering, but they are difficult to implement at the bedside. A clinician evaluating a patient in crisis needs to make binary decisions. They must decide whether to prescribe an antipsychotic medication, whether to admit the patient to an inpatient unit, and whether the patient requires a specific therapeutic intervention. Insurance companies require discrete codes to authorize payment for these treatments. The categorical approach forces a continuous reality into the binary boxes required for these clinical actions. A dimensional profile indicating a moderately high score on the internalizing spectrum offers more scientific accuracy, but it does not tell a physician whether the patient meets the threshold for an antidepressant prescription.

This forced translation from continuous distress to discrete diagnosis also fuels ongoing debates about overdiagnosis. When clinical guidelines impose a sharp threshold on a continuous spectrum of distress, the line dividing health from illness becomes a matter of professional consensus rather than biological fact. Moving the threshold slightly lower can instantly reclassify millions of people as ill, pathologizing normal human variation. The DSM requires clinicians to enforce these boundaries every day, evaluating patients against criteria that lack a firm foundation in neurobiology. Consequently, the manual continues to govern the clinic because the medical system requires actionable labels, even as the scientific frontier has moved permanently beyond the categorical approach.
