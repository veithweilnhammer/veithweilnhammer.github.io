# Evidence

Thesis: Psychiatry's leading research frameworks increasingly treat DSM diagnoses as practical labels for communication, records, billing, and clinical decision-making, while the science of mental illness is moving toward dimensions and mechanisms that cut across those labels.

1. The DSM-5-TR is still the standard U.S. manual for defining and classifying mental disorders, and its diagnostic codes are used for data collection and billing. Source: American Psychiatric Association, 2022, "About DSM-5-TR." Link: https://www.psychiatry.org/psychiatrists/practice/dsm/about-dsm
2. DSM-style diagnoses gained authority partly by improving reliability through operational criteria, while validity remained much weaker. Source: Steven E. Exact quotes verified against source:

- Thomas Insel, NIMH Director's Blog, 2013: "While DSM has been described as a “Bible” for the field, it is, at best, a dictionary, creating a set of labels and defining each."
- Thomas Insel, NIMH Director's Blog, 2013: "The weakness is its lack of validity."
- Thomas Insel, NIMH Director's Blog, 2013: "NIMH will be re-orienting its research away from DSM categories."
- Thomas Insel, NIMH Director's Blog, 2013: "RDoC, for now, is a research framework, not a clinical tool."
- NIMH current RDoC page meta description: "RDoC is a research framework for new approaches to investigating mental disorders, integrating many levels of information (from genomics and circuits to behavior and self-reports) to explore basic dimensions of functioning that span the full range of human behavior from normal to abnormal."

Do not overclaim:

- Do not say the DSM has been abandoned in clinical practice; it remains central to diagnosis, records, insurance, services, and trials.
- Do not say DSM categories are useless; the validity/utility distinction matters, and labels can still help clinicians communicate and make decisions.
- Do not say RDoC or HiTOP has solved psychiatric diagnosis; both remain incomplete and harder to use in ordinary clinical encounters than a categorical manual.
- Do not say all DSM disorders are equally invalid; some categories may prove more biologically or clinically coherent than others.
- Do not reduce the issue to brain biomarkers; RDoC and HiTOP include behavior, symptoms, self-report, circuits, and functional dimensions.
- Do not make the separate argument that the threshold for illness is socially decided; this post is about whether the categories behave like natural kinds.

Flags:

- The original NIMH URL for Insel's 2013 post now returns 404; the exact quotations above were verified against a 2017 Internet Archive capture of the NIMH page.
- Public summaries of HiTOP sometimes overstate its readiness for bedside use; the HiTOP Consortium itself describes the system as a work in progress.
- The DSM-5-TR text itself is copyrighted, so DSM claims here rely on public APA pages and secondary scholarly discussions rather than long direct quotations from the manual.
- Evidence for overdiagnosis varies by disorder, setting, age group, and health system; the draft treats it as a risk linked to categorical thresholds, not as a uniform property of all DSM diagnosis.

# Draft

A patient can leave a psychiatric clinic with a diagnosis that matters immediately for treatment, sick leave, insurance, eligibility for services, and the way future clinicians read the chart. The same diagnosis may matter much less to a researcher studying why the symptoms arose. This gap has become one of the central facts about modern psychiatry: the manual that organizes clinical practice no longer describes the units that many leading research programs use to study mental illness.

The manual is the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, currently DSM-5-TR. In the United States, it is the standard classification used by mental health professionals, and its diagnoses are linked to ICD codes that hospitals, clinics, insurers, and researchers use for records, billing, and data collection. The manual gives each disorder a name, a code, and a set of criteria. A person either meets the criteria for panic disorder, major depressive disorder, obsessive-compulsive disorder, or another diagnosis, or they do not. Clinicians can add specifiers and severity ratings, but the basic structure remains categorical.

This categorical structure solved a real problem. Before the modern DSM era, clinicians could use the same words in very different ways. Operational criteria made diagnosis more reliable by giving clinicians a shared checklist. If two psychiatrists examined the same patient, the manual gave them a better chance of reaching the same label. That achievement should not be dismissed, because reliable language is essential in medicine. A label that nobody uses consistently cannot support clinical records, research studies, or reimbursement systems.

Reliability, however, is weaker than validity. A group of clinicians can agree on a label even when the thing being labelled fails to form a single disease entity. In the rest of medicine, some diagnoses have become valid in a stronger sense because they track causes, mechanisms, pathology, prognosis, or treatment response. Ischemic heart disease is more than a checklist of chest pain features, because the label connects to narrowed coronary arteries, measurable risk factors, and interventions that target the mechanism. Most psychiatric diagnoses still lack that kind of map.

The old hope was that diagnostic labels would eventually line up with natural kinds of mental illness. A natural kind is a category with boundaries that come from the world, rather than from a committee decision or a practical convention. On that view, schizophrenia, major depression, panic disorder, and attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder would be like separate disease species. Each would have its own underlying mechanism, its own biological signature, and its own reasonably clear boundary from neighboring disorders and from ordinary variation.

Comorbidity is one reason that assumption has become harder to defend. In the National Comorbidity Survey Replication, Kessler and colleagues found that among people who met criteria for a DSM-IV disorder in the past year, 55% had one diagnosis, 22% had two, and 23% had three or more. Overlap is common enough to look like a property of the diagnostic system rather than a collection of unusual cases. Cleanly separated disease entities would produce fewer patients who keep landing in several boxes at once.

Fuzzy boundaries create a second problem because symptoms vary continuously across people and across time. Anxiety, low mood, compulsive checking, suspiciousness, inattention, and impulsivity usually vary by degree. They rise and fall in intensity, combine with other symptoms, and cross diagnostic thresholds when duration, impairment, exclusion rules, and clinical judgment line up. Thresholds can still be useful, but they often mark practical clinical decisions more than natural edges in the phenomenon itself.

Heterogeneity within a single diagnosis adds another strain on the natural-kinds view. Major depression is the clearest example because its criteria allow many routes into the same category. In the STAR*D depression study, Fried and Nesse examined 3,703 depressed outpatients and found 1,030 unique symptom profiles. The most common profile described only 1.8% of patients. A diagnosis that broad can still identify people who need help, but it groups together patients whose sleep, appetite, concentration, and suicidality may differ substantially. A researcher looking for a single biomarker for that entire group starts with a very mixed sample.

The fourth problem is the missing biological map. Psychiatry has produced important biological findings, including genetic risk factors, circuit-level findings, pharmacological mechanisms, and measurable changes in cognition and behavior. These findings rarely respect the borders of DSM categories. The same genetic risks can increase liability to several disorders. Reward learning problems can appear in depression, addiction, and schizophrenia. Threat responses can matter in phobias, post-traumatic stress, and obsessive-compulsive symptoms. The biology often seems to follow dimensions of function, while the manual follows named syndromes.

Thomas Insel's 2013 announcement at the U.S. National Institute of Mental Health turned this scientific dissatisfaction into a funding signal. Shortly before DSM-5 appeared, the director of the largest mental health research funder in the world wrote that NIMH would reorient its research away from DSM categories. The statement left ordinary diagnosis in place, while changing what NIMH wanted research to treat as the main units for discovering mechanisms. A clinical trial could study all patients in a mood clinic rather than only patients who met strict criteria for major depressive disorder. A biomarker study could begin with anhedonia, cognitive control, threat response, or another measurable process that cuts across several diagnoses.

That reorientation took the form of the Research Domain Criteria, or RDoC. RDoC organizes research around domains such as negative valence, positive valence, cognition, social processes, arousal and regulation, and sensorimotor function. These terms sound technical, but the basic idea is simple. Instead of starting with a diagnosis and asking what biology belongs to it, RDoC starts with a function, such as responding to threat or processing reward, and asks how that function varies across genes, circuits, physiology, behavior, and self-report. A person with panic disorder, depression, and alcohol dependence might all enter the same study if they share a relevant disturbance in threat or reward processing.

RDoC therefore changes the unit of explanation. The DSM asks whether a person meets criteria for a named disorder. RDoC asks how measurable systems are working, across the full range from typical function to severe impairment. Its official purpose is research, and it offers little bedside guidance, which matters because a clinician seeing a distressed patient at 4 p.m. cannot replace a diagnosis with a six-domain research matrix. The clinician needs to decide what to do, what to write, what risk to manage, which treatment to offer, and which service pathway the patient can access. RDoC is poorly suited to that immediate administrative and clinical task.

HiTOP, the Hierarchical Taxonomy of Psychopathology, pushes the same dissatisfaction in a different direction. It begins less from neuroscience and more from the statistical structure of symptoms and diagnoses. Its central claim is that psychopathology is better described as dimensions arranged in a hierarchy. Specific signs and symptoms cluster into narrower components; related components form syndromes; syndromes group into broader spectra such as internalizing and externalizing. Comorbidity becomes less surprising in this framework, because related disorders sit near one another within the same higher-level spectrum.

HiTOP also treats symptom severity as continuous. Symptoms become scientifically visible before a person crosses a categorical boundary. The model can represent mild, moderate, and severe versions of similar problems, and it can show where one person's symptoms lie relative to broader patterns. This is useful because many psychiatric presentations are mixtures. A patient may have social anxiety, low mood, insomnia, irritability, alcohol misuse, and obsessive checking without fitting neatly into one textbook category. HiTOP can describe that pattern as a profile across dimensions rather than as a pile of separate disorders.

Together, RDoC and HiTOP show that the field has already changed its working assumptions. One framework looks for mechanisms across domains of behavior and biology. The other maps symptoms into continuous, hierarchical spectra. They differ in emphasis, and the DSM still governs the clinic. Yet both frameworks treat DSM categories as imperfect starting points rather than natural units of mental illness. The quiet abandonment is a steady withdrawal of scientific faith from the manual's categories while its administrative role remains intact.

The phrase convenient fiction can sound harsher than intended. A convenient fiction is a label that remains useful even though nobody should mistake it for a natural kind. Money, legal adulthood, and national borders all depend on conventions, but they organize real behavior and have real consequences. Psychiatric diagnoses can work similarly. A diagnosis can help a clinician summarize a problem, communicate with colleagues, choose an evidence-based treatment, and unlock reimbursement. The same diagnosis can still fail to identify one underlying disease process shared by everyone who receives the label.

This distinction explains why the DSM persists. Clinical systems need stable names more than they need philosophically perfect categories. A service cannot run on a long vector of threat sensitivity, reward responsiveness, sleep timing, and compulsivity without clear rules for eligibility and payment. Patients also often need names for their problems. A diagnosis can validate suffering, guide treatment, and connect someone with others who have similar experiences. Removing labels without replacing their practical functions would make care worse.

The cost of keeping the labels is that people start to believe the labels are more precise than they are. A DSM diagnosis can look like an explanation when it is often a description. Saying that someone cannot work because they have major depressive disorder may be administratively necessary, but the label leaves open which mechanism produced their fatigue, insomnia, hopelessness, slowed movement, or suicidal thinking. The label gathers the symptoms under one heading. The harder scientific work is to explain why that particular person's symptoms took that form.

The same distinction matters for overdiagnosis. Categorical systems require thresholds, and thresholds create edge cases. If a diagnosis is treated as a natural kind, crossing the threshold can feel like discovering a hidden disease that was already there in full. If the category is treated as a practical construct, the threshold becomes a decision that should be used with caution, especially for mild, mixed, or transient symptoms. The main risk is that a label can freeze a complex and changing problem into a single administrative identity, including cases where ordinary distress has been medicalized too quickly.

Dimensional systems bring their own problems. They are harder to teach, harder to bill, and harder to communicate quickly. They can also create more measurement, more scores, and more opportunities to classify people as abnormal along some axis. A future system based on dimensions could still overreach if every variation in mood, attention, or fear became a target for monitoring and intervention. Better science does not automatically produce better restraint.

The honest position is therefore uncomfortable but workable. Psychiatry still needs the DSM because clinics, insurers, researchers, and patients need shared terms. Psychiatry also needs to stop pretending that those terms usually carve mental illness into natural kinds. The research world has already moved toward mechanisms, dimensions, and spectra because the evidence pushed it there: high comorbidity, fuzzy boundaries, heterogeneous categories, and weak biological specificity all point away from a simple disease-list model. The manual remains useful as an interface between suffering and institutions, but the science of mental illness is increasingly being built somewhere else.
