# Evidence

**One-sentence thesis:** Psychiatry's most influential research programs have stopped treating DSM diagnostic categories as natural kinds and now use them as administrative conveniences, so the manual that governs the clinic no longer matches how the field's own science describes mental illness.

## Key claims and sources

1. In April 2013 Thomas Insel, then director of the U.S. National Institute of Mental Health, announced that NIMH would reorient its research funding away from DSM categories toward its own Research Domain Criteria (RDoC) framework.
   - Insel, T. (2013). "Transforming Diagnosis." NIMH Director's Blog, 29 April 2013. Original URL now redirected; archived copy: https://psychrights.org/2013/130429NIMHTransformingDiagnosis.htm (verified full text) and https://web.archive.org/web/20140104203126/http://www.nimh.nih.gov/about/director/2013/transforming-diagnosis.shtml

2. Insel's stated reason: DSM has strong reliability but weak validity — its categories are consensus clusters of symptoms with no objective laboratory measure behind them.
   - Same source as (1). Exact quote transcribed below.

3. The DSM is a categorical manual: it defines discrete disorders a person either meets criteria for or does not, and it structures clinical diagnosis, insurance reimbursement, and (historically) research eligibility.
   - American Psychiatric Association (2013/2022), Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, 5th ed. / DSM-5-TR. https://doi.org/10.1176/appi.books.9780890425787

4. High comorbidity: in the National Comorbidity Survey Replication, 45% of respondents with any 12-month DSM-IV disorder met criteria for two or more disorders in the same year.
   - Kessler, R.C., Chiu, W.T., Demler, O., Walters, E.E. (2005). Arch Gen Psychiatry 62(6):617–627. https://doi.org/10.1001/archpsyc.62.6.617

5. Within-category heterogeneity: DSM-5 criteria allow 636,120 distinct symptom combinations that all qualify as PTSD; major depression admits hundreds of qualifying symptom profiles (commonly cited as 227).
   - Galatzer-Levy, I.R., Bryant, R.A. (2013). "636,120 Ways to Have Posttraumatic Stress Disorder." Perspectives on Psychological Science 8(6):651–662. https://doi.org/10.1177/1745691613504115

6. Modest test-retest reliability for common diagnoses in the DSM-5 field trials: major depressive disorder kappa ≈ 0.28, generalized anxiety disorder kappa ≈ 0.20 (both in the "questionable" range).
   - Regier, D.A., et al. (2013). "DSM-5 Field Trials, Part II." Am J Psychiatry 170(1):59–70. https://doi.org/10.1176/appi.ajp.2012.12070999

7. RDoC organizes research around functional domains and their mechanisms (e.g., negative valence/threat, positive valence/reward, cognitive systems) studied dimensionally across levels from genes to behavior, deliberately cutting across DSM categories.
   - NIMH RDoC framework: https://www.nimh.nih.gov/research/research-funded-by-nimh/rdoc

8. HiTOP is a dimensional, hierarchical alternative that models psychopathology as continuous spectra (internalizing, externalizing, thought disorder, etc.) built up from symptoms rather than as discrete disorders.
   - Kotov, R., et al. (2017). "The Hierarchical Taxonomy of Psychopathology (HiTOP): A Dimensional Alternative to Traditional Nosologies." J Abnorm Psychol 126(4):454–477. https://doi.org/10.1037/abn0000258

9. A general factor of psychopathology ("p factor") captures shared liability across internalizing, externalizing, and thought disorders, consistent with symptoms grading continuously rather than sorting into natural kinds.
   - Caspi, A., et al. (2014). "The p Factor: One General Psychopathology Factor in the Structure of Psychiatric Disorders?" Clinical Psychological Science 2(2):119–137. https://doi.org/10.1177/2167702613497473

10. Insel later publicly judged that the neuroscience-and-genetics push he led had not yet reduced suicide, hospitalization, or improved recovery at population scale — an acknowledgment that RDoC has not replaced DSM in the clinic. (Modern copyrighted interview; paraphrased, not quoted.)
    - Reported in Rogers, A. (2017), WIRED, "Star Neuroscientist Tom Insel Leaves the Google-Spawned Verily for … a Startup?" https://www.wired.com/2017/05/star-neuroscientist-tom-insel-leaves-google-spawned-verily-startup/

## Exact quotes (verified, public-facing primary text)

- Insel (2013), on reliability vs. validity: "While DSM has been described as a 'Bible' for the field, it is, at best, a dictionary, creating a set of labels and defining each. The strength of each of the editions of DSM has been 'reliability' – each edition has ensured that clinicians use the same terms in the same ways. The weakness is its lack of validity."
- Insel (2013), on the decision: "That is why NIMH will be re-orienting its research away from DSM categories."
- Insel (2013), the constraint: "But it is critical to realize that we cannot succeed if we use DSM categories as the 'gold standard.'"
  - All three transcribed exactly from the archived full text at psychrights.org (verified against the wording in the Wayback capture).

## Do not overclaim

- RDoC and HiTOP are research frameworks, not clinical replacements. No jurisdiction has replaced the DSM (or ICD) with either for routine diagnosis or billing. Say the science has moved, not that the clinic has.
- Insel did not "abolish" the DSM or ban DSM-based grants; NIMH kept funding varied work. The 2013 statement was a redirection of emphasis, later somewhat softened.
- "No biomarkers" should be stated carefully: there are replicated group-level differences, but none that reliably diagnose an individual and map cleanly onto a DSM label. Avoid implying zero biological findings.
- Low kappas come from field trials under specific conditions; do not present 0.28 as "the" reliability of depression in all settings.
- Do not claim comorbidity or heterogeneity "prove" categories are false; they strain the natural-kinds assumption and are read that way by dimensional researchers, which is the point.
- Avoid the material other posts own: value-laden thresholds, the 1973 homosexuality removal, and Hacking's looping effects. This post is about natural kinds, not about who draws the line.

## Flags

- The "227 symptom profiles for major depression" figure is widely repeated and appears in Galatzer-Levy & Bryant's framing, but the exact count depends on assumptions (e.g., whether compound criteria are split). The 636,120 PTSD figure is the paper's headline calculation and is solid; treat 227 as illustrative.
- The p-factor paper is Caspi et al. 2014 (first authors Caspi & Moffitt among a larger group); some sources cite it as "Caspi & Moffitt." Use "Caspi and colleagues."
- The Insel "I don't think we moved the needle / roughly $20 billion" remark circulates in several near-identical wordings across secondary outlets; I could not confirm a single authoritative transcript, so it is paraphrased and flagged rather than quoted.
- The original NIMH URL for the 2013 blog is dead/redirected; I verified the text via the Wayback Machine capture and the psychrights.org mirror, which match.

# Draft

In April 2013, a few weeks before the American Psychiatric Association released the fifth edition of its diagnostic manual, the person then running the largest mental-health research funder in the world published a short notice explaining why he would stop relying on it. Thomas Insel, director of the U.S. National Institute of Mental Health, wrote that the manual's categories rested on agreement about clusters of symptoms rather than on any measurable biological fact. "The strength of each of the editions of DSM has been 'reliability'," he wrote; "the weakness is its lack of validity." Clinicians using the manual agree with each other about what to call a given patient. Whether the resulting labels correspond to real, distinct diseases is a separate question, and Insel's answer was that they largely do not. NIMH, he announced, would begin reorienting its research away from those categories.

That notice is worth dwelling on because of who wrote it and what he controlled. The statement came from inside the field's own research establishment, from the person who directed how a large share of federal mental-health research money was spent, and it said plainly that the book governing everyday diagnosis was a poor foundation for science. NIMH then moved its funding to match, telling applicants that studies looking across or subdividing the manual's categories would be favored over studies that enrolled patients strictly by DSM criteria. In the decade since, two large research programs have grown up around that judgment. Both are built on the assumption that the manual's disorders are convenient administrative units rather than natural kinds — the term philosophers use for categories that carve nature at a real joint, the way "gold" or "influenza" pick out something with a stable underlying nature rather than a label we agreed to apply.

To see why that assumption came under strain, it helps to be clear about what the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders actually does. It is a categorical system. For each disorder it lists criteria, and a person either meets the threshold or does not: five of nine symptoms for two weeks, and the diagnosis is major depressive disorder; four and the diagnosis does not apply. Those yes-or-no labels then run through the whole apparatus of care. They determine which billing code a clinician submits, which treatments an insurer will reimburse, which patients a drug trial may enroll, and which diagnosis a chart will carry for years. The manual works, in the sense that it lets a psychiatrist in Boston and one in Berlin use the word "schizophrenia" to mean roughly the same list of features. The deeper claim built into its structure is stronger: that these categories name discrete conditions, each with its own boundaries and, eventually, its own biology.

Several features of the data sit awkwardly against that claim. The first is comorbidity. In the National Comorbidity Survey Replication, a large U.S. epidemiological study, 45 percent of people who met criteria for any disorder in a given year met criteria for at least one more in the same year. If depression, anxiety, and substance-use disorders were as separate as diabetes and a broken wrist, this degree of overlap would be strange; instead it is the norm, which suggests the boundaries between categories are drawn through continuous territory rather than around isolated conditions.

The second is heterogeneity within a single category. Because most DSM diagnoses require some number of symptoms from a longer menu, two people can receive the identical label while sharing almost none of the same problems. One analysis counted 636,120 distinct symptom combinations that all qualify as post-traumatic stress disorder under DSM-5. Major depression admits hundreds of qualifying profiles: one patient sleepless, agitated, and unable to eat, another sleeping constantly, slowed, and overeating, both carrying the same diagnosis and, in principle, the same treatment. A category that contains that much internal variety is hard to treat as one thing with one cause.

The third is reliability under scrutiny. When the DSM-5 was field-tested before publication, some of its most common diagnoses agreed with themselves only modestly on repeat assessment. Major depressive disorder reached a kappa around 0.28 and generalized anxiety disorder around 0.20 — values that sit in the range clinicians themselves describe as questionable. The reliability that Insel praised turned out to be uneven for exactly the disorders most people receive.

The fourth is the missing biology. Decades of imaging, genetics, and blood work have produced real findings, but none that reliably diagnose an individual and line up with a DSM label. Large genetic studies keep finding that the same inherited risk variants raise the odds of several disorders at once — schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, depression, and autism share substantial genetic overlap — so the risk spreads across diagnostic boundaries rather than tracking a single category. There is no scan or assay that confirms depression the way a biopsy confirms a lymphoma, which is precisely the comparison Insel drew when he pointed to ischemic heart disease and AIDS as diagnoses anchored in something a laboratory can measure.

The Research Domain Criteria framework, or RDoC, is NIMH's response to this pattern. Instead of asking which disorder a person has, RDoC asks how well-defined functional systems are working: how the brain responds to threat, how it processes reward, how attention and memory operate. Each of these domains is studied dimensionally, from genes and circuits up to behavior, and deliberately across diagnostic lines. A study built this way might recruit everyone in a mood clinic and examine the circuitry of anhedonia — the loss of pleasure — wherever it appears, rather than restricting itself to patients who clear the depression threshold. The wager is that the mechanisms of mental illness respect the brain's functional architecture rather than the chapter headings of a manual, and that a durable classification can only be built by measuring the mechanisms first.

HiTOP, the Hierarchical Taxonomy of Psychopathology, comes at the same problem from the clinical and statistical side. It starts from how symptoms actually cluster together in large samples and builds a structure upward: individual symptoms combine into narrow syndromes, syndromes into broad spectra such as internalizing (the anxious-depressive range) and externalizing (impulsive and disinhibited problems), and those into still broader factors. Everything in it is a matter of degree along a continuum, not a box a person is in or out of. Supporting this picture, several groups have found that a single general dimension — sometimes called the "p factor," by analogy with the general factor in intelligence research — captures a shared liability running through internalizing, externalizing, and thought disorders alike. If psychopathology grades continuously and shares a common core, the sharp walls between DSM categories start to look like lines drawn on a slope.

This is where "convenient fiction" becomes the accurate description rather than an insult. A convenient fiction is a representation everyone knows is not literally true but keeps using because it does useful work. The DSM categories still coordinate an enormous system. A diagnosis of major depressive disorder tells a colleague roughly what is going on, unlocks a reimbursable course of treatment, and gives a patient a name for their experience. Those functions do not require the category to be a natural kind. They only require it to be shared and stable. What the research frameworks have done is separate the two roles that the manual used to fuse: the categories can go on organizing communication and payment while the science stops treating them as descriptions of how illness is actually built. RDoC makes that split explicit by refusing to use DSM categories as its standard of truth; HiTOP makes it explicit by rebuilding the map from symptom data upward and letting the familiar disorder names fall where they may within a set of continuous spectra.

The honest complication is that the clinic cannot simply switch. Dimensional systems are more faithful to the data and much harder to use at the bedside. A clinician needs a decision — admit or discharge, prescribe or wait, code the visit so it can be billed — and a decision is easier to hang on a label than on a profile of scores across a dozen continua. Insurance, disability law, and prescribing guidelines are all written in categorical language, so the manual persists because the institutions around it are built from its terms. The same categorical convenience also has a cost that the dimensional critique sharpens: when a condition is a yes-or-no box, expanding the box quietly reclassifies more ordinary distress as disorder, and a system with no natural boundary to point to has weak defenses against that drift.

More than a decade after the 2013 announcement, the promised replacement has not arrived. RDoC has generated a great deal of research without yielding a diagnostic system a clinician can open on a Tuesday morning, and Insel himself later reflected that the neuroscience program he championed had not measurably reduced suicide or hospitalization at the scale that matters. The DSM, now in its text-revised fifth edition, remains the working manual of psychiatry, categorical as ever.

What has changed is quieter and, in its way, more consequential. The field's leading research institutions now organize their work around the premise that the manual's disorders are not the real units of mental illness. They keep the labels for the jobs labels do well and look elsewhere for the structure of the thing itself. Psychiatry has arrived at a working arrangement in which the book that defines its diagnoses and the science that studies them no longer make the same claim about what a mental disorder is — and everyone involved has decided, for now, that they can live with the gap.
