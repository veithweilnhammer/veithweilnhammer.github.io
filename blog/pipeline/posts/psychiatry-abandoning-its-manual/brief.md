# Brief: Psychiatry Is Quietly Abandoning Its Own Diagnostic Manual

## Slug
psychiatry-abandoning-its-manual

## Working title
Psychiatry Is Quietly Abandoning Its Own Diagnostic Manual

## One-line angle (also the Substack subtitle / SEO hook)
The DSM still rules the clinic, but the field's leading research frameworks now treat its categories as useful fictions rather than real, natural kinds of illness.

## Single argument (thesis)
Psychiatry's most influential research programs have stopped treating DSM diagnostic categories as natural kinds and instead treat them as convenient administrative fictions, which means the manual that governs clinical practice no longer matches how the science describes mental illness.

## Concrete anchor (optional — a real example to ground the argument)
The U.S. National Institute of Mental Health's decision (announced by Thomas Insel in 2013) to reorient its research funding away from DSM categories and toward the Research Domain Criteria (RDoC) framework — a funder effectively declaring the manual inadequate for science.

## What this post must cover
- What the DSM is for and how it is used: a categorical manual that structures diagnosis, billing, and research, treating disorders as discrete entities a person either has or does not.
- The natural-kinds assumption and why it is under strain: high comorbidity, fuzzy boundaries, heterogeneity within categories, and the absence of biological markers that map onto DSM labels.
- RDoC: NIMH's move to organize research around dimensions and mechanisms (e.g., threat, reward, cognition) that cut across, and ignore, DSM categories.
- HiTOP: the dimensional, hierarchical alternative that treats symptoms as continuous spectra rather than discrete disorders.
- What "convenient fictions" means here: the categories can remain useful for communication and reimbursement while no longer being taken as carving nature at its joints.
- The stakes and the honest caveat: clinicians still need workable labels, and dimensional models are harder to use at the bedside — so the manual persists in practice even as the science moves on. Connect briefly to overdiagnosis concerns.
