# Evidence

**Thesis:** Psychiatry's most influential research programs have stopped treating DSM diagnostic categories as natural kinds and instead use them as administrative conveniences, so the manual that governs the clinic no longer matches how the field's own science describes mental illness.

## Key claims and sources

1. In April 2013, shortly before DSM-5 was published, Thomas Insel, then director of the U.S. National Institute of Mental Health, announced that NIMH would reorient its research funding away from DSM categories toward its own Research Domain Criteria (RDoC) framework.
   - Insel, T. (2013). "Transforming Diagnosis." NIMH Director's Blog, 29 April 2013. Original URL now returns 404; verified against the Internet Archive capture: https://web.archive.org/web/20170404204638/https://www.nimh.nih.gov/about/directors/thomas-insel/blog/2013/transforming-diagnosis.shtml (cross-checked against the psychrights.org mirror, which matches).

2. Insel's stated reason: the DSM has strong reliability but weak validity — its categories are consensus clusters of symptoms with no objective laboratory measure behind them.
   - Same source as (1). Verified quotes below.

3. The DSM (currently DSM-5-TR) is the standard U.S. categorical manual: it defines discrete disorders a person either meets criteria for or does not, and it structures clinical diagnosis, records, insurance reimbursement, and (historically) research eligibility. Its diagnoses link to codes used for billing and data collection.
   - American Psychiatric Association (2022), "About DSM-5-TR." https://www.psychiatry.org/psychiatrists/practice/dsm/about-dsm ; DSM-5-TR: https://doi.org/10.1176/appi.books.9780890425787

4. The categorical DSM was consolidated in 1980 with DSM-III, led by Robert Spitzer, which shifted the field from psychoanalytic theory to operational symptom criteria and greatly improved diagnostic reliability. The natural-kinds ambition traces to Kraepelin's late-19th-century separation of dementia praecox from manic-depressive illness by clinical course.
   - Widely documented history; conceptual framing in Hyman, S.E. (2010), "The Diagnosis of Mental Disorders: The Problem of Reification." Annu Rev Clin Psychol 6:155–179. https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev.clinpsy.3.022806.091532

5. Reliability is not validity: clinicians can agree on a label even when the labelled category does not track a single disease with distinct causes, mechanisms, or boundaries. Most psychiatric diagnoses have not been shown to be discrete natural kinds, though many retain clinical utility.
   - Kendell, R., Jablensky, A. (2003). "Distinguishing Between the Validity and Utility of Psychiatric Diagnoses." Am J Psychiatry 160(1):4–12. https://doi.org/10.1176/appi.ajp.160.1.4

6. High comorbidity: in the U.S. National Comorbidity Survey Replication, among people with any 12-month DSM-IV disorder, 55% had one diagnosis, 22% had two, and 23% had three or more — i.e., 45% had two or more in the same year.
   - Kessler, R.C., Chiu, W.T., Demler, O., Walters, E.E. (2005). Arch Gen Psychiatry 62(6):617–627. https://doi.org/10.1001/archpsyc.62.6.617

7. Within-category heterogeneity in depression: in the STAR*D sample of 3,703 depressed outpatients, Fried and Nesse found 1,030 unique symptom profiles, the most common describing only 1.8% of patients.
   - Fried, E.I., Nesse, R.M. (2015). "Depression is not a consistent syndrome." J Affect Disord 172:96–102. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jad.2014.10.010

8. Within-category heterogeneity in PTSD: DSM-5 criteria admit 636,120 distinct symptom combinations that all qualify as post-traumatic stress disorder.
   - Galatzer-Levy, I.R., Bryant, R.A. (2013). "636,120 Ways to Have Posttraumatic Stress Disorder." Perspectives on Psychological Science 8(6):651–662. https://doi.org/10.1177/1745691613504115

9. Modest test-retest reliability for common diagnoses in the DSM-5 field trials: major depressive disorder kappa ≈ 0.28, generalized anxiety disorder kappa ≈ 0.20 (both in the range clinicians call questionable).
   - Regier, D.A., et al. (2013). "DSM-5 Field Trials, Part II." Am J Psychiatry 170(1):59–70. https://doi.org/10.1176/appi.ajp.2012.12070999

10. Shared genetic risk across disorders: large genomic analyses find the same inherited risk variants raise liability to several disorders at once (autism, ADHD, bipolar disorder, major depression, schizophrenia), so risk crosses diagnostic boundaries.
    - Cross-Disorder Group of the Psychiatric Genomics Consortium (2013). "Identification of risk loci with shared effects on five major psychiatric disorders." The Lancet 381(9875):1371–1379. https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(12)62129-1

11. RDoC organizes research around dimensions of functioning studied across levels (genes, circuits, physiology, behavior, self-report), deliberately cutting across DSM categories. Its domains are Negative Valence Systems, Positive Valence Systems, Cognitive Systems, Social Processes, Arousal/Regulatory Systems, and Sensorimotor Systems (the sixth added in 2019).
    - Insel, T., et al. (2010). "Research Domain Criteria (RDoC)." Am J Psychiatry 167(7):748–751. https://doi.org/10.1176/appi.ajp.2010.09091379 ; NIMH RDoC: https://www.nimh.nih.gov/research/research-funded-by-nimh/rdoc

12. HiTOP is a dimensional, hierarchical alternative that builds from symptoms into components, syndromes, and broad spectra (e.g., internalizing, externalizing, thought disorder), modelling psychopathology as continuous rather than as discrete disorders, explicitly to address arbitrary boundaries, co-occurrence, heterogeneity, and diagnostic instability.
    - Kotov, R., et al. (2017). "The Hierarchical Taxonomy of Psychopathology (HiTOP)." J Abnorm Psychol 126(4):454–477. https://doi.org/10.1037/abn0000258 ; HiTOP Consortium: https://www.hitop-system.org/the-framework

13. A general factor of psychopathology (the "p factor") captures shared liability across internalizing, externalizing, and thought disorders, consistent with symptoms grading continuously rather than sorting into natural kinds.
    - Caspi, A., and colleagues (2014). "The p Factor." Clinical Psychological Science 2(2):119–137. https://doi.org/10.1177/2167702613497473

14. Diagnostic inflation / overdiagnosis is a recognized concern, especially when categorical thresholds expand or are applied to mild, mixed, or transient problems.
    - Frances, A. (2013). "The New Crisis of Confidence in Psychiatric Diagnosis." Ann Intern Med 159(3):221–222. https://doi.org/10.7326/0003-4819-159-3-201308060-00655 ; Moynihan, R., et al. (2013). "Preventing overdiagnosis." BMJ 347:f6360. https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.f6360

15. Insel later reflected publicly that the neuroscience-and-genetics program he championed had not measurably reduced suicide or hospitalization at population scale — an acknowledgement that RDoC has not replaced the DSM in the clinic. (Paraphrased; see flag.)
    - Reported in Rogers, A. (2017), WIRED. https://www.wired.com/2017/05/star-neuroscientist-tom-insel-leaves-google-spawned-verily-startup/

## Exact quotes (verified against the archived primary text)

- "While DSM has been described as a 'Bible' for the field, it is, at best, a dictionary, creating a set of labels and defining each. The strength of each of the editions of DSM has been 'reliability' – each edition has ensured that clinicians use the same terms in the same ways. The weakness is its lack of validity." (Insel, 2013)
- "Unlike our definitions of ischemic heart disease, lymphoma, or AIDS, the DSM diagnoses are based on a consensus about clusters of clinical symptoms, not any objective laboratory measure." (Insel, 2013)
- "NIMH will be re-orienting its research away from DSM categories." (Insel, 2013)
- "RDoC, for now, is a research framework, not a clinical tool." (Insel, 2013)

## Do not overclaim

- RDoC and HiTOP are research frameworks, not clinical replacements. No jurisdiction has replaced the DSM (or ICD) for routine diagnosis or billing. Say the science has moved, not that the clinic has.
- Insel did not abolish the DSM or ban DSM-based grants; the 2013 statement redirected emphasis and was later somewhat softened after friction with the APA.
- State the biomarker point carefully: there are replicated group-level biological findings, but none that reliably diagnose an individual and map cleanly onto a DSM label. Do not imply zero biological findings.
- The low DSM-5 field-trial kappas come from specific conditions; do not present 0.28 as depression's reliability in all settings.
- Comorbidity and heterogeneity strain the natural-kinds assumption; they do not, by themselves, "prove" categories are false.
- Dimensional models have not solved diagnosis; they shift the problem from bounding diseases to bounding continua for clinical action, and bring their own risks.

## Flags

- The "227 symptom combinations for major depression" figure (used by one candidate and attributed to Fried & Nesse) is a combinatorial estimate from a different source, not Fried & Nesse's finding. Their actual STAR*D result is 1,030 empirically observed profiles; this integration uses 1,030 and drops the 227 figure as mis-attributed. The 636,120 PTSD figure is the Galatzer-Levy & Bryant headline calculation and is solid.
- The p-factor paper (Caspi et al., 2014) is sometimes cited as "Caspi & Moffitt"; cite as "Caspi and colleagues."
- Insel's "we didn't move the needle / ~$20 billion" remark circulates in several near-identical wordings without a single authoritative transcript; it is paraphrased and flagged, not quoted.
- The original NIMH URL for the 2013 blog is dead; text verified via the Wayback Machine capture and a matching mirror.
- The DSM-5-TR text is copyrighted; DSM claims here rely on public APA pages and secondary scholarship rather than long direct quotation.

# Draft

In April 2013, a few weeks before the American Psychiatric Association published the fifth edition of its diagnostic manual, the person then running the largest mental-health research funder in the world explained why he would stop relying on it. Thomas Insel, director of the U.S. National Institute of Mental Health, wrote that the manual's categories rested on agreement about clusters of symptoms rather than on any measurable biological fact. "The strength of each of the editions of DSM has been 'reliability'," he wrote; "the weakness is its lack of validity." Clinicians using the manual agree with one another about what to call a given patient. Whether those labels correspond to real, distinct diseases is a separate question, and Insel's answer was that they largely do not. NIMH, he announced, would begin reorienting its research away from DSM categories.

That announcement carried weight because of who made it and what he controlled. It came from inside the field's own research establishment, from the person who directed how a large share of federal mental-health research money was spent, and it said plainly that the book governing everyday diagnosis was a poor foundation for science. NIMH then moved its funding to match, favoring studies that looked across or subdivided the manual's categories over studies that enrolled patients strictly by DSM criteria. In the decade since, two large research programs have grown up around that judgment, and both are built on the same premise: the manual's disorders are convenient administrative units rather than natural kinds. A natural kind is a category whose boundaries come from the world rather than from a committee, the way "gold" or "influenza" pick out something with a stable underlying nature. The relevant distinction, drawn sharply by Robert Kendell and Assen Jablensky a decade earlier, is between the validity of a diagnosis, which asks whether it names a real thing, and its utility, which asks whether it is useful to apply. A label can score high on one and low on the other.

To see why the natural-kinds assumption came under strain, it helps to be clear about what the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders does and where it came from. It is a categorical system. For each disorder it lists criteria, and a person either meets the threshold or does not: five of nine symptoms for two weeks, and the diagnosis is major depressive disorder; four, and it does not apply. Those yes-or-no labels then run through the whole apparatus of care, determining which code a clinician submits, which treatments an insurer reimburses, which patients a drug trial can enroll, and which diagnosis a chart carries for years. This structure was consolidated in 1980, when DSM-III, led by the psychiatrist Robert Spitzer, replaced psychoanalytic theories of illness with explicit symptom checklists. The change solved a genuine problem: before it, two psychiatrists examining the same patient often reached different diagnoses, and operational criteria gave them a far better chance of agreeing. That reliability let psychiatry join the rest of evidence-based medicine, supporting trials, epidemiology, and standardized billing. Built into the categories, though, was a stronger claim, one that reached back to Emil Kraepelin's separation of dementia praecox from manic-depressive illness by their different courses: that these labels name discrete conditions, each with its own boundary and, eventually, its own biology. The working hope was that reliability would mature into validity.

Several features of the data sit awkwardly against that hope. The first is comorbidity. In the National Comorbidity Survey Replication, a large U.S. epidemiological study, 45 percent of people who met criteria for any disorder in a given year also met criteria for at least one more in the same year, with nearly a quarter meeting criteria for three or more. If depression, anxiety, and substance-use disorders were as separate as diabetes and a broken wrist, this degree of overlap would be surprising; instead it is the norm, which suggests the categories are boundaries drawn through continuous territory rather than walls around isolated conditions.

The second is heterogeneity within a single category. Because most DSM diagnoses require some number of symptoms from a longer menu, two people can receive the identical label while sharing almost none of the same problems. In the STAR*D depression study, Eiko Fried and Randolph Nesse found 1,030 distinct symptom profiles among 3,703 depressed outpatients, and the single most common profile fit only 1.8 percent of them. One patient may be sleepless, agitated, and unable to eat, another sleeping constantly, slowed, and overeating, both carrying the same diagnosis and, in principle, the same treatment. Post-traumatic stress disorder is more extreme still: the DSM-5 criteria admit 636,120 distinct symptom combinations that all qualify. A category holding that much internal variety is hard to treat as one thing with one cause.

The third is reliability under closer scrutiny. When the DSM-5 was field-tested before publication, some of its most common diagnoses agreed with themselves only modestly on repeat assessment. Major depressive disorder reached a kappa around 0.28 and generalized anxiety disorder around 0.20, values that fall in the range clinicians themselves call questionable. The reliability Insel praised turned out to be uneven for exactly the disorders most people receive.

The fourth is the missing biology. Decades of imaging, genetics, and blood work have produced real findings, but none that reliably diagnose an individual and line up with a DSM label. Large genomic analyses keep finding that the same inherited risk variants raise the odds of several disorders at once; a study of autism, ADHD, bipolar disorder, major depression, and schizophrenia found substantial shared genetic risk across all five, so the liability spreads across diagnostic boundaries rather than tracking one category. There is no scan or assay that confirms depression the way a biopsy confirms a lymphoma, which is the comparison Insel drew when he pointed to ischemic heart disease, lymphoma, and AIDS as diagnoses anchored in something a laboratory can measure.

The Research Domain Criteria framework, or RDoC, is NIMH's answer to this pattern. Instead of asking which disorder a person has, it asks how well-defined functional systems are working: how the brain responds to threat, how it processes reward, how attention and memory operate. RDoC now spans six such domains — negative valence, positive valence, cognitive systems, social processes, arousal and regulation, and sensorimotor function — each studied dimensionally from genes and circuits up to behavior, and deliberately across diagnostic lines. A study built this way might recruit everyone in a mood clinic and examine the circuitry of anhedonia, the loss of pleasure, wherever it appears, rather than restricting itself to patients who clear the depression threshold. The wager is that the mechanisms of mental illness follow the brain's functional architecture rather than the chapter headings of a manual, and that a durable classification has to be built by measuring those mechanisms first. Insel was explicit about the limits: RDoC, he wrote, is a research framework, not a clinical tool.

HiTOP, the Hierarchical Taxonomy of Psychopathology, comes at the same problem from the statistical and clinical side. It starts from how symptoms actually cluster together in large samples and builds upward: individual symptoms combine into narrow components and syndromes, and those into broad spectra such as internalizing, which covers the anxious-depressive range, externalizing, which covers impulsive and disinhibited problems, and thought disorder. Everything in it is a matter of degree along a continuum rather than a box a person is inside or outside. Comorbidity becomes less puzzling here, because related disorders sit near one another under the same higher-order spectrum. Supporting the picture, several groups have found that a single general dimension, sometimes called the p factor by analogy with the general factor in intelligence research, captures a shared liability running through internalizing, externalizing, and thought disorders alike. If psychopathology grades continuously and shares a common core, the sharp walls between DSM categories begin to look like thresholds imposed on a gradient.

This is where "convenient fiction" becomes an accurate description rather than an insult. A convenient fiction is a category everyone knows is not literally carved out by nature but keeps using because it does real work. Money, legal adulthood, and national borders all rest on convention, and all organize behavior and carry serious consequences. DSM categories work the same way. A diagnosis of major depressive disorder tells a colleague roughly what is going on, unlocks a reimbursable course of treatment, and gives a patient a name for their experience. None of those functions require the category to be a natural kind; they only require it to be shared and stable. What the research frameworks have done is pull apart two roles the manual used to fuse. The categories can keep organizing communication and payment while the science stops treating them as accounts of how illness is actually built. The risk in keeping the labels is that people mistake a description for an explanation: saying a patient cannot work because they have major depression is administratively necessary, but the label only gathers the symptoms under a heading and leaves untouched the harder question of why that person's fatigue, insomnia, and hopelessness took the form they did.

The honest complication is that the clinic cannot simply switch. Dimensional systems are more faithful to the data and much harder to use at the bedside. A clinician needs a decision — admit or discharge, prescribe or wait, code the visit so it can be billed — and a decision is easier to hang on a single label than on a profile of scores across a dozen continua. Insurance, disability law, and prescribing guidelines are all written in categorical language, so the manual persists in part because the institutions around it are built from its terms. Dimensional models carry their own hazards, too. They are harder to teach and to communicate quickly, and a system that scores everyone on many axes creates new opportunities to classify ordinary variation in mood, attention, or fear as abnormal along some dimension. Better measurement does not by itself produce more restraint.

The categorical habit also sharpens the long-running worry about overdiagnosis. When a disorder is a yes-or-no box, widening the box quietly reclassifies more people as ill, and a category with no natural boundary to point to has weak defenses against that drift, especially for mild, mixed, or transient problems. Critics such as Allen Frances have argued that successive expansions of diagnostic criteria have pulled ordinary distress into the clinic, and the general campaign against overdiagnosis in medicine makes the same case about acting on labels applied too readily. Treating a category as a discovered disease makes crossing its threshold feel like uncovering something that was already fully present; treating it as a practical construct keeps the threshold visible as a choice to be used with care.

More than a decade after the 2013 announcement, the promised replacement has not arrived. RDoC has generated a large body of research without producing a diagnostic system a clinician can use in an ordinary appointment, and Insel himself has since reflected that the neuroscience program he led did not measurably reduce suicide or hospitalization at the scale that matters. The DSM, now in its text-revised fifth edition, remains the working manual of psychiatry, as categorical as ever. What has changed is quieter and, in its way, more consequential: the field's leading research institutions now organize their work around the premise that the manual's disorders are not the real units of mental illness. They keep the labels for the jobs labels do well, and look elsewhere for the structure of the thing itself. Psychiatry has settled into a working arrangement in which the book that defines its diagnoses and the science that studies them no longer make the same claim about what a mental disorder is.
