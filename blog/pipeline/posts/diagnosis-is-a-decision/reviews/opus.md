# Review — Diagnosis Is a Decision, Not a Discovery (model: opus)

Reviewed: `integrated.md`. Standard: `blog/writing_style.md`. Cross-checked against
`brief.md`, the three candidates' evidence sections, and the sibling brief
`psychiatry-abandoning-its-manual`. Facts spot-checked by web search where quick.

The draft is substantively strong — accurate, well-grounded, one clear argument,
all "must cover" items present. The problems are almost entirely at the prose
level: a cluster of banned constructions (one hard-ban violation, several
antithesis "trade" pairs, several punchy one-line beats) that the style guide
names as exactly what "killed the first draft." All are line-level fixes.

---

## 1. Style-guide violations

### Hard ban — "not X but Y" (must fix)
The closing sentence contains the single biggest banned construction:

> "The 1973 vote is worth remembering **not as an embarrassment but as** an
> unusually honest moment, when a profession said out loud that the boundary of
> an illness was theirs to set."

This is the negation-definition antithesis the guide bans outright ("Ban every
variant of 'not merely X, it is Y' ... 'not just … but …'"). It is also a poetic
coda ("said out loud that the boundary of an illness was theirs to set"), which
violates "Ending states the substantive point plainly — no poetic coda."
*Rewrite:* "The 1973 vote is worth remembering because the profession made its
choice in the open: it stated that the boundary of an illness was one it had set
and could reset."

### Punchy announcement / meta-narration sentence
> "This is the point at which automated screening stops being a footnote and
> becomes the main event."

Banned "punchy 'setup' or announcement sentence," and it narrates the essay's
own structure ("footnote/main event") rather than stating the claim. *Rewrite:*
"Automated screening makes the threshold problem more consequential, because it
takes a cut-point some committee chose and applies it to many thousands of people
at once."

### Antithesis / parallel "trade" constructions for rhythm
The guide bans balanced "opposite trade" pairs. Three instances:

> "**Move the line down** and you catch more genuine cases and also more people
> who would have recovered on their own; **move it up** and you miss fewer well
> people and also more who needed help."

This one carries real content (the sensitivity/specificity trade), so it is the
most defensible, but the mirror-image rhythm is the banned pattern. Consider
de-symmetrizing: "Lowering the line catches more genuine cases but also flags
more people who would have recovered on their own; raising it does the reverse."

> "A human institution can hold a vote and rewrite a manual; **a model keeps
> enforcing the old norm** until someone retrains it on data that reflect the new
> one." (line 103)

> "**A threshold remembered as a decision** can be revisited when the evidence or
> the values behind it move ... **A threshold mistaken for a discovery** hardens
> into a supposed fact about people that no vote seems entitled to touch."
> (line 105)

The last pair is the most rhetorically "clever/hollow" of the three and I'd
rewrite it into two plain, non-mirrored sentences.

### Punchy short beats / fragments-for-effect
A string of short dramatic one-liners breaks the "measured, not punchy" cadence.
Each states a point that should sit inside a fuller sentence:

- "The fracture does not care what anyone votes." (para 2)
- "They come in degrees." (para 3)
- "Grant the definition and the diagnosis has to go." (para 5)
- "A diagnosis is a description that acts on the thing it describes." (para 7)
- "The category changed when the threshold changed." (para 6)

Individually minor; together they are the "series of punches" the guide warns
against. I'd keep at most one and fold the rest into their neighbouring
sentences. "They come in degrees" is the easiest to absorb: "The difficulty is
that most psychiatric symptoms come in degrees rather than in the two clean
states the fracture model needs."

### Repetition-for-rhythm in the opening
> "The boundary of one illness was redrawn, and it was **redrawn by a show of
> hands**."

The doubled "redrawn" is rhythm, not information. *Rewrite:* "The boundary of
one illness was redrawn by a show of hands." (The concrete "show of hands" image
is good — keep it, drop the echo.)

### Rhetorical understatement opener (borderline)
> "It would be too quick to read this as politics simply defeating science."

Adjacent to the banned "The tension is not academic" opener. It's softened by a
positive follow-up, so lower priority, but the plainer move is to lead with the
positive: "Both science and activism drove the 1973 change."

### Five-item setting list (borderline)
> "A school, an insurer, a hiring platform, a primary-care clinic, and a crisis
> line do not face the same costs of false positives and false negatives."

The guide says "Pick the two that matter and move on." The point lands with two
or three contrasting settings (e.g., a crisis line vs. a hiring platform); five
is a piled-up list for weight.

Overall the prose otherwise matches the measured, information-dense cadence of
the manuscripts well — the paragraphs on continua, the PHQ-9, and Hacking read
cleanly. The violations are concentrated at paragraph openings/closings and the
essay's ending, not spread throughout.

## 2. Factual grounding

Strong. Every load-bearing claim is supported by the candidates' evidence, and
the draft heeds the "do not overclaim" notes carefully (it credits both science
and activism, frames the referendum as ratification, and never says diagnosis is
"just" a construct). Spot-checks:

- **1974 referendum "5,854 votes to 3,810," members "upheld the removal."**
  Verified (Drescher 2015; APA archives). The framing is correct: the Board
  removed the category in December 1973 and the 1974 membership vote *upheld*
  it — the draft does not misattribute the removal to the referendum, which the
  evidence's "do not overclaim" note specifically warns against. Good.
- **PHQ-9 ≥10 → "about 88 percent ... about 88 percent" (sensitivity/specificity)
  and meta-analysis range "roughly 8 to 11."** Verified (Kroenke et al. 2001;
  Manea et al. 2012). The "88% caught / 88% correctly cleared" gloss is an
  accurate lay translation of sensitivity/specificity.
- **DSM history: 1952 "sociopathic personality disturbance" → 1968 "sexual
  deviations" → 1973 removal + narrower residual category → "finally deleted in
  1987."** Matches Drescher (2015); the 1987 endpoint (DSM-III-R removing
  ego-dystonic homosexuality) is correct.
- **MDD rule "at least five symptoms out of nine, present for at least two
  weeks"; nearby cutoffs capture equally impaired people.** Correct (DSM-5;
  Kendler & Gardner 1998).
- **Hacking quotes** ("moving targets ... looping effect"; "first, a person had
  two or three personalities ... within a decade the mean number was 17").
  Match the verified LRB (2006) source text in the evidence section.

Good discipline: the draft correctly **omitted** the unverified "13–0 with two
abstentions" Board tally that the Gemini candidate carried (and that Gemini
mislabeled with a malformed DOI, `10.3390/bs50100565`). No overclaims found.

One small wording caution, not a factual error: "no new fact about the body had
arrived" and "The physiology ... remained exactly as it had been" are rhetorical
absolutes. They are defensible given the evidence, but keep the claim as "no new
biological finding preceded the change" rather than implying nothing scientific
was in play — the very next paragraph rightly says research had weakened the
dysfunction claim.

## 3. Structure & clarity

Leads with the concrete anchor (the 1973 vote) stated flatly, then the discovery
framing, then the continua, the PHQ-9 cut-point, the homosexuality case as the
value decision, Hacking's looping, the synthesis, the AI implication, and the
honest balance. A non-specialist can follow it start to finish; jargon
("sensitivity/specificity," "looping effect") is translated into plain language
each time. The order earns each step.

Two redundancies to tighten:
- Paragraph 8 ("Put the continuum, the chosen cut-point, and the looping effect
  together...") and the synthesis it contains largely restate points already
  made, then paragraph 9 restates the cut-point-inheritance idea again. The
  argument would be crisper if para 8's synthesis were compressed into the lead
  of the AI section.
- The AI material (paras 9–11) is the longest stretch and repeats the "objective
  number / thermometer / inherited cut-point" point across three paragraphs.
  Merging paras 10 and 11 would remove the overlap without losing content.

## 4. One self-contained argument

**Thesis in one sentence:** In psychiatry the boundary between health and illness
is a chosen threshold on a continuum rather than a fact found in nature, so
diagnosis is a value-laden decision whose labels loop back and reshape the people
they name — and automated screening inherits and hardens that choice at scale.

The post develops this single argument from start to finish. The AI section is
the argument's applied payoff (required by the brief), not a second thesis, and
it stays tied to the same threshold-and-looping spine. No paragraph wanders off
the claim. The concrete anchor (homosexuality) sharpens rather than dilutes the
point. This criterion is satisfied.

## 5. Brief compliance & standalone

All six "must cover" items are present: discovery framing (X-ray/fracture,
para 2); thresholds as value judgments on continua (paras 3–4); the 1973
homosexuality case (paras 5–6); Hacking's looping effects (para 7); the AI
implication (paras 9–11); the honest balance that thresholds are necessary but
should stay revisable (para 12). Length looks in the ~1,600–2,000 band.

**Standalone:** clean. No series framing, no next/previous references, no "next
time" teaser, no subscribe/follow CTA. The "Further reading" list is
appropriately light and load-bearing.

**Ownership boundary with the sibling post (checked as instructed):** The sibling
`psychiatry-abandoning-its-manual` owns the DSM-categories-as-convenient-fictions
/ RDoC / HiTOP "natural kinds" story. **The draft does not re-tell it** — there
is no mention of RDoC, HiTOP, NIMH/Insel, "natural kinds," or the manual being
abandoned by researchers. It stays in its lane (the *threshold* for "ill" as a
chosen decision).

One phrase to watch so it doesn't drift into the sibling's territory: "it does
not sit at a **seam in nature**" and "two **natural classes**" (para 3). These
are about the *continuity of a symptom distribution* (this post's subject), not
about whether DSM categories are natural kinds, so they're fine as written — but
keep the framing on "where the cut falls on a continuum," not on "the categories
aren't real kinds," to avoid overlapping the sibling's thesis.

## 6. Top 3 priorities (ranked)

1. **Fix the hard-ban ending.** Rewrite "not as an embarrassment but as an
   unusually honest moment ..." — it is the banned "not X but Y" construction
   *and* a poetic coda, in the most prominent position in the piece. End on the
   plain substantive point instead.
2. **Break up the antithesis "trade" pairs and the punchy one-liners.** The three
   mirror-image pairs (para 4 "move the line down/up," line 103 "human institution
   / a model," line 105 "A threshold remembered / A threshold mistaken") and the
   short dramatic beats ("They come in degrees," "The fracture does not care what
   anyone votes," "Grant the definition and the diagnosis has to go," "A diagnosis
   is a description that acts on the thing it describes") are collectively the
   biggest cadence problem. Fold them into measured, non-mirrored sentences.
3. **Replace the meta-narration announcement and tighten the AI section.** Cut
   "stops being a footnote and becomes the main event," and merge the two
   overlapping paragraphs (10–11) that repeat the "objective number / inherited
   cut-point" point. Trim the five-item setting list to two or three.
