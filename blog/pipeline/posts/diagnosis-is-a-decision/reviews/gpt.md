# Stage 3 review — gpt

## 1. Style-guide violations

The draft is strong at the argument level, but it repeatedly uses constructions the style guide bans: negation-definition, punchy setup sentences, rhetorical understatement openers, and metaphorical closing flourishes. Specific fixes:

- **Line 83:** “No study had located a marker in the blood or the brain that separated the sick from the well and then failed to find it in gay men.”
  - Problem: dramatic negative framing; “sick from the well” is loaded; “gay men” is too narrow for the category.
  - Rewrite: “The change did not follow a new laboratory or imaging finding about same-sex orientation.”

- **Line 83:** “The boundary of one illness was redrawn by a show of hands.”
  - Problem: rhetorical flourish and slightly glib; it makes the referendum sound like the whole mechanism.
  - Rewrite: “The profession changed the category through its formal governance process.”

- **Line 87:** “The difficulty is that most psychiatric symptoms do not come in the two clean states the fracture model needs. They come in degrees.”
  - Problem: banned “not X. They are Y” cadence.
  - Rewrite: “Most psychiatric symptoms are graded rather than binary.”

- **Line 87:** “The five-and-two threshold organizes the continuum sensibly, but it does not sit at a seam in nature.”
  - Problem: “seam in nature” is a clever metaphor; the sentence also uses negation scaffolding.
  - Rewrite: “The five-symptom, two-week rule is a useful convention for organizing care, not a natural boundary in the data.”

- **Line 89:** “There is nothing in the arithmetic that singles out 10…”
  - Problem: negative setup.
  - Rewrite: “The choice of 10 reflects a tradeoff between sensitivity and specificity.”

- **Line 91:** “The homosexuality case is a stark version of the same move, stripped of the numbers.”
  - Problem: punchy announcement sentence; “stripped of the numbers” is essayistic.
  - Rewrite: “The 1973 DSM change shows the same threshold logic without a questionnaire score.”

- **Line 91:** “Grant the definition and the diagnosis has to go.”
  - Problem: too punchy and legalistic.
  - Rewrite: “Under that definition, homosexuality per se no longer met the proposed standard for disorder.”

- **Line 91:** “At every step the underlying human beings were the same; what changed was the decision about which of them counted as sick.”
  - Problem: antithesis-like flourish; also “sick” is blunt.
  - Rewrite: “Across these revisions, the orientation did not change; the diagnostic threshold for disorder did.”

- **Line 93:** “It would be too quick to read this as politics simply defeating science.”
  - Problem: rhetorical-understatement opener.
  - Rewrite: “The case involved both evidence and political pressure.”

- **Line 93:** “Science mattered… Activism mattered too…”
  - Problem: parallel essay cadence that reads AI-generated.
  - Rewrite: “Research weakened the claim that homosexuality inherently involved dysfunction, while activism forced the profession to confront the harm and values built into the label.”

- **Line 93:** “The category changed when the threshold changed.”
  - Problem: punchy wrap-up sentence.
  - Rewrite: “The revision followed from a changed standard for what psychiatry should count as disorder.”

- **Line 95:** “Once a decision like that is made, it does not sit quietly as a description.”
  - Problem: pseudo-poetic personification and negation.
  - Rewrite: “After a diagnostic decision is applied, it can change the person and institution using it.”

- **Line 95:** “A quark does not alter its behavior when a physicist names it…”
  - Problem: useful contrast, but built as a “physical object does not / person does” antithesis.
  - Rewrite: “Physical classifications do not usually change the object classified; human classifications can change self-understanding, behavior, and treatment by others.”

- **Line 97:** “This feedback is not always harmful.”
  - Problem: banned rhetorical understatement opener.
  - Rewrite: “This feedback can help or harm.”

- **Line 97:** “Put the continuum, the chosen cut-point, and the looping effect together, and the label is never inert.”
  - Problem: triadic setup and abstract flourish.
  - Rewrite: “A diagnostic label matters because it sorts a graded population and then changes how the sorted people are treated.”

- **Line 99:** “This is the point at which automated screening stops being a footnote and becomes the main event.”
  - Problem: punchy announcement and grand phrasing.
  - Rewrite: “Automated screening raises the stakes because it can apply the same threshold to many people at once.”

- **Line 101:** “A questionnaire score or a probability with several decimal places carries the flat confidence of a thermometer…”
  - Problem: metaphorical flourish.
  - Rewrite: “A questionnaire score or model probability can look more precise than the judgment behind it.”

- **Line 103:** “And the loop can run faster and wider than any clinic could manage.”
  - Problem: sentence-starting “And” for dramatic emphasis.
  - Rewrite: “Automated systems can also accelerate the feedback loop.”

- **Line 105:** “None of this is an argument against drawing lines.”
  - Problem: hard-ban negation framing.
  - Rewrite: “Thresholds remain necessary.”

- **Line 105:** “A threshold mistaken for a discovery hardens into a supposed fact about people that no vote seems entitled to touch…”
  - Problem: rhetorical crescendo and slightly melodramatic abstraction.
  - Rewrite: “When a threshold is treated as a discovered fact, it becomes harder to revise even when evidence, harms, or social values change.”

## 2. Factual grounding

Quick web checks support several core factual claims:

- The PHQ-9 claim is accurate: Kroenke, Spitzer, and Williams report that a score ≥10 had 88% sensitivity and 88% specificity for major depression against an independent mental-health professional interview: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1495268/
- The later PHQ-9 meta-analysis claim is accurate: Manea, Gilbody, and McMillan found acceptable diagnostic properties across cut-off scores 8–11 and no significant difference in sensitivity/specificity within that interval: https://www.cmaj.ca/content/184/3/E191
- The 1973 DSM claim is broadly accurate: the APA Foundation says DSM-II was modified in December 1973 to eliminate the general category of homosexuality and replace it with Sexual Orientation Disturbance: https://www.apaf.org/library-archives/galleries/lgbtq-leaders/history-of-dsm-and-homosexuality/
- Drescher supports the broader account: activism, APA deliberation, Board removal, referendum upheld by 58% of about 10,000 voting members, and later SOD/ego-dystonic homosexuality history: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4695779/
- The Hacking quotations are verified against the LRB page: https://www.lrb.co.uk/the-paper/v28/n16/ian-hacking/making-up-people

Claims to qualify or correct:

- **Line 83:** “voted to stop classifying homosexuality as a mental illness” should be “homosexuality per se” because Sexual Orientation Disturbance remained after the 1973 DSM-II modification.
- **Line 83:** “gay men” narrows the category incorrectly. Use “gay people,” “same-sex orientation,” or “homosexuality per se.”
- **Line 83:** “by a show of hands” should be removed. Drescher notes the membership voted to favor or oppose the Board decision and its process; the Board decision, not the referendum alone, removed the diagnosis.
- **Line 87:** “Someone with four symptoms for thirteen days is, by the rule, well” is an overclaim. They may not meet major-depression criteria, but they are not thereby “well,” and they might meet another depressive-disorder, adjustment, risk, or clinical-need category. Rewrite: “would not meet this specific major-depression threshold.”
- **Line 91:** “the first DSM in 1952 filed homosexuality under ‘sociopathic personality disturbance’” is compressed. Safer: “DSM-I listed homosexuality as a sexual deviation under sociopathic personality disturbance.”
- **Line 99:** “many thousands of people” is plausible, but the draft does not source a concrete deployed system. Keep general or cite an example if retained.
- **Line 101:** “A school, an insurer, a hiring platform…” expands beyond the candidate evidence. The sources support digital phenotyping and screening-governance concerns, but not specifically hiring platforms. Remove “hiring platform” unless a source is added.
- **Line 103:** “a model keeps enforcing the old norm until someone retrains it on data that reflect the new one” is too rigid. A deployed system can sometimes be changed by adjusting thresholds, labels, prompts, policies, or decommissioning, not only by retraining on new data. Rewrite: “a deployed model may keep applying the old threshold until its target, threshold, or deployment policy is changed.”
- **Line 103:** “flagged responses becomes new training data that hardens the original threshold into apparent fact” is plausible but speculative. Add “if those responses are later reused for training or evaluation.”

## 3. Structure & clarity

The draft leads with a concrete historical anchor rather than an abstract throat-clearing, which works. A non-specialist can follow the main path: discovery model → continua → cut-points → 1973 DSM change → looping effects → automation.

Main clarity issues:

- The first paragraph gives the 1973 case before explaining the discovery model. That is effective, but the final “show of hands” line pushes the tone too far and risks making the post sound like “science by vote,” which the next paragraphs work to avoid.
- Line 87 is overloaded. It covers continua, taxometric evidence, DSM symptom counts, duration thresholds, and nearby cutoffs in one paragraph. Split it after “The diagnostic rules inherit the same problem,” or cut one layer.
- The Hacking paragraph is useful but long. The multiple-personality example is vivid, but it risks becoming a second mini-essay. Keep one quote or shorten the example.
- The AI section is the right destination, but line 99 announces it too dramatically. Start with the practical implication instead.

## 4. One self-contained argument

Single thesis: psychiatric diagnoses turn graded suffering into categories through chosen thresholds, and because those labels change the people they name, automated screening must treat its thresholds as value-laden decisions rather than discovered facts.

The draft does develop that argument from start to finish. The homosexuality case, PHQ-9 cut-point, Hacking loop, and automated-screening implications all serve the thesis. The main risk is that the DSM edition chronology in line 91 becomes a separate “manual history” thread rather than staying focused on the chosen-threshold argument.

## 5. Brief compliance & standalone

The draft covers all must-cover items: the discovery framing, continua and cut-points, the 1973 homosexuality case, Hacking’s looping effects, AI screening, and the balanced point that thresholds remain necessary. It reads as standalone: no series framing, no next/previous references, and no subscribe/follow call.

Sibling-post warning: the brief says `psychiatry-abandoning-its-manual` owns the DSM/RDoC/manual story. This draft does **not** retell RDoC or HiTOP, but line 91 lingers on DSM edition history (“the first DSM in 1952… the 1968 edition… the 1973 revision… finally deleted in 1987”). Keep only the minimum needed for the homosexuality anchor and avoid making manual revision itself the story. Line 103’s “hold a vote and rewrite a manual” also nudges back toward the manual-governance story; rephrase around “revise the threshold or policy.”

## 6. Top 3 priorities

1. Remove the hard-ban style patterns: especially “do not come… They come,” “This is the point…,” “None of this…,” “It would be too quick…,” and the “show of hands” flourish.
2. Fix factual overstatements: “gay men,” “well,” “show of hands,” “hiring platform,” and the claim that retraining is the only way to update an automated threshold.
3. Compress the DSM edition-history material so the post stays on this brief’s threshold argument and does not drift into the sibling post’s manual-history territory.

Rating: `needs-rewrite` — the central argument is sound and well sourced, but the draft needs a focused style/fact pass before it matches the hard-ban style guide.
