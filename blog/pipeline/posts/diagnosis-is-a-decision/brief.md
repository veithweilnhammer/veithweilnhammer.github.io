# Brief: Diagnosis Is a Decision, Not a Discovery

## Slug
diagnosis-is-a-decision

## Working title
Diagnosis Is a Decision, Not a Discovery

## One-line angle (also the Substack subtitle / SEO hook)
Where a person crosses from "distressed" into "ill" is a threshold society sets, a value-laden decision dressed up as a medical discovery.

## Single argument (thesis)
The line between health and illness in psychiatry is set by a threshold that society chooses rather than found in nature, so diagnosis is a value-laden decision, and the labels we apply then reshape the people they name — with direct implications for automated screening.

## Concrete anchor (optional — a real example to ground the argument)
The 1973 removal of homosexuality from the DSM — a change decided by a vote and shifting social norms, not by a new biological finding — showing that where the line for "disorder" falls is a decision, not a discovery.

## What this post must cover
- The discovery framing and why it is intuitive: we imagine diagnosis reveals a fact about the body, like finding a fracture on an X-ray.
- Why psychiatric thresholds are decisions: symptoms lie on continua, and choosing the cut-point for "how much distress counts as disorder" is a value judgment about what is unacceptable or impairing.
- The homosexuality case: its 1973 removal from the DSM by decision and changing norms is a clear instance of the boundary of illness being set socially, not discovered.
- Hacking's looping effects: classifying people changes how they see themselves and behave, which feeds back and alters the category — so diagnostic labels are not inert descriptions.
- The implication for AI: automated screening tools inherit and harden a chosen threshold, applying it at scale as if it were an objective fact, and their labels loop back on the people they flag.
- The honest balance: thresholds are still necessary and often useful for allocating care; the point is to see them as chosen and revisable, not natural and fixed.
