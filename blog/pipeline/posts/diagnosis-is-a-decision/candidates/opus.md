# Evidence

**Thesis (one sentence):** The line between health and illness in psychiatry is set by a threshold that society chooses rather than one found in nature, so a diagnosis is a value-laden decision about where distress becomes disorder, and once applied the label reshapes the people it names — which is exactly what automated screening tools inherit and apply at scale.

## Key claims and sources

1. **In December 1973 the APA Board of Trustees voted to remove homosexuality as a mental disorder from DSM-II.** No new biological finding preceded the change; it followed activism and a reappraisal of what counts as a disorder.
   - Bayer, Ronald (1981/1987). *Homosexuality and American Psychiatry: The Politics of Diagnosis.* Princeton University Press. https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691028378/homosexuality-and-american-psychiatry
   - Drescher, Jack (2015). "Out of DSM: Depathologizing Homosexuality." *Behavioral Sciences* 5(4):565–575. https://doi.org/10.3390/bs5040565

2. **A faction opposed the decision and forced a referendum of the full APA membership in 1974; the removal was upheld, 5,854 to 3,810.** This makes the "boundary of illness" being set by vote explicit.
   - Bayer (1981), as above; Drescher (2015), https://doi.org/10.3390/bs5040565

3. **Homosexuality's classification had already shifted over editions: DSM-I (1952) listed it under "sociopathic personality disturbance"; DSM-II (1968) under "sexual deviations."** The category was moving well before it was removed.
   - Drescher (2015). https://doi.org/10.3390/bs5040565

4. **Robert Spitzer's argument for removal was definitional: a condition should count as a disorder only if it regularly causes subjective distress or generalized impairment in functioning; homosexuality per se met neither for many people.** This is an argument about where the line falls, not about a new fact of biology.
   - Spitzer, Robert L. (1981). "The diagnostic status of homosexuality in DSM-III: a reformulation of the issues." *American Journal of Psychiatry* 138(2):210–215. https://doi.org/10.1176/ajp.138.2.210
   - Bayer (1981); Drescher (2015).

5. **After removal, DSM-II added "sexual orientation disturbance" (later "ego-dystonic homosexuality" in DSM-III, 1980), fully removed in the 1987 DSM-III-R.** The compromise category shows the line was being negotiated, not read off nature.
   - Drescher (2015). https://doi.org/10.3390/bs5040565

6. **Psychiatric symptoms such as depression are continuously distributed in the population, with no natural cut-point where "disorder" begins; diagnosis imposes a threshold on that continuum.**
   - Kendler, K.S. & Gardner, C.O. (1998). "Boundaries of major depression: an evaluation of DSM-IV criteria." *American Journal of Psychiatry* 155(2):172–177. https://doi.org/10.1176/ajp.155.2.172
   - Ruscio, J. & Ruscio, A.M. (2000). "Informing the continuity controversy: a taxometric analysis of depression." *Journal of Abnormal Psychology* 109(3):473–487. https://doi.org/10.1037/0021-843X.109.3.473

7. **Screening instruments encode a chosen cut-point: the PHQ-9 commonly uses a score of ≥10 to flag probable depression, a value selected to trade sensitivity against specificity.**
   - Kroenke, K., Spitzer, R.L., Williams, J.B.W. (2001). "The PHQ-9: validity of a brief depression severity measure." *Journal of General Internal Medicine* 16(9):606–613. https://doi.org/10.1046/j.1525-1497.2001.016009606.x
   - Manea, L., Gilbody, S., McMillan, D. (2012). "Optimal cut-off score for diagnosing depression with the PHQ-9: a meta-analysis." *CMAJ* 184(3):E191–E196. https://doi.org/10.1503/cmaj.110829

8. **Ian Hacking's "looping effect": classifying people changes how they understand and behave, which feeds back and alters the category, so a human kind is a "moving target."**
   - Hacking, Ian (2006). "Making Up People." *London Review of Books* 28(16), 17 August 2006. https://www.lrb.co.uk/the-paper/v28/n16/ian-hacking/making-up-people
   - Hacking, Ian (1995). "The looping effects of human kinds," in Sperber, Premack & Premack (eds.), *Causal Cognition.* Oxford University Press.

## Exact quotes (verified)

- Ian Hacking, "Making Up People," LRB 2006 (verified against the linked page):
  > "They are moving targets because our investigations interact with them, and change them. And since they are changed, they are not quite the same kind of people as before. The target has moved. I call this the 'looping effect'. Sometimes, our sciences create kinds of people that in a certain sense did not exist before. I call this 'making up people'."

- Hacking, same essay, on the multiple-personality case (verified):
  > "First, a person had two or three personalities. Within a decade the mean number was 17."

## Do not overclaim

- Do not say diagnosis is "just" a social construct or arbitrary. Symptoms and suffering are real; thresholds organize real distributions. The claim is about where the cut falls, not whether anything is there.
- Do not imply the 1973 decision was made with no reasoning. Spitzer offered an explicit definitional argument; the point is that the argument was about values and definitions, not a laboratory finding.
- Do not present the referendum as the mechanism that removed the diagnosis. The Board of Trustees removed it in 1973; the 1974 referendum only ratified that decision against a challenge.
- Do not claim all psychiatric conditions are continuous. Some phenomena may be more discrete; depression and anxiety are the best-evidenced continua.
- Do not equate "chosen threshold" with "worthless." Thresholds are necessary for allocating scarce care.

## Flags

- The referendum tally is usually given as 5,854 to 3,810, with a small number of additional/abstaining ballots; exact secondary figures vary slightly. Bayer (1981) is the authoritative source.
- "Sociopathic personality disturbance" (DSM-I) vs. "sexual deviation" wording: DSM-I placed homosexuality under sociopathic personality disturbance as a sexual deviation; DSM-II (1968) listed it among "sexual deviations." Descriptions in secondary sources compress this; Drescher (2015) is reliable.
- The PHQ-9 ≥10 cut-point is the most cited but not universal; Manea et al. (2012) support a range of 8–11. Present ≥10 as "commonly used," not canonical.
- Spitzer's exact 1973 wording exists in APA position documents; the cleanest citable statement of the distress/impairment criterion is his 1981 AJP reformulation, which I cite instead of an unverified 1973 quotation.

# Draft

In December 1973 the board of the American Psychiatric Association voted to stop classifying homosexuality as a mental illness, and no new fact about the body had arrived to justify the change. No study had located a marker in the blood or the brain that separated the sick from the well and then failed to find it in gay men. What changed was the association's judgment about what should count as a disorder in the first place, together with several years of pressure from activists and from psychiatrists who found the old category indefensible. A faction that wanted the diagnosis kept forced the question to the full membership, and in 1974 the members ratified the removal by 5,854 votes to 3,810. The boundary of one illness was redrawn, and it was redrawn by a show of hands.

That history is uncomfortable partly because it clashes with how most of us picture diagnosis. We imagine it as a discovery: a clinician examines a person, finds something that was already there, and names it, the way a radiologist finds a fracture on an X-ray. The fracture does not care what anyone votes. It is present or absent, and a second radiologist looking at the same film will usually agree. This picture is intuitive because much of medicine really does work this way. A blood culture grows a bacterium or it does not. A tumor has a margin you can point to. When we extend the same picture to psychiatry, we assume that "depressed" or "anxious" or "disordered" names a fact about a person that the diagnosis merely uncovers.

The difficulty is that most psychiatric symptoms do not come in the two clean states that the fracture model needs. They come in degrees. Low mood, worry, difficulty concentrating, trouble sleeping, loss of interest — each of these runs smoothly across a population from none through mild and moderate to severe, with people spread all along the range and no gap in the middle that separates a healthy group from an ill one. When researchers have looked directly for such a gap in depression, using statistical methods designed to detect a hidden boundary between two natural classes, they have generally not found one; the data look like a single continuous dimension rather than two kinds of people. Suffering is real and often severe, but it is graded, and nature does not mark the point on the scale where ordinary unhappiness becomes major depressive disorder. The same holds for the diagnostic rules themselves. The standard definition of major depression asks for at least five symptoms out of nine, present for at least two weeks. Someone with four symptoms for thirteen days is, by the rule, well; a neighbor with five for fourteen days is ill; and when researchers have tested nearby cutoffs, requiring four symptoms or three weeks, the alternatives capture people just as impaired as the ones the official rule selects. The five-and-two threshold organizes the continuum sensibly, but it does not sit at a seam in nature.

Since the distribution is continuous and a clinician still has to decide whether to treat, someone has to choose a cut-point. That choice is where the value judgment enters. Consider the PHQ-9, a nine-item questionnaire used across primary care to screen for depression. Its score runs from 0 to 27, and a common convention treats a score of 10 or above as a probable case worth acting on. There is nothing in the arithmetic that singles out 10. It was selected because, in validation studies, it catches a reasonable share of the people a clinician would call depressed while not flagging too many who are merely having a hard week. Move the line down to 8 and you catch more genuine cases and also more people who would have recovered on their own; move it up to 12 and you miss fewer well people and also more who needed help. Every position on that scale trades one kind of error against another, and which error you would rather make — over-treating the mild or overlooking the serious — is a question about values, about what a health system owes people and what it can afford, not a question that the symptom scores can answer by themselves.

The homosexuality case is a stark version of the same move, stripped of the numbers. When Robert Spitzer, the psychiatrist who did the most to broker the 1973 change, argued for removal, his case was definitional. He proposed that a condition should count as a psychiatric disorder only if it regularly causes the person subjective distress or produces a general impairment in how they function. Homosexuality, on its own, did neither for a great many people; the distress that did occur usually traced to social hostility rather than to the orientation. Grant the definition and the diagnosis has to go, because the thing it named does not clear the bar the definition sets. The debate in 1973 was a debate about where to put the bar, and once the profession moved it, a whole class of people crossed from "ill" to "well" without any of them changing at all. The category had in fact been moving for decades: the first DSM in 1952 filed homosexuality under "sociopathic personality disturbance," the 1968 edition moved it among the "sexual deviations," the 1973 vote removed it and substituted a narrower diagnosis for people distressed by their orientation, and that residue was finally deleted in 1987. At every step the underlying human beings were the same; what changed was the decision about which of them counted as sick.

Once a decision like that is made, it does not sit quietly as a description. The philosopher of science Ian Hacking spent much of his career on what happens after a category is applied to people, and he argued that classifying people changes them in a way that classifying rocks does not. "They are moving targets because our investigations interact with them, and change them," he wrote of the kinds of people the human sciences invent. "And since they are changed, they are not quite the same kind of people as before. The target has moved. I call this the 'looping effect'." A person told they have a condition may come to understand their own moods, choices, and history through it, may seek out others with the same label, may adjust what they expect of themselves and what clinicians expect of them. Those changes feed back into the category, altering who fits it and what it seems to mean. Hacking's most vivid case was multiple personality: as the diagnosis spread through the 1980s the number of "alters" patients reported climbed steeply, until "first, a person had two or three personalities" and "within a decade the mean number was 17," each new expectation shaping the next round of patients. A diagnosis is a description that acts on the thing it describes.

Put the continuum, the chosen cut-point, and the looping effect together and it becomes clear why the label is never inert. A threshold sorts a graded population into the treated and the untreated. The people it flags are then handled, and come to regard themselves, as cases. That self-understanding and that handling change the population, which changes what the category picks out the next time it is applied. The line was drawn by a decision, and the drawing of it reaches back and reshapes the very distribution it was meant to divide.

This is the point at which automated screening stops being a footnote and becomes the main event. A tool that reads a questionnaire, or a stream of behavior — how someone types, sleeps, moves, or phrases their messages — and returns a judgment about their mental health does not escape the threshold problem. It inherits a cut-point that some committee chose, hard-codes it, and applies it to millions of people who never sat with a clinician who might have weighed their particular circumstances. The value judgment that a human clinician makes case by case, and can be argued with, is made once, invisibly, and then repeated at scale as though it were a reading off an instrument. The tool presents its output with the flat confidence of a thermometer, and its users are strongly inclined to treat a number from a machine as a discovery rather than a policy. Worse, the loop runs faster and wider. When a system flags a person as at risk, that flag can route them to services, change how a platform treats them, or shift how they see themselves, at a volume and speed no clinic could match, and the aggregate of those flagged responses becomes new training data that hardens the original threshold into apparent fact.

None of this is an argument against drawing lines. A health system with finite clinicians and finite hours cannot treat a continuum; it has to decide whom to see this week and whom to reassure, and a threshold is how that decision gets made consistently rather than by whim. Cut-points let different clinics compare cases, let trials define who was enrolled, and let a person be told something more useful than "your distress is somewhere on a scale." The problem is not that psychiatry uses thresholds. The problem is forgetting that it chose them. A threshold remembered as a decision can be revisited when the evidence or the values behind it shift, the way the line around homosexuality was revisited and then erased. A threshold mistaken for a discovery hardens into a fact about people that no vote seems entitled to touch, and that is the more dangerous error, because it hides a choice that someone made and that someone else has to live inside. The 1973 vote is worth remembering not as an embarrassment but as an unusually honest moment, when the profession admitted out loud that the boundary of an illness was theirs to move.
