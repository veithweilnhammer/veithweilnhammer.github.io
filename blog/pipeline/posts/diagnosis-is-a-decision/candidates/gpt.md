# Evidence

Thesis: The line between health and illness in psychiatry is set by a threshold that society chooses rather than found in nature, and diagnostic labels then reshape the people they name, which makes automated screening a value-laden act at scale.

Do not overclaim:

- The argument does not show that psychiatric diagnosis is arbitrary or useless; it shows that thresholds require judgment.
- The 1973 homosexuality case should not be presented as a pure vote on scientific truth; Drescher notes that the membership voted on whether to favor or oppose the Board’s decision and process.
- The removal of homosexuality did not instantly remove all pathologizing language; Sexual Orientation Disturbance remained in DSM-II, and Ego-Dystonic Homosexuality appeared in DSM-III before removal in DSM-III-R.
- Dimensional symptoms do not mean every diagnosis lacks biological grounding; they mean the health/illness boundary often needs a cut-point.
- AI screening tools do not create the value judgment from nothing; they inherit thresholds from training labels, questionnaires, triage protocols, and deployment policies.

Flags:

- Several sources report that the APA Board vote in December 1973 was 13-0 with 2 abstentions, but I did not find a directly accessible primary minutes scan during research, so I did not rely on the number in the draft.
- The exact DSM-II seventh-printing wording is available in scans and mirrored teaching PDFs, but because it is modern copyrighted material I did not quote it.
- The Hacking archive scan is a stable public scan of a copyrighted book chapter, so the draft paraphrases the concept and avoids direct quotation.

# Draft

Psychiatric diagnosis often looks like discovery because medical diagnosis usually teaches us to search for something already there. A chest X-ray can show a fracture, a blood culture can identify a bacterium, and a scan can reveal a tumour that was present before anyone named it. This model is powerful because the diagnosis appears to reveal a fact about the body that existed independently of the observer. It also shapes how people think about mental-health diagnosis, even when the evidence is built from words, behaviour, duration, distress, and impairment rather than a visible lesion.

Psychiatry has real discoveries. A delirious patient may have an infection, an intoxication, a seizure disorder, or a metabolic problem that requires urgent medical treatment. Some psychiatric symptoms cluster with genetic risk, brain development, sleep, inflammation, medication effects, and life events in ways that science can study. The difficulty begins at the boundary where ordinary variation becomes illness. Anxiety, sadness, suspiciousness, impulsivity, low energy, and grief all occur in the general population. They vary by degree, context, duration, and consequence. A diagnostic system has to decide when these experiences cross a threshold that justifies a medical label.

That threshold is a decision about harm and need. The DSM definition of mental disorder turns on clinically significant disturbance and on distress or disability in work, relationships, and other areas of life. The word “significant” does important work. It asks how much suffering, impairment, risk, or loss of control should count. It also asks which kinds of deviation from local expectations should be treated as illness and which should be treated as part of human difference. No laboratory instrument can answer that question on its own, because the answer depends on what a community regards as unacceptable suffering, impaired functioning, or legitimate grounds for care.

This is easiest to see when a continuous score becomes a yes-or-no result. The PHQ-9, a common depression questionnaire, gives a score from 0 to 27. In the original validation study, a cut-off of 10 or higher had 88% sensitivity and 88% specificity for major depression against an independent interview. That is useful information, but the cut-off still chooses a balance. A lower threshold catches more people who might need help and also flags more people whose symptoms would not meet a diagnostic interview. A higher threshold does the reverse. The score is measured; the boundary is selected.

The same problem appears throughout psychiatry because many symptoms sit on continua. A person can sleep badly for three nights, three weeks, or three months. A fear can be unpleasant, avoidance-producing, or life-narrowing. Suspicious thoughts can be understandable in context, excessive but contained, or part of a psychotic episode. The diagnostic act compresses this graded information into a category that carries consequences: eligibility for care, sick leave, medication, insurance reimbursement, legal protections, stigma, and a new way for the person to understand what has happened.

The 1973 removal of homosexuality from the DSM is the clearest historical example because the boundary of illness moved in public. DSM-I classified homosexuality under sociopathic personality disturbance, and DSM-II classified it under sexual deviations. After years of activism, internal APA debate, and growing research that challenged the pathological view, the APA Board of Trustees modified DSM-II in December 1973. The general category of homosexuality was eliminated and replaced with Sexual Orientation Disturbance, a narrower category directed at distress about one’s orientation. A later referendum among APA members upheld the Board’s decision by a 58% majority of about 10,000 voting members.

No new blood test discovered in 1973 that homosexuality had crossed from disease into health. No imaging result showed that the previous classification had been a lesion and the revised classification was normal anatomy. The relevant evidence included research, testimony, changing social norms, arguments about stigma, and a reconsideration of what psychiatry should call disorder. The manual changed because the profession revised the boundary between human variation and illness. That change was ethically necessary, and it also exposes the structure of the problem: a diagnostic category can carry the authority of medicine while resting partly on social judgments about harm, function, and acceptable difference.

This example is sometimes summarized too quickly as politics defeating science, which misses the harder point. Science mattered because research weakened the claim that homosexuality was inherently associated with psychological dysfunction. Politics also mattered because activists forced the profession to confront the harm produced by the label and the values hidden inside its diagnostic criteria. The decision became possible when the relevant question shifted from whether homosexuality departed from a social norm to whether it involved the kind of distress or impairment that psychiatry should treat as disorder. The category changed when the threshold changed.

The change also shows why diagnostic labels are more than descriptions. Ian Hacking used the phrase “looping effects” for a feature of human classifications: people respond to the categories applied to them, and those responses can alter the category itself. A chemical element does not change its conduct because chemists name it. A person classified as depressed, autistic, addicted, traumatised, or disordered can read the label, accept it, resist it, seek communities around it, receive different treatment from others, and reinterpret past experiences through it. The label then becomes part of the phenomenon it was meant to describe.

This feedback can help. A diagnosis can give a person language for a pattern that previously felt chaotic. It can reduce blame, open access to treatment, and make suffering legible to family, employers, schools, and health systems. It can also narrow a person’s sense of agency, make transient distress feel permanent, or encourage institutions to see the label before the individual. The same category can relieve one person and burden another. Hacking’s point matters because it prevents a simple view of diagnosis as a tag attached to an unchanged object.

Automated mental-health screening makes this threshold problem more consequential. A human clinician can, at least in principle, treat a threshold as a guide rather than a verdict. They can notice context, ask what changed, compare current symptoms with the person’s baseline, and decide that a score should prompt conversation rather than a label. A screening model has to operationalise the boundary. It needs a target label, a risk score, or a cut-off that converts uncertainty into an action: flag, escalate, reassure, notify, schedule, deny, or ignore.

Once that cut-off is built into software, it can look more objective than it is. A questionnaire score, cursor pattern, voice feature, search history, sleep trace, or chatbot transcript may produce a number with several decimal places. The precision of the output can hide the judgment that created the category being predicted. If the system is trained to identify “depression,” “risk,” or “crisis,” it inherits earlier decisions about who counted as a case, who counted as a control, and which level of distress justified intervention. Automation can then apply that inherited boundary across thousands or millions of people with a consistency that feels like neutrality.

Consistency is valuable when the threshold is well chosen and the pathway after screening is humane. It is dangerous when the threshold is poorly matched to the setting. A school, employer, insurer, platform, primary-care clinic, and crisis service do not face the same costs of false positives and false negatives. A low threshold may be appropriate when the next step is a private offer of support with good follow-up. The same threshold may be harmful if it triggers surveillance, exclusion, emergency escalation, or a permanent record. The statistical problem and the ethical problem cannot be separated, because the meaning of an error depends on what happens after the flag.

The label can also loop back faster in automated systems. A person told by an app that they are at high risk may become frightened, reassured, ashamed, validated, defensive, or dependent on repeated checking. A chatbot that repeatedly names a user’s distress as a disorder may change how the user narrates ordinary setbacks. A passive monitoring system that alerts a clinician, parent, or institution can alter how others treat the person, which then alters behaviour and future data. The model may later treat that changed behaviour as confirmation that the original label was correct.

Thresholds remain necessary. Health systems need categories because care, rights, benefits, and responsibilities have to be allocated. A person in a psychotic episode needs help even if psychosis lies on a spectrum with ordinary unusual beliefs. A person unable to work because of depression needs documentation as well as sympathy. Screening can bring people into care earlier, especially when they cannot find the words for what is happening or when clinicians are scarce. The honest position is that thresholds are necessary tools whose placement should remain visible, argued about, and revised.

The practical implication is straightforward. Any psychiatric screening system, human or automated, should name the decision it is making. It should distinguish symptom measurement from diagnosis, risk estimation from certainty, and a screening flag from a settled account of a person. It should be evaluated at the threshold where it will actually be used, with attention to who benefits, who is burdened, and what loop the label creates after it is delivered. A model that silently turns continuous distress into a categorical label is making a social decision while presenting it as technical output.

Psychiatric diagnosis is most honest when it admits this structure. The world gives us suffering, behaviour, risk, impairment, and difference in graded forms. Medicine gives us labels that can organise care. Society chooses where some of those labels begin. The task is to choose those boundaries carefully, keep them revisable, and remember that once a label is applied to a person, it becomes part of the situation it describes.
