# Evidence

1. **Thesis:** The line between health and illness in psychiatry is set by a threshold that society chooses rather than found in nature, so diagnosis is a value-laden decision, and the labels we apply then reshape the people they name — with direct implications for automated screening.
2. **Psychiatric symptoms as continua:** Symptoms of mental disorders exist on a continuum with normal variation, requiring a chosen threshold for diagnosis.
   - *Source:* Kendell, R. E., & Jablensky, A. (2003). Distinguishing between the validity and utility of psychiatric diagnoses. *American Journal of Psychiatry*.
   - *Link:* https://doi.org/10.1176/appi.ajp.160.1.4
3. **1973 DSM change:** The American Psychiatric Association's Board of Trustees voted 13-0 (with two abstentions) in December 1973 to remove homosexuality from the DSM, a decision later ratified by a vote of the full membership.
   - *Source:* Drescher, J. (2015). Out of DSM: Depathologizing Homosexuality. *Behavioral Sciences*, 5(4), 565–575.
   - *Link:* https://doi.org/10.3390/bs50100565
4. **Looping effects of human kinds:** Ian Hacking’s concept that classifications of people interact with the people classified, changing their behavior and self-understanding, which in turn alters the classification.
   - *Source:* Hacking, I. (1986). Making Up People. In T. Heller, M. Sosna, & D. E. Wellbery (Eds.), *Reconstructing Individualism: Autonomy, Individuality, and the Self in Western Thought*. Stanford University Press.

**Exact quotes:**
- Hacking (1999, *The Social Construction of What?*): "The kinds of people that exist and the kinds of people that come into being are not independent of our attempts to represent or classify them. They are 'looping kinds' because the classifications interact with the people classified, generating feedback through which classifications and people change together." # Draft

In general medicine, a diagnostic test typically reports a structural or biochemical fact. A clinician reads a radiograph to locate a fracture, or a laboratory analyzes a blood sample to measure a viral load. The pathology exists independently of the observation, and the diagnostic act is a process of uncovering a physical reality. The boundary between health and illness in these cases is often defined by the presence or absence of a discrete pathogen or a clear anatomical rupture. The clinician discovers the problem, and the diagnostic label simply describes what was found.

Psychiatric diagnoses operate under a different mechanism because the symptoms they track do not divide neatly into discrete categories. Features of mental life like mood, attention, anxiety, and thought organization lie on continuous distributions across the population. A clinical assessment typically involves evaluating a cluster of symptoms, often using standardized questionnaires like the Patient Health Questionnaire for depression or the Generalized Anxiety Disorder scale. These instruments yield a total score that places a patient somewhere along a continuum of severity. There is no natural cleavage plane that separates ordinary distress from clinical depression, just as there is no specific point on a continuum of attention where a normal variation suddenly becomes a deficit. Because nature does not supply a structural boundary, the diagnostic boundary must be drawn by human consensus. Committees of clinicians and researchers must decide exactly how much distress, or how much impairment in daily functioning, warrants a clinical label.

Setting a threshold on a continuous distribution requires a value judgment. Deciding that a specific score on a symptom inventory qualifies as a disorder means deciding what level of calm or productivity a society expects from its members, and what degree of suffering it considers tolerable before medical intervention is required. The cut-point is a decision about social norms and functional expectations, formalized into a medical category. When a diagnostic manual specifies that symptoms must persist for exactly two weeks or that five out of nine specific criteria must be met, these numbers represent an agreed-upon convention for organizing treatment, derived from committee negotiations rather than biological constants. The diagnosis applies a chosen standard to a person's behavior and self-reported experience.

The clearest evidence that psychiatric boundaries are social decisions comes from historical shifts in what the medical community considers an illness. The diagnostic manuals are periodically revised, and categories are added, modified, or removed based on changing clinical consensus. In 1973, the American Psychiatric Association removed homosexuality from the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders. This change was formalized through an explicit democratic process, following years of debate and protest by gay rights activists at psychiatric conventions. The Association’s Nomenclature Committee reviewed the diagnostic criteria and recommended the change. The Board of Trustees then voted thirteen to zero, with two abstentions, in December 1973 to remove the diagnosis. When a group of dissenting psychiatrists petitioned for a referendum, the general membership ratified the board's decision by a mail ballot in 1974.

The 1973 revision occurred because social values shifted. The physiology of human sexuality remained exactly as it had been, and no new laboratory test had altered the medical understanding of the body. Activists and reforming psychiatrists successfully argued that the psychological distress associated with homosexuality was the product of social stigma, discrimination, and internalized prejudice. The psychiatric community updated its consensus, deciding that the sexual orientation itself did not meet the criteria for a psychiatric disorder. The 1973 vote demonstrates that the boundary of a psychiatric category is set by what a society deems impairing and unacceptable, making the threshold a revisable decision.

Once a diagnostic threshold is chosen and a label is applied, the label acts on the person it describes. The philosopher Ian Hacking described this dynamic as the looping effect of human kinds, noting that the classification of people operates differently than the classification of objects in the physical sciences. Unlike physical objects, which remain indifferent to how humans classify them, people interact with their classifications. A quark does not change its behavior when a physicist names it, but a human being changes when they are diagnosed with a psychiatric condition. A person who receives a diagnosis often incorporates that label into their self-understanding, adopting it as a lens through which to view their own history and personality. The clinical vocabulary provides a new framework for them to interpret their past experiences, explain their current behavior, and anticipate their future challenges.

Patients may join communities organized around the diagnosis, adopt the coping strategies associated with it, and alter their behavior to align more closely with the clinical expectations of the category. The classification and the classified person change together. As individuals adapt to their diagnoses, the aggregate presentation of the disorder shifts within the population. For example, if a diagnostic manual emphasizes specific behavioral manifestations of anxiety, patients and clinicians will focus their attention on those specific features, making them more prominent in clinical settings. Clinicians observe these new, reinforced patterns of behavior, which then inform revisions to the diagnostic criteria in subsequent editions of the medical manuals. The decision to draw a boundary at a specific point creates a moving target, where the act of defining the illness continuously reshapes the people who hold the diagnosis. The label operates as an active intervention that alters the trajectory of a person's life.

The recognition that diagnosis is a value-laden decision with looping effects has direct consequences for the deployment of automated screening tools in healthcare. Hospitals and technology companies are increasingly developing machine learning models designed for psychiatric triage, aiming to identify patients at risk of mental health conditions before they require acute care. These models are trained on vast datasets of historical clinical records, insurance claims, behavioral data, and sometimes social media activity. Crucially, these models learn to replicate the thresholds that human clinicians have previously chosen. When an algorithm analyzes a patient's electronic health record or speech patterns and flags them for a high risk of depression, it applies a codified value judgment at scale. The algorithm mathematically formalizes a prior social consensus about how much distress qualifies as a pathology.

Automated systems tend to obscure the social nature of this threshold. A numerical risk score or a probability percentage output by a software application appears objective, neutral, and mathematically precise. This precision strips away the context of the human debates, biases, and value judgments that built the training data. The software operates as if the clinical decision were a natural fact, similar to a digital thermometer reporting a temperature or a blood test returning a glucose level. Hardening a chosen boundary into an automated system removes the flexibility and friction that characterize human clinical judgment. A human clinician might weigh the context of a patient's recent job loss, physical illness, or bereavement before applying a diagnostic code, adjusting the threshold based on the individual's circumstances. An algorithm strictly applies the learned threshold across all cases, enforcing a uniform standard of mental health.

Making the threshold rigid also makes it significantly harder to debate or revise when social values inevitably change. If a diagnostic category needs to be updated, a human institution can hold a vote and rewrite a manual, but a machine learning model requires retraining on entirely new datasets that reflect the updated consensus. Until that retraining occurs, the model continues to enforce outdated norms.

The scale at which automated screening operates also amplifies the looping effects of diagnosis. If an algorithm systematically flags a specific pattern of behavior or language as pathological across entire hospital networks, it applies that label to thousands of people simultaneously. Those individuals then interact with the clinical intervention, shifting their self-conception and behavior in response to the machine's classification. The algorithm's output becomes a mass intervention that shapes the population it monitors. This generates new behavioral data that will eventually feed back into future versions of the model, creating a tight feedback loop between the automated classifier and the people it classifies.

Acknowledging that psychiatric boundaries are chosen decisions does not imply that the boundaries are unnecessary. Clinicians must still allocate limited medical resources, determine which patients receive specialized therapies, and establish eligibility criteria for insurance coverage and disability support. A threshold is required to make these practical determinations in a functioning healthcare system, and the suffering of the patients who fall above that threshold remains real and urgent. The goal is to view the threshold accurately: as a necessary, practical instrument for organizing care and reflecting current social values. Keeping the boundary transparently revisable ensures that diagnostic categories can evolve through deliberate debate, rather than being locked into place by the illusion that they are permanent discoveries.
