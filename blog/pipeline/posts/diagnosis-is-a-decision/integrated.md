# Evidence

**Thesis:** The line between health and illness in psychiatry is set by a threshold that society chooses rather than one found in nature, so diagnosis is a value-laden decision about where distress becomes disorder; once applied, the label reshapes the people it names, and automated screening inherits that chosen threshold and applies it at scale.

## Continuous symptoms require a chosen cut-point

1. **Psychiatric symptoms and traits are dimensional, so categorical diagnosis depends on where a cut-point is placed.**
   - Widiger, T. A., & Samuel, D. B. (2005). Diagnostic categories or dimensions? A question for the DSM-V. *Journal of Abnormal Psychology*, 114(4), 494–504. https://doi.org/10.1037/0021-843X.114.4.494
   - Kendell, R. E., & Jablensky, A. (2003). Distinguishing between the validity and utility of psychiatric diagnoses. *American Journal of Psychiatry*, 160(1), 4–12. https://doi.org/10.1176/appi.ajp.160.1.4

2. **Depression in particular looks like a single continuous dimension rather than two natural classes, and nearby DSM cutoffs (e.g., four symptoms or three weeks instead of five and two) capture people just as impaired as the official rule.**
   - Kendler, K. S., & Gardner, C. O. (1998). Boundaries of major depression: an evaluation of DSM-IV criteria. *American Journal of Psychiatry*, 155(2), 172–177. https://doi.org/10.1176/ajp.155.2.172
   - Ruscio, J., & Ruscio, A. M. (2000). Informing the continuity controversy: a taxometric analysis of depression. *Journal of Abnormal Psychology*, 109(3), 473–487. https://doi.org/10.1037/0021-843X.109.3.473

3. **The concept of mental disorder combines a fact about dysfunction with a social judgment about harm; the DSM defines disorder partly through "clinically significant" distress, disability, or impairment, so the boundary depends on a significance threshold.**
   - Wakefield, J. C. (1992). The concept of mental disorder: on the boundary between biological facts and social values. *American Psychologist*, 47(3), 373–388. https://doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.47.3.373
   - American Psychiatric Association (2013). *DSM-5*; summarized in Stein et al. (2021), "What is a mental disorder? An exemplar-focused approach." https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8161428/

## Screening thresholds encode the chosen value

4. **A continuous score becomes a categorical flag at a chosen cut-point. In the original PHQ-9 validation study, a score of ≥10 gave 88% sensitivity and 88% specificity for major depression against an independent interview.**
   - Kroenke, K., Spitzer, R. L., & Williams, J. B. W. (2001). The PHQ-9: validity of a brief depression severity measure. *Journal of General Internal Medicine*, 16(9), 606–613. https://doi.org/10.1046/j.1525-1497.2001.016009606.x

5. **The ≥10 cut-point is commonly used but not canonical; a meta-analysis supports an optimal range of roughly 8–11, and each position trades sensitivity against specificity.**
   - Manea, L., Gilbody, S., & McMillan, D. (2012). Optimal cut-off score for diagnosing depression with the PHQ-9: a meta-analysis. *CMAJ*, 184(3), E191–E196. https://doi.org/10.1503/cmaj.110829

6. **Screening thresholds require institutional handling after a positive result: the USPSTF recommends adult depression screening only where systems exist for diagnostic confirmation, treatment, and follow-up.**
   - US Preventive Services Task Force (2023). Screening for Depression and Suicide Risk in Adults. https://www.uspreventiveservicestaskforce.org/uspstf/recommendation/screening-depression-suicide-risk-adults

7. **Smartphone-based digital phenotyping makes automated mental-health screening plausible while raising governance problems around consent, privacy, and intervention.**
   - Onnela, J.-P., & Rauch, S. L. (2016). Harnessing smartphone-based digital phenotyping to enhance behavioral and mental health. *Neuropsychopharmacology*, 41, 1691–1696. https://doi.org/10.1038/nbt.3567

## The 1973 homosexuality decision

8. **Homosexuality's classification shifted across editions before removal: DSM-I (1952) placed it under "sociopathic personality disturbance"; DSM-II (1968) listed it among "sexual deviations."**
   - Drescher, J. (2015). Out of DSM: Depathologizing Homosexuality. *Behavioral Sciences*, 5(4), 565–575. https://doi.org/10.3390/bs5040565

9. **In December 1973 the APA Board of Trustees modified DSM-II, eliminating the general category of homosexuality and replacing it with "Sexual Orientation Disturbance." No new biological finding preceded the change; it followed activism and a reappraisal of what counts as a disorder.**
   - APA Foundation, "History of DSM and Homosexuality." https://www.apaf.org/library-archives/galleries/lgbtq-leaders/history-of-dsm-and-homosexuality/
   - Bayer, R. (1981). *Homosexuality and American Psychiatry: The Politics of Diagnosis*. Princeton University Press. https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691028378/homosexuality-and-american-psychiatry
   - Drescher (2015), as above.

10. **A dissenting faction forced a 1974 referendum of the full APA membership, which upheld the Board's decision by 5,854 to 3,810 (about a 58% majority of roughly 10,000 voters). The referendum ratified the removal; it was not the mechanism that removed the diagnosis.**
   - Bayer (1981); Drescher (2015), as above.

11. **The compromise category persisted and was negotiated further: "ego-dystonic homosexuality" appeared in DSM-III (1980) and was fully removed in DSM-III-R (1987).**
   - Drescher (2015), as above.

12. **Robert Spitzer's argument for removal was definitional: a condition should count as a disorder only if it regularly causes subjective distress or generalized impairment in functioning; homosexuality per se met neither for many people, and associated distress usually traced to social stigma. The debate was about where the line falls, not about a new fact of biology.**
   - Spitzer, R. L. (1981). The diagnostic status of homosexuality in DSM-III: a reformulation of the issues. *American Journal of Psychiatry*, 138(2), 210–215. https://doi.org/10.1176/ajp.138.2.210
   - Bayer (1981); Drescher (2015).

13. **Bayer frames the controversy as a conflict over social values, the definition of disease, and sexuality, not a laboratory discovery.**
   - Bayer (1981), as above.

## Looping effects

14. **Ian Hacking's "looping effect": classifying people changes how they understand and conduct themselves, which feeds back and alters the category, so a human kind is a "moving target." Unlike physical objects, people interact with their classifications.**
   - Hacking, I. (1995). The looping effects of human kinds. In Sperber, Premack & Premack (eds.), *Causal Cognition: A Multidisciplinary Debate*. Oxford University Press.
   - Hacking, I. (1986). Making Up People. In Heller, Sosna & Wellbery (eds.), *Reconstructing Individualism*. Stanford University Press.

**Exact quotes (verified against the source page — Hacking, "Making Up People," *London Review of Books* 28(16), 17 August 2006, https://www.lrb.co.uk/the-paper/v28/n16/ian-hacking/making-up-people):**
- "They are moving targets because our investigations interact with them, and change them. And since they are changed, they are not quite the same kind of people as before. The target has moved. I call this the 'looping effect'. Sometimes, our sciences create kinds of people that in a certain sense did not exist before. I call this 'making up people'."
- On the multiple-personality case: "First, a person had two or three personalities. Within a decade the mean number was 17."

## Do not overclaim

- The argument does not say diagnosis is arbitrary, "just" a social construct, or worthless. Symptoms and suffering are real and often severe; the claim is about where the cut falls, not whether anything is there.
- The 1973 case should not be told as politics defeating science: research had weakened the claim that homosexuality was inherently associated with dysfunction, and Spitzer offered an explicit definitional argument. It also should not be told as science alone: activism forced the profession to confront the values inside its criteria.
- The referendum ratified the Board's decision; it was not what removed the diagnosis.
- Dimensional symptoms do not mean every diagnosis lacks biological grounding, and not all conditions are continuous; depression and anxiety are the best-evidenced continua.
- AI screening tools do not invent the value judgment; they inherit thresholds from training labels, questionnaires, triage protocols, and deployment policies.

# Draft

In December 1973 the board of the American Psychiatric Association voted to stop classifying homosexuality as a mental illness, and no new fact about the body had arrived to justify the change. No study had located a marker in the blood or the brain that separated the sick from the well and then failed to find it in gay men. What changed was the association's judgment about what should count as a disorder, together with several years of pressure from activists and from psychiatrists who found the old category indefensible. A faction that wanted the diagnosis kept forced the question to the full membership, and in 1974 the members upheld the removal by 5,854 votes to 3,810. The boundary of one illness was redrawn by a show of hands.

That history is uncomfortable partly because it clashes with how most of us picture diagnosis. We imagine it as a discovery: a clinician examines a person, finds something that was already there, and names it, the way a radiologist finds a fracture on an X-ray. The fracture does not care what anyone votes. It is present or absent, and a second radiologist looking at the same film will usually agree. Much of medicine really does work this way: a blood culture grows a bacterium or it does not, and a tumor has a margin you can point to. When we carry the same picture into psychiatry, we assume that "depressed" or "anxious" or "disordered" names a fact about a person that the diagnosis merely uncovers.

The difficulty is that most psychiatric symptoms do not come in the two clean states the fracture model needs. They come in degrees. Low mood, worry, difficulty concentrating, trouble sleeping, and loss of interest each run smoothly across a population from none through mild and moderate to severe, with people spread all along the range and no gap in the middle that separates a healthy group from an ill one. When researchers have looked directly for such a gap in depression, using statistical methods designed to detect a hidden boundary between two natural classes, they have generally not found one; the data look like a single continuous dimension rather than two kinds of people. Suffering is real and often severe, but it is graded, and nature does not mark the point on the scale where ordinary unhappiness becomes major depressive disorder. The diagnostic rules inherit the same problem. The standard definition of major depression asks for at least five symptoms out of nine, present for at least two weeks. Someone with four symptoms for thirteen days is, by the rule, well; a neighbor with five for fourteen days is ill; and when researchers have tested nearby cutoffs, requiring four symptoms or three weeks, the alternatives capture people just as impaired as the ones the official rule selects. The five-and-two threshold organizes the continuum sensibly, but it does not sit at a seam in nature.

Since the distribution is continuous and a clinician still has to decide whether to treat, someone has to choose a cut-point, and that choice is where the value judgment enters. Consider the PHQ-9, a nine-item questionnaire used across primary care to screen for depression. Its score runs from 0 to 27, and a common convention treats a score of 10 or above as a probable case worth acting on. In the study that validated it, that cut-off caught about 88 percent of the people a diagnostic interview would call depressed while correctly clearing about 88 percent of those it would not. There is nothing in the arithmetic that singles out 10; a later meta-analysis found that any value from roughly 8 to 11 performs about as well. Move the line down and you catch more genuine cases and also more people who would have recovered on their own; move it up and you miss fewer well people and also more who needed help. Which error you would rather make — over-treating the mild or overlooking the serious — is a question about what a health system owes people and what it can afford, not one the symptom scores can answer by themselves.

The homosexuality case is a stark version of the same move, stripped of the numbers. When Robert Spitzer, the psychiatrist who did the most to broker the 1973 change, argued for removal, his case was definitional. He proposed that a condition should count as a psychiatric disorder only if it regularly causes the person subjective distress or produces a general impairment in how they function. Homosexuality, on its own, did neither for a great many people, and the distress that did occur usually traced to social hostility rather than to the orientation. Grant the definition and the diagnosis has to go. The debate in 1973 was a debate about where to put that bar, and once the profession moved it, a whole class of people crossed from "ill" to "well" without any of them changing at all. The category had in fact been moving for decades: the first DSM in 1952 filed homosexuality under "sociopathic personality disturbance," the 1968 edition moved it among the "sexual deviations," the 1973 revision removed the general category and substituted a narrower diagnosis for people distressed by their orientation, and that residue was finally deleted in 1987. At every step the underlying human beings were the same; what changed was the decision about which of them counted as sick.

It would be too quick to read this as politics simply defeating science. Science mattered, because research had weakened the claim that homosexuality was inherently tied to psychological dysfunction. Activism mattered too, because it forced the profession to confront the harm the label produced and the values buried inside its criteria. The decision became possible when the question shifted from whether homosexuality departed from a social norm to whether it involved the kind of distress or impairment that psychiatry should treat as disorder. The category changed when the threshold changed.

Once a decision like that is made, it does not sit quietly as a description. The philosopher of science Ian Hacking argued that classifying people changes them in a way that classifying rocks does not. "They are moving targets because our investigations interact with them, and change them," he wrote of the kinds of people the human sciences invent. "And since they are changed, they are not quite the same kind of people as before. The target has moved. I call this the 'looping effect'." A quark does not alter its behavior when a physicist names it, but a person told they have a condition may come to understand their moods, choices, and history through it, may seek out others with the same label, and may adjust what they expect of themselves and what clinicians expect of them. Those changes feed back into the category, altering who fits it and what it seems to mean. Hacking's most vivid case was multiple personality: as the diagnosis spread through the 1980s the number of reported personalities climbed steeply, until, in his words, "first, a person had two or three personalities" and "within a decade the mean number was 17," each new expectation shaping the next round of patients. A diagnosis is a description that acts on the thing it describes.

This feedback is not always harmful. A diagnosis can give a person language for a pattern that previously felt chaotic, reduce self-blame, open access to treatment, and make suffering legible to family, employers, and health systems. It can also narrow a person's sense of what they can do, make a passing crisis feel permanent, or lead institutions to see the label before the individual. Put the continuum, the chosen cut-point, and the looping effect together, and the label is never inert. A threshold sorts a graded population into the treated and the untreated; the people it flags are then handled, and come to regard themselves, as cases; that handling and that self-understanding change the population, which changes what the category picks out the next time it is applied. The line was drawn by a decision, and the drawing of it reaches back and reshapes the distribution it was meant to divide.

This is the point at which automated screening stops being a footnote and becomes the main event. A tool that reads a questionnaire, or a stream of behavior — how someone types, sleeps, moves, or phrases their messages — and returns a judgment about their mental health inherits a cut-point that some committee chose, hard-codes it, and applies it to many thousands of people who never sat with a clinician who might have weighed their particular circumstances. A human clinician can treat a threshold as a guide rather than a verdict, noticing a recent job loss or a bereavement before applying a code. A model has to operationalize the boundary: it needs a target label and a cut-off that converts uncertainty into an action, whether to flag, escalate, reassure, or ignore. The value judgment a clinician makes case by case, and can be argued with, is made once, invisibly, and then repeated at scale.

Once that cut-off is built into software, it tends to look more objective than it is. A questionnaire score or a probability with several decimal places carries the flat confidence of a thermometer, and its users are strongly inclined to treat a number from a machine as a discovery rather than a policy. If the system was trained to identify "depression" or "risk," it inherits earlier decisions about who counted as a case, who counted as a control, and how much distress justified intervention, and then applies that inherited boundary with a consistency that feels like neutrality. Consistency helps when the threshold is well chosen and the pathway after a positive result is humane; it does harm when the threshold is poorly matched to the setting. A school, an insurer, a hiring platform, a primary-care clinic, and a crisis line do not face the same costs of false positives and false negatives. A low threshold can be appropriate when the next step is a private, well-supported offer of help, and damaging when it triggers surveillance, exclusion, or a permanent record. The statistical choice and the ethical choice cannot be separated, because the meaning of an error depends on what happens after the flag.

Hardening the boundary also makes it harder to revise when values shift, as they did around homosexuality. A human institution can hold a vote and rewrite a manual; a model keeps enforcing the old norm until someone retrains it on data that reflect the new one. And the loop can run faster and wider than any clinic could manage. When a system flags a person as at risk, that flag can route them to services, change how a platform treats them, or shift how they see themselves, and the aggregate of those flagged responses becomes new training data that hardens the original threshold into apparent fact.

None of this is an argument against drawing lines. A health system with finite clinicians and finite hours cannot treat a continuum; it has to decide whom to see this week and whom to reassure, and a threshold is how that decision gets made consistently rather than by whim. Cut-points let clinics compare cases, let trials define who was enrolled, and let a person be told something more useful than that their distress lies somewhere on a scale. The honest position is that thresholds are necessary tools whose placement should stay visible and open to argument. A threshold remembered as a decision can be revisited when the evidence or the values behind it move, the way the line around homosexuality was moved and then erased. A threshold mistaken for a discovery hardens into a supposed fact about people that no vote seems entitled to touch, which is the more dangerous error, because it hides a choice that someone made and that someone else has to live inside. The 1973 vote is worth remembering not as an embarrassment but as an unusually honest moment, when a profession said out loud that the boundary of an illness was theirs to set.
