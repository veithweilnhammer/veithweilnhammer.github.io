# Brief: Regulate AI Therapy by Use, Not by Intent

## Slug
regulate-ai-therapy-by-use

## Working title
Regulate AI Therapy by Use, Not by Intent

## One-line angle (also the Substack subtitle / SEO hook)
A general-purpose chatbot becomes a mental-health product the moment people use it as one, so rules aimed at the marketing label miss the actual practice.

## Single argument (thesis)
AI mental-health regulation should be triggered by how people actually use a system, not by how it is marketed, because millions get de facto therapy from general chatbots that no therapy-specific law reaches.

## Concrete anchor (optional — a real example to ground the argument)
Illinois's WOPR Act (2025) bans AI systems from providing licensed "therapy," yet leaves general-purpose chatbots — where most people actually take their mental-health conversations — untouched. The gap between the labeled product and the used product is the whole problem.

## What this post must cover
- The core mismatch: laws written around the label "therapy" regulate intent and marketing, while harm and benefit follow use, and most mental-health use happens on tools marketed as general assistants.
- Concrete regulatory attempts and their coverage gaps: Illinois WOPR Act (2025), Utah and Nevada disclosure laws, the FTC inquiry into AI companion apps, and the EU AI Act's risk-tier approach.
- Why a use-based trigger is coherent: when a system is functioning as a mental-health product for a user, the same duties (safety testing, crisis handling, disclosure) should apply regardless of branding.
- The objections and limits: use is harder to define and enforce than a label; risks of over-reach onto general software; the free-speech and access concerns.
- Why banning the label can backfire — pushing vulnerable users toward wholly unregulated general tools instead of safer, supervised ones.
- What a use-based standard could look like in practice: thresholds, disclosure at the point of mental-health use, and crisis-escalation requirements.
