# Evidence

**Thesis:** AI mental-health regulation should be triggered by how a system is
actually used, not by how it is marketed, because the label-based laws now on the
books leave the largest venue of de facto therapy — general-purpose chatbots —
outside their reach.

## Laws and regulatory actions

1. **Illinois WOPR Act (2025).** The Wellness and Oversight for Psychological
   Resources Act (HB 1806 / Public Act 104-0054) prohibits providing,
   advertising, or offering therapy or psychotherapy in Illinois unless a
   licensed professional delivers the clinical judgment, while permitting AI for
   "administrative and supplementary support." Effective August 1, 2025; civil
   penalties up to $10,000 per violation, enforced by IDFPR.
   - Public Act 104-0054: https://www.ilga.gov/Legislation/PublicActs/View/104-0054
   - IDFPR release (Aug 4, 2025): https://idfpr.illinois.gov/news/2025/gov-pritzker-signs-state-leg-prohibiting-ai-therapy-in-il.html
   - National Law Review: https://natlawreview.com/article/illinois-becomes-first-state-regulate-use-ai-mental-health-therapy-services

2. **Utah HB 452 (2025).** Defines a "mental health chatbot" as generative AI
   that a supplier represents, or that a reasonable person would believe, can
   provide mental-health therapy; requires the chatbot to "clearly and
   conspicuously" disclose that it is AI and not a human, restricts advertising
   based on user inputs, and protects identifiable health information. Effective
   May 7, 2025; up to $2,500 per violation.
   - Utah HB 452 text: https://le.utah.gov/Session/2025/bills/introduced/HB0452.pdf
   - Wilson Sonsini summary: https://www.wsgr.com/en/insights/utah-enacts-mental-health-chatbot-law.html

3. **Nevada AB 406 (2025).** Bars AI systems "programmed to provide" professional
   mental or behavioral health care, and bars public schools from using AI for
   counselor or psychologist functions. Effective July 1, 2025; civil penalties
   up to $15,000.
   - Bill text: https://www.leg.state.nv.us/Session/83rd2025/Bills/AB/AB406_EN.pdf
   - Wilson Sonsini Data Advisor: https://www.wsgrdataadvisor.com/2025/06/nevada-passes-law-limiting-ai-use-for-mental-and-behavioral-healthcare/

4. **FTC 6(b) inquiry into AI companions (Sept 11, 2025).** Orders to seven
   companies — Alphabet, Character Technologies (Character.AI), Instagram, Meta,
   OpenAI, Snap, and xAI — seeking information on how they measure, test, and
   monitor impacts on children and teens, disclosures, data practices, and
   monetization. Framed around the companion function rather than a marketing
   label.
   - FTC press release: https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2025/09/ftc-launches-inquiry-ai-chatbots-acting-companions

5. **EU AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689).** Classifies systems by intended
   purpose into risk tiers. Article 50 requires that systems interacting directly
   with people disclose that the user is talking to AI; high-risk duties attach
   to listed contexts (e.g., emergency healthcare triage, AI in regulated medical
   devices); some uses such as emotion recognition in workplaces and schools are
   prohibited.
   - Overview: https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/
   - Article 50: https://ai-act-law.eu/article/50/

6. **APA health advisory (Nov 2025).** Warns that generative-AI chatbots and
   wellness apps should not substitute for qualified care; urges FTC action,
   safeguards, crisis support, privacy protection, and clinician involvement; and
   asks that companies be stopped from marketing chatbots as licensed therapists.
   - APA release: https://www.apa.org/news/press/releases/2025/11/ai-wellness-apps-mental-health

## Scale of use

7. **OpenAI (Oct 27, 2025).** Reported that about 0.15% of ChatGPT's weekly users
   have conversations with explicit indicators of potential suicidal planning or
   intent, and about 0.07% show possible signs of psychosis or mania. Against a
   company-reported base near 800 million weekly users, 0.15% is more than a
   million people per week. OpenAI said it worked with 170+ mental-health
   clinicians on the update.
   - OpenAI: https://openai.com/index/strengthening-chatgpt-responses-in-sensitive-conversations/
   - TechCrunch: https://techcrunch.com/2025/10/27/openai-says-over-a-million-people-talk-to-chatgpt-about-suicide-weekly/

8. **HBR use-case analysis (Zao-Sanders, Apr 2025).** Ranked "therapy /
   companionship" as the most common observed use of generative AI in 2025.
   *Flag: qualitative analysis of forum posts, not a representative survey.*
   - https://hbr.org/2025/04/how-people-are-really-using-gen-ai-in-2025

9. **Common Sense Media (2025).** Nationally representative survey of 1,060 teens:
   72% have used an AI companion, and about one-third have discussed something
   serious with an AI companion instead of a person. Category includes general
   tools used for personal conversation.
   - https://www.commonsensemedia.org/research/talk-trust-and-trade-offs-how-and-why-teens-use-ai-companions

10. **Bipartisan Policy Center survey.** About three in ten U.S. adults had used
    self-guided digital tools for mental health or well-being (nearly half of
    adults 18–44); among users, nearly half had used a general chatbot.
    - https://bipartisanpolicy.org/article/survey-shows-widespread-use-of-apps-and-chatbots-for-mental-health-support/

11. **JAMA Network Open (Cantor et al., 2025).** Nationally representative survey
    of 1,058 U.S. adolescents and young adults (12–21): about one in eight had
    used AI chatbots for mental-health advice, rising to roughly one in five among
    ages 18–21.
    - https://sph.brown.edu/news/2025-11-18/teens-ai-chatbots

12. **Australian community survey (Cross et al., 2024, JMIR Mental Health).**
    27.8% of respondents had used AI for mental-health purposes, commonly for
    quick support or as a personal therapist. *Flag: community/convenience
    sample.*
    - https://doi.org/10.2196/60589

13. **SAMHSA 2024 NSDUH.** 61.5 million U.S. adults had any mental illness in
    2024, and about 52% received mental-health treatment — the access gap that
    helps explain demand for AI support.
    - https://www.samhsa.gov/data/data-we-collect/nsduh-national-survey-drug-use-and-health/national-releases

## Do not overclaim

- General chatbots are not marketed as therapy. Their distance from these laws
  comes from disclaimers that they are not medical or professional care, not from
  any therapy branding.
- Illinois, Utah, and Nevada each cover a defined category and add real duties
  (Utah on data and advertising, Nevada a schools ban); the shared weakness is the
  trigger, not that the laws do nothing.
- "Most mental-health use happens on general tools" is directionally supported by
  the usage figures above, not established by a clean census.
- The EU AI Act does not ignore general-purpose AI; it applies transparency
  duties but exempts such systems from high-risk medical-device obligations unless
  intended for that use.
- A use-based trigger is not obviously easy to enforce or clearly constitutional;
  the objections below are real.
- The point is which systems the rules reach, not a verdict on whether chatbots
  are net helpful or harmful, and not a claim that general tools are always more
  dangerous than purpose-built ones.

## Flags

- The HBR "#1 use" ranking is the least-certain load-bearing figure (forum
  analysis, not a survey). The "over a million per week" figure rests on OpenAI's
  own 0.15% and its self-reported ~800M denominator.
- Illinois WOPR contains no enumerated "ChatGPT is exempt" clause; the coverage
  gap is a reading of its scope (it regulates services offered *as* therapy).
- Effective dates and penalty amounts come from statutory text and reputable
  legal summaries; exact figures should be spot-checked before publication. The
  APA advisory page was blocked on direct fetch, so wording beyond the title
  should be confirmed. EU AI Act tier specifics for mental-health uses are still
  evolving through implementation.

# Draft

In August 2025, Illinois made it illegal for an artificial-intelligence system to
act as a therapist. The Wellness and Oversight for Psychological Resources Act
forbids any company from providing, advertising, or offering psychotherapy in the
state unless a licensed human professional carries the clinical judgment, and it
sets civil penalties of up to $10,000 for each violation. The law lets AI handle
scheduling, billing, and records, and it stops a licensed therapist from handing
the core of treatment to a model. It was written to stop firms from selling an
unlicensed chatbot as a stand-in for a clinician, and on those terms it does what
it set out to do.

During the same months, the tool that most people actually bring their
mental-health conversations to went untouched. In October 2025 OpenAI reported
that about 0.15% of ChatGPT's weekly users have conversations that contain
explicit indicators of potential suicidal planning or intent, and about 0.07%
show possible signs of psychosis or mania. Set against a base the company puts
near 800 million weekly users, 0.15% is more than a million people every week
discussing suicide with a general-purpose assistant. A widely cited analysis of
how people described their use of generative AI in 2025 placed therapy and
companionship as the most common purpose of all, ahead of writing, coding, and
search, though that ranking comes from online examples rather than a
representative survey. None of this traffic runs through a product sold as
therapy, so a law that regulates the word "therapy" never reaches it.

The gap follows directly from how these laws decide what they cover. Illinois, and
the other statutes passed in 2025, key their rules to what a system is marketed or
held out to do. A product advertised as counseling triggers the duties; a product
advertised as a general assistant does not, even when a user spends an hour a
night describing panic attacks to it. Utah's mental-health chatbot law, effective
in May 2025, defines the systems it governs as those a supplier represents, or
that a reasonable person would take, to provide therapy for a mental-health
condition; it then requires them to disclose "clearly and conspicuously" that the
user is talking to a machine, restricts advertising based on user inputs, and
limits what companies may do with the resulting health data. Nevada's AB 406,
effective in July, bars AI systems "programmed to provide" professional mental or
behavioral health care and stops public schools from using AI to perform a
counselor's or psychologist's duties. The European Union's AI Act sorts systems
into risk tiers by their intended purpose, requires any system that interacts
directly with people to tell them they are talking to AI, and reserves its
stricter duties for listed uses such as emergency healthcare triage. Each of these
fixes on the declared purpose of the software, and each leaves a general assistant
that quietly absorbs the same conversations outside the frame.

The label is also something a company can manage. A product that wants to stay
clear of these rules can write into its terms of service that it offers general
assistance or entertainment and is not a substitute for professional care, and
that sentence, rather than anything about the actual exchanges, is what keeps it
outside the therapy statutes. The distance between the declared product and the
used product grows the more people lean on the tool. In a 2025 nationally
representative survey, 72% of American teenagers reported using an AI companion,
and about a third said they had taken something serious to one instead of to a
person. A Bipartisan Policy Center survey found that about three in ten U.S.
adults had used self-guided digital tools for mental health, and that nearly half
of those users had turned to a general chatbot. A survey of adolescents and young
adults reported by Brown University found about one in eight had used AI chatbots
for mental-health advice, rising to one in five among those aged 18 to 21. The
demand behind these numbers is easy to understand: SAMHSA estimated that 61.5
million U.S. adults had a mental illness in 2024 and only about half received any
treatment, and a chatbot is immediate, private in the ordinary sense, cheap, and
willing to continue at three in the morning when no clinic is open.

Two recent federal actions point the other way, toward what systems do rather than
what they claim. The Federal Trade Commission's September 2025 inquiry sent orders
to seven companies, including OpenAI, Meta, Alphabet, Character.AI, Snap, and xAI,
and it is built around the companion function and its effects on children: it asks
how those systems behave when a young user confides in them, how the companies
test for harm, and how they handle the data. The American Psychological
Association's November advisory urges federal action and still frames part of its
ask around marketing, since it wants companies stopped from presenting chatbots as
licensed therapists, but it does so because it has watched people use ordinary
chatbots for support and receive unpredictable, sometimes dangerous responses.
Both are reacting to what these systems do in use, and neither has yet produced a
rule that attaches to use.

Harm and benefit in this setting follow the conversation, not the marketing page.
A model that affirms a user's delusion, encourages food restriction in someone
with anorexia, or fumbles a disclosure of suicidal intent produces that effect
inside the exchange, and the effect is the same whether the app store page said
"therapy" or "helpful assistant." A supervised, tested tool that handles the same
disclosure by naming the risk and routing the person to help produces a different
effect, also inside the exchange. The label sits on a page that no distressed user
is reading at the moment that matters. What matters is what the system says back
when someone types that they cannot go on.

That is the case for a use-based trigger. When a system is functioning as a
mental-health product for a given user — when the exchange is a sustained one
about distress, self-harm, diagnosis, medication, or psychiatric symptoms — the
duties we already recognize for such products should attach to it, regardless of
how the product is branded. The million weekly conversations about suicide would
fall inside the rules rather than outside them, because the rule would follow the
conversation.

The objections to this are real, and the strongest is enforcement. A marketing
label is public and static, so a regulator can read it once and know what a
product claims. Use is private, continuous, and defined only as it happens, which
makes it far harder to observe and to adjudicate after the fact. A use-based
standard also risks sweeping in software that no one thinks of as a health
product, because a person can type a despairing sentence into a search box, a
note-taking app, or a coding assistant, and a poorly drawn rule would convert
every one of those into a regulated medical device the moment they did. There is a
speech dimension as well: regulating what a general model may say to a person in
distress limits expression, and a workable rule has to constrain product duties
rather than approved opinions. And there is an access dimension, since many people
reach for a chatbot precisely because it is free and immediate when formal care is
not.

The access concern is also where banning the label can work against its own goal.
A prohibition aimed at products that call themselves therapy leaves the
supervised, disclosed, crisis-tested tools carrying legal risk while the unlabeled
general assistant carries none. A cautious company withdraws the safer product to
avoid liability, and the demand does not disappear, so the vulnerable user
migrates to the tool with no guardrails at all — the general assistant optimized
for engagement and broad capability rather than for recognizing suicidal ideation
or routing someone to emergency resources. A rule written around the word can push
people toward exactly the tools the word was meant to protect them from.

A use-based standard would look different in a few concrete ways, and it would
scale the burden to the risk of the exchange. It would set a threshold of
sustained mental-health use, so that duties attach when a pattern of clinical
conversation appears rather than when a single sad message does; a user drafting
an email would trigger nothing, while a user relying on the same model for daily
anxiety management would. It would require disclosure at the point of that use —
the system stating plainly, when the exchange turns clinical, that it is AI, that
it is not a clinician, that an ordinary chatbot conversation lacks the
confidentiality of therapy, and that a crisis needs human help — rather than a
line buried in terms of service. It would keep the health information from those
conversations out of targeted advertising and engagement tuning, following Utah's
restriction on using mental-health inputs to shape ads. It would require safety
testing for the categories that carry the most risk — suicidal intent, self-harm,
and signs of psychosis or mania — across multi-turn conversations, since a system
can pass a single well-formed question and then drift into agreement over the
course of an hour. And it would require a crisis mode that surfaces resources and
encourages human contact when a user signals imminent risk. These duties would
apply to any system that crosses the threshold, whether it is sold as a wellness
app, a companion, or a general assistant.

Part of the enforcement objection softens on inspection. Systems at this scale
already classify these conversations, and OpenAI could only report the 0.15%
figure because its models detect when an exchange carries signs of suicidal
intent. A standard that required a system to act on the detection it already
performs — to disclose its nature, surface crisis resources, and change how it
responds once a conversation crosses into clinical territory — would hold
companies accountable for a capability they have built and measured rather than
for a marketing claim they can rewrite. The hard part is setting the threshold and
auditing behavior around it, which is genuine work, though it sits closer to how
consumer-safety rules already operate than the label-based approach admits. The
law already makes this move elsewhere: driving rules apply when a vehicle is
driven, and medical-device duties can attach when software performs a medical
function.

None of this makes a chatbot a good therapist, and none of it settles whether AI
belongs in mental-health care at all. The narrower point is about where a law
points. A rule aimed at the label is easy to draft and easy to route around, since
a company can change its marketing copy or never adopt the label in the first
place, and the same conversations continue outside the rule's reach. A rule aimed
at the function is harder to write and harder to enforce, and it has to be bounded
so it does not swallow ordinary software, but it reaches the place where the
benefit and the harm actually occur. When more than a million people a week are
taking their worst moments to a system that no therapy-specific law touches, the
marketing label is the least informative thing about what that system has become.
