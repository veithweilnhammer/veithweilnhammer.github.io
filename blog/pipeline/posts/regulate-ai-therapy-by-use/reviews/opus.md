# Review — opus

**Thesis (one sentence):** AI mental-health rules should trigger on how a system
is actually used, not on how it is marketed, because label-based laws miss the
general chatbots where most de facto therapy happens. The post develops this one
argument start to finish and does not branch. Good.

## Style-guide violations (fix these first)

The style guide's #1 hard ban is antithesis / "X, not Y" / "opposite trade"
parallelism. The draft leans on it repeatedly:

1. **"Harm and benefit in this setting follow the conversation, not the marketing
   page."** — Textbook banned "X, not Y." Fix: "Harm and benefit in this setting
   are produced inside the conversation. What the marketing page says has no
   bearing on them."

2. **"A rule aimed at the label is easy to draft and easy to route around... A
   rule aimed at the function is harder to write and harder to enforce..."** —
   The whole closing paragraph is an "opposite trade" parallel (label/function,
   easy/hard). Break the symmetry: state what a label-based rule does and fails
   at, then, in separate non-mirrored sentences, what a use-based rule reaches and
   costs.

3. **"Two recent federal actions point the other way, toward what systems do
   rather than what they claim."** — "X rather than Y" antithesis plus a punchy
   setup ("point the other way"). Fix: "Two recent federal actions are organized
   around what these systems do in use. The FTC's..."

4. **"produces that effect inside the exchange... produces a different effect,
   also inside the exchange."** — Mirrored harmful-model/safe-model "trade"
   pairing. Keep the concrete content, drop the parallel echo.

5. **Piled-up list for rhythm:** **"a chatbot is immediate, private in the
   ordinary sense, cheap, and willing to continue at three in the morning when no
   clinic is open."** — Four-item cadence list + poetic "three in the morning."
   Fix: "a chatbot answers immediately, costs nothing, and is available when
   clinics are closed."

6. **Punchy setup / announcement sentence:** **"That is the case for a use-based
   trigger."** — Advertises the point instead of stating it. Fold into the
   substantive sentence that follows.

7. **Mild poetic closers to trim:**
   - **"What matters is what the system says back when someone types that they
     cannot go on."** — "cannot go on" is a poetic flourish and repeats
     "matters." Fix: "What matters is how the system responds when a user types
     that they are suicidal."
   - **"the marketing label is the least informative thing about what that system
     has become."** — abstract crescendo ending. State it plainly: "...the
     marketing label tells you the least about how the system is being used."
   - **"taking their worst moments to a system"** — swap for "taking suicidal and
     psychotic conversations to a system."

8. **Title:** "Regulate AI Therapy by Use, Not by Intent" is itself a banned "X,
   not Y" construction, and the "not by intent" framing recurs in the body. The
   brief mandates this title, so keep it, but avoid re-triggering the pattern in
   prose (see #1–#3).

Minor: opening lines of two paragraphs are short announcement-style ("The label
is also something a company can manage." / "Part of the enforcement objection
softens on inspection."). Acceptable, but the first could be merged into its
paragraph for a more measured cadence.

## Factual grounding (spot-checked)

Verified against evidence and web search — all sound:
- Illinois WOPR / HB 1806, $10,000 per violation, effective Aug 2025 — **correct.**
- OpenAI 0.15% suicidal / 0.07% psychosis-mania, ~800M weekly, ">1M/week" — **correct.**
- FTC Sept 2025 6(b) inquiry, seven firms incl. those named — **correct.**
- Utah (May 2025), Nevada AB 406 (July 2025), EU AI Act tiers/disclosure,
  Common Sense 72%, Bipartisan Policy Center, Brown/JAMA 1-in-8 (1-in-5 for
  18–21), SAMHSA 61.5M / ~half — **all check out.**

Two calibration flags:
- **Overclaim:** "the tool that most people actually bring their mental-health
  conversations to" and "where most people actually take their mental-health
  conversations" are stated as established fact. The evidence file explicitly
  cautions this is "directionally supported... not established by a clean
  census." Soften to "one of the main tools people bring..." or "very likely the
  largest venue for..."
- **Slight imprecision:** "more than a million people every week discussing
  suicide" broadens OpenAI's actual metric ("explicit indicators of potential
  suicidal planning or intent"). "discussing suicidal intent" is more accurate
  than "discussing suicide."
