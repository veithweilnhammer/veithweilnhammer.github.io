3. **Utah H.B. 452 (2025):** Utah requires clear disclosures by "suppliers" of AI mental health chatbots that users are interacting with AI, along with strict data privacy rules, but applies only to systems presented as providing mental health therapy. Source: Utah State Legislature, H.B. 452 (2025). Link: https://le.utah.gov/~2025/bills/hbillenr/HB0452.htm

5. **FTC Inquiry into AI Companions (2025):** The Federal Trade Commission launched a probe into seven major tech companies regarding the emotional risks and safety impacts of AI companion chatbots on children and teens. # Draft

In August 2025, Illinois enacted the Wellness and Oversight for Psychological Resources (WOPR) Act, becoming the first state to prohibit artificial intelligence from acting as an independent provider of clinical therapy. The law requires a licensed human professional to oversee any psychological treatment. Under these rules, a software application cannot diagnose a condition, formulate a treatment plan, or conduct a therapeutic intervention on its own. State legislators framed this intervention as a public health boundary, ensuring that algorithms do not replace trained clinicians in vulnerable moments. The mechanism of the law relies entirely on product categorization. If a company markets its software as a therapeutic tool or claims to treat mental illness, it falls under the jurisdiction of the WOPR Act, subjecting the developer to severe civil penalties for unauthorized medical practice.

This regulatory approach assumes that mental health care occurs strictly inside products labeled as mental health care. The boundary fails immediately when users seek support outside of those designated products. Millions of people currently use general-purpose large language models and companion chatbots to process trauma, manage anxiety, and seek behavioral advice. The companies building these general models explicitly state in their terms of service that their products do not provide medical advice or professional therapy. Because the manufacturers disclaim any clinical intent, their systems operate entirely outside the scope of laws like the WOPR Act. The legal definition depends on the marketing label, while the actual risk and benefit depend on the conversation taking place between the user and the software.

The gap between stated intent and actual use appears across multiple recent legislative efforts. In March 2025, Utah passed H.B. 452, a law requiring clear disclosures for AI-powered mental health chatbots. The Utah statute forces providers to inform users that they are interacting with a machine rather than a human clinician. It mandates these disclosures before the first interaction, after any seven-day gap in use, and anytime the user explicitly asks. Furthermore, the law imposes strict data privacy limits, preventing companies from selling conversational logs or targeting advertisements based on user inputs. Like the Illinois law, the Utah statute defines its scope based on the presentation of the product. The rules apply to systems that are presented as capable of providing mental health therapy. General-purpose assistants, which are presented as productivity tools or conversational companions, avoid these disclosure requirements and data restrictions entirely, even though users frequently steer them into deep therapeutic conversations.

The European Union’s Artificial Intelligence Act structures its regulatory tiers around a similar concept of intended purpose. The framework categorizes AI systems by risk, placing strict testing, logging, and human-oversight obligations on high-risk applications. A chatbot explicitly designed and marketed for medical diagnosis or psychiatric intervention lands in the high-risk tier. A general-purpose conversational model defaults to a lower risk category, carrying basic transparency requirements but escaping the stringent safety protocols of a medical device. If a user spends three hours a day discussing their depression with a general-purpose model, the system continues to be regulated as a low-risk text generator. The American Psychological Association has repeatedly warned against this direct-to-consumer flood of unregulated chatbots. In their congressional testimony, the APA pointed out that accessible, low-cost conversational agents often serve as a substitute for licensed care regardless of what the user agreement says, creating severe risks for vulnerable populations.

A regulatory framework that addresses actual harm must track how systems function in practice, rather than how they are branded. The U.S. Federal Trade Commission shifted toward this behavioral perspective in September 2025 when it launched an inquiry into AI companion applications. Rather than focusing on whether companies claimed to provide medical services, the FTC requested data on how manufacturers measure and monitor the emotional reliance users form with their systems. The inquiry specifically targeted the impacts on children and adolescents who build trusting relationships with conversational agents. By investigating how companies manage the emotional attachments generated by their software, the FTC approach acknowledges a fundamental reality: a general-purpose system functions as a psychological intervention when a user treats it as one.

When a system functions as a mental health product for a specific user, the corresponding duties of care should attach to that interaction. Those duties include basic safety testing for clinical edge cases, transparent disclosure of the system's limitations, and established protocols for handling psychiatric crises. Under a use-based framework, a company cannot bypass these obligations simply by placing a medical disclaimer in the user agreement. If the software sustains a long-term therapeutic interaction or provides directive behavioral advice, it crosses a functional threshold. The manufacturer must then ensure the system handles that specific conversation safely, providing the exact same algorithmic guardrails required of a dedicated mental health application.

Basing regulation on user behavior introduces significant enforcement and definition challenges. The primary objection is the difficulty of defining a mental health use in the context of an open-ended conversation. A user might discuss a stressful day at work, seek advice on a difficult relationship, or ask for strategies to manage grief. The line between general conversation, peer support, and clinical therapy is porous even in human interactions. Translating that boundary into a legal threshold for software requires precise conversational markers that regulators can measure and audit. Without clear definitions, a use-based standard risks massive regulatory over-reach, forcing developers of general-purpose software to implement medical-grade compliance for basic text generation.

Technology companies argue that holding them liable for how users repurpose their general tools violates free speech principles and stifles innovation. They maintain that a language model simply generates text based on probabilistic patterns, and restricting its output based on the user's emotional state amounts to content policing. Additionally, applying medical regulations to general platforms could reduce access to low-level support. If companies face high compliance costs the moment a conversation touches on mental health, they are highly likely to program their systems to refuse those topics entirely. This refusal would cut off a primary source of immediate, accessible support for users who cannot afford or access human clinicians, leaving them with no resources at all.

Attempting to solve this problem by banning the therapeutic label entirely often produces the exact opposite of the intended effect. Laws like the Illinois WOPR Act aim to protect consumers by ensuring only licensed professionals offer care. When states make it legally hazardous for technology companies to build dedicated, supervised mental health applications, legitimate developers exit the market to avoid the liability. The demand for accessible mental health support remains unchanged, so users migrate out of specialized applications and into the shadows. They take their therapeutic conversations to unregulated, general-purpose platforms that lack the specialized safety features, crisis escalation paths, and clinical oversight of purpose-built applications.

By driving mental health conversations onto general-purpose systems, label-based bans increase the overall risk to the user. A company building a dedicated mental health chatbot actively programs the system to recognize suicidal ideation, decline inappropriate medical requests, and refer users to emergency resources. A company building a general-purpose assistant optimizes for engagement, task completion, and broad capability, often leaving the system vulnerable to providing harmful advice or reinforcing dangerous behaviors. Regulatory frameworks that punish the explicit label while ignoring the implicit use effectively penalize the developers trying to build safe systems. At the same time, these frameworks grant immunity to the massive platforms where the vast majority of unregulated therapeutic interaction actually occurs.

A practical use-based standard requires dynamic regulation that activates at the point of interaction. Instead of classifying a software application statically at the time of release, the framework must mandate thresholds that trigger specific safety protocols during the conversation. Language models possess the technical capability to classify their own conversational context in real time. When a system detects that a user is seeking mental health support, discussing self-harm, or exhibiting signs of clinical distress, the software must automatically shift into a regulated mode. 

This triggered mode enforces a specific set of operational requirements. The system must immediately provide a clear, in-context disclosure that it is an artificial intelligence and cannot replace human psychiatric care. It must activate specialized safety filters designed by clinical professionals to prevent harmful output. Crucially, it must establish a direct escalation path to local crisis resources if the conversation indicates an immediate risk to the user's safety. The regulatory burden scales with the sensitivity of the interaction. A user asking a language model to draft an email generates minimal risk and requires minimal oversight. A user relying on that same model for daily anxiety management triggers the transparency and safety obligations appropriate for a clinical tool.

Regulation achieves its purpose only when it aligns with reality. As long as the law focuses exclusively on the marketing claims of the manufacturer, artificial intelligence in mental health will remain functionally unregulated for the vast majority of users. A system becomes a therapeutic intervention the moment a person relies on it to manage their psychological well-being. By shifting the regulatory trigger from the manufacturer's label to the user's behavior, lawmakers can ensure that basic safety, privacy, and crisis protocols protect people exactly where they are already having these conversations.
