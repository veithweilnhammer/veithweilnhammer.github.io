# Evidence

**One-sentence thesis:** AI mental-health regulation should be triggered by how a
system is actually used, not by how it is marketed, because the label-based laws
now on the books leave the largest venue of de facto therapy — general-purpose
chatbots — entirely uncovered.

## Key claims and sources

1. **Illinois banned AI-delivered therapy in 2025, keyed to the "therapy /
   psychotherapy services" label.** The Wellness and Oversight for Psychological
   Resources Act (WOPR Act, HB 1806 / Public Act 104-0054) prohibits providing,
   advertising, or offering therapy or psychotherapy to the public unless a
   licensed professional delivers the clinical judgment; civil penalties up to
   $10,000 per violation, enforced by IDFPR. Effective August 1, 2025.

     https://www.ilga.gov/Legislation/PublicActs/View/104-0054

     https://natlawreview.com/article/illinois-becomes-first-state-regulate-use-ai-mental-health-therapy-services

   with explicit indicators of potential suicidal planning or intent.** Against
   ~800 million weekly active users, that is more than a million people per week.
   OpenAI also reported ~0.07% weekly with possible signs of psychosis or mania,
   and said it worked with 170+ mental-health clinicians on the update.
   - OpenAI, "Strengthening ChatGPT's responses in sensitive conversations"

   - Marc Zao-Sanders, "How People Are Really Using Gen AI in 2025," Harvard

- No public-domain historical quotes are used in this post. All sources above are

## Do not overclaim

- **Illinois WOPR does not contain an explicit "ChatGPT is exempt" clause.** The
  coverage gap is a reading of its scope: it regulates services provided,
  advertised, or offered *as* therapy/psychotherapy, so a general assistant that
  does not hold itself out as therapy is not captured. State it as a scope
  argument, not as an enumerated exemption.
- **These laws are not identical or purely cosmetic.** Utah adds real data and
  advertising restrictions; Nevada adds a schools ban; the FTC inquiry is
  function-focused. The shared weakness is the *trigger*, not that they do
  nothing.
- **"Most mental-health use happens on general tools" is directionally supported,
  not precisely measured.** The HBR ranking and OpenAI's figures support the
  claim that enormous volume runs through general assistants; there is no clean
  census of "de facto therapy."
- **A use-based trigger is not obviously easy or clearly constitutional.** Present
  the enforcement, over-reach, and free-speech objections honestly.
- **Do not imply general chatbots are safe or unsafe overall.** The argument is
  about *who the rules reach*, not a verdict on net benefit or harm.

## Flags (could not fully verify / sources may disagree)

- **HBR "therapy and companionship is #1" is based on analysis of forum/Reddit
  posts, not a representative survey.** Treat as an indicator of a large,
  fast-growing use, not a population statistic. This is my least-certain load-
  bearing source.
- **OpenAI's 800M weekly-active-users figure** is a company-reported number from
  around the same period; the "over a million" derivation depends on it. The
  0.15% figure is OpenAI's own; the multiplication is straightforward but rests
  on a self-reported denominator.
- **A generic web summary conflated the Illinois "WOPR Act" with an unrelated
  "chatbot disclosure" concept and invented an expansion of the acronym.** I did
  not rely on it. WOPR = Wellness and Oversight for Psychological Resources Act;
  confirmed against IDFPR and the National Law Review.
- **Effective dates and penalty amounts** are from reputable legal summaries;

  quoted.
- **EU AI Act specifics** (which mental-health uses land in which tier) are

  the Act classifies by intended purpose.

# Draft

Illinois made it illegal in August 2025 for an artificial-intelligence system to
act as a therapist. The Wellness and Oversight for Psychological Resources Act,
signed that summer, forbids any company from providing, advertising, or offering
psychotherapy in the state unless a licensed human professional carries the
clinical judgment, and it sets civil penalties of up to $10,000 for each
violation. The law was written to stop firms from selling an unlicensed chatbot
as a stand-in for a clinician, and on those terms it works.

During the same months, the tool that most people actually bring their
mental-health conversations to went untouched. In October 2025 OpenAI reported
that about 0.15% of ChatGPT's weekly users have conversations that contain
explicit indicators of potential suicidal planning or intent. Set against a base
the company puts near 800 million weekly users, that share amounts to more than a
million people every week discussing suicide with a general-purpose assistant. A
widely cited analysis of how people describe their use of generative AI in 2025
placed therapy and companionship as the most common purpose of all, ahead of
writing, coding, and search. None of that traffic passes through a product sold
as therapy, so a law that regulates the word "therapy" never reaches it.

The gap follows directly from how these laws decide what they cover. Illinois,
like the other 2025 statutes, keys its rules to what a system is marketed or held
out to do. A product advertised as counseling triggers the duties; a product
advertised as a general assistant does not, even when a user spends an hour a
night describing panic attacks to it. The trigger is the label on the box.

The same design runs through the rest of the year's regulation. Utah's mental-
health chatbot law, effective in May 2025, defines the systems it governs as
those a supplier represents — or that a reasonable person would take — to provide
therapy for a mental-health condition, and then requires them to disclose that
the user is talking to a machine and restricts what they may do with the
resulting health data. Nevada's AB 406, effective in July, bars AI systems
"programmed to provide" professional mental or behavioral health care and stops
public schools from using AI to perform a counselor's or psychologist's duties.
The European Union's AI Act sorts systems into risk tiers by their intended
purpose, prohibiting emotion recognition in workplaces and schools while leaving
ordinary chatbots under a light transparency rule that they must tell users they
are not human. Each of these fixes on the declared purpose of the software. Each
leaves a general assistant that quietly absorbs the same conversations outside
the frame.

The label is also something a company can manage. A product that wants to stay
outside these rules can write into its terms of service that it offers general
wellness or entertainment and is not a substitute for professional care, and that
sentence, rather than anything about the actual exchanges, is what keeps it clear
of the therapy statutes. The distance between the declared product and the used
product widens the more people lean on the tool. In a 2025 survey of American
teenagers, 72% reported having used an AI companion, and about a third said they
had taken something serious to one instead of to a person. Many of those
conversations run on general assistants that describe themselves as anything but
therapy, and the description is what the law reads.

There are two partial exceptions worth naming, because they point the other way.
The Federal Trade Commission's September 2025 inquiry, which sent orders to seven
companies including OpenAI, Meta, Google, Character.AI, Snap, and xAI, is built
around the *companion* function and its effects on children rather than around a
marketing label, and it asks how those systems behave when a young user confides
in them. The American Psychological Association's November advisory, which urges
federal action, still frames part of its ask around marketing — it wants
companies stopped from presenting chatbots as licensed therapists — but it does
so because it has watched people use ordinary chatbots for support and get
unpredictable, sometimes dangerous responses. Both are reacting to what these
systems do in use. Neither has yet produced a rule that attaches to use.

Harm and benefit in this setting track use, not marketing. A model that affirms a
user's delusion, encourages food restriction in someone with anorexia, or fumbles
a disclosure of suicidal intent produces that effect inside the conversation, and
the effect is the same whether the app store page said "therapy" or "helpful
assistant." A supervised, tested clinical tool that handles the same disclosure by
naming the risk and routing the person to help produces a different effect, again
inside the conversation. The label sits on a marketing page that no distressed
user is reading at the moment that matters. What matters is what the system says
back when someone types that they cannot go on.

That is the case for a use-based trigger. When a system is functioning as a
mental-health product for a given user — when the exchange is a sustained one
about distress, self-harm, diagnosis, or medication — the duties that we already
recognize for such products should attach to it: disclosure that it is not a
clinician and cannot take responsibility for care, tested handling of crisis
situations, and safety evaluation aimed at the kinds of conversations that
actually occur. Branding would stop being the thing that decides whether any of
this applies. The million weekly conversations about suicide would fall inside the
rules rather than outside them, because the rule would follow the conversation.

The objections to this are real, and the strongest is enforcement. A marketing
label is public and static; a regulator can read it once and know what the
product claims. Use is private, continuous, and defined only as it happens, which
makes it far harder to observe and to adjudicate after the fact. A use-based
standard also risks sweeping in software that no one thinks of as a health
product. A person can type a despairing sentence into a search box, a note-taking
app, or a coding assistant, and a poorly drawn rule would convert every one of
those into a regulated medical device the moment they did. Any workable version
needs a threshold — repeated, extended engagement on clinical distress rather than
a single sad message — so that ordinary software is not pulled into a clinical
regime by one interaction.

There is a free-speech and access dimension as well. Restricting what a
general-purpose model may say to a person in distress limits expression, and it
limits a resource that many people reach for precisely because it is free,
immediate, and available at three in the morning when no clinic is. This is where
banning the label can work against its own goal. A prohibition aimed at products
that call themselves therapy leaves the supervised, disclosed, crisis-tested tools
carrying legal risk while the unlabeled general assistant carries none, so a
cautious company withdraws the safer product and the vulnerable user migrates to
the one with no guardrails at all. A rule written around the word can push people
toward the tools that word was meant to protect them from.

A use-based standard would look different in a few concrete ways. It would set a
threshold of sustained mental-health use, so that duties attach when a pattern of
clinical conversation appears, not when a single message does. It would require
disclosure at the point of that use — the system stating plainly, when the
exchange turns clinical, that it is not a clinician and cannot be responsible for
someone's care — rather than a line buried in terms of service that no one opens.
It would require tested behavior for the categories that carry the most risk:
suicidal intent, self-harm, and signs of psychosis or mania, which are exactly the
categories OpenAI has now started to measure and count in its own traffic. And it
would require that safety testing cover conversations as they unfold over many
turns, since a system can pass a single well-formed question and then drift into
agreement over the course of an hour. These duties would apply to any system that
crosses the threshold, whether it is sold as a wellness app, a companion, or a
general assistant.

Part of the enforcement objection softens on inspection. Systems at this scale
already classify these conversations, and OpenAI could only report the 0.15%
figure because its models detect when an exchange carries signs of suicidal
intent. A standard that required a system to act on the detection it already
performs — to disclose its nature, surface crisis resources, and change how it
responds once a conversation crosses into clinical territory — would hold
companies accountable for a capability they have built and measured rather than
for a marketing claim they can rewrite. The hard part is setting the threshold and
auditing behavior around it, which is genuine work, though it sits closer to how
consumer-safety rules already operate than the label-based approach admits.

None of this makes a chatbot a good therapist, and none of it settles whether AI
belongs in mental-health care at all. The narrower point is about where a law
points. A rule aimed at the label is easy to draft and easy to route around: a
company changes its marketing copy, or never adopts the label in the first place,
and the same conversations continue outside the rule's reach. A rule aimed at the
function is harder to write and harder to enforce, and it has to be bounded so it
does not swallow ordinary software. What it does, that the current laws do not, is
reach the place where the benefit and the harm actually occur. When more than a
million people a week are taking their worst moments to a system that no
therapy-specific law touches, the marketing label is the least informative thing
about what that system has become.
