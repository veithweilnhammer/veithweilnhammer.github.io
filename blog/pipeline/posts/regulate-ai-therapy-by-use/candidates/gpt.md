# Draft

Mental-health regulation for AI is being written around a category that users often never see: whether a company presents its product as therapy. That category matters for licensing and consumer protection, but it fits poorly with the way people now use general chatbots. A person who opens ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Character.AI, or another general assistant at 2 a.m. to talk through panic, suicidal thoughts, compulsive reassurance, relationship fear, or depression is using the system as a mental-health product for that interaction. The user’s risk depends on what happens in the conversation, even when the product has therapy branding, clinical registration, or any claim to replace a professional.

Illinois gives the problem its clearest form. In August 2025, Governor J.B. Pritzker signed the Wellness and Oversight for Psychological Resources Act, one of the first U.S. laws to draw a firm line around AI therapy. The state’s professional regulator described the law as prohibiting anyone from using AI to provide mental-health and therapeutic decision-making, while allowing AI for administrative and supplementary support by licensed behavioral-health professionals. Legal summaries describe a wide prohibition: internet-based AI may not provide, advertise, or offer therapy or psychotherapy in Illinois unless a licensed professional conducts the service. AI may help with scheduling, billing, and records, while independent therapeutic decisions, therapeutic communication, unreviewed treatment plans, and emotion detection are barred.

That is a serious attempt to protect patients from unlicensed therapy. It also shows the limit of regulating the label. The law is strongest when a product, provider, or clinician is already inside the therapy frame. It can prohibit a chatbot from acting as a therapist and keep licensed therapists from outsourcing the central parts of therapy to a model. The harder case is the ordinary general-purpose chatbot, marketed for writing, search, brainstorming, planning, companionship, or productivity, that becomes therapy-like because the user brings therapy-like needs into the box and the model responds with support, interpretation, coping advice, or guidance.

Therapy-like use has moved into ordinary consumer software. Marc Zao-Sanders’ 2025 Harvard Business Review analysis ranked therapy and companionship as the leading observed generative-AI use, although the analysis used collected online examples rather than a representative survey. A later Bipartisan Policy Center survey found that about three in ten U.S. adults had used self-guided digital tools for mental health or well-being, and that nearly half of those users had used a general chatbot. A nationally representative youth survey reported by Brown University found that about one in eight U.S. adolescents and young adults aged 12 to 21 had used AI chatbots for mental-health advice, rising to roughly one in five among ages 18 to 21.

The demand is easy to understand. Formal mental-health care is expensive, unevenly available, and often slow to access. In the United States, SAMHSA estimated that 61.5 million adults had any mental illness in 2024, and only about half received mental-health treatment. A chatbot is immediate, private in the ordinary social sense, cheap, and willing to continue. For someone ashamed, uninsured, rural, housebound, or awake during a crisis, those properties explain the appeal.

Regulation built around marketing intent will therefore miss part of the practice it is meant to govern. A company can avoid the words therapy, counselor, diagnosis, and treatment while still operating a system that provides emotionally consequential mental-health support. A model can give breathing exercises for panic, challenge or reinforce paranoid fears, advise a user about stopping medication, help draft a message to an abuser, normalize days without sleep, or respond to suicidal thinking. The relevant fact is the function performed in the exchange, because helpful and harmful responses can occur under the same general product label.

Other states have started to approach the problem from nearby angles. Utah’s 2025 law defines a mental-health chatbot as generative AI that has conversations similar to confidential communications with a licensed therapist and that a supplier represents, or a reasonable person would believe, can provide therapy or help manage or treat mental-health conditions. That “reasonable person would believe” language moves beyond explicit marketing claims. Utah also requires AI disclosure, restricts advertising based on user input, and protects identifiable health information. Nevada’s 2025 law, as summarized by legal analysts, takes a more restrictive approach to AI systems designed to provide direct professional mental or behavioral health care.

At the federal level, the FTC’s 2025 inquiry into AI companions shows a different route. The agency issued orders to Alphabet, Character Technologies, Instagram, Meta, OpenAI, Snap, and xAI, asking how they test child and teen impacts, mitigate harms, disclose risks, handle data, and monetize engagement. The inquiry focuses on companion chatbots that simulate interpersonal relationships and may affect vulnerable users, which brings the analysis closer to a use-based frame: what the system is doing to people in deployment.

The European Union’s AI Act also points in a useful direction, while leaving the general-chatbot problem partly open. Its basic structure classifies AI systems by risk. Systems that interact directly with people must tell them they are interacting with AI. Certain uses, such as emergency healthcare triage or AI embedded in regulated medical devices, can trigger high-risk obligations. This architecture has an advantage over a simple ban on AI therapy because it accepts that the same technical system can carry different duties in different contexts. A general chatbot answering a grammar question carries a different risk profile from the same chatbot responding to self-harm.

A use-based standard would make that distinction explicit. The trigger should be the system’s role in the interaction: when a user seeks help for mental or emotional distress, psychiatric symptoms, compulsive behavior, self-harm, trauma, relationship abuse, or substance use, and the system responds with assessment, coping guidance, therapeutic interpretation, crisis advice, or continuing emotional support, the system is functioning as a mental-health product for that user. The duties that follow should be proportionate to the risk of the exchange and should apply even when the product is branded as a general assistant.

The lowest tier is disclosure at the point of use. If a user begins a mental-health conversation with a general chatbot, the product should say plainly that it is AI, that it lacks clinical licensure, that ordinary chatbot conversations may lack the confidentiality protections of therapy, and that crisis situations require human help. This disclosure should appear when the mental-health use begins, because a terms-of-service document or an onboarding screen will often be too remote from the relevant moment. Utah’s disclosure rule for mental-health chatbots is a useful model, but general assistants need an equivalent when their use crosses into the same territory.

A second tier is data and advertising protection. Mental-health conversations reveal sensitive information even when they occur outside a hospital, insurer, or therapy practice. A user who tells a chatbot about panic attacks, self-harm, substance use, intrusive sexual thoughts, or domestic violence has disclosed health-relevant data. Platforms should exclude that information from targeted advertising, engagement tuning, and commercial vulnerability inference. Utah’s restriction on using mental-health chatbot inputs to determine or customize advertising captures the right principle. Emotional support should not be financed by making the user easier to manipulate.

A third tier is safety testing for mental-health behavior. General models are already tested for many forms of unsafe output, but mental-health safety requires more than filtering obvious self-harm instructions. Systems should be tested on whether they escalate emergencies, avoid reinforcing delusions or mania, resist compulsive reassurance loops, decline to provide pseudo-diagnosis where context is missing, and encourage appropriate human support when risk rises. The tests should include multi-turn conversations, because a single safe answer can still become unsafe when the same pattern repeats across an evening, a week, or a dependency-forming relationship.

A fourth tier is crisis handling. If a user expresses imminent intent to die, harm someone, stop essential medication in a dangerous context, or act on psychotic or manic beliefs, the system should move into a constrained mode with clear crisis resources, encouragement to contact emergency or trusted human support, and limits on open-ended continuation that could deepen the crisis. This requirement should apply to general chatbots as much as to apps that call themselves therapeutic. From the user’s perspective, the risk arises inside the exchange before the product category becomes clinical.

Use-based regulation creates hard boundary problems. People discuss sadness in emails, grief in search boxes, stress in productivity tools, and conflict in writing assistants. A broad rule could turn ordinary software into a regulated medical product whenever a user types an emotional sentence. That would be costly, intrusive, and bad for access. A threshold is more workable than a universal medical classification. A single low-risk mention of stress could trigger lightweight disclosure or no intervention. Repeated mental-health support, explicit symptom management, crisis language, therapy-like role-play, or model-generated treatment guidance should trigger stronger duties. The rule should look at both sides of the interaction: what the user is seeking and what the system provides.

There are also speech concerns. Chatbots produce language, and regulation of language can become regulation of viewpoints, advice, or emotional expression. A workable standard should therefore focus on product duties rather than approved opinions. It can require disclosure, testing, recordkeeping, crisis protocols, data limits, and human escalation pathways without prescribing a single theory of therapy or banning ordinary conversations about distress. The point is to regulate the service function created by a system that is deployed at scale, personalized through conversation, and often used by vulnerable people.

Access concerns are equally important. Many people use chatbots because the formal system is unavailable or unacceptable to them. A rule that simply bans “AI therapy” may protect licensed practice while pushing users toward the least accountable tools: general chatbots with weaker mental-health safeguards, offshore products, or companion apps that avoid clinical language. A safer supervised tool that is honest about its limits, tested for predictable failure modes, and connected to human care may be better than a legal environment that removes the label and leaves the behavior untouched.

Regulation by use is more coherent than regulation by intent because intent is easy for companies to edit. A product page can avoid therapeutic words. A system prompt can say the model is a general assistant. A disclaimer can state that the chatbot lacks therapist status. Those facts matter, but they describe only part of the interaction. Use is harder to define, yet it is closer to the place where benefit and harm occur. The law already makes similar moves elsewhere: driving rules apply when a vehicle is driven, privacy duties can attach when sensitive data is processed, and medical-device duties can attach when software performs a medical function. AI mental-health support needs the same functional logic.

A practical standard would define mental-health use by observable features of the interaction: distress disclosures, requests for coping or treatment guidance, crisis language, repeated emotional reliance, and system responses that provide support, interpretation, assessment, or advice. Second, scale duties by risk: disclosure and data limits for low-risk support, validated crisis handling and stronger testing for high-risk conversations, and full clinical oversight for tools that claim to diagnose, treat, or deliver structured therapy. Third, require platforms to monitor the aggregate prevalence and safety of these interactions, because a general chatbot used by millions for mental-health support has become part of the mental-health environment.

A use-based rule can leave ordinary conversation outside medical regulation while closing a predictable loophole. A general-purpose chatbot becomes a mental-health product during the conversations in which people use it for mental health and the system responds in kind. Rules that wait for the company to call the product therapy will arrive after the relevant practice has already moved elsewhere.
