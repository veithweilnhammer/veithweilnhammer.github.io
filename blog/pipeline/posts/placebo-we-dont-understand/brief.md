# Brief: Placebo Is a Treatment We Do Not Understand

## Slug
placebo-we-dont-understand

## Working title
Placebo Is a Treatment We Do Not Understand

## One-line angle (also the Substack subtitle / SEO hook)
The placebo response in psychiatry is not an absence of treatment but a real biological effect of expectation on the brain — one worth studying rather than subtracting away.

## Single argument (thesis)
The large placebo response in psychiatric treatment is a genuine biological effect in which expectation itself changes the brain, so treating placebo merely as a baseline to subtract misses a real therapeutic mechanism that matters for any therapy, including AI.

## Concrete anchor (optional — a real example to ground the argument)
The long-running debate over antidepressant efficacy, in which a large share of the measured improvement is matched by placebo response — often read as evidence the drugs barely work, but equally read as evidence that expectation is a powerful active ingredient.

## What this post must cover
- Reframe the default view: in trials the placebo arm is treated as "no treatment," a baseline to subtract, which quietly assumes nothing therapeutic happened there.
- The evidence that something real happens: expectation produces measurable changes in the brain and body (e.g., placebo analgesia involving endogenous opioids; conditioned physiological responses).
- The psychiatric case: antidepressant-efficacy debates, where a large placebo response is real and reproducible, not merely noise or regression to the mean.
- The reframing: if expectation reliably changes outcomes, placebo is an effect to understand and possibly harness, not only to control for.
- Why this matters for any therapy including AI: expectation, ritual, and the sense of being cared for are active ingredients that a chatbot interaction also engages — which complicates both hype and dismissal of AI therapy.
- The honest limits: placebo effects are often modest, variable, and easy to over-claim; harnessing them raises real ethical questions about deception and informed consent.

## Anchors (real sources this post is allowed to build on)
- Antidepressant efficacy / placebo-response debates (e.g., Kirsch's meta-analyses; Cipriani et al. 2018 network meta-analysis as counterpoint).
- Placebo neuroscience: expectation and endogenous opioids in placebo analgesia (e.g., Benedetti's work).
- The general framing of placebo as an active, studyable effect rather than a null condition.
