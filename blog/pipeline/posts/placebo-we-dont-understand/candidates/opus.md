# Evidence

**Thesis (one sentence):** The large placebo response in psychiatric treatment is
a genuine biological effect in which expectation changes the brain, so treating
the placebo arm of a trial merely as a baseline to subtract discards a real
therapeutic mechanism — one that matters for any therapy, including AI chatbots.

## Key claims and sources

1. **In antidepressant trials, most of the measured improvement is matched in the
   placebo arm.** Kirsch & Sapirstein's 1998 meta-analysis estimated ~75% of the
   drug response was duplicated by placebo; Rief et al. (2009) estimated ~78%.
   - Kirsch I, Sapirstein G (1998), "Listening to Prozac but Hearing Placebo: A
     Meta-Analysis of Antidepressant Medication," *Prevention & Treatment* 1(2).
     https://doi.org/10.1037/1522-3736.1.1.12a
   - Rief W, Nestoriuc Y, Weiss S, Welzel E, Barsky AJ, Hofmann SG (2009),
     "Meta-analysis of the placebo response in antidepressant trials," *J Affect
     Disord* 118(1–3):1–8. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jad.2009.01.029

2. **The mean drug–placebo difference on the Hamilton scale is small (~1.8
   points) and reaches conventional clinical significance only at the very severe
   end — and that gradient is driven by reduced placebo responsiveness in the
   very severe, not by increased drug responsiveness.** Kirsch et al. (2008),
   FDA-submitted trial data.
   - Kirsch I, Deacon BJ, Huedo-Medina TB, Scoboria A, Moore TJ, Johnson BT
     (2008), "Initial Severity and Antidepressant Benefits: A Meta-Analysis of
     Data Submitted to the Food and Drug Administration," *PLoS Medicine* 5(2):e45.
     https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.0050045

3. **Counterpoint: all 21 antidepressants outperformed placebo in a large network
   meta-analysis (odds ratios for response ~1.37–2.13), so the drugs do carry a
   specific effect.** Cipriani et al. (2018), *The Lancet*.
   - Cipriani A, Furukawa TA, Salanti G, et al. (2018), "Comparative efficacy and
     acceptability of 21 antidepressant drugs...," *Lancet* 391(10128):1357–1366.
     https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(17)32802-7

4. **Placebo analgesia is mediated by endogenous opioids: the opioid antagonist
   naloxone reverses placebo-induced pain relief.** Levine, Gordon & Fields
   (1978), postoperative dental pain.
   - Levine JD, Gordon NC, Fields HL (1978), "The mechanism of placebo
     analgesia," *Lancet* 2(8091):654–657. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/80579/

5. **In Parkinson's disease, placebo triggers measurable dopamine release
   (PET) and changes the firing of single neurons in the subthalamic nucleus.**
   - de la Fuente-Fernández R, Ruth TJ, Sossi V, Schulzer M, Calne DB, Stoessl AJ
     (2001), "Expectation and dopamine release: mechanism of the placebo effect
     in Parkinson's disease," *Science* 293(5532):1164–1166.
     https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1060937
   - Benedetti F, Colloca L, Torre E, et al. (2004), "Placebo-responsive
     Parkinson patients show decreased activity in single neurons of subthalamic
     nucleus," *Nature Neuroscience* 7(6):587–588. https://doi.org/10.1038/nn1250

6. **Placebo responses can be produced without deception: open-label placebo
   improved IBS symptoms (59% vs 35% adequate relief) even when patients were
   told the pills were inert.** Kaptchuk et al. (2010).
   - Kaptchuk TJ, Friedlander E, Kelley JM, et al. (2010), "Placebos without
     Deception: A Randomized Controlled Trial in Irritable Bowel Syndrome,"
     *PLoS ONE* 5(12):e15591. https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0015591

7. **The placebo response in depression trials is variable, substantial, and has
   increased over the decades.** Walsh et al. (2002), *JAMA*.
   - Walsh BT, Seidman SN, Sysko R, Gould M (2002), "Placebo response in studies
     of major depression: variable, substantial, and increasing," *JAMA*
     287(14):1840–1847. https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.287.14.1840

8. **Physiological responses can be conditioned like a Pavlovian reflex,
   including immunosuppression, showing placebo has a learnable, bodily
   component.** Ader & Cohen (1975).
   - Ader R, Cohen N (1975), "Behaviorally conditioned immunosuppression,"
     *Psychosomatic Medicine* 37(4):333–340.
     https://doi.org/10.1097/00006842-197507000-00007

## Do not overclaim

- Do not say antidepressants "don't work." Cipriani 2018 shows a real specific
  effect; the honest claim is that the placebo-matched share is large.
- Do not imply placebo "cures" objective disease. It reliably shifts symptom
  reports and some physiology (pain, motor symptoms, mood, IBS), but does not
  shrink tumors or replace insulin.
- Do not present the 75%/78% figures as "proportion of patients cured by
  placebo." They are proportions of the *drug response* (effect size) matched in
  placebo arms.
- Do not claim open-label placebo works as well as concealed placebo in general;
  the evidence base is smaller and condition-specific.
- Do not equate "expectation effect" with "not real." The point is the reverse:
  it is biologically real.

## Flags

- The 75% (Kirsch 1998) and 78% (Rief 2009) figures are computed differently
  across meta-analyses and are contested; treat them as indicative, not exact.
- Part of the placebo-arm improvement is natural course, regression to the mean,
  and measurement effects, not "placebo effect" proper. Walsh 2002 and the
  experimental (opioid/dopamine) studies are what license the claim that some of
  it is a genuine expectation effect; the exact partition is unknown.
- de la Fuente-Fernández (2001) and Benedetti (2004) are small human samples;
  robust as proof-of-mechanism, not as population effect sizes.
- Rief 2009 "78%" figure and the exact Ader & Cohen page numbers were verified
  via secondary summaries and PubMed metadata, not by reading the full PDFs;
  citations are standard and stable but flagged for the editor.
- The NICE ~3-point HAMD clinical-significance threshold is widely cited; I keep
  it out of the draft to avoid a number I did not verify against NICE directly.

# Draft

Randomized drug trials are built to remove the placebo response, and in
psychiatry that design choice quietly buries something worth studying. Everyone
enrolled in an antidepressant trial receives the same surrounding treatment: a
diagnostic assessment, repeated contact with a clinician, a pill taken on a fixed
schedule, and the expectation that it might help. The trial then subtracts the
improvement in the placebo arm from the improvement in the drug arm and reports
only the leftover difference as the effect of the medication. In depression that
subtracted portion is large. A 1998 meta-analysis by Irving Kirsch and Guy
Sapirstein estimated that about 75% of the improvement on antidepressants was
matched in the placebo groups, and a 2009 meta-analysis by Winfried Rief and
colleagues arrived at roughly 78%. These numbers are usually read as evidence
that antidepressants barely beat an inert pill. The same numbers support a
different question, which is what the placebo arm was actually doing when most of
the recovery happened there.

Some of what happens in a placebo arm is not a placebo effect at all. Depression
fluctuates, so many people enter a trial near a low point and improve on their
own. Symptom scores regress toward the mean, and the act of being measured
repeatedly nudges reports around. If that were the whole account, the placebo arm
would be a bookkeeping artifact and subtracting it would be exactly right. But
part of the response tracks expectation directly, and that part shows up in the
body in ways an artifact cannot explain. The clearest evidence comes from studies
that manipulate expectation and then measure the pharmacology it engages.

The oldest of these findings concerns pain. In 1978 Jon Levine, Newton Gordon and
Howard Fields studied patients recovering from dental surgery and gave some of
them a placebo they believed was a painkiller. The placebo reduced their pain. The
researchers then gave naloxone, a drug that blocks opioid receptors, and the pain
relief disappeared. Because naloxone reverses the effect of morphine and of the body's
own endorphins in the same way, its ability to cancel placebo analgesia points to
a concrete mechanism: expecting relief prompts the brain to release endogenous
opioids, the same chemistry a pain drug would recruit. The relief was produced by
the patient's own pharmacology, switched on by expectation.

The size of these responses behaves like a dose. Work by Benedetti and others has
shown that stronger verbal suggestion produces stronger placebo analgesia, that a
drug given openly by a clinician relieves more than the same drug delivered by a
hidden pump the patient does not know about, and that prior experience of a real
drug working raises the response to a placebo given afterward. This dose-like
structure is why the encounter around a treatment functions as an active
ingredient in ordinary care. The confidence a prescriber projects, the
plausibility of the explanation a patient is given, and the ritual of taking
something on a schedule all scale the response, so every genuine prescription
already carries an expectation component that no one measured or set out to
isolate.

Parkinson's disease shows the same pattern in a different neurotransmitter system.
The disorder comes from the loss of dopamine-producing neurons, and its motor
symptoms respond to drugs that restore dopamine. In 2001 Raúl de la
Fuente-Fernández and colleagues gave patients a placebo they were told was their
medication and used PET imaging to watch dopamine release in the brain; the
placebo triggered a substantial release, comparable to a dose of the active drug.
A few years later Fabrizio Benedetti's group recorded from single neurons in the
subthalamic nucleus of Parkinson's patients during surgery and found that a
placebo changed the firing of individual cells, and that the change tracked
whether the patient's rigidity actually improved. Expectation moved the exact
circuit the disease depletes, down to the activity of single neurons.

Part of what makes the placebo response biological rather than a matter of
conscious hope is that it can be learned. Pairing an inert substance with an
active drug repeatedly lets the inert substance start producing the drug's effect
on its own, the way a bell that has accompanied food comes to trigger salivation.
Robert Ader and Nicholas Cohen showed in 1975 that even immune suppression could
be conditioned in this way in animals, and later work extended conditioned
placebo responses to hormones and pain in humans. A conditioned response does not
require belief in the ordinary sense; it runs on the same associative machinery as
any other reflex, which is one reason the effect is stubborn and reproducible
rather than a fragile trick of attitude.

Return to psychiatry with this in hand and the antidepressant debate reads
differently. In 2008 Kirsch and colleagues analyzed the full set of trials
submitted to the US Food and Drug Administration, including unpublished ones, and
found a mean drug–placebo difference of under two points on the Hamilton
depression scale. The difference grew with how severe patients were at baseline
and reached conventional clinical significance only at the very severe end. The
mechanism behind that gradient is the informative part: the difference widened
not because the drug worked better in severe depression but because the placebo
response shrank there. For the mild and moderate cases where most patients
actually sit, the placebo arm captured most of the recovery. The drugs are not
inert, and the same dataset shows it. A 2018 network meta-analysis by Andrea
Cipriani and colleagues, covering more than five hundred trials, found that all 21
antidepressants studied beat placebo, with response odds ratios between roughly
1.4 and 2.1. Both results hold at once: the medications carry a specific
pharmacological effect, and a large, reproducible share of psychiatric recovery is
carried by the expectation-driven response the trial is designed to remove.

Once the placebo response is understood as a mechanism, the obvious next move is
to ask whether it can be used on purpose rather than only controlled for. The
main objection is ethical, because using expectation seems to require deceiving
the patient about what they are taking. That objection turns out to be weaker than
expected. In 2010 Ted Kaptchuk and colleagues gave patients with irritable bowel
syndrome a bottle of pills labelled as placebo, told them plainly the pills
contained no active ingredient, and explained that placebos can still produce real
effects through mind–body pathways. The open-label placebo group did better than
patients who received no pills, with 59% reporting adequate symptom relief against
35% in the no-treatment group. Telling people the truth did not abolish the
effect. That result is modest and specific to a condition with a strong
symptom-report component, but it shows the response does not depend on a lie, which
removes the sharpest reason to leave it unstudied.

This reframing changes how to think about therapies that work mainly through the
interaction itself, including AI chatbots used for mental health. The ingredients
that drive a placebo response are attention, a plausible explanation for what is
wrong, a structured ritual of care, and the expectation that the encounter will
help. A conversation with a responsive chatbot supplies all of them. When someone
reports feeling better after talking to such a system, that relief can be genuine
and can run through the same expectation pathways as any credible treatment, which
means the report tells us little on its own about whether the tool adds anything
beyond a well-staged placebo. The framing cuts against both easy hype and easy
dismissal. It rules out the claim that a chatbot must be inert because it is "just
talking," since talking that carries expectation is exactly what moves opioids and
dopamine in the studies above. It equally rules out treating a user's short-term
relief as proof the tool works, because the right comparison is a convincing
placebo interaction rather than an absence of contact. A trial of an AI therapy that beats no contact has
shown almost nothing; one that beats a matched, credible sham interaction has
shown something real. Most published trials of mental-health apps compare them
against a waitlist or no support, which is why their encouraging results are hard
to interpret: a waitlist controls for the passage of time but not for the
expectation and ritual the app itself supplies, so the reported benefit blends a
possible specific effect with a placebo effect that the design cannot separate.
The same logic explains why unblinded psychotherapy trials are difficult, since a
person always knows whether they are receiving the intervention or sitting on a
list, and knowing is part of what produces the response.

The honest limits matter, because the placebo response is easy to over-sell. It is
large in pain, depression, Parkinson's motor symptoms and irritable bowel
syndrome, and small or absent elsewhere; it shifts symptoms and some physiology
but does not shrink tumors, clear infections or replace insulin. Its size varies
widely between people and conditions and even between trials of the same drug,
which is part of why Walsh and colleagues found in 2002 that placebo response in
depression trials was substantial and had been rising steadily over the decades.
None of this is a mechanism anyone can yet dose or predict, and using it on purpose
still raises a real ethical question, because amplifying expectation shades toward
managing what a patient believes. The open-label results suggest the honest
version is possible, where a clinician explains that the ritual and relationship
around a treatment have measurable effects and enlists the patient in them rather
than concealing an inert pill. What the evidence does
establish is that the placebo arm of a psychiatric trial is not empty. It contains
a treatment that recruits the brain's own opioid and dopamine systems, that can be
conditioned like a reflex, that survives being described honestly to the patient,
and that accounts for much of the recovery we attribute elsewhere. Subtracting it
away keeps trials clean, but it also throws out the data that would let us
understand how expectation heals.
