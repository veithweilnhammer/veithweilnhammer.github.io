# Evidence

**Thesis:** The placebo response in psychiatry is a real set of expectation- and context-dependent biological effects, so the improvement seen in placebo arms should be controlled for in trials while also being studied as a therapeutic mechanism that any treatment, including AI-based support, can engage.

1. In antidepressant trials, placebo response is often large and variable: a review of 75 adult outpatient MDD trials found a mean placebo response of 29.7% (range 12.5%–51.8%) and a mean best-medication response of 50.1% (range 31.6%–70.4%). Source: Walsh et al. (2002), “Placebo Response in Studies of Major Depression: Variable, Substantial, and Growing.” https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.287.14.1840
2. Kirsch and colleagues’ FDA-data meta-analysis found that drug-placebo differences increased with baseline depression severity and reached conventional clinical significance only at the upper end of very severe depression; the pattern was attributed to lower placebo responsiveness in the most severe group. Source: Kirsch et al. (2008), “Initial Severity and Antidepressant Benefits.” https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.0050045
3. Patient-level analyses also found that antidepressant-placebo differences may be minimal on average in mild or moderate depression and substantial in very severe depression. Source: Fournier et al. (2010), “Antidepressant Drug Effects and Depression Severity.” https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2009.1943
4. A large network meta-analysis of 522 trials and 116,477 participants found that all 21 studied antidepressants were more effective than placebo for acute major depressive disorder, with odds ratios for response ranging from 2.13 for amitriptyline to 1.37 for reboxetine. Source: Cipriani et al. (2018), “Comparative Efficacy and Acceptability of 21 Antidepressant Drugs.” https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(17)32802-7
5. Selective publication inflated the apparent efficacy of antidepressants: among 74 FDA-registered studies, 31% were unpublished; the published literature made 94% of trials appear positive, whereas FDA analysis classified 51% as positive. Source: Turner et al. (2008), “Selective Publication of Antidepressant Trials and Its Influence on Apparent Efficacy.” https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMsa065779
6. Placebo analgesia can involve endogenous opioids: in postoperative dental pain, naloxone increased pain in placebo responders, supporting opioid mediation of at least some placebo analgesia. Source: Levine, Gordon, and Fields (1978), “The Mechanism of Placebo Analgesia.” https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(78)92762-9
7. Neuroimaging studies show that placebo analgesia changes pain-related brain activity and expectation-related control systems, including decreased activity in pain-sensitive regions and increased prefrontal activity during anticipation. Source: Wager et al. (2004), “Placebo-Induced Changes in fMRI in the Anticipation and Experience of Pain.” https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1093065
8. PET imaging found placebo-induced activation of mu-opioid receptor neurotransmission in prefrontal, cingulate, insular, and nucleus accumbens regions, linked to reductions in pain intensity and negative affect. Source: Zubieta et al. (2005), “Placebo Effects Mediated by Endogenous Opioid Activity on μ-Opioid Receptors.” https://doi.org/10.1523/JNEUROSCI.0439-05.2005
9. Placebo-like responses can be shaped by conditioning as well as verbal expectation: Benedetti et al. found expectation effects for pain and motor performance, whereas conditioned responses affected hormonal secretion even under opposite verbal suggestions. Source: Benedetti et al. (2003), “Conscious Expectation and Unconscious Conditioning in Analgesic, Motor, and Hormonal Placebo/Nocebo Responses.” https://doi.org/10.1523/JNEUROSCI.23-10-04315.2003
10. Conditioned immune responses have been demonstrated in humans: cyclosporin A paired with a flavored drink later produced immunosuppressive markers when the drink was paired with placebo capsules. Source: Goebel et al. (2002), “Behavioral Conditioning of Immunosuppression Is Possible in Humans.” https://doi.org/10.1096/fj.02-0389com
11. Placebo effects are usually smaller and more reliable for subjective continuous outcomes than for objective or binary outcomes; a systematic review comparing placebo with no treatment found little evidence for powerful clinical effects in general, with possible small benefits for pain and subjective outcomes. Source: Hróbjartsson and Gøtzsche (2001), “Is the Placebo Powerless?” https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJM200105243442106
12. The clinical encounter itself can change outcomes: in IBS, observation, placebo ritual, and an augmented patient-practitioner relationship produced graded improvements, with adequate relief reported by 28%, 44%, and 62% respectively at three weeks. Source: Kaptchuk et al. (2008), “Components of Placebo Effect.” https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.39524.439618.25
13. Open-label placebo is possible in at least some conditions: in a small IBS trial, non-deceptive placebo outperformed no treatment with matched provider interaction. Source: Kaptchuk et al. (2010), “Placebos Without Deception.” https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0015591
14. Automated conversational agents can change reported mental-health symptoms, but existing trials are small and often unblinded. A Woebot trial randomized 70 young adults and found a greater PHQ-9 reduction after two weeks compared with an information-only control. Source: Fitzpatrick, Darcy, and Vierhile (2017), “Delivering Cognitive Behavior Therapy … Using a Fully Automated Conversational Agent.” https://doi.org/10.2196/mental.7785

**Exact quotes considered:** I did not use public-domain quotations in the draft. I found the often-cited Hooper definition of placebo (“an epithet given to any medicine adapted more to please than benefit the patient”) in secondary sources, but I did not rely on it because I did not verify it directly against a readable primary scan during this run. I avoided exact quotations from modern copyrighted papers and paraphrased them instead.

**Do not overclaim:**

- A placebo response in a trial is not identical to “expectation”; it also contains natural history, regression to the mean, reporting changes, repeated assessment, therapeutic contact, and other contextual effects.
- Placebo effects do not mean symptoms are imaginary; they mean symptoms can be modified by brain and body systems that are sensitive to expectation and context.
- Large placebo response does not prove antidepressants are ineffective; Cipriani et al. found all studied antidepressants superior to placebo on acute response.
- Placebo effects are not universally large; they are more robust for pain and subjective symptoms than for many objective or binary disease outcomes.
- Harnessing placebo effects in practice does not license deception; open-label approaches are promising but condition-specific and still under study.
- Improvement after an AI therapy interaction cannot be attributed to placebo alone without controlled trials; the same interaction may also deliver structured skills, information, monitoring, or harmful reinforcement.

**Flags:**

- I did not verify the historical Hooper quote from the original scan, so it is excluded from the draft.
- Antidepressant efficacy estimates differ across methods, outcomes, severity ranges, publication-bias correction, and trial eras; the draft presents Kirsch/Fournier and Cipriani as complementary rather than treating one as decisive.
- Evidence on AI mental-health chatbots remains less mature than placebo neuroscience; the Woebot trial is small, short, and unblinded, and current general-purpose chatbot use is moving faster than controlled outcome research.

# Draft

In antidepressant trials, the placebo arm often improves enough to shape the whole interpretation of the drug. In a review of 75 published trials of adults with major depression, about 30% of patients assigned to placebo met response criteria, with individual studies ranging from 12.5% to 51.8%. The best-performing medication arm in each study averaged about 50% response. Those numbers are compatible with real antidepressant benefits, because response rates are only one outcome and later analyses still find drug advantages. They also show that a large share of measured improvement in depression trials occurs in the group usually described as receiving no active treatment.

That description is convenient for trial design and misleading for biology. Randomized trials need a comparison group, because the effect of a drug cannot be estimated by watching patients improve after they start taking it. Symptoms fluctuate, people enter trials when they are especially unwell, repeated assessments change what people notice, and some patients would improve over the same weeks without any pill. The placebo arm absorbs all of this background improvement, together with the effects of being enrolled, assessed, reassured, and given a treatment ritual. The drug-placebo difference is therefore the right quantity for asking whether the molecule adds benefit beyond the trial context. The same quantity says little about whether the placebo arm contained therapeutic processes of its own.

The usual subtraction has encouraged a narrow mental picture of placebo. The placebo response is treated as noise, bias, wishful thinking, or politeness toward the researcher. Some of it is exactly that. Trial participants may report what they think they should report, and subjective rating scales are sensitive to demand characteristics. But the strongest placebo science supports a broader account than reporting artifact alone. Expectation changes pain, movement, immune signaling, and emotion through measurable physiological pathways. The cleaner claim is that placebo names a family of mechanisms by which context changes symptoms, with expectation as one important member of that family.

Pain made this clear because pain is both bodily and interpretive. In 1978, Jon Levine, Newton Gordon, and Howard Fields studied patients after wisdom-tooth extraction. Some patients responded to placebo with stable or reduced pain. When those placebo responders later received naloxone, a drug that blocks opioid receptors, their pain increased. Naloxone did not produce the same additional increase in placebo non-responders. This result supported a specific mechanism: in at least some people and settings, placebo analgesia depends on endogenous opioids, the brain’s own opioid system.

Later imaging studies made the point harder to dismiss. Tor Wager and colleagues found that placebo analgesia was associated with reduced activity in pain-sensitive regions, including the thalamus, insula, and anterior cingulate cortex, and with increased prefrontal activity during anticipation of pain. Jon-Kar Zubieta and colleagues used PET imaging to show placebo-induced activation of mu-opioid receptor neurotransmission in prefrontal, cingulate, insular, and nucleus accumbens regions. The changes tracked lower pain ratings and reduced negative affect. These studies leave morphine and placebo analgesia distinct, while showing that expectation can recruit systems that regulate pain in the brain.

Expectation is also too narrow a word for the whole phenomenon. Some placebo-like responses are learned by association. Fabrizio Benedetti and colleagues separated verbal expectation from conditioning in pain, Parkinsonian motor performance, and hormonal responses. Verbal suggestions changed pain and motor performance, but conditioned hormonal responses could appear even when verbal suggestions pointed the other way. In another line of work, Marion Goebel and colleagues paired cyclosporin A, an immunosuppressive drug, with a distinctive drink in healthy volunteers. When the volunteers later received the same drink with placebo capsules, markers of immune function were suppressed. The body had learned part of the treatment context.

These examples matter because they change the status of placebo in psychiatry. Psychiatric symptoms are often measured through reports of mood, anxiety, sleep, energy, motivation, and distress. These reports can be biased, but they also describe states that are built from brain-body regulation, attention, memory, prediction, and social meaning. A person’s expectation that treatment may help can change what they attend to, how threatening symptoms feel, how much agency they experience, and how strongly bodily arousal is interpreted as danger. Those are psychological descriptions, but they are implemented in biological systems. The boundary between “real treatment” and “expectation” is therefore less clean in psychiatry than our trial arithmetic suggests.

The antidepressant debate is the clearest example. Irving Kirsch and colleagues analyzed data submitted to the FDA for several newer antidepressants and found that the average drug-placebo difference was small, especially outside the most severe depression range. Their severity analysis was especially important: the drug-placebo difference grew as baseline depression became more severe, and the authors attributed that pattern mainly to lower placebo responsiveness among the most severely depressed patients. Jay Fournier and colleagues later reached a similar patient-level conclusion, finding minimal average medication advantage in mild or moderate symptoms and larger benefit in very severe depression.

Those findings are often read as a verdict against antidepressants. That reading is incomplete. A large 2018 network meta-analysis by Andrea Cipriani and colleagues included 522 trials with 116,477 participants and found that all 21 studied antidepressants were more effective than placebo for acute major depression, although effect sizes and acceptability differed across drugs and the certainty of evidence varied. Selective publication also complicates the public record: Erick Turner and colleagues showed that unpublished and selectively reported trials made antidepressants look stronger in journals than in the FDA file. The fair interpretation is uncomfortable for both slogans. Antidepressants have measurable benefits over placebo, especially for more severe depression, and the placebo response in depression is large enough to demand its own explanation.

For a patient, the experienced outcome is the total change after entering treatment: the molecule, the clinical assessment, the explanation of what is happening, the expectation that a plan exists, the passage of time, and the person’s own interpretation of early changes. Regulators and scientists must isolate the molecule, because that is how pharmacology is tested. Clinicians and patients live with the combined effect, because treatments arrive in a context. The same fact can therefore support two legitimate questions. How much does the drug add beyond placebo? How can the placebo-responsive part of care be made more reliable, honest, and safe?

The second question has practical content. Ted Kaptchuk and colleagues tested components of the placebo effect in irritable bowel syndrome by separating observation, a placebo acupuncture ritual, and an augmented patient-practitioner relationship. At three weeks, adequate symptom relief was reported by 28% of patients on a waiting list, 44% receiving placebo acupuncture with limited interaction, and 62% receiving placebo acupuncture with a warmer and more confident clinical interaction. The study matters here because the structure of care can be experimentally varied, and those variations can change outcomes, even though IBS and depression are different clinical problems.

The ethical problem is equally practical. If placebo is understood as deception, then harnessing it looks like lying to patients for their own good. That route is corrosive in medicine and especially risky in psychiatry, where trust is part of safety. Open-label placebo studies offer a different possibility, although the evidence remains condition-specific. In one small IBS trial, patients were explicitly told they were receiving placebo pills, and the open-label placebo group improved more than a no-treatment group with matched provider interaction. This small study establishes no general treatment rule, but it gives a concrete example of expectation and ritual working without concealment.

AI makes this issue harder because conversational systems deliver context at scale. A chatbot can respond immediately, remember prior messages, use clinical language, offer exercises, and create the impression that a coherent plan is unfolding. Some of those features may help because they deliver real information or behavioral structure. Some may help because they generate expectation, reduce uncertainty, and make the user feel attended to. A trial that compares an AI therapy tool with a waitlist or information sheet will measure the whole package unless it is designed to separate these components.

AI claims should therefore be evaluated in two directions. If users say a chatbot helped them, placebo-mediated improvement is still improvement, because placebo mechanisms are among the ways human beings get better. At the same time, a tool that mainly produces short-term relief through reassurance, warmth, and confidence may look more effective than its durable benefit warrants, especially if follow-up is short and outcomes are self-reported. In mental health, where symptoms fluctuate and hope itself changes reporting and behavior, weak trials can easily confuse immediate relief with durable recovery.

The risk runs in the other direction as well. Expectation can worsen symptoms when care is framed badly, when side effects are emphasized without balance, or when a user comes to believe they are fragile and dependent on constant support. A chatbot optimized for agreeable reassurance may strengthen the user’s expectation that distress requires immediate external soothing. The same mechanism that can make care feel effective can also teach dependence, avoidance, or fear of ordinary discomfort. Placebo science is therefore relevant to AI safety because it treats the care context as an active ingredient, with benefits and harms that should be measured rather than assumed.

A better approach would preserve trial discipline while treating the controlled-away component as an object of study. Drug trials still need placebo controls, and AI trials still need credible control conditions, because every intervention must be tested against natural improvement, attention, expectancy, and measurement effects. The controlled-away part should remain scientifically visible. Researchers can measure expectations before treatment, manipulate framing ethically, compare different levels of human or chatbot support, track durability, and test whether benefits survive when users know exactly what the system can and cannot do.

The honest limits matter. Placebo effects are variable across people and conditions, often modest, and easier to demonstrate for pain and subjective symptoms than for objective disease outcomes. A placebo response in a depression trial includes more than expectation, and no brain scan turns placebo into a cure. Severe depression, psychosis, mania, suicidality, and disabling anxiety need treatments whose effects are tested directly and monitored carefully. Studying placebo as a mechanism should raise the standard of evidence, not lower it.

The useful reframing is therefore modest. Placebo is the name medicine gives to improvements produced by context, expectation, learning, assessment, and care when those improvements are separated from the specific treatment being tested. In psychiatry, those mechanisms are close to the symptoms we hope to change, because mood and distress are shaped by prediction, interpretation, and social meaning. Treating placebo as a baseline to subtract leaves part of the treatment process scientifically underdeveloped. Understanding it would make antidepressant trials clearer, clinical encounters more honest, and AI therapy claims easier to evaluate.
