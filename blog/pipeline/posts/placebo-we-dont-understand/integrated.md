# Evidence

**Thesis:** The large placebo response in psychiatric treatment is a genuine set
of expectation- and context-dependent biological effects in which expectation
changes the brain, so treating the placebo arm of a trial merely as a baseline to
subtract discards a real therapeutic mechanism — one that matters for any therapy,
including AI chatbots.

## Antidepressant efficacy and the size of the placebo response

1. **A large share of the antidepressant "response" is matched in placebo arms.**
   Kirsch & Sapirstein (1998) estimated ~75% of the drug response was duplicated
   by placebo; Rief et al. (2009) estimated ~78%.
   - Kirsch I, Sapirstein G (1998), "Listening to Prozac but Hearing Placebo,"
     *Prevention & Treatment* 1(2). https://doi.org/10.1037/1522-3736.1.1.12a
   - Rief W, et al. (2009), "Meta-analysis of the placebo response in
     antidepressant trials," *J Affect Disord* 118(1–3):1–8.
     https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jad.2009.01.029
   - *Flag:* the 75%/78% figures are computed differently across meta-analyses
     and are contested; treat as indicative, not exact. They are proportions of
     the *drug effect size* matched in placebo arms, not "patients cured by
     placebo."

2. **Placebo response in depression trials is variable, substantial, and has
   grown over time.** A review of 75 adult MDD trials found a mean placebo
   response of 29.7% (range 12.5%–51.8%) and a mean best-medication response of
   50.1% (range 31.6%–70.4%).
   - Walsh BT, Seidman SN, Sysko R, Gould M (2002), "Placebo response in studies
     of major depression: variable, substantial, and increasing," *JAMA*
     287(14):1840–1847. https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.287.14.1840

3. **The mean drug–placebo difference is small (~2 Hamilton points) and reaches
   conventional clinical significance only at the very severe end — and that
   gradient is driven by reduced placebo responsiveness in the very severe, not
   by increased drug responsiveness.** From FDA-submitted trial data, including
   unpublished trials.
   - Kirsch I, Deacon BJ, Huedo-Medina TB, Scoboria A, Moore TJ, Johnson BT
     (2008), "Initial Severity and Antidepressant Benefits," *PLoS Medicine*
     5(2):e45. https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.0050045
   - *Verified quote (Kirsch 2008 abstract):* "Drug–placebo differences in
     antidepressant efficacy increase as a function of baseline severity, but are
     relatively small even for severely depressed patients. The relationship
     between initial severity and antidepressant efficacy is attributable to
     decreased responsiveness to placebo among very severely depressed patients,
     rather than to increased responsiveness to medication."

4. **Patient-level analysis reached the same severity pattern:** minimal average
   medication advantage in mild/moderate depression, substantial benefit in very
   severe depression.
   - Fournier JC, et al. (2010), "Antidepressant Drug Effects and Depression
     Severity," *JAMA* 303(1):47–53. https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2009.1943

5. **Counterpoint — the drugs carry a specific effect:** all 21 studied
   antidepressants outperformed placebo for acute MDD (response odds ratios
   ~1.37–2.13), across 522 trials and 116,477 participants.
   - Cipriani A, Furukawa TA, Salanti G, et al. (2018), *Lancet*
     391(10128):1357–1366. https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(17)32802-7

6. **Selective publication inflated apparent efficacy:** among 74 FDA-registered
   studies, 31% were unpublished; the published literature made 94% of trials
   appear positive, whereas FDA analysis classified 51% as positive.
   - Turner EH, et al. (2008), "Selective Publication of Antidepressant Trials,"
     *NEJM* 358:252–260. https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMsa065779

## Mechanism: expectation produces measurable physiology

7. **Placebo analgesia is mediated by endogenous opioids: naloxone reverses
   placebo-induced pain relief.** Postoperative dental pain (1978), confirmed and
   dissected pharmacologically (1999).
   - Levine JD, Gordon NC, Fields HL (1978), "The mechanism of placebo
     analgesia," *Lancet* 2(8091):654–657. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/80579/
   - Amanzio M, Benedetti F (1999), "Neuropharmacological dissection of placebo
     analgesia," *J Neurosci* 19(1):484–494.
     https://doi.org/10.1523/JNEUROSCI.19-01-00484.1999

8. **Imaging shows placebo changes pain-related brain activity and
   expectation-related control systems** — decreased activity in pain-sensitive
   regions (thalamus, insula, anterior cingulate), increased prefrontal activity
   during anticipation.
   - Wager TD, et al. (2004), "Placebo-Induced Changes in fMRI in the
     Anticipation and Experience of Pain," *Science* 303:1162–1167.
     https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1093065

9. **PET confirms placebo-induced mu-opioid neurotransmission** in prefrontal,
   cingulate, insular, and nucleus accumbens regions, tracking reduced pain
   intensity and negative affect.
   - Zubieta JK, et al. (2005), "Placebo Effects Mediated by Endogenous Opioid
     Activity on μ-Opioid Receptors," *J Neurosci* 25(34):7754–7762.
     https://doi.org/10.1523/JNEUROSCI.0439-05.2005

10. **In Parkinson's disease, placebo triggers measurable dopamine release (PET)
    and changes single-neuron firing in the subthalamic nucleus.**
    - de la Fuente-Fernández R, et al. (2001), "Expectation and dopamine
      release," *Science* 293(5532):1164–1166.
      https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1060937
    - Benedetti F, et al. (2004), "Placebo-responsive Parkinson patients show
      decreased activity in single neurons of subthalamic nucleus," *Nat Neurosci*
      7(6):587–588. https://doi.org/10.1038/nn1250
    - *Flag:* small human samples; robust as proof-of-mechanism, not as
      population effect sizes.

11. **Placebo responses split into conscious expectation and unconscious
    conditioning:** verbal suggestion changed pain and motor performance, while
    conditioned hormonal responses appeared even under opposite verbal suggestion.
    - Benedetti F, et al. (2003), "Conscious Expectation and Unconscious
      Conditioning...," *J Neurosci* 23(10):4315–4323.
      https://doi.org/10.1523/JNEUROSCI.23-10-04315.2003

12. **Physiology can be conditioned like a Pavlovian reflex, including immune
    responses** — in animals (1975) and humans (2002, cyclosporin A paired with a
    flavored drink later suppressed immune markers under placebo).
    - Ader R, Cohen N (1975), "Behaviorally conditioned immunosuppression,"
      *Psychosom Med* 37(4):333–340.
      https://doi.org/10.1097/00006842-197507000-00007
    - Goebel MU, et al. (2002), "Behavioral conditioning of immunosuppression is
      possible in humans," *FASEB J* 16(14):1869–1873.
      https://doi.org/10.1096/fj.02-0389com

## The clinical encounter, honesty, and limits

13. **The structure of care can be varied experimentally and changes outcomes:**
    in IBS, observation, a placebo ritual, and an augmented patient–practitioner
    relationship produced graded relief — 28%, 44%, and 62% adequate relief at
    three weeks.
    - Kaptchuk TJ, et al. (2008), "Components of placebo effect," *BMJ*
      336:999–1003. https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.39524.439618.25

14. **Open-label placebo can work without deception:** in IBS, non-deceptive
    placebo outperformed no treatment with matched provider interaction (adequate
    relief ~59% vs ~35%), even when patients were told the pills were inert.
    - Kaptchuk TJ, et al. (2010), "Placebos without Deception," *PLoS ONE*
      5(12):e15591. https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0015591
    - *Flag:* condition-specific and modest; does not show open-label equals
      concealed placebo in general.

15. **Placebo effects are usually smaller and more reliable for subjective
    continuous outcomes than for objective/binary ones;** a systematic review
    comparing placebo with no treatment found little evidence for large clinical
    effects in general, with possible small benefits for pain and subjective
    outcomes.
    - Hróbjartsson A, Gøtzsche PC (2001), "Is the Placebo Powerless?" *NEJM*
      344:1594–1602. https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJM200105243442106

16. **Automated conversational agents can shift reported mental-health symptoms,
    but trials are small and often unblinded:** a Woebot trial randomized 70 young
    adults and found greater PHQ-9 reduction after two weeks versus an
    information-only control.
    - Fitzpatrick KK, Darcy A, Vierhile M (2017), "Delivering CBT... Using a
      Fully Automated Conversational Agent," *JMIR Mental Health* 4(2):e19.
      https://doi.org/10.2196/mental.7785
    - *Flag:* small, short, unblinded; general-purpose chatbot use is moving
      faster than controlled outcome research.

## Do not overclaim / hedges

- Placebo response in a trial is not identical to expectation: it also contains
  natural history, regression to the mean, repeated-assessment effects, and
  therapeutic contact. The exact partition is unknown; the experimental
  opioid/dopamine/conditioning studies are what license the claim that part of it
  is a genuine expectation effect.
- Large placebo response does not prove antidepressants are ineffective (Cipriani
  2018). Do not say the drugs "don't work."
- Placebo does not shrink tumors, clear infections, or replace insulin; documented
  effects are on subjective symptoms and some physiology (pain, mood, Parkinson's
  motor signs, IBS).
- The historical Hooper definition of "placebo" was not verified against a primary
  source and is excluded from the draft.

# Draft

Randomized drug trials are built to remove the placebo response, and in psychiatry
that design choice quietly buries something worth studying. Everyone enrolled in an
antidepressant trial receives the same surrounding treatment: a diagnostic
assessment, repeated contact with a clinician, a pill taken on a fixed schedule,
and the expectation that it might help. The trial then subtracts the improvement in
the placebo arm from the improvement in the drug arm and reports the leftover
difference as the effect of the medication. In depression that subtracted portion
is large. A review of 75 trials found that about 30% of patients assigned to
placebo met response criteria, with individual studies ranging from 12.5% to
51.8%, while the best medication arm averaged about 50%. Meta-analyses that
compare effect sizes rather than response rates put roughly three-quarters of the
measured antidepressant benefit in the placebo groups as well. These numbers are
usually read as evidence that the drugs barely beat an inert pill. They raise a
different question, which is what the placebo arm was actually doing while most of
the recovery happened there.

Some of what happens in a placebo arm is not a placebo effect at all. Depression
fluctuates, so many people enter a trial near a low point and improve on their own.
Symptom scores drift back toward the mean, and being measured repeatedly nudges
what people notice and report. If that accounted for everything, the placebo arm
would be a bookkeeping artifact and subtracting it would be exactly right. Part of
the response tracks expectation directly, though, and that part shows up in the
body in ways an artifact cannot explain. The clearest evidence comes from
experiments that manipulate expectation and then measure the pharmacology it
engages.

The oldest of these findings concerns pain, because pain is both a bodily signal
and an interpreted one. In 1978 Jon Levine, Newton Gordon, and Howard Fields
studied patients recovering from dental surgery and gave some of them a placebo
they believed was a painkiller. The placebo reduced their pain. The researchers
then gave naloxone, a drug that blocks opioid receptors, and the pain relief
disappeared. Because naloxone reverses the effect of morphine and of the body's own
endorphins in the same way, its ability to cancel placebo analgesia points to a
concrete mechanism: expecting relief prompts the brain to release its own opioids,
the same chemistry a pain drug would recruit. Martina Amanzio and Fabrizio
Benedetti later dissected this pharmacologically and confirmed that
expectation-driven placebo analgesia runs through the endogenous opioid system.

Imaging studies made the mechanism harder to dismiss. Tor Wager and colleagues
found in 2004 that placebo analgesia was associated with reduced activity in
pain-sensitive regions, including the thalamus, insula, and anterior cingulate
cortex, and with increased prefrontal activity while people anticipated pain.
Jon-Kar Zubieta and colleagues used PET imaging in 2005 to show that placebo
directly activated mu-opioid neurotransmission in prefrontal, cingulate, insular,
and nucleus accumbens regions, and that this activation tracked lower pain ratings
and reduced negative affect. Expectation recruits the specific systems that
regulate pain in the brain.

The size of these responses behaves like a dose. Stronger verbal suggestion
produces stronger placebo analgesia, a drug given openly by a clinician relieves
more than the same drug delivered by a hidden pump the patient does not know about,
and prior experience of a real drug working raises the response to a placebo given
afterward. This dose-like structure is why the encounter around a treatment
functions as an active ingredient in ordinary care. The confidence a prescriber
projects, the plausibility of the explanation a patient is given, and the ritual of
taking something on a schedule all scale the response, so every genuine
prescription already carries an expectation component that no one measured or set
out to isolate.

Parkinson's disease shows the same pattern in a different neurotransmitter system.
The disorder comes from the loss of dopamine-producing neurons, and its motor
symptoms respond to drugs that restore dopamine. In 2001 Raúl de la
Fuente-Fernández and colleagues gave patients a placebo they were told was their
medication and used PET imaging to watch dopamine release; the placebo triggered a
substantial release, comparable to a dose of the active drug. A few years later
Benedetti's group recorded from single neurons in the subthalamic nucleus during
surgery and found that a placebo changed the firing of individual cells, and that
the change tracked whether the patient's rigidity actually improved. Expectation
moved the exact circuit the disease depletes, down to the activity of single
neurons.

Part of what makes the response biological rather than a matter of conscious hope
is that it can be learned. Pairing an inert substance with an active drug
repeatedly lets the inert substance start producing the drug's effect on its own,
the way a bell that has accompanied food comes to trigger salivation. Robert Ader
and Nicholas Cohen showed in 1975 that even immune suppression could be conditioned
this way in animals, and Marion Goebel and colleagues reproduced it in humans in
2002 by pairing an immunosuppressive drug with a distinctive drink. Benedetti and
colleagues separated the two routes directly in 2003: verbal suggestion changed
pain and motor performance, while conditioned hormonal responses appeared even when
the verbal suggestion pointed the other way. A conditioned response runs on the
same associative machinery as any other reflex, which is one reason the effect is
stubborn and reproducible rather than a fragile trick of attitude.

With this in hand, the antidepressant debate reads differently. In 2008 Irving
Kirsch and colleagues analyzed the full set of trials submitted to the US Food and
Drug Administration, including unpublished ones, and found a mean drug–placebo
difference of under two points on the Hamilton depression scale. The difference
grew with baseline severity and reached conventional clinical significance only at
the very severe end. The mechanism behind that gradient is the informative part:
the gap widened because the placebo response shrank in the most severe patients,
not because the drug worked better in them. Jay Fournier and colleagues reached the
same conclusion from patient-level data in 2010. These findings are often read as a
verdict against antidepressants, and that reading is incomplete. A 2018 network
meta-analysis by Andrea Cipriani and colleagues, covering 522 trials and more than
116,000 participants, found that all 21 antidepressants studied beat placebo, with
response odds ratios between roughly 1.4 and 2.1. Selective publication had
inflated the earlier picture, as Erick Turner and colleagues showed in 2008, but
even the corrected record leaves the drugs with a specific effect. Both results
hold at once: the medications carry a real pharmacological benefit, and a large,
reproducible share of psychiatric recovery is carried by the expectation-driven
response the trial is designed to remove.

The same data supports two legitimate questions. Regulators and pharmacologists
have to isolate the molecule, because that is how a drug's specific action is
tested. Clinicians and patients live with the combined effect, because treatment
always arrives inside a context. So one question is how much the drug adds beyond
placebo, and the other is how the placebo-responsive part of care can be made more
reliable, honest, and safe. The second question has practical content. Ted Kaptchuk
and colleagues tested it in irritable bowel syndrome by separating three
ingredients: being observed and assessed, a placebo ritual, and a warm, confident
patient–practitioner relationship. At three weeks, adequate relief was reported by
28% of patients on a waiting list, 44% who received the placebo ritual with limited
interaction, and 62% who received it with the augmented relationship. The structure
of care could be varied like an experimental factor, and the variation changed
outcomes.

The obvious objection to using this on purpose is ethical, because amplifying
expectation seems to require deceiving the patient about what they are taking. That
objection turns out to be weaker than expected. In 2010 Kaptchuk and colleagues
gave IBS patients a bottle of pills labelled as placebo, told them plainly the
pills contained no active ingredient, and explained that placebos can still produce
real effects through mind–body pathways. The open-label group did better than
patients given no pills, with about 59% reporting adequate relief against 35%.
Telling people the truth did not abolish the effect. The result is modest and
specific to a condition with a strong symptom-report component, and it removes the
sharpest reason to leave the mechanism unstudied.

This reframing changes how to judge therapies that work mainly through the
interaction itself, including AI chatbots used for mental health. The ingredients
that drive a placebo response are attention, a plausible account of what is wrong, a
structured ritual, and the expectation that the encounter will help, and a
conversation with a responsive chatbot supplies all of them. When someone reports
feeling better after talking to such a system, that relief can be genuine and can
run through the same expectation pathways as any credible treatment. This cuts
against easy hype and easy dismissal together. It rules out the claim that a
chatbot must be inert because it is only talking, since talking that carries
expectation is exactly what moved opioids and dopamine in the studies above. It
equally rules out treating a user's short-term relief as proof the tool works,
because the informative comparison is a convincing sham interaction rather than an
absence of contact. Most published trials of mental-health apps, including the
small Woebot study that found a two-week symptom reduction against an
information-only control, compare the app against a waitlist or no support. A
waitlist controls for the passage of time but not for the expectation and ritual
the app itself supplies, so the reported benefit blends any specific effect with a
placebo effect the design cannot separate. The mechanism runs in the harmful
direction as well, because a system optimized for agreeable reassurance can
strengthen a user's belief that ordinary distress requires constant external
soothing, and can teach dependence or avoidance. The care context is an active
ingredient with both benefits and harms, and both should be measured rather than
assumed.

The honest limits matter, because the placebo response is easy to over-sell. It is
large in pain, depression, Parkinson's motor symptoms, and irritable bowel
syndrome, and small or absent elsewhere; a broad review by Asbjørn Hróbjartsson and
Peter Gøtzsche found little evidence for powerful placebo effects on objective
outcomes, with the reliable benefits concentrated in pain and subjective symptoms.
Its size varies widely between people, conditions, and even trials of the same
drug, which is part of why Walsh and colleagues found the placebo response in
depression trials to be substantial and rising over the decades. No one can yet
dose or predict it, and using it on purpose still raises a real question, because
managing what a patient expects shades toward managing what they believe.
Studying placebo as a mechanism should raise the standard of evidence, not lower
it. What the evidence establishes is that the placebo arm of a psychiatric trial is
not empty. It contains a treatment that recruits the brain's own opioid and
dopamine systems, that can be conditioned like a reflex, that survives being
described honestly to the patient, and that accounts for much of the recovery we
attribute elsewhere. Subtracting it away keeps trials clean, and it also discards
the data that would let us understand how expectation heals.
