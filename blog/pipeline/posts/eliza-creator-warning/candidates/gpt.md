# Evidence

Thesis: Joseph Weizenbaum's reversal after ELIZA matters because he saw that people could knowingly attach emotional meaning to a simple conversational program, and he concluded that tasks involving care and judgment should remain human responsibilities even when computers can imitate the surface of the interaction.

## Exact quotes considered

- No exact quotations from modern copyrighted sources are used in the draft. The draft paraphrases Weizenbaum's 1966 and 1976 claims, the secretary anecdote, and modern clinical-trial findings rather than reproducing copyrighted passages.
- Short terms used as terms of art: “ELIZA,” “DOCTOR,” “ELIZA effect,” and *Computer Power and Human Reason: From Judgment to Calculation*.

## Do not overclaim

- Do not say ELIZA understood users, diagnosed them, or provided therapy; it transformed text according to rules.
- Do not say Weizenbaum proved that computers can never support mental-health care; his core objection was about substitution for human care and judgment.
- Do not treat the secretary anecdote as a controlled psychological experiment; it is a famous first-person account and later historical touchstone.
- Do not imply that every modern therapy chatbot is unsafe or ineffective; early trials show potential benefits under careful design and oversight.
- Do not turn the post into a general history of AI or a general anthropomorphism essay; the focus is Weizenbaum's reversal.

## Flags

- The secretary's identity remains unverified, and recent historical work emphasizes that the story comes mostly through Weizenbaum's account.
- Exact page references in *Computer Power and Human Reason* vary by edition and scan; the draft relies on the book, MIT's obituary summary, and secondary historical sources rather than quoting page-specific text.
- The phrase “ELIZA effect” is often linked to Weizenbaum's observations, but Douglas Hofstadter's 1995 discussion is a prominent later naming and definition source.
- Evidence on generative-AI therapy chatbots is early and heterogeneous; Therabot was studied under research oversight, which differs from open-ended consumer chatbot use.

# Draft

Joseph Weizenbaum built ELIZA to show how little a computer needed in order to sustain a typed conversation, and the reaction to the program changed what he thought the demonstration meant. In 1966, ELIZA was a technical paper in *Communications of the ACM*, describing a program that analyzed natural-language input through keywords and transformation rules. Ten years later, Weizenbaum published *Computer Power and Human Reason*, a book-length warning about the temptation to replace human judgment with computation. The same object sits at the center of both works. ELIZA began as a demonstration of text processing, then became evidence for Weizenbaum that people could turn a surface imitation of care into something emotionally real.

ELIZA was simple in the way early computer programs often were simple: the mechanism was narrow, explicit, and surprisingly effective within a carefully chosen setting. A user typed a sentence into a terminal. The program scanned the sentence for a keyword, selected a rule associated with that keyword, broke the sentence into pieces, and reassembled those pieces into a reply. If the input was “I am very unhappy these days,” the important structure was “I am ...,” and the response could become a question about how long the user had been unhappy. A representation of unhappiness, biography, or distress was unnecessary for that reply. The rule converted one bit of language into another.

The famous version of ELIZA used a script that came to be known as DOCTOR. It mimicked a Rogerian therapist, a style of non-directive psychotherapy in which the therapist often reflects the client's own words back as prompts for further exploration. This was an ingenious design choice because it made the weakest part of the program look like part of the role. A therapist can ask a client to elaborate without giving factual advice, and a reflective question can sound attentive even when it adds very little. Weizenbaum understood this clearly in the 1966 paper. The user's own interpretation supplied much of the apparent meaning. The program offered a linguistic surface on which the user could project a relationship.

That projection was the part Weizenbaum had underestimated. The story that later defined ELIZA was about his secretary, who had watched him work on the program and therefore knew that it had no understanding hidden behind its replies. When she tried the system, she reportedly asked Weizenbaum to leave the room so she could continue the conversation privately. The detail matters because the reaction did not depend on ignorance in the ordinary sense. The user knew that the machine was a machine. She still treated the exchange as if it deserved privacy.

The term ELIZA effect describes this gap between mechanism and experience. A system can invite human interpretation through the form of conversation even when the person understands, at some level, that the system is only manipulating symbols. In ELIZA's case, the gap between mechanism and response was enormous. The program had no memory in the human sense, no inner model of the user, no capacity to care, and no responsibility for what happened after the exchange. The user experience could still feel personal because language carries social assumptions. A question about sadness sounds different from a line of code because people have learned to treat questions about sadness as signs of attention.

Weizenbaum's later criticism is sometimes flattened into a claim about what computers can or cannot do. His actual concern was sharper. He had spent his career building useful computer systems, from list-processing tools to ELIZA itself, and he understood the practical value of computation from the inside. His objection was aimed at a culture that treated formalization as permission to substitute computers for humans in domains where the human role matters. In *Computer Power and Human Reason*, the contrast in the subtitle is between judgment and calculation. Calculation follows explicit procedures. Judgment involves responsibility, context, values, and respect for the person who is affected by the decision.

Therapy was an obvious boundary case for him because it combines language with care. A therapist generates sentences within a larger practice that includes attention to a person's history, risk, ambivalence, and dignity. The work also involves a relationship in which another human being can be answerable for what they say and do. ELIZA exposed how easily the outer form of that relationship could be copied. Weizenbaum's conclusion was that copying the outer form could make substitution more dangerous, because the copy might satisfy enough of the user's expectations to hide what had been removed.

The same warning applies more strongly to current systems because the surface has improved. ELIZA relied on rules that could be printed and inspected. Modern chatbots generate fluent replies across almost any topic, remember conversational context within a session, adapt tone, and produce language that often sounds warmer than the institutional language people receive from rushed services. The user no longer has to cooperate as much to sustain the illusion. A contemporary model can ask coherent follow-up questions, summarize a user's history, name emotions, and produce a plan that resembles therapeutic guidance.

Weizenbaum's question is easier to test in settings that now matter. People already use general chatbots for emotional support, relationship advice, crisis reassurance, and informal therapy. Surveys of digital mental-health use show that chatbots have entered the same space as mental-health apps and online self-help tools. Clinical research has also moved beyond curiosity. Woebot, an earlier structured conversational agent, showed preliminary benefits in a randomized trial for young adults with depression and anxiety symptoms. Therabot, a generative-AI therapy chatbot tested under research oversight, produced symptom reductions in a recent randomized trial and users reported a therapeutic alliance comparable to what patients often report with human providers.

These systems also differ from ELIZA in the institutional setting around them. A research chatbot can be designed for a defined population, monitored by a study team, constrained by safety procedures, and evaluated with symptom scales. A consumer chatbot is often reached through a general-purpose interface, at the user's moment of need, with unclear boundaries between advice, companionship, and care. The psychological mechanism Weizenbaum noticed can appear in both settings, but the safeguards around the interaction determine how much trust the system is allowed to absorb.

Those findings create the honest tension that Weizenbaum's strongest admirers sometimes avoid. Mental-health care is scarce, expensive, and unevenly distributed. A system that is available at three in the morning, costs little to run, and helps some users practice evidence-based coping skills deserves serious consideration. If a carefully designed chatbot reduces symptoms for people who otherwise receive no help, the moral argument has to account for that benefit. Refusing all machine support in the name of human care can leave real people alone.

At the same time, the access argument leaves the substitution question unresolved. A trial conducted with screening, monitoring, safety procedures, and clinical oversight is a different object from an open-ended consumer chatbot that a distressed person treats as a private therapist. The benefits of a tool do not automatically justify transferring responsibility to the tool. Weizenbaum's point was about that transfer. Once a machine occupies a role that people associate with care, users may disclose more, trust more, and defer more than the system deserves. The risk comes from the match between human vulnerability and conversational form.

The secretary anecdote remains useful because it strips away a comforting explanation. People confided in ELIZA despite the lack of technical complexity. They responded because the interaction had the shape of being listened to. Modern systems intensify the same structure with vastly better language. They can produce empathy markers, remember details, and maintain a supportive tone across long conversations. These improvements may increase usefulness, and they also increase the chance that users experience the system as a caring other rather than as software.

A careful response should separate assistance from replacement. Computers can help with scheduling, psychoeducation, symptom tracking, reminders, structured exercises, and even some forms of guided self-help. They can widen access when human services are unavailable. They can also help clinicians by reducing administrative burden or by offering patients support between appointments. These uses keep responsibility anchored in human systems of care. The harder case begins when the machine becomes the primary confidant, the risk assessor, the judge of what a person's suffering means, or the substitute relationship offered because actual care is too expensive.

Weizenbaum's warning was partly empirical and partly moral. The empirical part says that people attribute understanding to conversational systems more readily than the mechanism warrants. ELIZA gave an early demonstration, and modern chatbots give the problem scale. The moral part says that some roles should be protected because replacing the person changes the meaning of the act. Reasonable people can disagree about where that boundary belongs. They can also agree that the boundary should be drawn deliberately, with evidence about benefits and harms, rather than by commercial deployment and user need.

The lesson from ELIZA is therefore narrower and more durable than a general rejection of artificial intelligence. A program that imitates the language of care can evoke real disclosure and attachment before it has earned the responsibilities that care requires. Weizenbaum saw that reaction in a system whose workings he could explain line by line. The present danger is that we may treat greater fluency as if it solved the problem he identified, when it mainly makes the imitation more persuasive. If machines are used in mental health, the first question should be where human responsibility remains visible, reachable, and accountable. That was the warning ELIZA's creator spent the rest of his life trying to make clear.
