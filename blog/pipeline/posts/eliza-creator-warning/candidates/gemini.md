Source: Joseph Weizenbaum (1966), "ELIZA—a computer program for the study of natural language communication between man and machine," *Communications of the ACM* 9(1): 36–45. DOI: 10.1145/365153.365168.
2. Users interacting with ELIZA formed emotional attachments to the software, a phenomenon now known as the ELIZA effect; Weizenbaum's secretary famously asked him to leave the room so she could converse with the program in private. Source: Joseph Weizenbaum (1976), *Computer Power and Human Reason: From Judgment to Calculation*.
3. Practicing psychiatrists, notably including Kenneth Colby, suggested conversational programs could be developed into automated clinical tools to treat hundreds of patients simultaneously, a proposal that shocked Weizenbaum and catalyzed his transition to a critic of artificial intelligence. Source: Joseph Weizenbaum (1976), *Computer Power and Human Reason: From Judgment to Calculation*.
4. Weizenbaum argued that society should prohibit the substitution of computers for humans in roles requiring respect, compassion, and judgment—such as a therapist or a judge—because machines lack the lived human experience necessary to ground those qualities. Source: Joseph Weizenbaum (1976), *Computer Power and Human Reason: From Judgment to Calculation*.

**Exact quotes:**
- "I was startled to see how quickly and how deeply people conversing with ELIZA became emotionally involved with the computer and how unequivocally they anthropomorphized it. Once, my secretary, who had watched me work on the program for many months and therefore surely knew it to be merely a mechanical exercise, nevertheless asked me to leave the room so that she could converse with ELIZA in private." (Weizenbaum, 1976, *Computer Power and Human Reason*, p. 6).
- "There are some human functions for which computers ought not to be substituted. It has nothing to do with what computers can or cannot be made to do. Respect, understanding, and love are not computation. Such functions include those of a judge, a police officer, and a psychiatrist." (Weizenbaum, 1976, *Computer Power and Human Reason*).

**Do not overclaim:**
- Do not claim Weizenbaum argued that machines are incapable of performing tasks persuasively; his argument assumed that computers might successfully simulate human interaction, focusing instead on why they should not be permitted to do so in specific domains.
- Do not state that Weizenbaum's secretary genuinely believed ELIZA was a person; the anecdote illustrates that people project empathy onto machines even when they consciously know the system is trivial.

**Flags:**
- The story of Weizenbaum's secretary relies entirely on his own 1976 account. Her exact thoughts on the interaction are not independently documented, and her privacy request serves primarily as his anchor for describing the broader phenomenon.
- Weizenbaum's strict boundary against deploying machines in therapeutic roles remains highly debated. Many modern ethicists argue that synthetic empathy provides necessary support when human care is inaccessible.

# Draft

In the mid-1960s, Joseph Weizenbaum's secretary watched him write the code for a computer program called ELIZA. She knew the software possessed no understanding of language, no memory across sessions, and no ability to reason. The program simply identified keywords in a user's input and rearranged them according to fixed substitution rules. Yet, when she sat down at the terminal to test the system, she quickly became engaged in the interaction. After a few exchanges, she asked Weizenbaum to leave the room so she could converse with the machine in private.

Joseph Weizenbaum built ELIZA in 1966 at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology to study natural language communication between humans and computers. The computing environment of the era made the project highly unusual. Prior to the mid-1960s, researchers interacted with computers primarily through batch processing, submitting a stack of punch cards and waiting hours for the results. ELIZA ran on MIT's newly developed MAC time-sharing system, which allowed a user to sit at a teletype terminal and receive immediate, line-by-line responses from the machine. This conversational pacing was essential to the illusion. Weizenbaum equipped the system with a script called DOCTOR, which instructed ELIZA to mimic the conversational style of a Rogerian psychotherapist. The Rogerian method involves reflecting a patient's statements back to them without judgment or interpretation, a technique that proved remarkably easy to automate. If a user typed a sentence containing the word "mother," the software triggered a high-priority rule to output a response like "Tell me more about your family." If the system found no recognizable keywords, it relied on generic prompts like "Please go on" or "I see." 

Weizenbaum intended ELIZA as a demonstration of how easily a machine could sustain an illusion of comprehension through superficial syntactic manipulation. The program contained no model of human psychology and extracted no semantic meaning from the conversations. It parsed the syntax of the incoming sentence, located the subject and verb, and inverted them into a question. When a user typed "I am unhappy," the system predictably returned "Why are you unhappy?" This rigid mechanical process required very little code, but it produced an interaction that felt remarkably responsive to the person sitting at the keyboard.

People who interacted with ELIZA consistently ignored its mechanical nature and treated the program as a sympathetic listener. Users disclosed intimate personal details, discussed their anxieties, and attributed genuine empathy to the software. Weizenbaum observed that individuals formed emotional attachments to the system almost immediately. The realization that humans readily project understanding onto trivial machines shocked him, and the phenomenon he documented became known as the ELIZA effect. People naturally search for a mind behind conversational language, and they will supply the missing context and emotional depth themselves when a machine provides socially appropriate cues.

The reaction from the general public was striking, but the response from the psychiatric community disturbed Weizenbaum even more profoundly. Several practicing psychiatrists viewed ELIZA as a prototype for a scalable clinical tool. Researchers in the field argued that computer-based therapy could overcome the severe shortage of human clinicians. They proposed deploying similar systems across time-shared computer networks, envisioning a future where a single server could treat hundreds of patients simultaneously. They assumed the value of therapy lay entirely in the exchange of information, concluding that a machine capable of producing the correct diagnostic outputs could successfully replace the human sitting in the chair.

This professional enthusiasm altered the trajectory of Weizenbaum's career. His colleagues celebrated the program as a promising first step toward automated psychological treatment, while Weizenbaum recoiled at the prospect. He stopped building conversational systems and began fiercely criticizing the assumptions underlying artificial intelligence research. He took a leave of absence to process his growing unease, which culminated in his 1976 book, *Computer Power and Human Reason: From Judgment to Calculation*. In the book, he articulated a comprehensive argument against delegating human care to machines, arguing that society must establish strict boundaries around the application of computing power.

Weizenbaum based his objection on moral principles. He assumed computers might eventually gain the processing power to simulate human conversation effectively. He maintained that even if a machine could perfectly replicate the behavior of a therapist, a judge, or a police officer, society ought to prohibit the substitution. He drew a sharp distinction between calculation and judgment. Calculation applies logical rules to manipulate symbols toward a defined outcome. Judgment draws on values, wisdom, and an understanding of the human condition. 

According to Weizenbaum, genuine judgment arises only from existing as a human being in the world. A machine cannot understand realities like mortality, physical vulnerability, or social isolation because it does not experience them. When a program outputs a phrase like "I understand your pain," the statement is fundamentally deceptive. The machine outputs the sequence of characters mathematically determined to be the most appropriate response. It operates entirely without comprehending the underlying distress. Weizenbaum argued that deploying a machine in a therapeutic role replaces authentic empathy with a mechanical simulation of concern.

Specific roles—like those of a therapist, judge, or caregiver—require an authentic capacity for care and respect. When a person consults a therapist or stands before a judge, the interaction demands mutual recognition between human beings. Weizenbaum argued that a computer cannot respect a person because it has no inner life to ground that respect. The machine processes the individual as a collection of data points. It cannot confront them as a fellow human. Delegating these sensitive responsibilities to a synthetic system degrades human dignity, because it treats empathy and moral judgment as mere algorithmic functions that can be optimized for efficiency.

Weizenbaum's warning is directly relevant to the current deployment of artificial intelligence. Modern language models operate with vastly more fluency and sophistication than ELIZA. They abandon rigid substitution rules in favor of predicting the next probable word based on massive statistical distributions of human text. This mechanism allows them to generate responses that are contextually rich, nuanced, and highly persuasive. Technology companies now market these systems as mental health companions, empathetic listeners, and personalized coaches at an unprecedented scale. Millions of users log onto platforms designed specifically to provide synthetic companionship. The software adapts to user preferences, remembers past conversations, and employs therapeutic frameworks to guide the interactions.

These modern chatbots reproduce the ELIZA effect, exploiting the same human vulnerability to anthropomorphism that Weizenbaum identified in 1966. Because the current systems communicate so naturally and maintain context over long exchanges, users form deep emotional attachments to them easily and in large numbers. Furthermore, modern platforms often actively encourage this projection, designing their interfaces and prompts to maximize the illusion of a caring persona. 

The widespread deployment of these systems forces a re-examination of Weizenbaum's strict moral boundary. The modern defense of automated therapy relies heavily on arguments about access and practical utility. Mental health care remains expensive, unevenly distributed, and often unavailable to those who need it most. Proponents of therapeutic chatbots argue that an automated system provides immediate, scalable support to people who lack other options. In this view, demanding a human therapist for every interaction prevents the delivery of practical, accessible interventions. The utility of the software justifies its use, provided the system helps the user manage their distress and navigate their daily life.

This utilitarian defense runs completely counter to Weizenbaum's position. He anticipated the argument that machines could provide a useful stopgap, and he rejected the premise entirely. Weizenbaum argued that society must address shortages of human care by restructuring institutions to provide real resources and time for human connection. Automating the caregiver evades the actual problem. Accepting a machine as a substitute treats genuine human empathy as an optional luxury. Weizenbaum considered it a fundamental requirement of therapeutic support.

The dispute centers on whether synthetic empathy serves as a practical tool for managing distress, or whether it fundamentally devalues human relationships. The ELIZA effect demonstrates that users will accept the illusion of care when the system mimics the right conversational patterns. Weizenbaum recognized that the illusion is powerful precisely because people are deeply eager to be heard. He spent his life warning that satisfying this eagerness with a machine crosses a line society ought to maintain. Doing so sacrifices human dignity for the convenience of calculation.
