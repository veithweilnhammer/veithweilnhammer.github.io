# Brief: ELIZA's Creator Spent His Life Warning Us About ELIZA

## Slug

## Working title
ELIZA's Creator Spent His Life Warning Us About ELIZA

## One-line angle (also the Substack subtitle / SEO hook)
Joseph Weizenbaum built the first therapy chatbot in 1966, watched people confide in it despite knowing it was a trick, and spent the rest of his life arguing we should not do this.

## Single argument (thesis)
Joseph Weizenbaum, who built ELIZA in 1966, became its fiercest critic because he saw that people formed real emotional attachments to a program they knew was simple, and his specific reversal — from builder to critic — is a warning about delegating human care to machines that remains directly relevant.

## Concrete anchor (optional — a real example to ground the argument)
Weizenbaum's own account of watching his secretary ask him to leave the room so she could talk to ELIZA privately — a person who knew exactly how trivial the program was, yet treated it as a confidant.

## What this post must cover
- What ELIZA was: a 1966 program whose DOCTOR script mimicked a Rogerian therapist by reflecting users' statements back as questions, with no understanding of anything.
- Weizenbaum's shock: people, including those who knew how it worked, disclosed intimate feelings and attributed understanding to it — the phenomenon that came to be called the "ELIZA effect."
- The specific reversal: Weizenbaum went from building the system to writing *Computer Power and Human Reason* (1976), arguing there are domains — those requiring care, judgment, and human respect — where computers should not be substituted for people, even if they can be.
- His actual argument, kept precise: not that machines can't do things, but that some tasks (therapy, judgment about human problems) should not be handed to them regardless of capability.
- Why the reversal matters now: today's therapy chatbots reproduce the ELIZA effect at vastly greater fluency and scale, so his objection is more testable and more pressing, not less.
- The honest tension: Weizenbaum's stance was partly moral rather than empirical, and reasonable people dispute where the line falls — engage that rather than treating him as simply vindicated.
