# Evidence

Thesis: Engineered hesitations in AI voices borrow the vocal signs people normally use to infer a speaker's effort, presence, and sincerity, so their main design risk is manufactured trust disguised as speech quality.

1. Human filled pauses are structured signals in spontaneous speech, with “uh” and “um” marking different expected delays in speech planning. Source: Herbert H. Clark & Jean E. Fox Tree, 2002, “Using uh and um in spontaneous speaking.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1016/S0010-0277(02)00017-3
2. Listeners use prosody and filled pauses to infer another speaker's knowledge state and confidence during question answering. Source: Susan E. Brennan & Maurice Williams, 1995, “The Feeling of Another's Knowing: Prosody and Filled Pauses as Cues to Listeners about the Metacognitive States of Speakers.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1006/jmla.1995.1017
3. Hesitations affect real-time comprehension: listeners use disfluency to predict that a speaker may be referring to something new or harder to name. Source: Jennifer E. Arnold, Maria Fagnano & Michael K. Tanenhaus, 2003, “Disfluencies signal theee, uh, new information.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1023/A:1021980931292
4. Hesitation during answering has long been studied as a cue to monitoring cognition, especially uncertainty and retrieval difficulty. Source: Edward E. Smith & Herbert H. Clark, 1993, “On the course of answering questions.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1016/S0010-0277(05)80006-6
5. Hearing a person's voice can increase attribution of thoughtfulness and human mind compared with reading the same words as text, because speech carries paralinguistic cues absent from text. Source: Juliana Schroeder, Michael Kardas & Nicholas Epley, 2017, “The Humanizing Voice: Speech Reveals, and Text Conceals, a More Thoughtful Mind in the Midst of Disagreement.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1177/0956797617713798
6. OpenAI's GPT-4o system card treats audio as a modality that may heighten anthropomorphization and emotional reliance, and explicitly warns that a human-like, high-fidelity voice may contribute to miscalibrated trust. Source: OpenAI, 2024, “GPT-4o System Card.” Link: https://cdn.openai.com/gpt-4o-system-card.pdf
7. OpenAI's GPT-4o launch positioned the system as a real-time multimodal model that accepts and generates audio directly, with conversational response times close to human turn-taking. Source: OpenAI, 2024, “Hello GPT-4o.” Link: https://openai.com/index/hello-gpt-4o/
8. Commercial expressive TTS systems now give developers explicit control over human-like nonverbal cues. ElevenLabs v3 Audio Tags include examples such as `[sighs]`, `[laughs]`, `[pause]`, and delivery controls for pacing and emotion. Source: Ryan Morrison / ElevenLabs, 2025, “What are Eleven v3 Audio Tags — and why they matter.” Link: https://elevenlabs.io/blog/v3-audiotags
9. Hume's Empathic Voice Interface markets a real-time voice AI that measures vocal prosody and responds with an empathic, naturalistic tone of voice. Source: Hume AI documentation, “Speech-to-Speech (EVI).” Link: https://dev.hume.ai/docs/speech-to-speech-evi/overview.md
10. Speech-synthesis research treats filled-pause placement as a modeling target for spontaneous speech, while also showing that poorly placed filled pauses can degrade perceived quality. Source: Yuta Matsunaga, Takaaki Saeki, Shinnosuke Takamichi & Hiroshi Saruwatari, 2022, “Empirical Study Incorporating Linguistic Knowledge on Filled Pauses for Personalized Spontaneous Speech Synthesis.” Link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.07559
11. People apply social rules to computers in ways that can be automatic and mindless, making voice cues especially important in human-computer interaction. Source: Clifford Nass & Youngme Moon, 2000, “Machines and Mindlessness: Social Responses to Computers.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1111/0022-4537.00153

Exact quotes I might use:

Do not overclaim:

- Do not claim that every pause in AI speech is deceptive; some pauses improve turn-taking, comprehension, latency masking, and accessibility.
- Do not claim that human disfluencies always increase trust; they can also signal uncertainty, incompetence, or low confidence, depending on context and frequency.
- Do not claim that current AI systems have no internal delay; they have computation, routing, and latency constraints, but these constraints do not require human-like breaths or filler words.
- Do not claim that synthetic disfluencies have been proven to “bypass critical defenses” in all listeners; the strongest evidence supports the component mechanisms: listeners use disfluencies socially, voice increases mind attribution, and companies are adding controllable paralinguistic cues.
- Do not retell the general anthropomorphism argument; keep the focus on engineered vocal disfluency and trust cues.

Flags:

- OpenAI's public system card discusses human-like voice, anthropomorphization, emotional reliance, and miscalibrated trust, but it does not give a feature list of breaths, “ums,” or laughter in Advanced Voice Mode. The commercial and user-facing evidence for those concrete cues is stronger in demos, reviews, and comparable TTS documentation than in OpenAI's formal documentation.
- Direct experimental evidence that synthetic “ums” specifically lower users' critical scrutiny of AI advice is limited. The argument relies on converging evidence from psycholinguistics, mind perception from voice, and commercial design practice.
- ElevenLabs v3 Audio Tags are a 2025 source, so they are best used as evidence of the broader design trend, not as a 2024 example.

# Draft

The new generation of AI voice systems can add the sounds people normally associate with a person thinking while speaking. A voice assistant can pause before answering, breathe between clauses, laugh softly after a joke, or place an “um” before a delicate suggestion. These sounds carry little propositional information, but they change the kind of event the listener thinks is happening. The same sentence heard in a smooth synthetic voice can feel like output from a machine; heard with a small hesitation and a conversational tone, it can feel like the response of someone present in the exchange.

This shift matters because hesitation has a function in human speech. People rarely produce spoken language as a clean transcript. They search for words, revise plans mid-sentence, manage turn-taking, and monitor whether the listener is following. Pauses, filled pauses, repairs, and breaths are part of that process. They make speech less efficient as text, but more informative as interaction. A listener hears more than the words. The timing and delivery say something about how the speaker is producing them.

Psycholinguists have studied this for decades. Herbert Clark and Jean Fox Tree argued that “uh” and “um” act as signals in spontaneous speech, with “uh” tending to mark a shorter delay and “um” a longer one. Susan Brennan and Maurice Williams showed that listeners use prosody and filled pauses to infer a speaker's knowledge state. Jennifer Arnold and colleagues found that a hesitation can guide listeners' attention toward a referent that is new or harder to describe. These effects are small in daily life because they are routine, but they show that disfluency is processed as evidence about a mind at work.

That evidence is useful because human speech is constrained by real processes. A person who pauses before answering may be retrieving a memory, choosing a safer formulation, suppressing a more impulsive reply, or trying to be accurate. A breath marks a body that must manage air while speaking. A self-correction shows that the speaker monitored what they just said and changed course. None of these cues gives direct access to sincerity or truth, and all can be performed by skilled speakers. Still, in ordinary conversation they are coupled often enough to effort and attention that listeners use them automatically.

Synthetic voice design separates these cues from the biological processes that normally produce them. A language model has processing costs and latency, but it has no lungs, no vocal tract, and no need to buy time with an “um” while a word becomes available. When a synthetic voice breathes or hesitates, the sound is part of the generated performance. It may be produced by an end-to-end audio model, a text-to-speech layer, a voice style setting, or an instruction in a developer tool. In each case, the system can place the cue because it improves the perceived interaction, even when the cue has no matching human process behind it.

OpenAI's Advanced Voice Mode made this design direction visible to a mass audience. GPT-4o was introduced as a model that could handle audio directly and respond in conversational time, replacing the older sequence of speech recognition, text generation, and speech synthesis. OpenAI's own system card treats the audio modality as a social risk as well as a product feature. It notes that human-like audio can heighten anthropomorphization and emotional reliance, and that high-fidelity voice may worsen miscalibrated trust. Those warnings are useful because they acknowledge the central issue: voice changes how users calibrate the system.

Comparable products make the design logic even more explicit. ElevenLabs v3 lets creators put tags such as `[sighs]`, `[laughs]`, and `[pause]` into a script to control how an AI voice performs the line. Hume's Empathic Voice Interface describes a system that measures vocal prosody and responds with an empathic, naturalistic tone. These tools sell control over delivery, pacing, emotional tone, and nonverbal vocal behavior because those features make synthetic speech feel less like reading and more like conversation.

Naturalness has real value. A voice that leaves sensible gaps is easier to interrupt and easier to follow. Pauses can prevent a fast synthetic monologue from becoming tiring. Expressive tone can help a visually impaired user, a child, or an older adult understand whether the system is asking a question, giving an instruction, or acknowledging confusion. In many settings, a flat voice is worse design because it forces the user to do extra work. The concern begins when naturalness is used to simulate the specific cues by which people judge effort, care, and sincerity.

The effect is clearest at moments of uncertainty. A system can say, “I think you should speak to a doctor,” in a neutral voice. It can also pause, lower its pitch, and add a small hesitation before the same recommendation. The wording is identical, while the second version invites a different interpretation. It sounds as though the speaker is weighing the advice carefully, perhaps with reluctance, perhaps with concern. In a human clinician or friend, that delivery might reflect a real social and cognitive state. In an AI system, the delivery may have been selected because it makes the advice feel more careful.

This matters because speech is a privileged channel for mind perception. Juliana Schroeder, Michael Kardas, and Nicholas Epley found that hearing a person speak can make them seem more thoughtful and mentally present than reading the same words. The voice carries timing, intonation, and emphasis, and listeners treat those cues as signs of a person behind the message. Synthetic voices inherit that channel. When they gain the timing and imperfections of spontaneous speech, they also gain access to the inferences people usually reserve for other humans.

This changes how much scrutiny the answer receives. Text on a screen is easy to inspect as an artifact. A reader can reread a sentence, notice a hedge, compare claims, and remember that the output came from a model. Voice arrives as a social event unfolding in time. The listener has to keep up, respond, and manage the interaction. A hesitant voice increases the sense that there is someone on the other side doing the same thing. That sense can make the answer feel less like generated text requiring evaluation and more like advice from an interlocutor who is trying.

The risk is especially sharp in products that handle emotional, medical, financial, or intimate decisions. In those settings, trust is part of the service being delivered. A calm pause before a reassurance, a breath before an apology, or a soft laugh after a vulnerable disclosure can all make the system feel attuned. The words may still be safe and accurate, but the voice adds a layer of implied presence. Users may disclose more, challenge less, or accept a recommendation with less checking because the interaction feels socially grounded.

The mechanism follows from ordinary listening, without any claim that users are foolish or that machines are conscious. Human beings learned speech as an interaction with other people, and the cues of speech production are among the tools used to navigate that interaction. A filler word says that the speaker is holding the floor while planning. A repair says that the speaker caught an error. A breath and a pause help organize attention. When these cues are synthesized, they continue to activate the habits of interpretation that make conversation possible.

Designers therefore need a more specific vocabulary than “natural voice.” Some naturalistic cues help comprehension, while others simulate mental states. A pause that gives the user time to interrupt is a usability feature. A hesitation inserted before an emotionally loaded answer is a trust cue. A brief processing delay caused by computation is a system constraint. A filled pause generated to make the delay feel thoughtful is a social signal. These distinctions matter because regulation and product review usually focus on the semantic content of the answer, while the persuasive force may sit in the delivery.

One practical norm would be to treat disfluency as a design element that requires justification. If a system inserts breaths, sighs, or hesitation, the developer should be able to say what user need the cue serves. Accessibility, turn-taking, and comprehension are legitimate answers. Making advice feel more sincere is a different answer and should be evaluated as persuasion. Products that give health guidance, companionship, education, or financial advice should be especially cautious about simulated uncertainty and concern, because those cues can make an automated recommendation feel personally considered.

A second norm would be to preserve calibration. Users should know when they are hearing a generated performance separated from any real-time human process. Product-level honesty matters more than a disclosure before every sentence. The product should avoid building its authority from cues that imply human effort where no such effort exists. A system can sound clear, warm, and easy to use without pretending to search for a word, swallow emotion, or breathe before difficult advice.

The broader point is that the interface is becoming part of the argument. The safety of an AI answer depends on factual accuracy, refusal behavior, and domain limits, but voice adds another layer: how the answer asks to be received. Engineered hesitation sits in that layer. It borrows the acoustic signs of effort from human conversation and attaches them to machine output. Used carefully, those signs can make voice systems easier to use. Used to manufacture sincerity, they make the machine feel more trustworthy than its underlying judgment deserves.
