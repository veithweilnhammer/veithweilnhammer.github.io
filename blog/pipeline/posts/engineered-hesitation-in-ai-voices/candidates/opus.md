# Evidence

**One-sentence thesis:** The breaths, pauses, and filler sounds engineered into
current AI voices reproduce the human cues that listeners automatically read as
signs of a mind thinking in real time, and because those cues normally certify
effort and presence, adding them to a system that has neither works to lower the
listener's guard at exactly the moments when its claims deserve scrutiny.

## Key claims and sources

1. **Filled pauses ("um"/"uh") are conventional words that speakers plan and
   produce to announce an upcoming delay in speaking.** Clark, H. H., & Fox Tree,
   J. E. (2002). Using uh and um in spontaneous speaking. *Cognition*, 84(1),
   73–111. PMID 12062148. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12062148/
   — Verified abstract: "speakers use uh and um to announce that they are
   initiating what they expect to be a minor (uh), or major (um), delay in
   speaking… uh and um are conventional English words, and speakers plan for,
   formulate, and produce them just as they would any word."

2. **Listeners use filled pauses to infer a speaker's metacognitive state (how
   well the speaker knows the answer).** Brennan, S. E., & Williams, M. (1995).
   The Feeling of Another's Knowing: Prosody and Filled Pauses as Cues to
   Listeners about the Metacognitive States of Speakers. *Journal of Memory and
   Language*, 34(3), 383–398. DOI 10.1006/jmla.1995.1017.
   https://doi.org/10.1006/jmla.1995.1017 — Title and metadata verified via
   Crossref. Finding: filled pauses and rising intonation before an answer led
   listeners to judge the speaker less likely to actually know the answer, i.e.
   the hesitation is read as a genuine window into the speaker's certainty.

3. **Hesitation before a word changes how listeners process and remember it, and
   the effect is fast and largely automatic.** Corley, M., MacGregor, L. J., &
   Donaldson, D. I. (2007). It's the way that you, er, say it: Hesitations in
   speech affect language comprehension. *Cognition*, 105(3), 658–668. PMID
   17173887. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17173887/ — Verified abstract: an
   N400 effect for unpredictable words "was reduced in cases where the target
   words were preceded by a hesitation marked by the word er," and "words
   preceded by disfluency were more likely to be remembered."

4. **Hesitations orient listeners' attention (direct ERP evidence).** Collard, P.,
   Corley, M., MacGregor, L. J., & Donaldson, D. I. (2008). Attention orienting
   effects of hesitations in speech: evidence from ERPs. *Journal of Experimental
   Psychology: LMC*, 34(3), 696–702. PMID 18444766.
   https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18444766/ — Verified abstract: "Hesitations
   orient listeners' attention, with consequences for the immediate processing and
   later representation of an utterance."

5. **People apply human social responses to media and computers automatically,
   even when they know the source is not a person.** Reeves, B., & Nass, C.
   (1996). *The Media Equation: How People Treat Computers, Television, and New
   Media Like Real People and Places*. CSLI/Cambridge University Press. (Classic
   secondary anchor; used for the "social reflex" mechanism, not a specific
   number.)

6. **OpenAI's GPT-4o audio interface adds human-like paralinguistic delivery
   (expressive prosody, breaths, laughter, shifts in pace) as a naturalness
   feature.** OpenAI (2024). *Hello GPT-4o.*
   https://openai.com/index/hello-gpt-4o/ — Announcement of the emotive,
   real-time voice model behind Advanced Voice Mode.

7. **OpenAI itself flags that a human-like, high-fidelity voice can raise
   anthropomorphization and "miscalibrated trust," and reports users forming
   emotional bonds with the voice.** OpenAI (2024). *GPT-4o System Card*, §5.1
   "Anthropomorphization and Emotional Reliance." arXiv:2410.21276.
   https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.21276 — Verified exact text (§5.1): "This risk may
   be heightened by the audio capabilities of GPT-4o, which facilitate more
   human-like interactions with the model." "Generation of content through a
   human-like, high-fidelity voice may exacerbate these issues, leading to
   increasingly miscalibrated trust." "we observed users using language that might
   indicate forming connections with the model. For example, this includes
   language expressing shared bonds, such as 'This is our last day together.'"

8. **OpenAI classified GPT-4o as "medium" overall risk, driven by persuasion,
   which it rated "borderline medium risk" and evaluated on the audio capability
   as well as text.** OpenAI (2024). *GPT-4o System Card*, §3.4 Preparedness
   Framework Evaluations. arXiv:2410.21276. https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.21276 —
   Verified text: "the Safety Advisory Group recommended classifying GPT-4o before
   mitigations as borderline medium risk for persuasion, and low risk in all
   others… the overall risk score for GPT-4o is classified as medium," and
   "persuasion was also evaluated on audio capabilities."

## Do not overclaim

- Do **not** claim experiments prove that synthetic disfluencies specifically
  "bypass critical thinking" or measurably increase belief in AI voices. No such
  study is cited here; the trust mechanism is a reasoned synthesis of (a) what
  disfluencies signal in humans and (b) OpenAI's own note about miscalibrated
  trust. Frame it as an argument, not a measured result.
- Do **not** claim disfluency universally makes speakers seem *more* trustworthy.
  The robust finding (Brennan & Williams) is that fillers signal *lower* certainty
  / not knowing; folk beliefs about "um means honest" or "um means lying" are
  weak and context-dependent.
- Do **not** claim GPT-4o has a discrete "um-inserting module." The audio is
  generated end-to-end; describe the paralinguistic features (breaths, laughs,
  pauses) as produced for naturalness, without inventing an architecture.
- Do **not** claim naturalness is inherently deceptive. Clearer prosody genuinely
  aids comprehension; the concern is narrowly about simulated sincerity cues at
  trust-bearing moments.
- Avoid re-telling the general "anthropomorphism reflex" account or any rights /
  attention-economy framing (owned by sibling posts).

## Flags (could not fully verify / sources disagree)

- The claim that added disfluency *lowers scrutiny of content* is not directly
  tested in the literature I found. It is inferred. Flagged as argument.
- The empirical relationship between disfluency and perceived honesty/deception is
  genuinely mixed across studies; I deliberately avoid resting the argument on it
  and lean instead on the better-established "signals a real-time thinking mind"
  reading.
- The `hello-gpt-4o` page is JavaScript-rendered and could not be scraped as clean
  text; its content (expressive, emotive real-time voice) is well documented and
  uncontroversial, but I did not extract a verbatim sentence from it. The System
  Card (arXiv) text *was* verified verbatim.
- Reeves & Nass (1996) is cited from established secondary knowledge of the book's
  central finding, not from a re-read of the primary text in this session.

# Draft

The voice in OpenAI's Advanced Voice Mode draws breath before a long sentence,
drops in a short laugh, and pauses in the middle of a clause as though it were
searching for the next word. None of these sounds carry information about the
answer it is giving, and the underlying system does not need any of them to
produce speech. They are added on purpose, as part of what the developers call
natural delivery, and they work on a specific human reflex: we hear a breath or a
hesitation and read it as the sound of a mind thinking in real time. What happens
to that reflex when the thinking it reports is not taking place is the question
worth sitting with.

In human speech, disfluencies are reliable by-products of the work of talking.
Herbert Clark and Jean Fox Tree showed that "um" and "uh" are
conventional words, planned and produced like any other, that a speaker uses to
announce a coming delay — a short one with "uh," a longer one with "um." Their
evidence came from large collections of recorded conversation, where speakers turn
out to fit these words into the flow of speech deliberately, sometimes attaching
them to the previous word as in "and-uh," and sometimes drawing them out when the
delay ahead is longer. The speaker's planning has fallen behind their speaking,
and the filled pause marks the gap while they retrieve a word or decide what to
say. A breath before a long sentence works the same way, as the physical cost of
producing a stretch of speech. In each case the audible event is tied to something
happening inside the speaker at that moment, which is why the cue is hard to fake
convincingly in real conversation: a person who is fluent and certain does not
generate the delay that "um" is there to cover.

Listeners use these markers, and they use them without deciding to. Susan Brennan
and Maurice Williams asked people to judge whether a speaker knew the answer to a
question, and found that a filled pause before the answer led listeners to judge
the speaker less likely to actually know it. The hesitation was read as a window
into the speaker's own sense of their knowledge, and it was a reasonably accurate
window, because a person who is unsure really does hesitate more. The processing
runs faster than deliberate judgment. Martin Corley and colleagues recorded brain
responses as people heard sentences, and found that a hesitation before an
unexpected word smoothed the way that word was integrated and made it more likely
to be remembered later; a follow-up study traced the effect to attention, with
hesitations pulling the listener's focus toward whatever came next. These
responses appear within a few hundred milliseconds, before any conscious appraisal
of the speaker.

Taken together, this makes disfluency a form of evidence. A breath, a pause, an
"um" each covary with something real in the speaker: the load of composing speech,
the degree of certainty, the effort of finding a word. Because the correlation is
dependable, a listener can treat the audible signal as a readout of a hidden
mental state without any intermediate reasoning, and mostly without noticing that
they are doing it. This is the mechanism the newest synthetic voices reach for.

The problem with copying these cues into a synthetic voice is that the process
that gave them meaning is missing. A text-to-speech system composes its whole
utterance before it produces a sound. There is no planning bottleneck that trails
the delivery, no retrieval that stalls and recovers, no uncertainty being resolved
as the sentence unfolds. When such a system breathes or pauses mid-clause, the
pause has been placed there by the rendering, timed to fall where a human's would
fall. The audible cue is present, and the internal state it normally reports is
absent.

Adding those cues is a deliberate design choice, and it is defended in ordinary
product terms. OpenAI presented GPT-4o's voice as expressive and emotive, able to
shift pace, laugh, and deliver speech that sounds close to conversation, and
naturalness of this kind is a genuine usability gain rather than a trick on its
own. Smoother prosody and well-placed pauses help a listener parse a long spoken
answer. The difficulty is that the same features that aid comprehension also
engage the machinery by which we register another mind. Byron Reeves and Clifford
Nass documented decades ago that people respond to computers and media with the
social reflexes they use on each other — politeness and misplaced trust —
automatically, and even when they know perfectly well that there is
no person there. A voice that seems to pause and think recruits that response more
strongly than flat synthetic speech, because hesitation is one of the cues by
which we sense another mind as present and engaged. This response is especially
hard to resist with voice, because speech arrives in time, one word after another,
so a gap in it is legible in a way a gap in writing is not; a written draft hides
the pauses that went into it, while a spoken sentence exposes its own timing. The
listener hears what is said and also the pace at which it is assembled, and that
pace is where the cues of effort and hesitation live.

The effect of engaging that response is a shift in how the output gets treated.
Text on a screen is read as output: something a system generated, which can be
reread, checked against other sources, and doubted. A voice that appears
to compose its answer in the moment invites the stance we take toward a
conversational partner, in which we track sincerity and presence and extend a
working level of trust that lets the exchange move forward. As a voice comes to
sound more like an effortful, present speaker, its content is less likely to be
held at arm's length as machine-generated text, and more likely to be received the
way we receive what a person tells us in conversation.

A concrete case makes the shift easier to see. Someone asks a voice assistant
whether a particular chest symptom needs urgent attention, and the assistant
answers, "Hmm… let me think — I'd say it's probably nothing to worry about."
The short "hmm" and the pause read as weighing, as a mind sizing up a hard call
before committing to it. The system, though, arrived at that answer the way it
arrives at every answer, with no moment of weighing that the pause could report.
A listener who hears deliberation is more inclined to accept the conclusion and
less inclined to do the thing the situation calls for, which is to treat an
automated triage judgment with suspicion and check it against a clinician. The
hesitation has done work that its content did not earn.

OpenAI describes this consequence in its own documentation. The GPT-4o system card
notes that anthropomorphization "may be heightened by the audio capabilities" of
the model, and that content delivered "through a human-like, high-fidelity voice"
can lead to "increasingly miscalibrated trust." During early testing the company
observed users speaking to the model as to a companion, including one who said
"This is our last day together." OpenAI files these observations under
anthropomorphization and emotional reliance and flags them for further study. The
paralinguistic features that drive the effect are the same breaths, laughs, and
hesitations that were engineered in to make the voice feel natural.

The honest limit of this concern is worth stating plainly, because naturalness
does real good. Much of what makes these voices pleasant is unobjectionable:
clearer intonation, sensible pacing, a rise that marks a question or the end of a
list. The narrow worry is the deliberate simulation of sincerity cues at the
moments when a listener is deciding how much to trust what they hear. A real
hesitation covaries with the speaker's knowledge state, which is why a careful
listener can learn something from it. A synthetic hesitation covaries with nothing
about the reliability of the answer, because it was timed for effect rather than
produced by any difficulty. When it lands just before a medical suggestion or a
word of reassurance, it lends the felt weight of deliberation
to content that was generated with none. OpenAI's own risk assessment gives this
some weight: the company rated GPT-4o "medium" risk overall, a rating driven
entirely by persuasion, which it flagged as borderline and evaluated on the voice
capability as well as on text. A voice that can be persuasive, and that carries
cues telling the listener a sincere mind stands behind the words, is persuasive in
part through those cues.

The reflex being borrowed here is old and useful. Reading effort and presence in
another person's voice helps us judge whom to believe and how closely to listen,
and it works because in people the cue and the state behind it travel together.
Engineering disfluency into an AI voice reproduces the cue while the state it
normally reports stays absent, so the instinct fires on a signal that carries no
information about whether the answer is sound. A natural-sounding system can still
be an accurate and useful one, and the pause is no reason on its own to distrust
it. The pause is a setting in a speech model, chosen so the voice will feel like a
person, and it tells you nothing about whether the voice is right. Keeping that
distinction in mind is the whole of the defense: hear the hesitation, notice that
it was designed, and give the answer the same scrutiny you would give the same
words on a screen.
