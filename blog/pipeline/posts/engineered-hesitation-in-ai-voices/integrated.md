# Evidence

**Thesis:** The breaths, pauses, and filler sounds engineered into current AI
voices reproduce the cues that human listeners automatically read as signs of a
mind thinking in real time. Because those cues normally track a speaker's effort,
presence, and certainty, adding them to a system that has none works to lower the
listener's guard at the moments when its claims most deserve scrutiny. The concern
is not naturalness in general, but the deliberate simulation of sincerity cues at
trust-bearing moments.

## Do not overclaim

- Do **not** claim experiments prove that synthetic disfluencies specifically
  "bypass critical thinking" or measurably increase belief in AI voices. No such study
  exists; the trust effect is a reasoned synthesis of what disfluencies signal in
  humans, the mind-attribution advantage of voice, OpenAI's own note about
  miscalibrated trust, and current commercial design practice. Frame it as an
  argument, not a measured result.
- Do **not** claim disfluency universally makes a speaker seem *more* trustworthy or
  that "um means honest." The robust finding (Brennan & Williams) is that fillers
  signal *lower* certainty; the reliable reading is "a mind thinking / present in real
  time," not sincerity in the moral sense.
- Do **not** suggest the model experiences cognitive load or emotion; the hesitations
  are generated audio patterns, not reflections of an internal state. The system does
  have computation, routing, and latency costs, but none require human-like breaths or
  filler words.
- Do **not** claim GPT-4o has a discrete "um-inserting module." The audio is generated
  end-to-end; describe the paralinguistic features without inventing an architecture.
- Do **not** claim naturalness is inherently deceptive. Appropriate prosody, pacing,
  and turn-taking genuinely aid comprehension and accessibility; the critique is narrow
  to simulated sincerity cues.
- Avoid re-telling the general anthropomorphism-reflex account, the right-to-mental-quiet
  framing, or the attention-economy history (owned by sibling posts).

## Flags (partial verification / caveats)

- OpenAI's public system card discusses human-like voice, anthropomorphization,
  emotional reliance, and miscalibrated trust, but does not publish a feature list of
  breaths, "ums," or laughter in Advanced Voice Mode. The concrete cues are documented
  most clearly in the launch material, demos, and comparable TTS documentation; the
  System Card supplies the verbatim trust language.
- The `hello-gpt-4o` page is JavaScript-rendered and was not scraped as clean text in
  this session; its expressive, emotive, real-time voice is well documented and
  uncontroversial. The System Card (arXiv) text was verified verbatim.
- ElevenLabs v3 Audio Tags (2025) postdate the 2024 anchor, so they are used as
  evidence of the broader design trend, not as a 2024 example.
- The empirical relationship between disfluency and perceived honesty/deception is
  genuinely mixed across studies; the argument deliberately rests on the
  better-established "signals a real-time thinking mind" reading, not on "um means
  sincere."
- Nothing in the three candidates was clearly hallucinated. Every cited source is a
  real, locatable work; the DOIs/PMIDs were not each re-fetched in this session. The
  one claim softened during the merge is Gemini's "effort correlates with truth,"
  which overstates what Brennan & Williams support and is reframed as "a mind thinking
  in real time."

# Draft

In May 2024, OpenAI released a voice for ChatGPT that draws a breath before a long
sentence, drops in a short laugh, and pauses in the middle of a clause as though it
were searching for the next word. None of these sounds carry information about the
answer it is giving, and the underlying system does not need any of them to produce
speech. They were added on purpose, as part of what the developers call natural
delivery, and they work on a specific human reflex: we hear a breath or a hesitation
and read it as the sound of a mind thinking in real time. The breaths, pauses, and
filler sounds engineered into current AI voices reproduce the cues that listeners
automatically read as signs of effort and presence, and because those cues normally
track a real process, adding them to a system that has no such process works to lower
the listener's guard at the moments when its claims most deserve scrutiny.

In human speech, hesitations are reliable by-products of the work of talking. People
rarely produce spoken language as a clean transcript. They search for words, revise
plans mid-sentence, manage whose turn it is, and check whether the listener is
following. Herbert Clark and Jean Fox Tree showed that "um" and "uh" are conventional
words, planned and produced like any other, that a speaker uses to announce a coming
delay — a short one with "uh," a longer one with "um." Their evidence came from large
collections of recorded conversation, where speakers fit these words into the flow of
speech deliberately, sometimes attaching them to the previous word as in "and-uh," and
sometimes drawing them out when the delay ahead is longer. The speaker's planning has
fallen behind their speaking, and the filled pause marks the gap while they retrieve a
word or decide what to say. A breath before a long sentence works the same way, as the
physical cost of producing a stretch of speech.

Listeners use these markers, and they use them without deciding to. Susan Brennan and
Maurice Williams asked people to judge whether a speaker knew the answer to a question,
and found that a filled pause before the answer led listeners to judge the speaker less
likely to actually know it. The hesitation was read as a window into the speaker's own
sense of their knowledge, and it was a reasonably accurate window, because a person who
is unsure really does hesitate more. The processing runs faster than deliberate
judgment. Martin Corley and colleagues recorded brain responses as people heard
sentences, and found that a hesitation before an unexpected word smoothed the way that
word was integrated and made it more likely to be remembered later; a follow-up study
traced the effect to attention, with hesitations pulling the listener's focus toward
whatever came next. Jennifer Arnold and colleagues found the same orienting effect at
the level of reference, where a hesitation led listeners to expect something new or
harder to name. These responses appear within a few hundred milliseconds, before any
conscious appraisal of the speaker.

Taken together, this makes disfluency a form of evidence. A breath, a pause, an "um"
each covary with something real in the speaker: the load of composing speech, the
degree of certainty, the effort of finding a word. Because the correlation is
dependable, a listener can treat the audible signal as a readout of a hidden mental
state without any intermediate reasoning, and mostly without noticing that they are
doing it. In ordinary conversation the cue is hard to fake convincingly, because a
person who is fluent and certain does not generate the delay that "um" is there to
cover. This is the mechanism the newest synthetic voices reach for.

The problem with copying these cues into a synthetic voice is that the process that
gave them meaning is missing. A speech system composes its whole utterance before it
produces a sound. There is no planning bottleneck that trails the delivery, no
retrieval that stalls and recovers, no uncertainty being resolved as the sentence
unfolds. The system has computation and latency costs, but none of them require a
breath or an "um." When such a system breathes or pauses mid-clause, the pause has been
placed there by the rendering, timed to fall where a human's would fall. The audible
cue is present, and the internal state it normally reports is absent.

Adding those cues is a deliberate design choice, and it is defended in ordinary product
terms. OpenAI presented GPT-4o's voice as expressive and emotive, able to shift pace,
laugh, and respond in close to conversational time. Comparable products make the logic
explicit. ElevenLabs lets creators drop tags such as `[sighs]`, `[laughs]`, and
`[pause]` into a script to control how an AI voice performs the line. Hume's Empathic
Voice Interface measures vocal prosody and answers in a matching, naturalistic tone.
Speech-synthesis research now treats where to place a filled pause as a modeling target
in its own right. These tools sell control over delivery, pacing, and nonverbal vocal
behavior, because those features make synthetic speech feel less like reading aloud and
more like conversation.

The effect works because speech is a privileged channel for sensing another mind.
Juliana Schroeder, Michael Kardas, and Nicholas Epley found that hearing a person say
something makes them seem more thoughtful and mentally present than reading the same
words, because the voice carries timing, intonation, and emphasis that text drops.
People also apply social responses to computers automatically, even when they know
there is no person there. Synthetic voices inherit both tendencies. When they gain the
timing and small imperfections of spontaneous speech, they gain access to inferences
people usually reserve for other humans. Voice is especially hard to resist here,
because speech arrives in time, one word after another, so a gap in it is legible in a
way a gap in writing is not. A written draft hides the pauses that went into it, while a
spoken sentence exposes its own timing, and that timing is where the cues of effort and
hesitation live.

The consequence is a shift in how the output gets treated. Text on a screen is read as
output: something a system generated, which can be reread, quoted, checked against other
sources, and doubted. A reader expects errors and hallucinations, and approaches the
words as an object to evaluate. A voice that appears to compose its answer in the moment
invites the stance we take toward a conversational partner, in which we track sincerity
and presence and extend a working level of trust that lets the exchange move forward. As
a voice comes to sound more like an effortful, present speaker, its content is less
likely to be held at arm's length as machine-generated text, and more likely to be
received the way we receive what a person tells us.

A concrete case makes the shift easier to see. Someone asks a voice assistant whether a
particular chest symptom needs urgent attention, and the assistant answers, "Hmm… let me
think — I'd say it's probably nothing to worry about." The short "hmm" and the pause read
as weighing, as a mind sizing up a hard call before committing to it. The system,
though, arrived at that answer the way it arrives at every answer, with no moment of
weighing that the pause could report. A listener who hears deliberation is more inclined
to accept the conclusion and less inclined to do the thing the situation calls for, which
is to treat an automated triage judgment with suspicion and check it against a clinician.
The hesitation has done work that the content did not earn.

OpenAI describes this consequence in its own documentation. The GPT-4o system card notes
that anthropomorphization "may be heightened by the audio capabilities" of the model, and
that content delivered "through a human-like, high-fidelity voice" can lead to
"increasingly miscalibrated trust." During early testing the company observed users
speaking to the model as to a companion, including one who said "This is our last day
together." OpenAI classified the model as medium risk overall, a rating it attributed to
persuasion, which it flagged as borderline and evaluated on the voice capability as well
as on text. A voice that can be persuasive, and that carries cues telling the listener a
sincere mind stands behind the words, is persuasive in part through those cues.

The honest limit of this concern is worth stating plainly, because naturalness does real
good. A voice that leaves sensible gaps is easier to interrupt and easier to follow.
Clear intonation, a rise that marks a question, and steady pacing help a listener parse a
long spoken answer, and they help a visually impaired user, a child, or an older adult
more than a flat monotone would. Designers therefore need a sharper distinction than
"natural voice." A pause that gives the user time to interrupt is a usability feature. A
brief delay caused by computation is a system constraint. A hesitation inserted before an
emotionally loaded answer is a trust cue, and it belongs in a different category. A real
hesitation covaries with the speaker's knowledge state, which is why a careful listener
can learn something from it. A synthetic hesitation covaries with nothing about the
reliability of the answer, because it was timed for effect rather than produced by any
difficulty. When it lands just before a medical suggestion, a factual claim, or a word of
reassurance, it lends the felt weight of deliberation to content that was generated with
none.

The reflex being borrowed here is old and useful. Reading effort and presence in another
person's voice helps us judge whom to believe and how closely to listen, and it works
because in people the cue and the state behind it travel together. Engineering disfluency
into an AI voice reproduces the cue while the state it normally reports stays absent, so
the instinct fires on a signal that carries no information about whether the answer is
sound. A natural-sounding system can still be accurate and useful, and the pause is no
reason on its own to distrust it. The pause is a setting in a speech model, chosen so the
voice will feel like a person, and it tells you nothing about whether the voice is right.
Keeping that distinction in mind is most of the defense: hear the hesitation, notice that
it was designed, and give the answer the same scrutiny you would give the same words on a
screen.
