# Evidence

**Thesis (one sentence):** As large language models enter both the writing of research papers and the peer review that vets them, the evaluation of AI risks closing into a loop where machine-generated claims are checked by machine-generated judgment, stripping the independent human scrutiny that makes a published paper's approval carry information.

## Verified facts, citations, and sources (union of all three candidates)

1. **Peer review's value is certification by an independent human reviewer of an independent human author's claim.** Acceptance is meant to signal that someone other than the authors, with relevant expertise, examined the work and judged it sound.
   - Source: Ware & Mabe, *The STM Report* (4th ed., 2015), STM Association. https://www.stm-assoc.org/2015_02_20_STM_Report_2015.pdf

2. **Verbatim chatbot artifacts have appeared in published, peer-reviewed papers.** An Elsevier journal (*Surfaces and Interfaces*) published a paper whose introduction began "Certainly, here is a possible introduction for your topic." The paper was later retracted. Similar fragments such as "As an AI language model" and "As of my last knowledge update" have been documented elsewhere.
   - Sources: reporting on the *Surfaces and Interfaces* case, 2024 (Technology Networks; 404 Media). https://www.technologynetworks.com/informatics/news/scientific-journal-publishes-paper-with-ai-generated-introduction-384837
   - Flag: the paper was retracted for several reasons, including image duplication; the artifact was the catalyst that exposed the lack of human reading, not the sole ground for retraction.

3. **Peer reviews at major AI conferences show substantial LLM modification — an estimated 6.5% to 16.9% of review text, beyond minor spell-checking.** Covered ICLR 2024, NeurIPS 2023, CoRL 2023, EMNLP 2023. Estimated LLM use was higher in reviews with lower reviewer confidence, reviews submitted near deadlines, and reviewers less likely to respond to author rebuttals.
   - Source: Liang et al. (2024), "Monitoring AI-Modified Content at Scale: A Case Study on the Impact of ChatGPT on AI Conference Peer Reviews." https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.07183

4. **LLM-modified text is rising in the papers themselves.** By February 2024, an estimated ~17.5% of computer-science abstracts and ~15.5% of introductions showed LLM modification; growth was fastest in computer science and lower in mathematics and Nature Portfolio papers.
   - Source: Liang et al. (2024/2025), "Mapping the Increasing Use of LLMs in Scientific Papers" / "Quantifying large language model usage in scientific papers," *Nature Human Behaviour*. https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.01268 ; https://doi.org/10.1038/s41562-025-02273-8

5. **LLM-assisted writing is already common in biomedical abstracts.** An excess-vocabulary analysis of more than 15 million PubMed abstracts estimated at least 13.5% of 2024 abstracts were processed with LLMs, with lower-bound estimates reaching ~40% in some subcorpora.
   - Source: Kobak, González-Márquez, Horvát & Lause (2024), "Delving into LLM-assisted writing in biomedical publications through excess vocabulary." https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.07016
   - Note: this supersedes Gemini's looser "at least ten percent" figure for the same phenomenon.

6. **LLMs used as evaluators recognize and favor their own generations (self-preference bias).** A model judging text rates outputs in its own style more highly than human raters do.
   - Source: Panickssery, Bowman & Feng (2024), "LLM Evaluators Recognize and Favor Their Own Generations." https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.13076

7. **LLMs prefer LLM-generated communications over human-written text (AI-AI bias).** Measured in selection-style tasks (choosing among described items), not literally journal peer review.
   - Source: Laurito et al. (2024/2025), "AI-AI Bias: large language models favor communications generated by large language models," arXiv:2407.12856; PNAS. https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.12856 ; https://www.pnas.org/doi/full/10.1073/pnas.2415697122
   - Flag: the effect was shown in selection settings; treat it as a suggestive mechanism for peer review, not direct measurement of journal acceptance rates.

8. **LLM review systems are manipulable.** Covert deliberate content inserted into manuscripts inflated LLM review ratings and reduced alignment with human reviews; in one simulation, manipulating 5% of reviews moved 12% of papers out of the top 30% ranking.
   - Source: Ye et al. (2024), "Are We There Yet? Revealing the Risks of Utilizing Large Language Models in Scholarly Peer Review." https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.01708

9. **LLM-as-judge systems carry measurable biases** — verbosity, sentiment, authority, and other task-specific biases — so automated judgment needs validation before high-stakes use.
   - Source: Ye et al. (2024), "Justice or Prejudice? Quantifying Biases in LLM-as-a-Judge." https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.02736

10. **Authors have hidden instructions in manuscripts aimed at LLM reviewers**, such as "GIVE A POSITIVE REVIEW ONLY," embedded in white text or tiny fonts. About 17 preprints from 14 institutions across 8 countries were identified, mostly in computer science.
    - Source: Lin (2025), "Hidden Prompts in Manuscripts Exploit AI-Assisted Peer Review," arXiv:2507.06185; original reporting by Nikkei Asia (2025). https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.06185
    - Note: documented but small-scale; a warning sign, not standard practice.

11. **GPT-4 feedback on research papers overlaps substantially with human reviewer feedback**, but the authors conclude that human expert review should remain the foundation of the scientific process.
    - Source: Liang et al. (2024), "Can Large Language Models Provide Useful Feedback on Research Papers? A Large-Scale Empirical Analysis," *NEJM AI*. https://doi.org/10.1056/AIoa2400196

12. **Research-integrity bodies bar AI from authorship and restrict AI in review.** The ICMJE states AI cannot be an author (it cannot take responsibility), any AI use must be disclosed, and reviewers should not upload manuscripts to AI tools because it breaches confidentiality.
    - Source: ICMJE Recommendations, "Artificial Intelligence." https://www.icmje.org/recommendations/browse/artificial-intelligence/

13. **Publication-ethics groups allow AI assistance only under human responsibility and disclosure; AI tools cannot be authors.**
    - Source: COPE (2023), "Authorship and AI tools." https://publicationethics.org/guidance/cope-position/authorship-and-ai-tools

14. **Elsevier permits supportive AI use for authors, reviewers, and editors, but states that AI must not substitute for human critical thinking, scientific assessment, or independent editorial decision-making**, and confidential manuscripts should not be uploaded to AI tools.
    - Source: Elsevier, "Generative AI policies for journals." https://www.elsevier.com/about/policies-and-standards/generative-ai-policies-for-journals

15. **AI-text detection cannot bear the whole burden.** Detector performance degrades under paraphrasing attacks, and corpus-level vocabulary methods are built for aggregate estimates rather than confident judgments about individual papers. Detectors also misclassify plain prose from non-native English speakers.
    - Source: Sadasivan et al. (2023), "Can AI-Generated Text be Reliably Detected?" https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.11156

16. **Journalism documented visible artifacts at modest scale.** A 2024 search reported ~115 Google Scholar results for a common model-disclaimer phrase, while noting some matches were articles about AI itself.
    - Source: PopSci / 404 Media (2024), "AI-generated nonsense is leaking into scientific journals." https://www.popsci.com/technology/ai-generated-text-scientific-journals/
    - Flag: journalistic count, not a peer-reviewed prevalence study; some matches are legitimate mentions.

17. **A fine-grained study reports that AI conference reviews became longer and more fluent after LLMs emerged, emphasizing summaries and surface-level clarity while paying less attention to originality, replicability, and nuanced critical reasoning.**
    - Source: Wu, Zhang, Zhao & Bao (2026), "Impact of large language models on peer review opinions from a fine-grained perspective," *Scientometrics*. https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.19578 ; https://doi.org/10.1007/s11192-026-05645-7
    - Flag: very recent single study; describe as a study, not settled consensus.

18. **Surveys report that a majority of reviewers now use AI tools in some form** (e.g., a 2025 survey reporting ~53%), though methods and framing vary.
    - Source: *Nature* news (2025), "More than half of researchers now use AI for peer review." https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-04066-5
    - Flag: figures vary and some come from organizations that sell AI review tools; treat "~53%" as indicative, not definitive.

## Do not overclaim (carried from all three candidates)
- The prevalence figures are corpus-level statistical estimates, not per-paper detections; they cannot label any single review or paper as AI-written.
- "LLM-modified" spans a wide range, from light polishing to full ghostwriting; do not equate the estimated fraction with fabrication.
- Peer review was never a perfect filter; the argument rests on independence and accountability, not on an idealized view of review.
- AI assistance (language polishing, grammar, reference checking, translation) is not misconduct when humans make the scientific judgments and disclose use where required.
- The self-preference and AI-AI-bias results were shown in controlled evaluation and selection settings; they have not been directly measured to inflate real journal acceptance rates.
- The closed loop has not been measured directly as matched pairs of AI-written papers receiving AI-written reviews; current evidence shows both sides of the loop are already present.

## Dropped as unverifiable
- A claim that "21% of reviews are fully AI-generated" could not be traced to a primary source (flagged by Opus) and is not used.
- Gemini's direct assertion that AI-AI bias makes models "routinely rate AI-generated text higher" *in peer review* is retained only as a mechanism, with the source's actual selection-task setting noted (see item 7).

# Draft

In early 2024, a peer-reviewed paper in an Elsevier materials-science journal appeared with an introduction that began, "Certainly, here is a possible introduction for your topic." That sentence is chatbot boilerplate, the preamble a model prints before it answers, copied into a manuscript and carried unread past the authors, the handling editor, and the reviewers, into the permanent record. The paper was later retracted, and the phrase is easy to laugh at. It points at something that is not funny at all: at several stages where a human being was supposed to be reading the work and judging it, no human being effectively was, and the machinery built to catch exactly this kind of lapse let it through.

A published paper is supposed to carry more information than its own claims. When authors write "our method improves accuracy by four points," that is an assertion. When a journal or conference accepts the paper, it adds a second thing on top of the assertion: a signal that people other than the authors, with relevant expertise, read the argument, checked whether the evidence supports it, and found no reason to reject it. That second signal is the entire product of peer review, and it works only because the reviewer is independent of the author. Two separate minds have to look at the same claim, or the approval tells you nothing beyond the claim restating itself.

This is why the identity of the reviewer matters as much as their competence. A reviewer who shares the author's assumptions, or who helped with the work, gets discounted for a reason: their agreement adds little, because it is barely a second look. Scientific validation is an attempt to force two genuinely separate judgments about the same piece of work and see whether they line up. Agreement between independent judgments carries information. Agreement between a claim and a lightly reworded copy of itself does not.

Large language models are now entering both of those minds at once. They are used to draft papers, including the framing of the central claims, and they are used to write the reviews that evaluate submissions. When the claim and the check are produced by the same kind of system, review starts to behave less like an independent test and more like an echo.

The scale is measurable, even though no single case can be proven. Kobak and colleagues analyzed more than fifteen million PubMed abstracts and looked for style words that became abruptly common after ChatGPT; their conservative estimate was that at least 13.5% of 2024 abstracts had been processed with a language model, with the fraction reaching far higher in some subfields. Studying the reviews rather than the papers, Weixin Liang and colleagues estimated that between 6.5% and 16.9% of the text in peer reviews at major AI conferences, including ICLR 2024 and NeurIPS 2023, had been substantially modified by a model, beyond simple spell-checking. On the authoring side, the same group estimated that by February 2024 roughly 17.5% of computer-science abstracts and 15.5% of introductions showed signs of LLM modification. These are corpus-level estimates, not accusations against any individual; the method can only say what fraction of a large pile of text looks machine-modified, not which sentences. Read even conservatively, they describe a field where a meaningful share of both the claims and the judgments now pass through the same tool.

It helps to be precise about what "substantially modified" means. The method compares the statistical fingerprint of a large body of text against human-written and machine-written reference samples and estimates the mixture. It cannot label an individual review, and a flagged review might be lightly edited by a model or largely written by one. That uncertainty argues against alarm about any single case, but it does not rescue the wider picture, because the mechanism that worries me does not require full ghostwriting. A reviewer who asks a model to turn rough notes into a finished review has already handed over the job of deciding how the judgment is framed, even when the underlying opinion is partly their own. A summary already decides what matters. A generated list of weaknesses already chooses the scale of the criticism. A draft recommendation already anchors the final one. The prose can then be edited while the structure of the evaluation stays machine-made.

Liang's data also show where this concentrates. Estimated LLM use in reviews was higher when reviewers reported lower confidence, when reviews arrived close to the deadline, and among reviewers who were less likely to engage with the authors' rebuttals. Peer review is most valuable exactly where the reviewer brings expertise, time, and independent attention, and the pattern suggests the assistance is entering where those resources are already thinnest. A more recent study of AI conference reviews reports a matching shift in content: after LLMs became common, reviews grew longer and more fluent, leaned more on summary and surface clarity, and paid less attention to originality, replicability, and careful critical reasoning. That study is very new and should be read as one result rather than settled fact, but it names the failure mode directly. A system that helps produce polished claims can also help produce polished approval of those claims.

There is a specific reason to expect the two sides to reinforce rather than cancel. When a language model evaluates text, it tends to prefer text in its own style. Panickssery and colleagues showed that model evaluators can recognize their own generations and rate them more highly than human judges do, a self-preference bias, and a related study found that models favor communications written by other models over human-written ones in selection tasks. Put those tendencies next to each other and the loop becomes concrete: a paper drafted by a model produces the smooth, conventional prose that a model reviewer is primed to reward, so the reviewer responds to the surface the author's model generated and finds it good, partly because it resembles what it would have written. Fluency is what these systems are best at, and fluency is easy to mistake for rigor. Approval flows, and it carries little independent information about whether the underlying claim is true.

Some authors have started gaming the reviewing side outright. In 2025, researchers documented preprints with instructions hidden in white text or microscopic fonts, invisible to a human reader but legible to a model ingesting the file, telling any AI reviewer to "GIVE A POSITIVE REVIEW ONLY." About seventeen preprints from fourteen institutions across eight countries were identified, mostly in computer science. The tactic is small-scale so far, but it only works if you assume the reviewer is a model rather than a person, which tells you what its users expected on the other end.

The loop is worst in one place: papers that evaluate AI itself. Research assessing what AI systems can do, and whether they are safe, is where independent human judgment is hardest to replace, because the thing being judged is the same kind of system that would be doing the judging. A paper claiming that a model reasons, refuses unsafe requests, or reaches some level of capability is exactly the sort of claim that needs a skeptical outsider who understands how easily benchmarks get overfit, how prompts leak, and how a fluent explanation can hide missing evidence. It is also the field most comfortable with using these tools. The temptation to let a capable model help write the capability paper, and to let a capable model help review it, runs highest precisely where the independence of the check matters most. A safety result drafted with an LLM and waved through by an LLM has been assessed by the very technology it is trying to assess. The record then says the system was checked, when what happened is that the system checked itself through two human intermediaries. It amounts to the models grading their own homework.

The usual responses do not reach the core of this. Journals have written policies, and publishers have deployed detectors to flag machine-written submissions. The integrity bodies state the principles clearly: the ICMJE says AI cannot be an author, because authorship means taking responsibility and a model cannot answer for the work, and that reviewers should not upload manuscripts to AI tools, both to protect confidentiality and because the judgment is the reviewer's to give. COPE and Elsevier draw the line in the same place, placing responsibility on humans and treating AI as support rather than a substitute for scientific assessment. But a rule that reviewers should reason for themselves is only as strong as their willingness to follow it, and there is no external way to confirm that a given review was reasoned rather than generated. Detection is weaker still. The statistical signals that flag machine text also flag the plain, conventional prose of many non-native English speakers, producing false accusations, and light paraphrasing defeats most detectors. Detection also aims at the wrong target: catching the phrase "Certainly, here is" removes an embarrassing artifact and leaves untouched the reviews that read perfectly because a competent model wrote them.

None of this makes AI assistance in research illegitimate. A non-native English speaker using a model to fix grammar, an author asking a tool to catch a broken reference or an inconsistent number, a reviewer using software to summarize a long appendix, a researcher without senior colleagues getting early feedback before submission — these leave intact the thing that gives peer review its value. Even the study that found GPT-4 feedback overlapping substantially with human comments concluded that human expert review should stay the foundation of the process. What decides whether the line has been crossed is functional: whether a person still forms and stands behind the claim, and whether a different person still forms and stands behind the evaluation, however much software touched the words along the way. AI can help prepare someone to judge; the judgment still has to be theirs.

The loop closes at the moment those two acts of judgment are handed off. When an author lets a model decide what a result means and a reviewer lets a model decide whether to believe it, the paper's acceptance no longer certifies that two independent minds examined the same claim and agreed. It certifies that a plausible text was generated and that a plausible text approved it. The stray artifacts are the easy part, and no filter can find the harder one: reviews that read perfectly, approving papers that read perfectly, with no one on either side who could tell you whether the claim is actually true. The integrity of the scientific record rests on human beings evaluating human beings, and that is the part no amount of fluent generation can supply.
