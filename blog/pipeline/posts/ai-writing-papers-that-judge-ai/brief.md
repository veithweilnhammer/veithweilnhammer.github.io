# Brief: Scientists Are Using AI to Write the Papers That Judge AI

## Slug
ai-writing-papers-that-judge-ai

## Working title
Scientists Are Using AI to Write the Papers That Judge AI

## One-line angle (also the Substack subtitle / SEO hook)
When AI drafts the papers evaluating AI and AI writes the peer reviews that approve them, the scientific record risks becoming a loop that validates machine-made claims with machine-made judgment.

## Single argument (thesis)
As large language models seep into both the drafting of research papers and the peer review that vets them, the evaluation of AI risks becoming a closed loop in which machine-generated claims are checked by machine-generated reviews, weakening the independent human judgment that scientific validation depends on.

## Concrete anchor (optional — a real example to ground the argument)
2024–2026 reports of telltale LLM phrasing ("as an AI language model," "certainly, here is...") appearing in published papers and in submitted peer reviews, plus studies estimating that a substantial fraction of recent reviews and abstracts show signs of AI generation.

## What this post must cover
- How scientific validation is supposed to work: independent human authors make claims, and independent human reviewers scrutinize them, so that approval carries information beyond the claim itself.
- The two intrusions: AI is increasingly used to draft papers (including the claims and their framing) and to write peer reviews that evaluate submissions.
- The loop that results: when both sides are machine-generated, review stops being an independent check and can rubber-stamp fluent, plausible text — especially since LLM reviewers favor exactly the fluency LLM authors produce.
- The specific danger for AI research: papers assessing AI capabilities and safety are precisely where independent human judgment matters most, and precisely where AI assistance is most tempting.
- Evidence it is already happening: 2024–2026 detections of AI-authored text in papers and reviews, and estimates of its prevalence, alongside the research-integrity debate over disclosure and detection.
- The honest boundary: AI assistance is not inherently illegitimate (language polishing, error-checking), so the argument must locate where assistance crosses into abdicating the human judgment that makes review meaningful.

## Anchors (real sources this post is allowed to build on)
- 2024–2026 reports of LLM-generated phrasing found in published papers and peer reviews (e.g., "certainly, here is a possible..." artifacts).

- Research-integrity and publisher-policy debates over AI in authorship and review (ICMJE, major-publisher guidelines).
