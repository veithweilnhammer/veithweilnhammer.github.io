# Draft

As large language models seep into both the drafting of research papers and the peer review that vets them, the evaluation of AI risks becoming a closed loop in which machine-generated claims are checked by machine-generated reviews, weakening the independent human judgment that scientific validation depends on.

In early 2024, the journal *Surfaces and Interfaces* published a peer-reviewed paper on the design of lithium metal batteries. The research survived the authors' internal drafting process, the journal's editorial checks, the scrutiny of external peer reviewers, and the final typesetting by the publisher. Yet the very first line of the paper’s introduction read: “Certainly, here is a possible introduction for your topic.” This sentence is the standard conversational preamble of a large language model tuned with human feedback. Its presence in a published manuscript proved that the authors pasted a prompt into a chatbot, copied the generated text, and submitted it without reading the first line. More importantly, it proved that the handling editor and the peer reviewers evaluated the paper's scientific merit without noticing or caring about the artifact. The incident demonstrates a mechanical failure in the system of scientific publication. An entire chain of supposed human experts processed a scientific manuscript without actually reading it.

This failure matters because the scientific literature is currently absorbing a massive influx of text generated by large language models. Authors use AI to draft their papers, and reviewers use AI to write the peer reviews that judge those submissions. As large language models seep into both sides of the publication process, scientific evaluation risks becoming a closed loop. Machine-generated claims are increasingly checked by machine-generated reviews, which weakens the independent human judgment that scientific validation depends on.

Science relies on the friction of independent evaluation. A scientific paper is a set of specific claims about how the physical world works, and the publication process is designed to subject those claims to skeptical scrutiny. The authors assemble their evidence, construct a logical argument, and commit to an interpretation of their data. Then, external experts read the manuscript to locate flaws in the logic, errors in the methodology, and overstatements in the conclusions.

When a paper passes peer review, the publication carries an institutional signal. It indicates that an independent human expert examined the work, tested its structural integrity, and found it defensible. The value of this review lies entirely in the independence and the cognitive effort involved. The reviewer brings their own clinical or laboratory experience, their own theoretical commitments, and their own capacity for doubt to the text. This friction improves the claims. Reviewers force authors to clarify their definitions, acknowledge alternative explanations, and hedge their conclusions. The effort of writing and the effort of reviewing force both parties to commit to specific, defensible interpretations. When researchers remove the effort from this process, they also remove the validation.

The pressure to publish drives this structural failure. Researchers are evaluated, funded, and promoted based on the volume of their scientific output. At the same time, the number of submitted papers has grown exponentially, placing an unsustainable burden on the peer review system. Reviewers are constantly inundated with requests to evaluate manuscripts, a task for which they receive neither pay nor formal academic credit. This creates an environment defined by an extreme scarcity of human attention. When a system demands vast quantities of written output and vast quantities of written evaluation, but provides no time to perform the cognitive labor required for either, the participants inevitably seek out tools that simulate the labor. Generative AI provides exactly this simulation.

Generative AI models excel at producing fluent, conventional academic prose. This capability provides a genuine, legitimate benefit to science. Researchers routinely use language models to polish their English, fix grammatical errors, format citations, and translate rough drafts into standard academic formats. This kind of assistance levels the playing field for non-native speakers and removes administrative friction from the scientific process. The International Committee of Medical Journal Editors explicitly permits authors to use AI for these tasks, provided they disclose the assistance and take full responsibility for the final text. In these cases, the human author still performs the cognitive labor of deciding exactly what the paper claims.

The boundary is crossed when researchers use models to generate the scientific claims, construct the arguments, and interpret the data. A large language model operates by predicting highly probable sequences of words based on its training data. It does not understand the physical system it describes or the constraints of the experiment. When an author prompts a model to write a discussion section based on a few keywords, the model produces text that mimics the statistical shape of a standard discussion section. It generates the expected hedging, the typical calls for future research, and the conventional transitions. The resulting text is smooth and highly readable, but it lacks the specific, hard-won commitments of an author who has actually wrestled with anomalous data. A massive 2024 analysis of millions of PubMed abstracts revealed that at least ten percent of recent scientific abstracts exhibit vocabulary and language patterns directly traceable to large language models. The literature is rapidly filling with fluent, machine-generated approximations of scientific reasoning. 

The intrusion of AI into peer review represents a more severe structural risk to scientific integrity. A researcher can paste a submitted manuscript into a large language model, ask it to identify weaknesses and generate a review, and receive a highly structured, authoritative-sounding critique in seconds. Recent studies confirm that this practice is already widespread. A 2024 analysis by Weixin Liang and colleagues examined the text of peer reviews submitted to major artificial intelligence conferences, including the International Conference on Learning Representations. They estimated that up to seventeen percent of the reviews showed substantial signs of large language model modification or generation. The researchers found clear behavioral patterns associated with these AI-generated reviews. The machine-generated reviews tended to appear closer to the review submission deadlines, and they were often submitted by reviewers who reported lower self-assessed confidence in the subject matter.

Crucially, the study noted that reviewers who used AI were significantly less likely to engage with the authors during the rebuttal phase. When the human authors pushed back against the machine-generated critique, the reviewer simply abandoned the conversation. This proves the lack of actual cognitive engagement. The reviewer did not read the paper deeply enough to defend their critique when challenged. The AI-generated reviews typically summarize the paper well and offer mathematically probable, generalized critiques—such as requesting more extensive baseline comparisons or clearer definitions of terms. These critiques apply to almost any paper and require zero comprehension of the actual claims. The human reviewer outsources the cognitive labor of evaluation to a statistical pattern-matching system.

When AI-assisted authors submit papers to AI-assisted reviewers, the system creates a closed validation loop. The human participants act merely as administrative conduits, moving text from one language model prompt to another. The authors prompt an AI to write a manuscript; the reviewers prompt an AI to review it; the two machines communicate through the proxy of the academic publication system. This loop systematically degrades the quality of scientific evaluation because of how language models evaluate text.

Research into the behavior of large language models demonstrates that they exhibit a strong, systematic preference for text generated by other language models over text generated by humans. A 2024 study published in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences detailed this "AI-AI bias." The bias occurs because language models evaluate text based on fluency, conventional structure, and statistical predictability—exactly the features that generative models are optimized to produce. When an AI reviewer evaluates a human-written paper, it often flags awkward phrasing, unconventional structures, or highly novel logical leaps. But when an AI reviewer evaluates an AI-written paper, it encounters the exact statistical distributions of language it was trained to recognize and produce. The AI reviewer perceives the AI author's text as highly coherent, clear, and well-structured. As a result, the model routinely rates AI-generated text higher than equivalent human-written text. In a closed loop, the review process ceases to be an independent check on the validity of the claims. Instead, it becomes a rubber stamp for fluency. The system validates the text simply because the text looks exactly like what the system expects to see.

This automated loop poses a specific and acute danger to the field of artificial intelligence research itself. Researchers studying AI capabilities, model alignment, and system safety must constantly evaluate whether new models behave as expected, whether they exhibit dangerous emergent capabilities, and whether they understand the constraints placed upon them by their developers. These are complex, high-stakes judgments that require intense, adversarial human scrutiny. If the research papers evaluating the safety and capabilities of a new language model are drafted by language models, and the peer reviews approving those papers are also written by language models, the field loses its independent, external vantage point. The models are effectively grading their own homework. A language model cannot independently verify whether another model poses a security risk; it can only generate text that statistically mimics how human researchers write about security risks. The evaluation of AI systems relies entirely on human researchers stepping outside the computational system to measure it against physical reality and social consequences. When researchers delegate the drafting of safety claims and the evaluation of those claims to the models themselves, they abdicate the independent human judgment that makes safety research meaningful.

The scientific publishing industry has responded to this shift by updating editorial guidelines and deploying detection tools, but these efforts largely fail to address the structural problem. Major guidelines, such as those from the International Committee of Medical Journal Editors, explicitly state that AI tools cannot be listed as authors because they cannot take legal or scientific responsibility for the work. The guidelines also strictly prohibit peer reviewers from uploading confidential, unpublished manuscripts into public language models, as doing so violates confidentiality agreements and exposes sensitive data to corporate servers. 

However, these policies rely almost entirely on the honor system. In an attempt to enforce the rules, publishers have invested heavily in automated AI detection software to screen incoming submissions. These technical solutions are fundamentally unreliable. Detection algorithms routinely struggle to distinguish between text written by a language model and text written by a non-native English speaker, leading to false accusations against international researchers. Furthermore, the detectors are easily defeated by simple prompting techniques or secondary paraphrasing tools that introduce artificial human-like variations into the text. Focusing on detection treats the problem as a simple issue of individual cheating. The real crisis is structural. The modern academic system demands exceptionally high volumes of publication and peer review from its participants, but it does not provide the time or resources for humans to do the slow, careful reading required. Generative AI exploits this structural weakness perfectly. It produces the exact formatting and tone of scientific labor without requiring the underlying cognitive effort.

The process of scientific validation cannot be automated, because the value of the validation is the human effort itself. A large language model can generate a plausible hypothesis, format a methodology section perfectly, and produce a highly structured critique of a manuscript. But it cannot stand behind the claims it generates. It has no professional reputation to risk, no theoretical commitments to defend, and no physical connection to the experimental results it describes. When both the scientific claim and the evaluation of that claim are machine-generated, the scientific record fills with plausible assertions that no human being has actually thought through. As large language models become deeply embedded in the mechanics of academic publishing, researchers must recognize that fluency is not evidence, and formatting is not logic. Breaking the loop requires researchers to commit to the slow, friction-filled work of reading and judging the science for themselves. The integrity of the scientific record depends entirely on human beings evaluating human beings.
