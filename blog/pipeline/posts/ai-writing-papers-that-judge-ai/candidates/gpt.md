# Evidence

Thesis: AI becomes a threat to scientific validation when the work of framing claims and the work of judging those claims are both delegated to language models, because peer review only carries useful information when it adds an accountable, independent human check.

1. Peer review policy treats manuscripts as confidential and review as an accountable human judgment. Source: ICMJE, 2024, “Use of Artificial Intelligence in Publishing.” Link: https://www.icmje.org/recommendations/browse/artificial-intelligence/
2. Publication-ethics groups allow AI assistance only under human responsibility and disclosure; AI tools cannot be authors because they cannot take responsibility for the work. Source: COPE, 2023, “Authorship and AI tools.” Link: https://publicationethics.org/guidance/cope-position/authorship-and-ai-tools
3. Elsevier permits supportive AI use for authors, reviewers, and editors, but states that AI tools must not substitute for human critical thinking, scientific assessment, or independent editorial decision-making. Source: Elsevier, current journal policy, “Generative AI policies for journals.” Link: https://www.elsevier.com/about/policies-and-standards/generative-ai-policies-for-journals
4. LLM-assisted writing is already common in biomedical abstracts: an excess-vocabulary analysis of more than 15 million PubMed abstracts estimated that at least 13.5% of 2024 abstracts were processed with LLMs, with lower-bound estimates reaching 40% in some subcorpora. Source: Kobak, González-Márquez, Horvát, and Lause, 2024, “Delving into LLM-assisted writing in biomedical publications through excess vocabulary.” Link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.07016
5. LLM-modified scientific writing increased after ChatGPT across arXiv, bioRxiv, and Nature Portfolio papers; the largest growth was in computer science papers, reaching up to 17.5% in the authors’ early-2024 analysis, while mathematics and Nature Portfolio papers were lower. Source: Liang et al., 2024/2025, “Mapping the Increasing Use of LLMs in Scientific Papers” / “Quantifying large language model usage in scientific papers.” Links: https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.01268 and https://doi.org/10.1038/s41562-025-02273-8
6. AI conference peer reviews also show evidence of LLM modification: Liang et al. estimated that 6.5% to 16.9% of text in reviews for ICLR 2024, NeurIPS 2023, CoRL 2023, and EMNLP 2023 was substantially modified by LLMs, beyond minor spell-checking. Source: Liang et al., 2024, “Monitoring AI-Modified Content at Scale.” Link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.07183
7. In those AI conference reviews, estimated LLM use was higher for reviews with lower reviewer confidence, reviews submitted near deadlines, and reviewers less likely to respond to rebuttals. Source: Liang et al., 2024, “Monitoring AI-Modified Content at Scale.” Link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.07183
8. A 2026 fine-grained study of AI conference reviews reported that, after LLMs emerged, peer-review reports became longer and more fluent, emphasized summaries and surface-level clarity more, and paid less attention to originality, replicability, and nuanced critical reasoning. Source: Wu, Zhang, Zhao, and Bao, 2026, “Impact of large language models on peer review opinions from a fine-grained perspective.” Links: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.19578 and https://doi.org/10.1007/s11192-026-05645-7
9. GPT-4 feedback on research papers can overlap substantially with human reviewer feedback, but the authors conclude that human expert review should remain the foundation of the scientific process. Source: Liang et al., 2024, “Can Large Language Models Provide Useful Feedback on Research Papers? A Large-Scale Empirical Analysis.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1056/AIoa2400196
10. LLM review systems are vulnerable to manipulation: Ye et al. found that covert deliberate content in manuscripts could inflate LLM review ratings and reduce alignment with human reviews; in one simulation, manipulating 5% of reviews could move 12% of papers out of the top 30% ranking. Source: Ye et al., 2024, “Are We There Yet? Revealing the Risks of Utilizing Large Language Models in Scholarly Peer Review.” Link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.01708
11. LLM-as-judge systems have measurable biases, including verbosity, sentiment, authority, and other task-specific biases, so automated judgment needs validation before it can support high-stakes evaluation. Source: Ye et al., 2024, “Justice or Prejudice? Quantifying Biases in LLM-as-a-Judge.” Link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.02736
12. Reports in 2024 found explicit LLM artifact phrases in published papers; one 404 Media search, summarized by Popular Science, reported 115 Google Scholar results for a common model-disclaimer phrase, while noting that some apparent matches involved articles about AI itself. Source: PopSci / 404 Media reporting, 2024, “AI-generated nonsense is leaking into scientific journals.” Link: https://www.popsci.com/technology/ai-generated-text-scientific-journals/
13. AI-text detection cannot bear the whole burden: detector performance can degrade under paraphrasing attacks, and corpus-level vocabulary methods are designed for aggregate estimates rather than confident judgments about individual papers. Source: Sadasivan et al., 2023, “Can AI-Generated Text be Reliably Detected?” Link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.11156

Exact quotes considered:

- I will not quote modern copyrighted passages in the draft.
- Short generic artifact strings that can be mentioned as examples, without relying on any one copyrighted article: “As of my last knowledge update,” “As an AI language model,” and “regenerate response.”

Do not overclaim:

- Peer review was never a perfect filter; the argument depends on independence and accountability, not on an idealized view of peer review.
- AI-assisted writing is not misconduct by itself when humans verify facts, make the scientific judgments, and disclose use where required.
- Corpus-level prevalence estimates do not prove that any individual paper or review was written by AI.
- Obvious artifact phrases are useful anecdotes, but they undercount polished AI use and can create false positives in papers that discuss AI.
- LLM feedback can be useful before submission; the boundary problem arises when it replaces accountable review.
- The closed-loop risk has not been measured directly as matched pairs of AI-written papers receiving AI-written reviews; current evidence shows both sides of the loop are already present.

Flags:

- The 115-article artifact count comes from journalism rather than a peer-reviewed prevalence study, and some examples may be legitimate mentions of AI phrases.
- The 2026 study of peer-review opinions is very recent; use it cautiously and describe its claims as a study rather than as settled consensus.
- Publisher policies differ and change quickly; ICMJE, COPE, and Elsevier agree on responsibility and disclosure, but journals implement the boundary differently.
- Estimates of LLM use vary by method, field, corpus, and time window.

# Draft

Peer review gives scientific approval its meaning because the reviewer has a different job from the author. The author proposes a claim, chooses the framing, selects the evidence, and writes the argument in the most persuasive form they can defend. The reviewer is supposed to interrupt that process from the outside, asking whether the methods support the claim, whether the missing controls matter, whether the alternative explanation has been treated fairly, and whether the paper should enter the scientific record in its present form. This division of labor is imperfect, but it is the basic reason acceptance means more than successful self-presentation.

Large language models put pressure on both sides of this division at the same time. They help authors draft abstracts, introductions, literature reviews, responses to reviewers, and sometimes the framing of the contribution itself. They also help reviewers produce reports under deadline pressure, often by turning a manuscript into a summary, a list of strengths, a list of weaknesses, and a recommendation. Each use can look modest in isolation. A paragraph becomes clearer. A review arrives on time. A non-native speaker gets help with idiom. The problem appears when the scientific claim and the judgment of that claim are both partly generated by systems optimized for fluent, plausible text.

The scale is already large enough to matter. Kobak and colleagues analyzed more than 15 million PubMed abstracts and looked for abrupt increases in style words that became common after ChatGPT. Their estimate was conservative by design, because it only counted detectable vocabulary shifts, yet it suggested that at least 13.5% of 2024 PubMed abstracts had been processed with LLMs. Liang and colleagues used a related population-level method across arXiv, bioRxiv, and Nature Portfolio papers and found the fastest growth in computer science, where estimated LLM modification reached 17.5% in their early-2024 analysis. These numbers do not identify individual authors, and they do not separate harmless editing from substantive rewriting. They show that LLM-assisted scientific prose is no longer a marginal practice.

The same pattern has reached peer review, including the AI conferences that evaluate much of the research on modern AI systems. In a study of reviews from ICLR 2024, NeurIPS 2023, CoRL 2023, and EMNLP 2023, Liang and colleagues estimated that 6.5% to 16.9% of review text had been substantially modified by LLMs, beyond minor spell-checking. The estimated use was higher in reviews where reviewers reported lower confidence, submitted close to the deadline, and were less likely to respond to author rebuttals. That pattern matters because peer review is most valuable where the reviewer brings expertise, time, and independent attention. The data suggest that LLM assistance may be entering precisely where those resources are weakest.

A later fine-grained study of AI conference reviews reported a related shift in the content of reviews after LLMs became widely available. Review reports became longer and more fluent, and they placed more emphasis on summaries and surface-level clarity. At the same time, attention to originality, replicability, and nuanced critical reasoning declined. This is the central mechanism of the loop. LLMs make papers sound more coherent, and LLM-assisted reviews may become more responsive to the same surface features. A system that helps produce polished claims can also help produce polished approval of those claims.

This pattern can arise without bad faith. Most researchers who use AI tools are likely trying to save time, improve English, organize material, or get early feedback. A reviewer who asks a model to summarize a manuscript may believe they are creating more room for judgment, because the routine work has been accelerated. But a summary already decides what matters. A generated list of weaknesses already chooses the scale of criticism. A draft recommendation already anchors the final recommendation. Once these intermediate judgments come from a model, the human reviewer may edit the prose while leaving the structure of the evaluation intact.

The risk is sharper in AI research than in many other fields. Papers in this area often make claims about the capabilities, safety, reasoning, alignment, or evaluation of AI systems. Those claims are technically difficult, commercially valuable, and prone to benchmark gaming. They also sit inside a research culture that is unusually comfortable with using LLMs for coding, writing, and reviewing. The people most likely to evaluate AI papers are also the people most able and tempted to automate parts of that evaluation. If a paper about model safety is drafted with heavy model assistance and then reviewed through a model-generated report, the scientific record starts to validate machine-made claims with machine-made judgment.

The more realistic danger is loss of independence. Many AI-assisted sentences are accurate, and many AI-edited papers report real work. Scientific validation depends on a second mind with different incentives and different failure modes. Human reviewers miss things, carry biases, and disagree with one another, but their judgment is at least partly independent of the system that produced the manuscript. A language model used by both author and reviewer may share training data, stylistic preferences, benchmark exposure, and blind spots with the model being studied. It can also be manipulated in ways that are specific to automated reading. Ye and colleagues showed that covert deliberate content inserted into manuscripts could inflate LLM review ratings and reduce agreement with human reviews. In their simulation, manipulating 5% of reviews could move 12% of papers out of the top 30% ranking.

There is also a measurement problem. The obvious failures are easy to mock: phrases such as “As of my last knowledge update” or “As an AI language model” have appeared in searches of published literature, and journalists have documented cases where model artifacts seem to have survived editing. These examples are useful because they prove that some AI text passed through publication checks. They also catch the sloppiest cases, while competent use leaves no such trace. The better evidence comes from corpus-level studies that detect shifts in vocabulary across millions of abstracts or thousands of reviews. Those methods are valuable for measuring the phenomenon, but they cannot adjudicate individual responsibility. Detection will not restore independence on its own.

Publisher policies have converged on a sensible boundary. The ICMJE, COPE, and Elsevier all place responsibility on humans, require transparency where AI tools are used, and reject AI tools as authors because they cannot answer for the integrity of the work. Elsevier’s reviewer policy is especially direct: reviewers remain responsible for scientific assessment and judgment, and submitted manuscripts should not be uploaded into AI tools when confidentiality cannot be assured. These policies accept that AI can support writing and editing while preserving the human duties that make publication meaningful.

That boundary is easier to state than to enforce. Language polishing, grammar checking, and translation support are legitimate uses when the underlying claim, evidence, and interpretation remain the author’s. Pre-submission feedback from an LLM can be useful when it is treated like a checklist or a hostile first reader, especially for researchers without easy access to senior colleagues. Even GPT-4 feedback on research papers has shown substantial overlap with human reviewer comments in a large empirical study by Liang and colleagues. The same study still concluded that human expert review should remain the foundation of the scientific process. The distinction is functional: AI can help prepare a person to judge; the final judgment still belongs to the person.

For authors, that means disclosure should describe more than the tool name. A useful statement says whether the model corrected language, reorganized sections, summarized literature, generated candidate interpretations, wrote code, or drafted responses to reviewers. The scientific risk differs across those uses. Language editing leaves the intellectual chain mostly intact. Generating a discussion section or a limitation analysis can change the scientific meaning of the paper, because it changes which uncertainties are visible to the reader.

For reviewers, the threshold should be stricter because the manuscript is confidential and the reviewer is acting as an independent assessor. A reviewer can use private tools to improve the clarity of their own report if the manuscript content is protected and the journal permits it. They should not outsource the reading, the methodological critique, the novelty assessment, or the recommendation. Editors could make this explicit by asking reviewers to certify that any AI assistance did not involve uploading confidential manuscript content to an unapproved system and did not generate the scientific assessment. Journals that want AI support can provide a separate, labeled technical screen that checks references, reporting completeness, statistical consistency, or image integrity, while keeping editorial judgment visibly human.

AI research needs this separation most. A paper claiming that a model reasons, refuses unsafe requests, detects deception, or improves medical advice should be evaluated by people who understand how easily benchmarks can be overfit, prompts can leak, and fluent explanations can conceal missing evidence. Automated review can help find clerical errors, but the central questions require independent scientific skepticism. Does the benchmark measure the construct named in the title? Are the baselines fair? Did the authors test the failure cases that would weaken their claim? Does the safety conclusion survive outside the prompt distribution used in the paper? These are judgments about meaning and consequence, with textual quality as only one small part of the assessment.

The scientific record can absorb AI assistance if the human chain of responsibility stays intact. Treating machine-generated review as an ordinary substitute for independent scrutiny would change what acceptance means. The practical aim should therefore be narrow and enforceable: disclose substantive AI use, keep confidential manuscripts out of unapproved tools, label automated checks as automated checks, and reserve acceptance decisions for accountable humans who have read and judged the work themselves. The value of peer review lies in the extra information added by an independent reviewer. Once that independence is replaced by another layer of fluent generation, approval becomes easier to produce and less informative to trust.
