# Evidence

**Thesis:** As synthetic text, voice, and faces become cheap and indistinguishable from human-made content, a credible signal that something came from a specific real person turns scarce, and scarcity is what trust systems price. That is why proof-of-personhood and provenance are becoming central problems, and why the way these systems are built matters as much as whether they are built at all.

## Key claims and sources

1. **Deepfake fraud at Arup.** In January 2024, a finance employee at the engineering firm Arup, in its Hong Kong office, paid out about HK$200 million (roughly US$25.6 million) in fifteen transactions after a video call in which every other participant — including the company's CFO — was a deepfake generated from publicly available footage. The employee was initially skeptical of an email and asked for the meeting to confirm a real person was behind the request; the meeting is what convinced them. The fraud was uncovered only when the employee later contacted headquarters. Widely reported as one of the largest deepfake frauds on record; funds not recovered.
   - *Source:* Heather Chen and Kathleen Magramo, "Finance worker pays out $25 million after video call with deepfake 'chief financial officer'," CNN, Feb 4 2024. https://www.cnn.com/2024/02/04/asia/deepfake-cfo-scam-hong-kong-intl-hnk
   - *Corroboration:* World Economic Forum, "Cybercrime: Lessons learned from a $25m deepfake attack," Feb 2025.

2. **AI-generated text is credible and hard to distinguish.** Readers were largely unable to tell machine-written news-style stories from human-written ones.
   - *Source:* Kreps, McCain, and Brundage (2020), "All the News That's Fit to Fabricate: AI-Generated Text as a Tool of Media Misinformation," *Journal of Experimental Political Science*. https://doi.org/10.1017/XPS.2020.37

3. **AI-synthesized faces are indistinguishable and rated more trustworthy.** In a PNAS study, observers could not reliably separate synthetic faces from real ones, and rated the synthetic faces as slightly more trustworthy on average.
   - *Source:* Nightingale and Farid (2022), PNAS. https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2120481119

4. **Humans cannot reliably detect speech deepfakes.** In a multilingual (English and Mandarin) experiment, listeners correctly identified speech deepfakes only 73% of the time; example-based familiarization helped only slightly.
   - *Source:* Mai, Bray, Davies, and Griffin (2023), PLoS ONE. https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0285333

5. **Detection is adversarial and unreliable.** Paraphrasing can sharply reduce AI-text detection rates, and watermarking schemes can be vulnerable to spoofing.
   - *Source:* Sadasivan et al. (2023), "Can AI-Generated Text be Reliably Detected?" https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.11156

6. **Bot traffic is now roughly half of measured web traffic.** Imperva estimated bots at 49.6% of internet traffic in 2023 (bad bots 32%, human 50.4%), automated traffic rising for the fifth consecutive year — a trend the report ties to generative AI lowering the cost of building bots. Imperva's 2025 report put automated traffic at 51% of 2024 traffic (the first time bots exceeded human traffic), with bad bots at 37%.
   - *Sources:* Imperva/Thales, 2024 Bad Bot Report (Apr 2024), https://www.imperva.com/company/press_releases/bots-make-up-half-of-all-internet-traffic-globally/ ; Imperva, 2025 Bad Bot Report, https://www.imperva.com/resources/resource-library/reports/2025-bad-bot-report/

7. **Dead internet theory.** Articulated in a January 2021 forum thread (user "IlluminatiPirate," Agora Road's Macintosh Cafe) and popularized by Kaitlyn Tiffany, "Maybe You Missed It, but the Internet 'Died' Five Years Ago" (The Atlantic, Aug 31 2021). In its strong form — a coordinated bot takeover — it is a conspiracy theory; the defensible core is that suspicion of automated/synthetic activity has become mainstream.
   - *Source:* https://www.theatlantic.com/technology/archive/2021/08/dead-internet-theory-wrong-but-feels-true/619937/

8. **Content provenance standards.** The Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity (C2PA) maintains an open standard (Content Credentials) for attaching signed origin and edit-history information to digital media.
   - *Source:* https://c2pa.org/

9. **Market for lemons.** When buyers cannot tell high-quality goods from imitations, bad signals drive out good ones unless credible information mechanisms exist — the economic intuition for why reliable provenance gains value when imitation becomes cheap.
   - *Source:* Akerlof (1970), "The Market for 'Lemons'," *Quarterly Journal of Economics*. https://doi.org/10.2307/1879431

10. **World ID / Worldcoin.** Worldcoin, co-founded by OpenAI CEO Sam Altman and rebranded to "World" in 2024, issues a "World ID" proof-of-personhood credential by scanning a user's iris with a device called the Orb. It is designed to store a cryptographic derivative (iris code) rather than raw images, and to use zero-knowledge proofs so apps can confirm uniqueness without tracking a user across services. By late 2025, more than 12 million people had verified with the Orb (about 7 million in October 2024), with a stated ambition to reach a billion.
    - *Sources:* World documentation and blog, https://world.org/blog ; World, "Humanness in the age of AI" (2023), https://world.org/blog/engineering/humanness-in-the-age-of-ai ; Vitalik Buterin (2023), https://vitalik.eth.limo/general/2023/07/24/biometric.html

11. **Commercial adoption of World ID.** In 2025 Match Group began piloting World ID on Tinder in Japan to verify age (18+) and "humanness," issuing a badge aimed at romance scams and AI-generated profiles; World also announced a partnership with Visa.
    - *Source:* TechCrunch, "World partners with Tinder, Visa to bring its ID-verifying tech to more places," Apr 30 2025. https://techcrunch.com/2025/04/30/world-partners-with-tinder-visa-to-bring-its-id-verifying-tech-to-more-places/

12. **Regulatory backlash to iris collection.** Spain's AEPD ordered a temporary halt to data collection in March 2024, citing insufficient information, data from minors, inability to withdraw consent, and the special sensitivity of biometric data under the GDPR. Portugal's regulator ordered a 90-day suspension after complaints. Germany's Bavarian authority (BayLDA) ordered corrective measures and deletion of non-compliant data in December 2024. Kenya suspended enrolment in 2023 and, in May 2025, a Kenyan court ruled the collection unlawful and ordered the data deleted.
    - *Sources:* AEPD press release (Mar 2024), https://www.aepd.es/en/press-and-communication/press-releases/agency-orders-precautionary-measure-which-prevents-Worldcoin-from-continuing-toprocess-personal-data-in-spain ; Infosecurity Magazine (2024), https://www.infosecurity-magazine.com/news/portugal-worldcoin-stop-biometric/ ; BayLDA (Dec 2024), https://www.lda.bayern.de/de/pressemitteilungen.html ; TechCrunch (2023), https://techcrunch.com/2023/08/02/kenya-suspends-worldcoin-scans-over-security-privacy-and-financial-concerns/ ; Reuters, "Kenya court orders Worldcoin to delete data" (May 2025).

13. **Proof-of-personhood as the "unique-human" problem, and its trade-offs.** One registered credential per real person, ideally without revealing which person. Buterin frames biometric personhood as carrying structural risks — irreversible biometric leaks, erosion of anonymous internet use, coercion by authoritarian governments, and the difficulty of being secure and decentralized at once. Non-biometric designs exist: social-graph systems (BrightID, Proof of Humanity, Idena, Circles) and zero-knowledge privacy layers (Sismo), some aggregated in Gitcoin Passport. None is inclusive, secure, decentralized, and private all at once.
    - *Source:* Buterin (2023), https://vitalik.eth.limo/general/2023/07/24/biometric.html

## Do not overclaim

- The dead internet theory in its strong form (the web is *mostly* AI-generated by design) is a conspiracy theory, not fact. The defensible claim is narrower: automated traffic is now roughly half of measured web traffic, and suspicion of fakery is widespread.
- Imperva's percentages measure traffic its network can see, not the share of *content* that is synthetic, and not human belief. Do not equate the three.
- Synthetic text, voice, and faces are not always indistinguishable. The evidence supports the weaker claim: many people struggle, and detection is becoming less reliable.
- World ID is not "banned everywhere." Actions differ by country (temporary suspensions, corrective orders, one court ruling), and the company contests several and continues to operate in many markets.
- Proof-of-personhood asserts one-human-one-account; it does not verify that a statement is true or that a specific file is authentic. Do not conflate personhood with content provenance.
- Do not present decentralized/social-graph personhood as solved or clearly superior; every approach carries serious trade-offs.

## Flags

- Verified-user counts for World ID move quickly and vary across sources (7M Oct 2024; 12M+ late 2025). The draft uses "more than 12 million" and flags imprecision; pull the current figure from world.org before publication.
- Exact regulator dates and legal postures evolve (appeals, partial lifts). Spain, Portugal, Germany, and Kenya are well documented in aggregate; confirm precise current status before publication.
- The Arup figure is reported via Hong Kong police briefings and press, not a court judgment; Arup confirmed the incident publicly.
- Imperva is a security vendor; treat its numbers as an indicator of scale, not a definitive internet-wide measurement.

# Draft

In January 2024, a finance employee at the engineering firm Arup joined a video call with what looked like the company's chief financial officer and several colleagues. The faces were familiar, the voices matched, and the request fit a senior finance meeting: authorize a set of confidential transfers. Over the following days the employee sent about 200 million Hong Kong dollars, roughly 25 million US dollars, in fifteen separate payments. Every person on that call except the employee had been synthesized from publicly available footage. Hong Kong police described it as one of the largest deepfake frauds on record, and the money was never recovered.

The detail that matters is not the size of the loss but what failed. The employee was skeptical of the initial email and asked for a video meeting precisely to confirm that a real person stood behind the request. The meeting is what removed the doubt. For most of human history, seeing a face and hearing a voice in real time was a reliable way to establish that you were dealing with a specific person. That check now returns the wrong answer, and it does so cheaply and at scale.

Synthetic text, voice, and images have crossed the point where the apparent realism of something no longer certifies that a human made it. A convincing face can be generated, a familiar voice cloned from a few seconds of audio, and fluent prose produced at essentially zero marginal cost. The evidence for this is broad rather than anecdotal. Sarah Kreps and colleagues found that readers could barely distinguish AI-written news stories from human-written ones. Sophie Nightingale and Hany Farid showed that observers could not reliably separate AI-synthesized faces from real photographs, and rated the synthetic faces as slightly more trustworthy on average. Kimberly Mai and colleagues played people genuine and synthetic speech in English and Mandarin, and listeners caught the deepfakes only 73% of the time, even after training on examples. These studies use different methods and test different technologies, and together they show that ordinary perception has become a weak certificate of origin.

Detection tools help, but they cannot restore the old baseline. A detector can flag a likely synthetic text or a manipulated image under one set of assumptions, while the next model or a simple paraphrase shifts the distribution it was trained to recognize. Work on AI-text detection has shown that paraphrasing sharply reduces detection rates and that watermarking schemes create their own spoofing problems. The generator, the detector, and the person trying to evade the detector can all adapt, so detection stays a partial defense rather than a settled answer.

The change shows up first in how people read everything online. The "dead internet theory," posted on a small forum in 2021 and picked up by The Atlantic the same year, holds that much of what we encounter on the web is machine-generated and that genuine human activity is a shrinking fraction of the whole. As a literal account of a coordinated takeover it is a conspiracy theory. As a description of a mood it has gone mainstream, and there are measurements underneath the mood. Imperva estimated that bots made up 49.6% of internet traffic in 2023, and its later report put automated traffic above half for the first time in 2024, with the rise tied directly to generative AI making bots cheap to build. A reader who assumes that a reply, a review, or a face might be automated is now working from a reasonable prior rather than a paranoid one. Default trust in anonymous online content erodes because the base rate of fakery has genuinely climbed.

When something becomes abundant and cheap, whatever stays scarce gains value. Convincing content is now abundant, which makes a credible signal that a specific real person produced a specific thing scarce. This is a version of the problem George Akerlof described in the market for used cars: when buyers cannot separate good goods from imitations, the market needs credible information mechanisms or it collapses toward the worst case. Online media now faces that structure directly. The valuable thing becomes the hard-to-fake connection between a message and a source with a real history and a reputation that can be held to account. So the focus of verification moves away from the artifact and toward the origin, and the question shifts from whether content looks legitimate to whether the entity behind it can show that it is human.

The pricing is already becoming literal. In 2025 Match Group began piloting World ID on Tinder in Japan, so that users could earn a badge certifying they are a real, unique person over eighteen, aimed at romance scams and AI-generated profiles; World also announced a partnership with Visa. Platforms that once handed out a checkmark for a phone number are now attaching verification to a scarce credential, and banks increasingly add a human-confirmation step before a large transfer. Each of these treats "this action came from a real, distinct person" as information worth paying for, precisely because it can no longer be assumed from the content.

Two related things are being asked for, and it helps to keep them apart. Content provenance is a claim about a particular artifact: that this recording, image, or message came from a known source and was altered only in recorded ways. The Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity, which maintains the C2PA standard, is building an open system for attaching signed origin and edit-history information to media, so a photograph can carry a checkable chain of custody back to a camera or publisher. Proof of personhood is a claim about an actor: that there is one distinct human behind an account, without necessarily revealing who. The word "unique" carries the weight, because most online attacks rely on scale — one operator creating thousands of accounts, reviews, or votes — and a system that ties one action to one person changes the economics of those attacks. The Arup case needed the second kind. The employee did not need to authenticate a file; they needed to know that a real, authorized person was on the other end. That is the harder guarantee to rebuild, because it means tying a digital action to a body in the world without turning the link into a tracking device.

The most visible attempt to supply it is Worldcoin, co-founded by OpenAI's Sam Altman and since rebranded to World. Its premise is that as AI makes humans and bots indistinguishable in text, we will need hardware that certifies humanness directly. A device called the Orb scans a person's iris and issues a credential, World ID, that lets them later prove they are a unique human without re-scanning. By late 2025 more than twelve million people had signed up, and the project talks openly about reaching a billion. The design does try to limit exposure: it is meant to store a cryptographic derivative of the iris rather than raw images, and to prove uniqueness through zero-knowledge techniques that reveal nothing else about the user.

The reception shows why this is genuinely hard. Spain's data-protection authority ordered a temporary halt to iris collection in March 2024. Germany's Bavarian regulator ordered corrective measures and deletion of non-compliant data at the end of that year. Portugal ordered a ninety-day suspension, and in May 2025 a Kenyan court ruled the collection unlawful and ordered the data deleted. The recurring objection concedes that iris scanning works and focuses on how it works: by concentrating the most permanent kind of personal data — a biometric you cannot change if it leaks — under the control of a private system, often gathered from people who were offered tokens to take part. A remedy for the fakery problem that requires a global iris database creates a different problem of surveillance and control, and regulators are treating that second problem as serious in its own right.

Vitalik Buterin set out the underlying tension carefully in 2023. Any biometric personhood system, he argued, carries risks that are structural rather than fixable by better engineering: biometric leaks are irreversible, mandatory personhood checks erode the ability to use the internet anonymously, authoritarian governments can coerce enrollment, and it is difficult to be secure and decentralized at the same time. A single Orb network that everyone passes through is convenient because it is a chokepoint, and a chokepoint is exactly what a government or a company can later lean on.

This forces a narrow design question: whether verifying humanness requires a central gatekeeper holding biometrics, or whether it can be done in a more distributed, privacy-respecting way. Attempts at the second route already exist. Social-graph systems such as BrightID and Proof of Humanity establish that an account is a unique person through webs of vouching rather than a scanned body, and zero-knowledge methods let someone prove they hold a valid credential without revealing which one. A peer-to-peer authenticator, in which people verify each other directly instead of routing every check through one authority, is a further illustration that the centralized-biometric model is a choice rather than the only available shape. None of these alternatives is finished, and each has its own failure modes: social graphs can exclude the isolated and be gamed by tight groups, and distributed systems are harder to make robust against a determined attacker. No one has a personhood system that is inclusive, secure, decentralized, and privacy-preserving all at once, so the real choice is between imperfect designs, and the differences between them fall almost entirely on where the sensitive data sits and who can be excluded or coerced.

That unsolved state is why the details matter now, while the standards are being set. The demand for proof of personhood is real and will grow, because the loss of default trust is real and already priced into how people behave. The danger is that the first system to reach global scale becomes the default by inertia, and if that system is a single private holder of everyone's biometrics, we will have answered the fakery problem by building the surveillance infrastructure that critics have warned about for decades. A world where you must scan your iris through one company's device to prove you are a person trades a problem of too many convincing fakes for a problem of one entity that decides who counts as real.

The verifiable human is becoming valuable, and that part is hard to stop. What remains open is whether we verify humanness through a chokepoint that concentrates the most sensitive data we have, or through methods that let a person prove they are one of us without handing over the parts of themselves they can never get back. The Arup employee needed a way to know that a real, accountable person stood behind a request. Supplying that assurance at internet scale is worth doing, and the form it takes will decide whether it protects people or catalogs them.
