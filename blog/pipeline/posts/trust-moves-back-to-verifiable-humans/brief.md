# Brief: In a World Full of Convincing Fakes, Trust Moves Back to People You Can Verify

## Slug
trust-moves-back-to-verifiable-humans

## Working title
In a World Full of Convincing Fakes, Trust Moves Back to People You Can Verify

## One-line angle (also the Substack subtitle / SEO hook)
As synthetic text, voice, and faces become indistinguishable from real ones, verified human provenance becomes scarce — and scarcity is what makes it valuable.

## Single argument (thesis)
As synthetic media become indistinguishable from human-made content, the ability to verify that something genuinely came from a specific human becomes a scarce and valuable form of trust, which is why provenance and proof-of-personhood are emerging as central problems — and why how we build them matters.

## Concrete anchor (optional — a real example to ground the argument)
The "dead internet" debate — the claim that a growing share of online content and interaction is machine-generated — set against Worldcoin's World ID, which scans irises to issue a proof-of-personhood credential, and the privacy backlash it provoked.

## What this post must cover
- The shift: text, voice, and faces can now be synthesized convincingly, so "it looks real" no longer certifies a human origin, and the default trust we placed in media erodes.
- Why verified human provenance becomes valuable: when fakes are cheap and abundant, a credible signal that content came from a specific, real person is scarce, and scarcity is what markets and trust systems price.
- The "dead internet" framing as evidence the problem is already felt: rising suspicion that much online activity is synthetic changes how people read everything.
- The proposed solutions and their tensions: proof-of-personhood systems like Worldcoin's World ID promise to certify humanness but concentrate sensitive biometric data and control, provoking privacy and centralization objections.
- The design question this raises: whether verifying humanness requires centralized biometric gatekeepers, or whether more privacy-respecting, decentralized approaches to human provenance are possible.
- The stakes: getting this wrong trades a fake-content problem for a surveillance-and-gatekeeping problem, so the how of verification matters as much as the that.
