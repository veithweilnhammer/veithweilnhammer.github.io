# Evidence

**Thesis:** As synthetic text, voice, and faces become indistinguishable from
human-made content, a credible signal that something came from a specific real
person turns scarce, and scarcity is what trust systems price — which is why
proof-of-personhood is becoming a central problem, and why the way we build it
matters as much as whether we build it at all.

## Do not overclaim

- The dead internet theory in its strong form (that the internet is *mostly*
  AI-generated by design, coordinated by governments) is a conspiracy theory, not
  an established fact. The defensible claim is narrower: automated traffic is now
  roughly half of measured web traffic, and suspicion of fakery is widespread.
- Imperva's "49.6% bots" measures traffic to sites using its network, not the
  share of *content* that is synthetic, and not human belief. Do not equate the
  two.
- Worldcoin is not "banned everywhere." Actions differ by country (temporary
  suspensions, corrective orders, one court ruling), and the company contests
  several of them and continues to operate in many markets.
- Proof-of-personhood does not verify *what* a person said or that content is
  true; it asserts one-human-one-account. Do not conflate personhood with content
  authenticity or provenance of a specific file.
- Do not present decentralized/social-graph personhood as solved or clearly
  superior; Buterin himself frames all approaches as carrying serious trade-offs.

  summarized or evaluated here.

# Draft

In January 2024, a finance employee at the engineering firm Arup joined a video
call with what looked like the company's chief financial officer and several
colleagues. The faces were familiar, the voices matched, and the request was
routine enough for a senior finance meeting: authorize a set of confidential
transfers. Over the following days the employee sent about HK$200 million,
roughly 25 million US dollars, in fifteen separate payments. Every person on that
call except the employee had been synthesized from publicly available footage.
Hong Kong police described it as one of the largest deepfake frauds on record, and
the money was never recovered.

The detail that matters is not the size of the loss but what failed. The employee
was skeptical of the initial email and asked for a meeting precisely to confirm
that a real person stood behind the request. The meeting is what convinced them.
For most of history, seeing a face and hearing a voice in real time was a reliable
way to establish that you were dealing with a specific human being. That check
now returns the wrong answer.

Synthetic text, voice, and images have crossed the point where "it looks real" no
longer certifies a human origin. A convincing face can be generated, a familiar
voice cloned from a few seconds of audio, and fluent prose produced at essentially
zero marginal cost. The signal we used for millennia to tell person from
imitation — the apparent realism of the thing itself — has been decoupled from the
fact it used to guarantee. This is a general property of the current technology,
not a property of one clever scam. When the surface can be manufactured, the
surface stops being evidence.

The consequence shows up first as a change in how people read everything online.
The "dead internet theory," posted in 2021 on a small forum and picked up by *The
Atlantic* the same year, holds that much of what we encounter on the web is
machine-generated and that genuine human activity is a shrinking fraction of the
whole. As a literal account of a coordinated takeover it is a conspiracy theory.
As a description of a mood it has become mainstream, and there are measurements
underneath the mood. Imperva's 2024 report estimated that bots made up 49.6% of
internet traffic in 2023, with automated traffic rising for the fifth year
running, a trend the report ties directly to generative AI making bots cheap to
build. A reader who assumes that a reply, a review, or a face might be automated is
now making a reasonable prior, not a paranoid one. Default trust in anonymous
online content is eroding because the base rate of fakery has genuinely climbed.

When something becomes abundant and cheap, whatever remains scarce gains value.
Convincing content is now abundant, which makes a credible signal that a specific
real person produced a specific thing scarce. That scarcity is why proof of human
origin is turning into an economic and social good rather than a background
assumption. Platforms want it to price advertising and fight fraud, banks want it
to authorize transactions, and ordinary people want it to know whether the account
arguing with them belongs to anyone at all. The market is already forming around
the guarantee that used to be free.

The pricing is becoming literal. In 2025 Match Group began piloting World ID on
Tinder in Japan, so that users could earn a badge certifying they are a real,
unique person over eighteen, aimed squarely at romance scams and AI-generated
profiles; World also announced a partnership with Visa. Platforms that once gave
away a checkmark for a phone number are now attaching verification to a scarce
credential and, in some cases, rewarding people who obtain it. The same logic runs
through paid verification badges on social networks and the growing willingness of
banks to add a human-confirmation step before a large transfer. Each of these is a
small acknowledgment that "this action came from a real, distinct person" has
become information worth paying for, precisely because it can no longer be assumed
from the content itself.

Two related things are being asked for, and it helps to keep them apart.
Provenance is a claim about a particular artifact: that this recording, image, or
message came from a known source and has not been altered. Proof of personhood is
a claim about an account or an actor: that there is one distinct human behind it,
without necessarily revealing who. The Arup case needed the second kind. The
employee did not need to authenticate a file; they needed to know that a real,
authorized person was on the other end. Much of the trust we are losing is trust
of that second kind, and it is the harder one to rebuild, because it requires
tying a digital action to a body in the world without turning that link into a
tracking device.

The most visible attempt to supply it is Worldcoin, co-founded by OpenAI's Sam
Altman and since rebranded to "World." Its premise is that as AI makes humans and
bots indistinguishable in text, we will need hardware that certifies humanness
directly. A device called the Orb scans a person's iris and issues a credential,
World ID, that lets them later prove they are a unique human without re-scanning.
By late 2025 more than twelve million people had signed up, and the project talks
openly about reaching a billion. The design does try to limit exposure: the system
is meant to store a cryptographic derivative rather than raw images, and to prove
uniqueness without revealing identity.

The reception shows why this is genuinely hard. Spain's data-protection authority
ordered a temporary halt to iris collection in March 2024. Germany's Bavarian
regulator ordered corrective measures and deletion of non-compliant data at the
end of that year. In May 2025 a Kenyan court ruled the collection unlawful and
ordered the data deleted. The recurring objection is not that iris scanning fails
to work; it is that it works by concentrating the most permanent kind of personal
data — a biometric you cannot change if it leaks — under the control of a private
system, often gathered from people who were offered tokens to participate. A
solution to the fakery problem that requires a global iris database raises a
different problem of surveillance and control, and regulators are treating that
second problem as serious in its own right.

Vitalik Buterin, writing in 2023, set out the underlying tension more carefully
than most of the marketing on either side. He noted that any biometric personhood
system carries risks that are structural rather than fixable by better engineering:
biometric leaks are irreversible, mandatory personhood checks erode the ability to
use the internet anonymously, authoritarian governments can coerce enrollment, and
it is difficult to be secure and decentralized at the same time. A single Orb
network that everyone must pass through is convenient precisely because it is a
chokepoint, and a chokepoint is exactly what a government or a company can later
lean on.

The design question this forces is whether verifying humanness requires a
centralized gatekeeper holding biometrics, or whether it can be done in a more
distributed, privacy-respecting way. There are existing attempts at the second
route. Social-graph systems such as BrightID and Proof of Humanity establish that
an account is a unique person through webs of vouching and verification rather than
a scanned body, and zero-knowledge techniques let someone prove they hold a valid
credential without revealing which one. My own work sketches a peer-to-peer
version of the same intuition, where people authenticate each other directly
rather than routing every check through one authority — offered here only as
evidence that the centralized-biometric model is a choice, not the only shape the
solution can take. None of these alternatives is finished, and each has its own
failure modes: social graphs can exclude the isolated and be gamed by tight
groups, and distributed systems are harder to make robust against a determined
attacker. The honest position is that no one has a personhood system that is
inclusive, secure, decentralized, and privacy-preserving all at once. The choice
in front of us is between imperfect designs, and the differences between those
designs fall almost entirely on where the sensitive data sits and who can be
excluded or coerced.

That unsolved state is exactly why the details matter now, while the standards are
being set. The demand for proof of personhood is real and will grow, because the
loss of default trust is real and is already priced into how people behave. The
danger is that the first system to reach global scale becomes the default by
inertia, and if that system is a single private holder of everyone's biometrics,
we will have answered the fakery problem by building the surveillance
infrastructure that critics have warned about for decades. A world where you must
scan your iris to prove you are a person, through one company's device, is not
obviously better than a world where you cannot tell people from bots. It trades a
problem of too many convincing fakes for a problem of one entity that can decide
who counts as real.

The verifiable human is becoming valuable, and that part is hard to stop. What
remains open is whether we verify humanness through a chokepoint that concentrates
the most sensitive data we have, or through methods that let a person prove they
are one of us without handing over the parts of themselves they can never get
back. The Arup employee needed a way to know that a real, accountable person stood
behind a request. Supplying that assurance at internet scale is worth doing. The
form it takes will determine whether it protects people or catalogs them.
