# Evidence

Thesis: As synthetic media become cheap and convincing, trust shifts from judging whether a message looks real to verifying whether it came from a real, accountable human, so proof-of-personhood and provenance systems become valuable infrastructure whose design determines whether they protect trust or centralize surveillance power.

Do not overclaim:

- Do not present “dead internet theory” as true; treat it as a cultural signal of suspicion.
- Do not claim all or most online content is already AI-generated. Bot traffic estimates vary by vendor, measurement method, and traffic type.
- Do not claim synthetic text, voice, or faces are always indistinguishable from real media. The evidence supports weaker claims: many people struggle, and detection is becoming less reliable.
- Do not claim World ID stores raw iris images by default. World says images are processed locally and destroyed unless users opt into backup, while iris codes or related uniqueness data remain central to the system.
- Do not claim proof of personhood proves that a statement is true. It can prove uniqueness or provenance, while truth still requires evidence.
- Do not claim decentralization automatically solves privacy or coercion risks. Social-graph and biometric systems both create attack surfaces.

# Draft

For most of the social internet, the default test for human origin was informal and visual. A post sounded like something a person would write, a voice carried the small irregularities of a familiar speaker, and a face looked like a face in a photograph or video. These checks were always imperfect, but they were good enough for everyday trust because producing convincing substitutes required time, skill, access, or obvious fraud. Generative AI changes that baseline. Text, voices, and faces can now be synthesized at low cost, which means that the appearance of humanness carries less information than it used to. As synthetic media become abundant, the scarce signal becomes a credible link to a real human source.

Evidence from text, face, and speech synthesis already shows the problem. In experiments on AI-generated news-style text, Sarah Kreps and colleagues found that readers were largely unable to distinguish machine-written stories from human-written ones. Sophie Nightingale and Hany Farid showed that AI-synthesized faces had crossed a practical threshold in which observers could not reliably separate them from real faces, and even rated the synthetic faces as more trustworthy on average. In speech, Kimberly Mai and colleagues presented people with genuine and synthetic audio in English and Mandarin; listeners correctly spotted the deepfakes only 73% of the time, and training with examples improved performance only slightly. These studies differ in method and in the technologies they test. Together, they show that ordinary perception is a weak certificate of origin.

Detection tools help, although they cannot restore the old baseline. A detector can flag a likely synthetic text or manipulated image under one set of assumptions, while the next model, editing step, or paraphrase can change the distribution it was trained to recognize. Work on AI-text detection has shown that paraphrasing can sharply reduce detection rates, and that watermarking schemes can create their own spoofing problems. Detection remains useful as a partial defense in an adversarial setting, where the generator, the detector, and the person trying to evade the detector can all adapt.

The “dead internet” debate matters because it describes the psychological effect of this shift before the measurement problem is fully solved. The theory itself, in its strongest form, is a conspiracy claim that the internet has already been mostly replaced by bots and AI-generated content. That claim should be rejected. The weaker and more important observation is that people increasingly encounter online spaces where the human status of posts, replies, likes, reviews, and accounts feels uncertain. Imperva’s 2025 Bad Bot Report estimated that automated traffic had reached 51% of web traffic and that bad bots alone accounted for 37%. Those numbers should be treated as one vendor’s measurement of traffic it can see. They still explain why suspicion has become easier to maintain. Once a reader believes a large share of activity may be synthetic, every ordinary signal becomes a little less reassuring.

Trust systems respond to abundance by pricing scarcity. When anyone can create a plausible review, a plausible profile picture, or a plausible voice message, the valuable thing becomes the hard-to-fake connection between a message and a source with a real history and reputation. This is the same basic problem George Akerlof described in the market for used cars: when buyers cannot tell high-quality goods from low-quality imitations, the market needs credible information mechanisms or trust collapses toward the worst case. Online media now faces a version of that problem. The question increasingly becomes whether a message can be traced to a source whose origin, continuity, and accountability are hard to counterfeit.

There are two related forms of provenance here. Content provenance records where a file came from and what happened to it. The Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity, which maintains the C2PA standard, is building an open technical system for attaching origin and edit-history information to digital media. A photograph might carry a signed record that it came from a particular camera or publisher and was edited in particular ways. That record gives the reader a chain of custody that can be checked, while leaving the photograph’s importance and interpretation to ordinary evidence.

Human provenance asks a different question. It tries to establish whether an account, action, or credential belongs to a real and unique person. This is the proof-of-personhood problem. A useful proof-of-personhood system lets a person show, for example, that they are one human participant in a vote, one claimant for a scarce benefit, or one real user behind an account, while revealing as little else as possible. The word “unique” matters because many online attacks rely on scale: one operator creating thousands of accounts, thousands of reviews, or thousands of votes. A system that limits one action to one person changes the economics of those attacks.

World ID is the best-known attempt to build this kind of infrastructure at global scale. World, formerly Worldcoin, describes World ID as a privacy-preserving protocol that lets people prove they are real and unique online without disclosing personal information such as a name, phone number, or email address. Its highest-assurance “Proof of Human” credential depends on Orb verification. In World’s account, the Orb verifies that a person is live and unique, processes iris images locally, creates an iris code for uniqueness checking, and then allows the person to use zero-knowledge proofs so that apps can confirm uniqueness without tracking the same user across services.

The appeal of this design is straightforward. If AI makes digital behavior easy to simulate, a real-world attestation can reintroduce friction at the point where it matters. A person can use one credential across many services without repeatedly handing over passports, phone numbers, or platform-specific identity documents. In the ideal version, the internet gains a reusable proof that a user is a unique human while apps learn almost nothing else about that user. For online voting, anti-spam systems, one-person-one-claim distributions, and spaces where people want to filter for human accounts, that would be useful.

The backlash to World ID shows why the method of verification matters as much as the existence of verification. In March 2024, Spain’s data protection authority ordered Tools for Humanity to stop collecting and processing personal data in Spain for the Worldcoin project and to block data already collected. The agency cited complaints about insufficient information, collection of data from minors, and inability to withdraw consent, and emphasized that biometric data receives special protection under the GDPR because of its sensitive nature. Portugal’s regulator soon ordered a 90-day suspension of biometric data collection after complaints, and Kenya had already suspended Worldcoin enrolment while authorities investigated security, financial-services, and data-protection concerns.

These regulatory actions leave the technical merits of World ID open. They identify the social cost of turning human verification into infrastructure. Biometric identifiers are hard to change after exposure. A password can be reset, and a device key can be rotated. A face or iris belongs to the body. Even if raw images are deleted, users still need to trust the capture device, the software, the governance process, the enrolment operators, and the institutions that can pressure or regulate them. A proof-of-personhood system also decides who gets counted, who is excluded, and which applications can demand proof. That makes it a gatekeeping system, even when it is built for good reasons.

The central design question is therefore narrower than the public debate often suggests. The question is how to verify human provenance with the least durable personal exposure and the least institutional concentration. Biometric systems offer strong uniqueness checks, but they concentrate trust in hardware, enrolment networks, and biometric processing. Social-graph systems use vouching between people, which can reduce biometric dependence but may reproduce social exclusion and make privacy harder in other ways. Document-based systems build on state identity, which is convenient for some users and inaccessible or dangerous for others. Cryptographic systems can hide links between uses, but they still need some initial source of trust.

A good system will probably combine several weaker proofs instead of demanding one universal credential. Some contexts need high assurance: a national benefit, a binding vote, or a financial service vulnerable to large-scale fraud. Other contexts need lighter signals: a forum may only need evidence that an account has a long-lived human reputation, or that a post came from a known authorial key. The design should follow the risk. Systems should disclose the minimum information needed for the specific action, separate proof of uniqueness from proof of legal identity, allow pseudonymous participation where possible, and make refusal realistic rather than punitive.

This distinction also matters for journalism, science, and public speech. Provenance can tell a reader that an image came from a newsroom, a research group, or a named photographer. It can tell an editor that a submission came from the same person who has an established record. It can help a platform slow down mass automation without forcing every user into a public identity regime. Provenance cannot decide what is true. A real person can lie, misunderstand, or manipulate evidence, and a synthetic system can reproduce a true statement. The value of provenance is that it restores accountability to the chain through which claims arrive.

Doing nothing allows default suspicion to rise. If readers assume that comments, reviews, profiles, and images may be synthetic, they become easier to manipulate in two directions. They may believe convincing fakes when those fakes fit their expectations, and they may dismiss genuine evidence as fabricated when it becomes inconvenient. This “liar’s dividend” is already familiar from deepfake debates: the existence of plausible fakes gives bad actors a ready excuse to deny real recordings. A provenance layer makes denial more costly when a source and chain of custody are clear, even though motivated disbelief will remain.

The risk of doing verification badly is a different failure. A system built around a small number of biometric gatekeepers could make everyday participation depend on enrolment in infrastructure people cannot inspect, negotiate with, or leave. That would make online life more verifiable while turning the conditions for verification into a new site of surveillance and exclusion. The history of identity systems suggests that tools introduced for fraud prevention often spread into employment, policing, migration control, and access to public services. Proof of humanness should therefore be designed as a constrained credential, not as a general license to participate in society.

The most useful direction is plural, privacy-preserving provenance. Content credentials can help establish the source and edit history of media. Human credentials can help establish uniqueness or continuity without exposing a full identity. Reputation can remain local to communities instead of being collapsed into a universal score. Open standards and independent audits can make verification systems easier to inspect, while legal limits can restrict when proof of personhood may be required. These choices are less dramatic than a single global identity layer, but they match the structure of the problem. Trust online is contextual, so verification should be contextual as well.

Synthetic media makes humanness valuable because it makes humanness harder to infer. The old internet allowed many interactions to proceed on weak evidence because imitation was costly enough. That assumption is fading. The next layer of trust will depend on systems that can say, with appropriate limits, that a message came from a particular source or that an action came from a unique human. The hard part is building those systems so that they verify people without turning verification into a new form of control.
