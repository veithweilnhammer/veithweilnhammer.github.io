## Top priorities

1. **Hedge the substitution-at-scale claims.** Quote: “If tens of millions of people who might otherwise have been driven to reconnect instead resolve the feeling with an app…” Fix: make this conditional and evidence-limited: “If companion use replaces attempts at human contact for many users, isolation could become easier to tolerate.” Current evidence supports short-term loneliness relief, not population-level substitution.
2. **Remove style-guide throat-clearing / announcement openers.** Several paragraphs begin with essayistic signposts rather than claims. Replace them with direct topic sentences.
3. **Tighten factual/product claims about AI companions.** Quote: “retain what you told them last week” and “Character.AI” in the loneliness-reduction evidence paragraph. Fix: say “can preserve conversational context or user memories, depending on the product,” and keep the De Freitas loneliness claim separate from app examples not studied there.

## Style-guide violations

- Quote: “The question worth asking is whether…” Problem: meta/essay framing. Fix: “The important issue is whether these systems restore the social connection that loneliness normally signals, or mainly reduce the feeling.”
- Quote: “The signal tracks perceived connection, not a simple count of people.” Problem: banned “not X” construction. Fix: “The signal tracks perceived connection: the gap between wanted and felt connection, rather than the number of people nearby.”
- Quote: “A second layer of the account matters here.” Problem: punchy announcement sentence. Fix: “Loneliness also changes how people interpret social cues.”
- Quote: “Loneliness does not only push you toward people; it also changes how you read them.” Problem: banned “not only … also” construction. Fix: “Loneliness pushes people toward contact while making them more alert to rejection and hostility.”
- Quote: “The concern begins where that relief separates…” Problem: dramatic setup. Fix: “The risk is that short-term relief can separate the feeling from the social condition it normally reports.”
- Quote: “Someone can spend an evening in warm conversation with a chatbot… same empty calendar… anyone who would notice if they disappeared.” Problem: too evocative/scene-setting. Fix: “A person may feel heard after using a chatbot while still having no more human contact or practical support than before.”
- Quote: “The scale of adoption is what raises the stakes…” Problem: announcement opener. Fix: “Population scale matters because the health risks attach to isolation itself, not only to the feeling of loneliness.”
- Quote: “The other side deserves to be taken seriously…” Problem: rhetorical signpost. Fix: “For some people, AI companions may reduce suffering when ordinary reconnection is not available.”
- Quote: “The line between a bridge and a substitute is where the whole question turns…” Problem: essayistic flourish. Fix: “The bridge-versus-substitute distinction should be measured by what happens after use.”
- Quote: “once the screen is dark.” Problem: poetic coda. Fix: “after companion use.”

## Factual grounding

- Verified: the Surgeon General figures are supported by the 2023 advisory/HHS summaries: about half of U.S. adults report loneliness; weak social connection is linked to mortality risk comparable to smoking up to 15 cigarettes/day; 29% coronary heart disease, 32% stroke, and about 50% dementia risk figures are accurate.
- Verified: De Freitas et al., “AI Companions Reduce Loneliness,” *Journal of Consumer Research*, DOI 10.1093/jcr/ucaf040, reports loneliness reduction, roughly comparable to human interaction in the experiment, and “feeling heard” as a mechanism. Add “in short-term studies” whenever using “reduced loneliness.”
- Verified: Maples et al. surveyed 1,006 Replika student users; about 30/1,006 spontaneously reported halted suicidal ideation. Current wording correctly flags self-report and selection limits.
- Hedge: “public-health problem on the scale of a major chronic disease” slightly overstates the advisory. Use “as a major public-health problem” unless citing exact comparative language.
- Hedge: “people born with congenital insensitivity to pain… tend to die young.” Medical sources support repeated injuries and reduced life expectancy, but “tend to die young” is broad. Use “can suffer repeated injuries and reduced life expectancy.”
- Hedge/remove: “reconnection is easiest early” and “tens of millions… might otherwise have been driven to reconnect” are plausible but not directly supported by the cited evidence.

## Structure & clarity

The draft leads with the real problem, explains Cacioppo’s alarm model clearly, then applies it to AI companions. A non-specialist can follow it. The main weakness is repetition in the alarm analogies: hunger, pain, congenital pain insensitivity, hunger-without-calories, and fracture pain all do similar work. Keep one or two; cut the “substance that removes hunger without calories” comparison or merge it with the fracture example.

## One self-contained argument

Thesis: loneliness is an evolved alarm for unmet social connection, and AI companions may reduce the feeling without restoring the connection the feeling is meant to prompt. The draft develops this one argument consistently. The hypervigilance paragraph serves the argument, but its final sentence (“held at a low, chronic level”) is unclear; revise to “when it persists chronically.”
