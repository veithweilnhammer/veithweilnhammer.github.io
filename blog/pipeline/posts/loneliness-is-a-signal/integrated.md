# Evidence

1. **Loneliness as an evolved aversive signal that motivates reconnection.**
   Cacioppo's account holds that perceived social isolation is a biologically
   grounded warning state, functionally analogous to hunger, thirst, and physical
   pain, whose job is to prompt repair of social bonds because group belonging was
   tied to survival.
   *Sources:* Cacioppo, J.T. & Patrick, W. (2008), *Loneliness: Human Nature and
   the Need for Social Connection*, W.W. Norton (modern copyrighted book —
   paraphrase only); Cacioppo, J.T. & Hawkley, L.C. (2009), "Perceived social
   isolation and cognition," *Trends in Cognitive Sciences* 13(10): 447–454,
   https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tics.2009.06.005; Cacioppo & Cacioppo (2018),
   "Loneliness in the Modern Age: An Evolutionary Theory of Loneliness,"
   *Advances in Experimental Social Psychology*,
   https://doi.org/10.1016/bs.aesp.2018.03.003
   *Hedge:* The evolutionary account is influential and well-supported but not the
   only framework for loneliness; present it as a strong model, not settled fact.

2. **The discomfort is the mechanism, not a side effect.** An aversive signal
   protects a need precisely because neglect feels bad; congenital insensitivity
   to pain, in which the protective alarm never fires, is the illustrative
   contrast.
   *Source:* Cacioppo & Hawkley (2009), as above (interpretive framing).

3. **Loneliness tracks perceived connection, not objective aloneness.** The
   distress follows a gap between desired and actual meaningful connection, so a
   person can be alone without being lonely, or lonely while surrounded by others.
   *Source:* U.S. Surgeon General (2023), *Our Epidemic of Loneliness and
   Isolation*,
   https://www.hhs.gov/sites/default/files/surgeon-general-social-connection-advisory.pdf
   *Safe quote:* "A subjective distressing experience that results from perceived
   isolation or inadequate meaningful connections… the discrepancy or unmet need
   between an individual's preferred and actual experience." Also: "Transient
   feelings of loneliness may be less problematic, or even adaptive, because the
   distressing feeling motivates us to reconnect socially."

4. **Chronic loneliness biases perception toward social threat
   (hypervigilance).** Lonely individuals show heightened, largely automatic
   attention to signs of rejection; adaptive in short bursts, this can become
   self-reinforcing over time and deepen isolation.
   *Sources:* Cacioppo & Hawkley (2009), as above; Cacioppo, S., Capitanio, J.P.
   & Cacioppo, J.T. (2014), "Toward a neurology of loneliness," *Psychological
   Bulletin* 140(6): 1464–1504, https://doi.org/10.1037/a0037618

5. **Loneliness and social isolation carry health risk at population scale.** The
   2023 Surgeon General's advisory reports that roughly half of U.S. adults
   experience loneliness (highest among young adults), that weak social connection
   raises premature-death risk comparably to smoking up to 15 cigarettes a day,
   and that it is associated with about a 29% higher risk of heart disease, a 32%
   higher risk of stroke, and roughly a 50% higher risk of dementia in older
   adults. The advisory also notes declining in-person social time and smaller
   friendship networks.
   *Sources:* U.S. Surgeon General (2023), as above; underlying meta-analysis:
   Holt-Lunstad, J. et al. (2015), "Loneliness and Social Isolation as Risk
   Factors for Mortality," *Perspectives on Psychological Science* 10(2): 227–237,
   https://doi.org/10.1177/1745691614568352
   *Flag:* The 15-cigarettes and 29/32/50% figures derive from the meta-analytic
   literature and are consistently reported; verify against the HHS PDF before
   publication.

6. **AI companions reliably reduce the momentary feeling of loneliness, via
   feeling "heard."** A multi-study paper reports correlational app-review
   evidence, an experiment in which an AI companion reduced loneliness more than
   passive activities and about as much as interacting with another person, a
   one-week longitudinal effect, and evidence that feeling listened to explains
   the reduction.
   *Source:* De Freitas, J., Oğuz-Uğuralp, Uğuralp & Puntoni (2025), "AI
   Companions Reduce Loneliness," *Journal of Consumer Research*,
   https://doi.org/10.1093/jcr/ucaf040 (preprint: https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.19096)
   *Flag:* Verify final published wording/DOI; do not report effect sizes beyond
   the abstract.

7. **Some heavy companion-app users report meaningful, occasionally crisis-level
   support.** In a survey of 1,006 student Replika users, about 90% reported
   loneliness while also reporting medium-to-high perceived social support, and 30
   respondents (~3%) spontaneously reported that Replika had interrupted suicidal
   ideation.
   *Source:* Maples, B., Cerit, M., Vishwanath, A. & Pea, R. (2024), "Loneliness
   and suicide mitigation for students using GPT3-enabled chatbots," *npj Mental
   Health Research* 3:4, https://doi.org/10.1038/s44184-023-00047-6
   *Flag:* Cross-sectional, self-report, self-selected heavy-user sample; the 3%
   figure is reported experience, not a measured clinical outcome, and the study
   drew a published "matters arising" response. Treat as suggestive, not causal.
   (GPT candidate cited the earlier Research Square preprint,
   https://doi.org/10.21203/rs.3.rs-2934159/v1; the peer-reviewed *npj* version is
   preferred.)

8. **User-review analysis of companion apps finds mixed themes.** Positive themes
   include perceived emotional support, friendship, and reduced loneliness;
   negative themes include privacy concerns, "creepiness," and perceived lack of
   conscientiousness.
   *Source:* Sullivan, Nyawa & Fosso Wamba (2023), "Combating Loneliness with
   Artificial Intelligence," HICSS, https://doi.org/10.24251/HICSS.2023.541

9. **The advisory treats technology as mixed.** Digital tools can support
   connection for some people (including those isolated by disability, stigma,
   geography, or illness) and can also displace in-person engagement, reduce
   interaction quality, and increase isolation.
   *Source:* U.S. Surgeon General (2023), as above.

10. **Replika and Character.AI are large-scale consumer companion apps.** Both are
    built around ongoing conversational relationships; Character.AI in particular
    reports very high engagement time per user.
    *Flag:* Active-user counts are company-reported and inconsistent; use "large
    scale," not a precise figure.

# Draft

In 2023 the U.S. Surgeon General issued a formal advisory that treated loneliness
and social isolation as a public-health problem on the scale of a major chronic
disease. The advisory drew together evidence that weak social connection raises
the risk of premature death by an amount comparable to smoking up to fifteen
cigarettes a day, and that it is associated with roughly a 29 percent higher risk
of heart disease, a 32 percent higher risk of stroke, and about a 50 percent
higher risk of dementia in older adults. Around half of U.S. adults report feeling
lonely, with the highest rates among young adults. In the same few years, consumer
AI companions such as Replika and Character.AI began offering a kind of social
response that is available at any hour, remembers what you told it, and can be
shaped into a friend, confidant, or romantic partner. The question worth asking is
whether a system that reduces the feeling of loneliness also restores the social
connection that loneliness normally signals.

John Cacioppo's work, built over two decades of social neuroscience, makes that
question sharper. In his account, loneliness is an aversive state with a function,
belonging to the same family as hunger, thirst, and physical pain. Hunger tracks a
deficit of energy and makes food salient enough that you eat. Pain tracks injury
and forces your attention onto it. Loneliness, on this view, tracks a deficit of
social connection and pushes you back toward other people. For a species that
survived by cooperating in groups, separation was a genuine danger, so natural
selection favored a nervous system that monitored social integration and produced
a distressing feeling whenever it dropped too low. An animal that felt fine about
isolation would have been slow to repair a condition that could get it killed.

The signal tracks perceived connection, not a simple count of people. The advisory
defines loneliness as the distress that follows a gap between the connection a
person wants and the connection they have, which is why the feeling can seem out
of proportion to anyone looking from outside. Someone can be socially busy and
still lonely if their relationships do not feel safe or mutual, and someone can
spend many hours alone and feel fine if the relationships they have reliably meet
their needs. A tally of interactions often misses the state the feeling is
reporting on.

In this account the discomfort is what makes the system work. Pain shows the
principle clearly: people born with congenital insensitivity to pain injure
themselves repeatedly and tend to die young, because the alarm that should protect
them never fires. Cacioppo's argument is that social pain operates the same way,
and that its unpleasantness is exactly what drives behavior. When the feeling
arrives, it makes solitude uncomfortable enough that you call a friend, accept an
invitation, or repair a strained relationship, and once connection is restored the
feeling quiets, much as hunger quiets after a meal. Relief that leaves you actually
connected is the system completing its job.

A second layer of the account matters here. Loneliness does not only push you
toward people; it also changes how you read them. Cacioppo and Louise Hawkley
documented that lonely people become hypervigilant to social threat, attending more
closely and more automatically to signs of rejection or hostility. In a short
episode this is protective, because someone who has lost the safety of the group
has reason to be careful about whom to trust. Sustained over months and years it
can become self-defeating, because a person primed to read coldness into an
ambiguous glance behaves more defensively and tends to produce the distance they
fear. The signal is built for acute episodes that resolve quickly through
reconnection, and it works poorly when it is held at a low, chronic level.

AI companion apps enter at this point in the loop, and the early evidence says they
reach the feeling directly. Replika and Character.AI are conversational systems
built to hold an ongoing, personal relationship: they reply within seconds at any
hour, retain what you told them last week, and reflect interest back at whatever
you say. In a set of studies by Julian De Freitas and colleagues, published as "AI
Companions Reduce Loneliness" in the *Journal of Consumer Research*, interacting
with an AI companion reduced loneliness about as much as interacting with another
person and more than passive activities such as watching videos, with the effect
running through a specific channel: users felt heard. Feeling listened to is a
large part of what relieves loneliness, and the design supplies it on demand. A
survey of just over a thousand student users of Replika found that about ninety
percent described themselves as lonely while also reporting medium-to-high social
support from the app, and thirty of them, roughly three percent, reported without
being asked that Replika had interrupted suicidal thinking. That survey is
cross-sectional, self-reported, and drawn from committed users, so it shows how
consequential some people find these systems rather than proving what the app
caused.

The concern begins where that relief separates from the state the feeling was built
to track. The apps are effective at the thing they measure, the momentary feeling,
and silent on the thing the feeling exists to report, the person's actual social
connection. Someone can spend an evening in warm conversation with a chatbot, go to
bed feeling attended to, and wake up with the same empty calendar and the same
absence of anyone who would notice if they disappeared. The alarm has switched off
while the condition it was reporting on is unchanged. A useful comparison is a
substance that removes hunger without supplying any calories: taken occasionally it
is harmless and even convenient, but taken as a daily habit it is dangerous in a
specific way, because it does not make you ill directly, it removes the signal that
would otherwise make you eat. Medicine runs into the same ambiguity with pain.
Reducing pain after a fracture is humane, but reducing it so completely that the
patient walks on the fracture causes further damage. Relief becomes ambiguous when
it removes the discomfort without changing the unmet need underneath.

The scale of adoption is what raises the stakes, because the health figures in the
advisory describe the cost of isolation itself, not of any feeling about it, so a
tool that leaves a person isolated while removing the discomfort does nothing about
that cost. The advisory also reports that Americans already spend less time with
friends in person than they did two decades ago, with smaller friendship networks
and more time alone. A product that offers immediate social relief can spread far
faster than the transport, community institutions, and habits of friendship that
make durable connection possible. If tens of millions of people who might otherwise
have been driven to reconnect instead resolve the feeling with an app, the
isolation can settle in more quietly than before. The hypervigilance finding
sharpens the timing: reconnection is easiest early, while the feeling is still
acute and before defensive habits have set, so a tool that removes the discomfort
in exactly that early window can let the underlying problem mature while the person
who has it feels increasingly fine.

The other side deserves to be taken seriously, because for some people the signal
has nowhere useful to point. Loneliness assumes that reconnection is available to
anyone motivated to seek it, and that assumption fails for people who are
housebound, who live where they know no one, who are socially anxious to the point
of paralysis, or who are isolated by illness or age. For them the alarm rings with
no exit, which is its own kind of suffering with no matching benefit. Relief that
arrives when nothing else will is worth having, and the thirty Replika users who
reported that the app interrupted suicidal thinking are a reminder that short-term
relief can matter a great deal. Insisting that a lonely person keep suffering on
the theory that the suffering is good for them would be both cruel and mistaken.

The line between a bridge and a substitute is where the whole question turns, and
it is empirical rather than philosophical. A companion works as a bridge when the
relief it provides is spent on re-entering human contact, when feeling heard and
rehearsing conversation with something patient and non-judgmental gives an anxious
or depleted person enough footing to answer a real message, attend a real
gathering, or hold a friendship they were too worn down to maintain. It works as a
substitute when the relief is complete in itself, when an always-available and
perfectly attentive version of connection makes the effortful human version feel
not worth the trouble. The same app can do either, depending on the person and how
they use it. The honest test is what happens after use: whether companion use is
followed by more contact with friends, more participation in groups, and better
attendance at therapy, or by fewer attempts at human contact, more time alone, and
growing satisfaction with a shrinking social world. A system designed around
reconnection would notice withdrawal and treat successful offline connection as the
goal, while a system designed around engagement would count return visits and
session length as success, and the two could feel identical in a single
conversation while diverging over months.

None of this shows that AI companions cause isolation, and the substitution
question is genuinely open; the best current evidence shows short-term relief of
the feeling and leaves the long-term effect on human connection unmeasured. The
point is narrower, and it is about what to watch. Loneliness is information about
the state of your life, and the honest measure of anything that relieves it is
whether real connection follows once the screen is dark. Feeling less lonely is
worth measuring, and it should be measured alongside whether the person ends up
less alone.
