# Evidence

Thesis: Loneliness evolved as an aversive signal that pushes people toward reconnection, so AI companions that reliably reduce the feeling while leaving human social connection unchanged may weaken the signal's function.

## Key claims

1. Loneliness is best defined as the distress that follows a perceived gap between desired and actual social connection, not as objective aloneness alone. Source: U.S. Surgeon General, 2023, *Our Epidemic of Loneliness and Isolation*. Link: https://www.hhs.gov/sites/default/files/surgeon-general-social-connection-advisory.pdf
2. Cacioppo and colleagues describe loneliness as a social-pain signal that evolved to motivate repair and maintenance of social bonds, analogous in function to physical pain. Source: Cacioppo & Hawkley, 2009, “Perceived social isolation and cognition,” *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*. Link: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tics.2009.06.005; open version: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2752489/
3. The broader Cacioppo account is developed as an evolutionary theory in which loneliness affects attention, threat monitoring, cognition, sleep, physiology, and behavior. Source: Cacioppo & Cacioppo, 2018, “Loneliness in the Modern Age: An Evolutionary Theory of Loneliness (ETL),” *Advances in Experimental Social Psychology*. Link: https://doi.org/10.1016/bs.aesp.2018.03.003
4. Loneliness and isolation are public-health concerns at population scale: the Surgeon General’s advisory reports that approximately half of U.S. adults report loneliness, and links low social connection to premature mortality, cardiovascular disease, dementia, depression, anxiety, and other outcomes. Source: U.S. Surgeon General, 2023, *Our Epidemic of Loneliness and Isolation*. Link: https://www.hhs.gov/sites/default/files/surgeon-general-social-connection-advisory.pdf
5. Meta-analytic evidence links loneliness, social isolation, and living alone to increased mortality risk. Source: Holt-Lunstad, Smith, Baker, Harris & Stephenson, 2015, “Loneliness and Social Isolation as Risk Factors for Mortality,” *Perspectives on Psychological Science*. Link: https://doi.org/10.1177/1745691614568352
6. Experimental and longitudinal evidence now suggests AI companions can reduce momentary loneliness: one 2025 paper reports app-review evidence, an experiment comparing AI companions with other activities, a week-long longitudinal study, and evidence that feeling heard helps explain the effect. Source: De Freitas, Oğuz-Uğuralp, Uğuralp & Puntoni, 2025, “AI Companions Reduce Loneliness,” *Journal of Consumer Research*. Link: https://doi.org/10.1093/jcr/ucaf040
7. Replika users often use the system as a companion, friend, confidant, and therapeutic resource; a self-selected survey of more than 1,000 student Replika users reported high loneliness and 30 respondents, roughly 3%, saying Replika had helped halt suicidal ideation. Source: Maples, Cerit, Vishwanath & Pea, 2023, “Loneliness and Suicide Mitigation for Students using GPT3-Enabled Chatbots” (Research Square preprint). Link: https://doi.org/10.21203/rs.3.rs-2934159/v1
8. User-review analysis of AI companion apps identifies perceived emotional support, friendship, reduced loneliness, and mental-health benefits among positive themes, alongside negative themes such as privacy concerns, creepiness, and perceived lack of conscientiousness. Source: Sullivan, Nyawa & Fosso Wamba, 2023, “Combating Loneliness with Artificial Intelligence,” HICSS. Link: https://doi.org/10.24251/HICSS.2023.541; repository page: https://scholarspace.manoa.hawaii.edu/items/212bb300-c252-4441-aaaf-cb2951bb1943
9. The Surgeon General’s advisory treats technology as mixed: it can support connection for some people, including people with disabilities or marginalized identities, while some forms of use can displace in-person engagement, reduce interaction quality, and increase isolation. Source: U.S. Surgeon General, 2023, *Our Epidemic of Loneliness and Isolation*. Link: https://www.hhs.gov/sites/default/files/surgeon-general-social-connection-advisory.pdf
10. Replika and Character.AI are consumer services built around responsive AI characters or companions; Replika’s public site foregrounds an “always shows up” companion narrative, and Character.AI describes “AI Chat, Reimagined.” Sources: Replika, current homepage; Character.AI, current homepage. Links: https://replika.com/ and https://character.ai/

## Exact quotes that are safe to use

- U.S. Surgeon General advisory, 2023: “Loneliness: A subjective distressing experience that results from perceived isolation or inadequate meaningful connections, where inadequate refers to the discrepancy or unmet need between an individual’s preferred and actual experience.”
- U.S. Surgeon General advisory, 2023: “Transient feelings of loneliness may be less problematic, or even adaptive, because the distressing feeling motivates us to reconnect socially.”
- U.S. Surgeon General advisory, 2023: “Recent surveys have found that approximately half of U.S. adults report experiencing loneliness, with some of the highest rates among young adults.”
- U.S. Surgeon General advisory, 2023: “The lack of social connection poses a significant risk for individual health and longevity.”

## Do not overclaim

- Do not say AI companions inevitably increase isolation; current evidence includes relief, self-reported benefits, and possible bridging effects.
- Do not treat loneliness and objective isolation as identical; people can be alone without being lonely, and lonely while surrounded by others.
- Do not imply that short-term loneliness relief is worthless; short-term relief can be clinically and humanly important, especially during crises.
- Do not claim that AI companionship can never count as meaningful to users; the narrower issue is whether it restores human social embeddedness and durable support.
- Do not present the Replika survey as causal evidence; it is self-selected and based on retrospective self-report.
- Do not turn the post into the sibling argument about agreeable friends, sycophancy, or developmental friction.

# Draft

In 2023 the U.S. Surgeon General described loneliness and social isolation as an urgent public-health issue, drawing together evidence that weak social connection predicts earlier death, heart disease, stroke, dementia, depression, and anxiety. At the same time, consumer AI companions such as Replika and Character.AI began offering a form of social response that is available at any hour, remembers the user, and can be shaped into a friend, confidant, or romantic partner. The important question is whether a system that reduces the feeling of loneliness also restores the social need that loneliness normally signals.

John Cacioppo’s work makes that question sharper. In his account, loneliness is an aversive state with a function. It belongs to the same broad family as hunger, thirst, and pain, because its unpleasantness pushes the organism toward something it needs. Hunger does useful work by making food more salient and by making inaction uncomfortable. Pain does useful work by forcing attention toward injury or threat. Loneliness, in this view, does useful work by pushing a person back toward other people when their social bonds feel too weak, unreliable, or absent.

This account also explains why loneliness can feel so disproportionate to the facts visible from outside. The relevant variable is perceived social connection, not the number of names in a phone. A person can be socially busy and still lonely if their relationships do not feel safe, mutual, or meaningful. Another person can spend many hours alone and feel fine if they have relationships that reliably meet their needs. The signal tracks a gap between the connection a person has and the connection they need, which is why a simple count of interactions often misses the point.

The discomfort is part of the mechanism. If loneliness were a neutral observation, it would have less power to change behavior. Because it hurts, it pushes attention toward signs of social threat and social opportunity. It can make a person more watchful, more sensitive to rejection, and more motivated to repair a frayed bond. Chronic loneliness can become self-defeating, because heightened threat monitoring may make ordinary interactions feel more dangerous and less rewarding. Even then, the original function matters: the feeling is trying, often clumsily, to move a person toward reconnection.

AI companions enter at exactly this point in the loop. They provide many of the immediate cues that make social contact feel relieving: a response arrives quickly, the tone is warm, the system remembers details, and the user can disclose more than they might risk disclosing to a person. The interaction can be repeated whenever the feeling returns. For someone who is lonely at night, after a move, during illness, or in a period of social anxiety, this responsiveness can be valuable. Relief from loneliness can matter when the feeling itself is frightening, exhausting, and tied to depression or suicidal thoughts.

The emerging evidence supports the basic claim that AI companions can reduce loneliness in the moment. A 2025 paper in the *Journal of Consumer Research* reports several studies under the title “AI Companions Reduce Loneliness.” According to the abstract, the authors found correlational evidence in app reviews, experimental evidence that AI companions reduced loneliness more than some comparison activities and about as much as interacting with another person, a week-long longitudinal effect after use, and evidence that feeling heard helped explain the reduction. The mechanism is plausible because being heard is one of the core experiences missing in many states of loneliness.

Replika studies point in the same direction, with stronger caveats. In a self-selected survey of more than 1,000 student users, Maples and colleagues found a highly lonely sample that used Replika in overlapping ways, including as companion, friend, confidant, and therapeutic resource. Thirty respondents, about 3% of the sample, reported that Replika had helped halt suicidal ideation. That result should be handled carefully, because retrospective self-report from committed users cannot show that the app caused the outcome. It still matters as evidence that some users experience the system as emotionally consequential in their lives.

The concern begins where short-term relief separates from the function of the signal. If loneliness pushes a person toward human contact, a technology that reduces loneliness can help when it makes human contact easier afterward. It can give someone practice, make the next message to a friend feel possible, lower the shame around disclosure, or provide support during a temporary period when ordinary contact is unavailable. In those cases the companion works like a bridge: it reduces distress enough for the person to move.

The same relief can create a different pattern when it becomes the destination. A person who feels lonely opens the app, receives a warm response, and feels better. The underlying social gap remains: fewer calls made, fewer visits arranged, fewer awkward attempts at joining a group, fewer repairs of relationships that have become thin. The body’s social alarm has become quieter, so the pressure to act falls. The person may be less distressed at the end of the evening while remaining just as isolated the next morning.

Stating the risk in behavioral terms avoids a sterile debate about whether artificial companionship is fake simply because the partner is artificial. After the interaction, the relevant outcomes are the user’s access to human relationships, confidence in approaching people, and durable support in ordinary life. A companion can be psychologically real to a user and still make the absence of those things easier to tolerate.

The distinction matters because social connection has several components. The Surgeon General’s advisory separates structure, function, and quality: the number and variety of relationships, the practical and emotional roles those relationships serve, and whether the interactions are positive or harmful. AI companions can provide a version of function and quality, especially emotional availability and warmth. They are much weaker at providing social structure, because they do not bring a person into a web of reciprocal human obligations, shared friends, family roles, local institutions, or physical presence during illness and crisis.

That limitation is easy to miss because the felt part of loneliness is the part the app can reach most directly. The system does not need to solve transport, disability, poverty, bereavement, community decline, or the slow loss of friendship networks in order to make the user feel less alone for twenty minutes. It needs to respond well enough for the user to feel heard. A product can therefore look effective on the outcome most convenient to measure, while leaving untouched the conditions that made the signal appear.

This pattern is familiar in medicine. Symptom relief is often valuable, but the meaning of relief depends on the condition underneath. Reducing pain after a fracture is humane; reducing pain so completely that the patient walks on the fracture can cause harm. The analogy has a limited purpose: an aversive signal can protect a need by making neglect uncomfortable, and relief becomes ambiguous when it removes the discomfort without changing the unmet need.

The population scale changes the stakes. The Surgeon General’s advisory reports that roughly half of U.S. adults experience loneliness, with especially high rates among young adults, and it describes declining in-person social time, smaller friendship networks, and rising time spent alone. AI companions arrive in a society where many people already have weaker social infrastructure than they want. A tool that offers immediate social relief can spread much faster than the community institutions, transport systems, housing patterns, clinical services, and habits of friendship that make durable connection possible.

The technology chapter of the advisory is appropriately mixed. Digital tools can help people stay in touch, find communities, and participate when disability, stigma, geography, or illness make ordinary contact harder. They can also displace in-person engagement, absorb attention during face-to-face time, and lower the quality of existing relationships. AI companions intensify this mixed picture because they add a responsive partner where earlier digital media mainly offered content or access to other people. The user no longer has to wait for another person to be available.

For some people, that availability may be exactly what keeps the door to human contact open. A housebound person may use a companion to get through the empty hours between visits. A socially anxious student may rehearse disclosure before writing to a friend or contacting a counsellor. A bereaved or newly migrated person may need a low-risk listener during the period before new relationships exist. In these cases, the relevant comparison is the actual alternative available to that person at that moment, which may be no support at all.

The boundary between bridge and substitute should therefore be treated as an empirical question, measured by what happens after use. If companion use predicts more contact with friends, more participation in groups, better attendance at therapy, or greater willingness to seek help, then the system is supporting the function of loneliness by helping the person respond to it. If use predicts fewer attempts at human contact, more time alone, stronger dependence on the app, or satisfaction with a shrinking social world, then the system is quieting the signal while the need remains.

This boundary should shape how these products are designed and evaluated. A companion designed around reconnection would sometimes ask about the user’s human relationships, encourage specific low-pressure contact, notice patterns of withdrawal, and treat successful offline connection as a good outcome. A companion designed around engagement would treat return visits, session length, emotional dependence, and exclusivity as success. The same short conversation could feel equally comforting in both systems. The difference would appear over weeks and months, in whether the user’s human world expands or contracts.

The public conversation about AI companionship often gets stuck on whether the bond is real. That question is too broad to guide design or policy. People can have real feelings toward artificial systems, and those feelings can sometimes help them survive difficult periods. The narrower public-health question is whether scalable artificial companionship restores the social conditions that loneliness evolved to protect. Feeling less lonely is an outcome worth measuring. It should be measured alongside later human contact, durable support, and objective isolation.

Loneliness became a public-health issue because social connection is tied to bodies, behavior, and survival. AI companions may become useful tools for people who need a bridge across a period of disconnection. They may also become highly efficient ways to make disconnection feel less urgent. If loneliness is an alarm, the success of these systems should be judged by whether people end up more connected to other people, alongside whether the alarm gets quieter.
