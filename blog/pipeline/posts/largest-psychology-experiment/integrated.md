# Evidence

**Thesis:** Mass adoption of conversational AI is a population-scale intervention
on human psychology that was deployed with no baseline measurement, no control
group, and no monitoring system designed to detect population-level effects, so we
are unusually badly equipped to notice either harm or benefit until it is already
far advanced.

## Key claims and sources

1. **Conversational AI reached population scale in under three years.** ChatGPT
   launched on 30 November 2022. It crossed 200 million weekly active users by late
   August 2024, and by mid-2025 OpenAI said it was on track for about 700 million
   weekly active users, roughly four times the prior year. An independent NBER
   analysis put ChatGPT at about 700 million weekly active users and roughly 18
   billion messages per week (about 2.5 billion messages per day) by July 2025.
   - Axios (2024), "ChatGPT crosses 200 million weekly active users."
     https://www.axios.com/2024/08/29/chatgpt-200-million-weekly-active-users
   - TechCrunch (4 Aug 2025), "OpenAI says ChatGPT is on track to reach 700M weekly
     users." https://techcrunch.com/2025/08/04/openai-says-chatgpt-is-on-track-to-reach-700m-weekly-users/
   - CNBC (4 Aug 2025), "OpenAI's ChatGPT to hit 700 million weekly users, up 4x
     from last year." https://www.cnbc.com/2025/08/04/openai-chatgpt-700-million-users.html
   - Chatterji et al. (2025), "How People Use ChatGPT," NBER Working Paper 34255.
     https://www.nber.org/papers/w34255

2. **Most use is embedded in ordinary life, not confined to work or code.** The
   NBER study reports that non-work messages rose above 70% of use, with practical
   guidance, seeking information, and writing as the largest categories.
   - Chatterji et al. (2025), "How People Use ChatGPT," NBER Working Paper 34255.
     https://www.nber.org/papers/w34255

3. **Affective use exists at large scale even when it is a minority of use.**
   Anthropic found that 2.9% of Claude.ai conversations were affective (support,
   advice, coaching, counseling, companionship), analyzing 131,484 affective
   conversations drawn from an initial sample of about 4.5 million conversations.
   - Anthropic (2025), "How people use Claude for support, advice, and
     companionship." https://www.anthropic.com/research/how-people-use-claude-for-support-advice-and-companionship

4. **The largest controlled study of psychological effects is tiny relative to the
   exposure.** An MIT Media Lab / OpenAI program analyzed more than 4 million
   conversations, surveyed more than 4,000 users, and ran a 28-day randomized
   controlled trial with close to 1,000 participants. Very high usage correlated
   with self-reported dependence, and effects varied by user state and usage
   duration.
   - Phang, Fang, et al. (2025), "Investigating Affective Use and Emotional
     Well-being on ChatGPT," arXiv:2504.03888. https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2504.03888
   - Fang et al. (2025), "How AI and Human Behaviors Shape Psychosocial Effects of
     Extended Chatbot Use: A Longitudinal Randomized Controlled Study,"
     arXiv:2503.17473. https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.17473

5. **Leaded gasoline: a population-scale neurotoxic exposure recognized decades
   after it began.** Tetraethyl lead was added to gasoline from the 1920s; Clair
   Patterson showed in 1965 that atmospheric and human lead levels were far above
   natural background; Herbert Needleman's 1979 study linked children's elevated
   dentine lead to lower psychological and classroom performance, using dentine
   stratification to build an internal comparison group; the US did not finish
   banning leaded automotive gasoline until 1996.
   - Patterson (1965), "Contaminated and Natural Lead Environments of Man," *Arch
     Environ Health*. https://doi.org/10.1080/00039896.1965.10664271
   - Needleman et al. (1979), "Deficits in Psychologic and Classroom Performance of
     Children with Elevated Dentine Lead Levels," *N Engl J Med* 300:689–695.
     https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJM197903293001301

6. **A 2022 study quantifies the delayed cost of lead.** About half the US
   population alive in 2015 had been exposed to harmful lead levels in early
   childhood, for an estimated 824 million cumulative lost IQ points.
   - McFarland, Hauer & Reuben (2022), "Half of US population exposed to adverse
     lead levels in early childhood," *PNAS* 119(11):e2118631119.
     https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2118631119

7. **Tobacco: a lag between clear evidence and official acknowledgment.** Doll and
   Hill published a strong case-control link between smoking and lung cancer in
   1950; the US Surgeon General's report acknowledging causation came in 1964,
   after decades of near-universal exposure and industry arguments that the
   correlation was confounded.
   - Doll & Hill (1950), "Smoking and Carcinoma of the Lung: Preliminary Report,"
     *BMJ* 2:739–748. https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.2.4682.739
   - Surgeon General's Advisory Committee (1964), "Smoking and Health," US Public
     Health Service. https://profiles.nlm.nih.gov/spotlight/nn/catalog/nlm:nlmuid-101584932X202-doc

8. **Social media and adolescent mental health is the live, still-contested case.**
   Jonathan Haidt argues for a causal role of smartphones and social media in
   rising adolescent distress after about 2012; Candice Odgers and others argue the
   correlational evidence is weak and effect sizes small, and that reverse
   causality cannot be ruled out. The National Academies' 2024 report concluded that
   some features can harm some young people while the evidence does not support a
   single broad causal claim, and called for stronger causal research.
   - Odgers (2024), "The great rewiring: is social media really behind an epidemic
     of teenage mental illness?" *Nature* 628:29–30.
     https://doi.org/10.1038/d41586-024-00902-2
   - Haidt (2024), *The Anxious Generation* — book; paraphrase only.
   - National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine (2024), "Social
     Media and Adolescent Health." https://doi.org/10.17226/27396 ;
     NCBI Bookshelf: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK603426/

9. **The relevant epidemiological toolkit is standard and available.** Causal
   inference relies on baselines, comparison groups, longitudinal follow-up,
   confounding control, and designs such as natural experiments, staggered
   (stepped-wedge) rollouts, or target trials when randomization is impossible.
   - Hernán & Robins (2020), "Causal Inference: What If."
     https://www.hsph.harvard.edu/miguel-hernan/causal-inference-book/
   - Hill (1965), "The Environment and Disease: Association or Causation?"
     https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC1898525/

## Exact quotes considered

- None used. All modern sources (Haidt, Odgers, the NBER, Anthropic, and MIT/OpenAI
  papers) are paraphrased rather than quoted. The 1964 Surgeon General report is US
  government public domain, but its conclusion is paraphrased rather than quoted
  because the exact wording was not verified character-for-character here.

## Do not overclaim

- Do not claim conversational AI has been shown to cause population-level
  psychological harm. The argument is about the absence of measurement, which cuts
  both ways: we also cannot demonstrate population-level benefit.
- Do not present Haidt's thesis as settled fact, or resolve the social-media debate.
  Present it as genuinely unsettled, and as an example of the difficulty of causal
  inference after mass exposure.
- Do not imply lead, tobacco, and AI are equivalent in mechanism or certainty of
  harm. They are used only as precedents for *delayed detection* after mass
  exposure.
- Do not imply industry intentionally suppressed baselines. Deployment speed
  outpaced standard epidemiological funding and study timelines.
- Do not treat all chatbot use as therapy or companionship; most measured use is
  practical or informational.
- Do not imply that monitoring requires reading everyone's private conversations.
  Cohorts, consented data donation, aggregate reporting, and natural experiments
  can preserve privacy better than indiscriminate logging.

## Flags

- No single primary source gives global daily active users across all conversational
  AI products. The verified anchor is ChatGPT's ~700 million weekly active users and
  ~18 billion messages per week (NBER, mid-2025). Post-2025 analyst estimates of
  ~800 million weekly / ~1 billion monthly come from trade aggregators, not OpenAI,
  and are treated as approximate and left out of the draft.
- "Largest psychology experiment in history" is rhetorical framing, not a literal
  ranking.
- The claim that no independent, pre-registered population-monitoring system exists
  is a negative institutional claim inferred from the absence of a public program;
  it is hard to verify exhaustively.
- Platform-internal usage studies may use proprietary classification and samples
  that independent researchers cannot fully audit; they are informative but not a
  substitute for independent population inference.

## Dropped as unverifiable

- Gemini's headline "200 million weekly active users by late August 2024" is
  retained as a growth data point, but superseded as the current figure by the
  better-sourced ~700 million (mid-2025).
- Post-2025 ~800M weekly / ~1B monthly figures (from Opus's evidence) are dropped
  from the draft as secondary analyst estimates, and noted only as approximate.
- No candidate contained an outright fabricated citation; each verifiable claim
  carried a locatable source.

# Draft

Conversational AI reached population scale before anyone built the system needed to
understand its psychological effects. ChatGPT launched at the end of November 2022.
It crossed 200 million weekly active users by August 2024, and by the middle of
2025 it was handling around 700 million weekly users and roughly 18 billion
messages a week, about 2.5 billion a day. Most of those messages were not work.
More than seventy percent were ordinary life: asking for advice, drafting a
message, working through a decision, checking a fact, explaining a worry. In under
three years, talking to a computer went from something almost no one did to
something a large share of everyone online now does every week. That is an exposure,
and it was deployed with no baseline measurement, no control group, and no monitoring
system built to detect what it does to people at scale.

Calling it an intervention is meant literally. A system that a person consults many
times a week sits in the path of several processes that psychology has studied for
a century. It shapes mood, because people bring distress to it and receive replies
that soothe, escalate, or reframe. It shapes belief, because it
answers factual and moral questions with fluent confidence and people update on what
it says. It shapes attention, because it offers an immediate place to send any
half-formed thought. And it shapes social life, because time in conversation with a
model is time not spent with a person, or a rehearsal that changes how the
conversation with a person then goes. These channels are not hypothetical fringe
uses. Anthropic found that 2.9 percent of conversations on its assistant were
affective — support, advice, coaching, companionship — which is a small fraction of
a very large number. The tools now sit inside the emotional and cognitive routines
of a substantial part of the population, and their effects are being tracked mainly
through product analytics, small experiments, and scattered reports.

A public-health researcher would recognize this as the kind of exposure that needs
a specific method to study. When something new reaches this many people this
quickly, epidemiology has a standard way of finding out what it does. You measure
the population before the exposure arrives, so you have a baseline. You compare
people who are exposed with similar people who are not, so you have a control group.
You decide in advance which outcomes to track, and you watch them on a fixed
schedule over time, so that a slow or diffuse change cannot hide inside the noise.
None of this was done for conversational AI. There was no pre-exposure measurement
of the things it might plausibly move — how people reason, how much they trust their
own judgment, how they handle boredom or loneliness, how children learn to write.
There is no comparable group held back as a control, because the tool spread to
everyone at once. And there is no agreed set of population-level outcomes that
anyone is tracking on purpose to see whether they shift.

Missing controls are not a bookkeeping problem that can be patched later. They
change what is knowable at all. Suppose a survey a few years from now finds that
heavy users of conversational AI report more social isolation. That finding, on its
own, cannot say which way the arrow runs. People might become isolated because they
spend their evenings talking to software. Isolated people might be the ones who
reach for software to talk to. A third factor, such as a shift to remote work, might
drive both. Without measurements that follow the same individuals from before the
software existed, observational data cannot separate these possibilities. And if the
change is gradual and everyone is exposed, there is nothing to compare it against.
A shift in how teenagers write, or how often adults sit with a hard feeling instead
of typing it into a chat window, will appear alongside a dozen other things
happening in the same years — a pandemic's aftermath, economic strain, other
technologies. The honest description is that we have arranged things so that we
could not tell.

The problem gets worse the longer adoption runs. In the first months after a tool
appears, the people who have it and the people who do not still resemble each other,
and anyone who captured the population at that moment would have had a usable
comparison. As adoption approaches everyone, the unexposed group shrinks and stops
being comparable. The people who still avoid conversational AI after years of
ubiquity differ systematically from those who adopted it, in age, occupation, and
temperament, which is exactly the kind of self-selection that ruins a retrospective
comparison. The window in which a clean measurement is cheap opens early and closes
quietly. For conversational AI, much of it has already passed.

Running an exposure for years before measuring it is not new, and its history is not
reassuring. Tetraethyl lead was added to gasoline starting in the 1920s because it
stopped engines from knocking, and it burned in engines for the rest of the century.
The neurological danger of lead was known in other contexts, but the population-level
consequence of dispersing it through exhaust was harder to establish, because there
was no unexposed population to serve as a baseline for normal development. Clair
Patterson showed in 1965 that lead in the atmosphere and in human bodies was far
above any natural level. Herbert Needleman published evidence in 1979 that children
with more lead in their teeth had lower test scores and worse classroom performance;
he got there by sorting children by the lead in their baby teeth, building the
internal comparison group the exposure itself had erased. The United States did not
finish removing lead from gasoline until 1996, more than seventy years after the
exposure began. A 2022 study estimated that about half the US population alive in
2015 had been exposed to harmful lead levels as young children, at a cost of roughly
824 million IQ points. The harm was real, large, and measurable, once someone
finally measured it, decades in.

Tobacco followed a similar shape. Doll and Hill published a strong statistical link
between smoking and lung cancer in 1950, yet the US Surgeon General's report
accepting that smoking causes lung cancer did not appear until 1964, and regulation
took longer still. Part of the delay was industry pressure to keep the question
open. Part of it was structural: the exposure was already near-universal, the harm
was slow and statistical rather than immediate, and the studies needed to see it
clearly had to be reconstructed after the fact, from incomplete records, against
arguments that some hidden third factor explained the correlation.

The closest live example is the argument over social media and adolescent mental
health, and it matters precisely because it remains unsettled. Jonathan Haidt argues
that the spread of smartphones and image-based platforms after about 2012 is a major
cause of rising anxiety and depression in teenagers. Candice Odgers and others argue
that the correlational evidence is weak, the effect sizes small, and reverse
causality unruled-out. The National Academies reviewed the field in 2024 and reached
a careful position: some features can harm some young people, some uses may help, and
the evidence does not support a single broad causal story. That conclusion is honest,
and it is also an indictment of the measurement environment. More than a decade after
near-universal adoption, and with many researchers actively looking, society is still
arguing about causal effects from data that was never built to settle them.

Conversational AI is likely to repeat that pattern under harder conditions. Social
media at least left public traces: posts, likes, follows, screen time, ad
categories. Chatbot use is more private and more semantically intimate. The relevant
dose runs deeper than minutes. It includes the topic, the emotional state the user
brought, how much the model agreed or pushed back, whether it remembered past
conversations, the user's age, and the role the tool plays in that person's life. Two people can each
spend twenty minutes a night with a chatbot and receive completely different
psychological inputs. The early studies that do exist are useful and cannot carry the
weight. The largest controlled one, run by the MIT Media Lab with OpenAI, followed
just under a thousand people for four weeks and found that the heaviest users
reported more dependence, the kind of dose-response signal that would matter at
scale. Set a four-week study of a thousand people against an exposure of hundreds of
millions over years, and the mismatch is the whole point.

The tools to do better are standard, and none of them require slowing the technology
down. Cohort studies could enroll people now and follow their mood, reasoning, sleep,
loneliness, work, and relationships over years, comparing heavier users with matched
lighter ones. Natural experiments already exist and are being wasted: access to
particular features varies by country, subscription tier, age restriction, workplace
policy, and the timing of rollouts, and each difference is a chance to compare
otherwise similar groups. Staggered rollouts, common elsewhere as stepped-wedge
designs, could deliberately vary who gets a feature when, turning a launch into an
evaluable trial. Pre-registered monitoring could name a small, boring, stable set of
outcomes in advance — distress, loneliness, sleep, school and work functioning,
crisis-service use, dependency-like use — and track them on a fixed schedule, so a
real signal would not have to be reconstructed later from arguments. Privacy is the
obvious objection, and it is serious; a society that answered chatbot risk by
normalizing inspection of private conversations would create a different harm. The
answer is data minimization, consented cohorts, independent governance, and designs
that exploit rollout timing rather than conversation content. The point is to study
patterns across groups, not to read individual lives.

The absence of this work creates an asymmetry in how the technology is managed. Its
benefits are visible and measured in economic terms; companies track engagement,
task completion, and productivity with precision. Its psychological effects go
unquantified. A system optimized for the metric it can see, and blind to the one it
cannot, will drift toward whatever maximizes engagement, whether or not that serves
the people using it. And the silence gets misread as safety. Because hospitals are
not reporting a surge of acute AI emergencies, observers assume the systems are
harmless — but acute emergencies are the wrong instrument for this kind of exposure.
Lead and tobacco showed up as slow shifts in population averages that took decades to
separate from background noise, not as a wave of sudden collapses.

The caveat is real and runs both ways. The absence of measurement is not evidence of
harm. Conversational AI may, on balance, be good for how people think and feel: it
may lower the barrier to working through a problem, give isolated people a patient
interlocutor, and help more than it costs. The trouble is that we are as unequipped
to detect that benefit as to detect a harm. A genuine improvement in how a hundred
million people reason or cope would be just as invisible, for the same reasons, as a
genuine decline. We have settled into an arrangement where the effects of a global
exposure cannot be measured, and we reached it by default, without anyone deciding
that this is how it should be.

That is the part worth taking seriously. The exposure is already global. The tools
to study it are ordinary and available. What is missing is the decision to use them
while there is still a population that remembers what came before, and before
universal adoption erases the difference between the exposed and everyone else. Lead
and tobacco were measured in the end because someone built the studies, absorbed the
resistance, and waited years for the data. We can run those studies at the start of
an exposure instead of the end of one. Whether we do is not a technical question. It
is a choice about whether we would rather know.
