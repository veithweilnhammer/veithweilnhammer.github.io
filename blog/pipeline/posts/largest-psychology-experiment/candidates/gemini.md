# Draft

By late August 2024, conversational artificial intelligence systems were processing interactions from more than two hundred million active users every week. These systems function as a population-scale intervention on human psychology. They change how people access information, manage attention, and interact socially. In a clinical trial for a new psychological intervention, researchers establish a baseline before the exposure and compare the outcomes against a control group that receives a placebo. For conversational AI, the technology industry deployed the intervention globally in less than two years, without capturing a pre-exposure baseline and without maintaining an unexposed control group. The result is the largest psychological experiment in history, running without the epidemiological tools required to measure its effects.

Conversational AI functions as a social agent. Where a search engine returns links in response to queries, a large language model generates fluent, context-aware dialogue that mimics human conversational turn-taking. This design triggers deep-seated human social heuristics. Users naturally attribute intentionality, empathy, and personality to the software, which changes the nature of the interaction from information retrieval to a social exchange. Millions of people now rely on these systems to process difficult emotions, draft sensitive interpersonal communications, and seek daily companionship. This constitutes a massive shift in how humans regulate their emotions and interact with their environments.

Measuring the impact of a massive shift in behavior requires precise chronological data. Epidemiology relies on prospective cohort studies to establish causality. Researchers recruit a large, representative sample of individuals and measure their psychological and physical health before they encounter the intervention. This pre-exposure measurement provides the baseline. When the population eventually encounters the intervention, the researchers can compare the new data against the baseline to isolate changes in mood and social behavior. The technology industry deployed conversational AI at a speed that vastly outpaced academic funding cycles and study design, closing the window to capture a clean baseline before the exposure became universal.

The absence of a baseline is compounded by the lack of a control group. To detect whether an exposure genuinely causes a change in human behavior, researchers must compare the exposed population against people who remained unexposed. The unexposed group controls for background cultural trends, economic shifts, and historical events that might otherwise explain the changes in the data. If anxiety levels rise across the entire population over a five-year period, the control group allows researchers to determine whether the intervention caused the increase or if a separate variable drove the trend.

When an intervention rolls out to hundreds of millions of people simultaneously, the unexposed control group disappears. The technology companies provided the tools to anyone with an internet connection simultaneously, prioritizing rapid user acquisition over measured deployment. This decision maximized market share while removing the opportunity to conduct natural experiments based on geography or access. Without a dedicated control group, researchers cannot easily separate the psychological effects of conversational AI from the general noise of global cultural and economic shifts.

When researchers attempt to measure effects after the fact, they face the statistical problem of confounding variables. If a retrospective survey finds that heavy users of conversational AI report higher rates of social isolation, the researchers cannot determine the direction of causality. People might become isolated because they spend their time talking to software. Alternatively, socially isolated people might be more likely to seek out software for conversation. A third factor, such as a shift toward remote work, might drive both the isolation and the software use. Without a longitudinal baseline that tracks individuals from a time before the software existed, observational data cannot distinguish between these possibilities.

The lack of a control group is particularly damaging when the effects of an intervention are slow and diffuse. Acute harms reveal themselves quickly even without a formal control group. If a chemical exposure causes immediate respiratory failure, the cause and effect are obvious. Psychological effects usually accumulate gradually. Changes in baseline attention span and shifts in social trust manifest as subtle shifts in population averages over years. When everyone is exposed at once, these gradual shifts look like general cultural evolution rather than the specific results of a technological intervention.

The history of public health shows exactly what happens when a population-scale exposure lacks a control group. In the 1920s, the automotive industry added tetraethyllead to gasoline to reduce engine knocking. For the next fifty years, lead emissions spread through the atmosphere globally. Because the exposure was universal, researchers struggled to prove that it was causing harm. There was no unexposed population available to serve as a baseline for normal neurological development. The diffuse psychological effects, which included lowered impulse control and decreased cognitive performance in children, were easily attributed to poverty, urban environments, or poor schooling. The universal nature of the exposure masked the specific harm it caused.

The link between low-level lead exposure and psychological deficits was finally established when researchers found a proxy for a control group. In 1979, Herbert Needleman and his colleagues collected baby teeth from children in Massachusetts to measure their cumulative lead exposure. By stratifying the children based on the lead levels in their dentine, Needleman created an internal comparison group. The children with the lowest lead levels served as the control for those with the highest levels. The study demonstrated a clear correlation between lead exposure and deficits in classroom performance. The fifty-year delay in regulating leaded gasoline was a direct result of deploying the chemical before establishing a system to measure its effects.

A similar dynamic delayed the recognition of the health effects of tobacco. Mass production of cigarettes in the early twentieth century led to widespread adoption before researchers established systems to monitor long-term health outcomes. The delay between exposure and the onset of lung cancer spans decades. By the time the incidence of lung cancer spiked, the majority of the adult population smoked, and researchers had to reconstruct the causal link retrospectively.

The absence of clean baseline data allowed the tobacco industry to argue that the correlation was spurious or driven by confounding factors for decades. Industry statisticians argued that a genetic predisposition might cause both the desire to smoke and the susceptibility to lung cancer. Because the exposure had saturated the population without a controlled rollout, public health officials spent years conducting painstaking retrospective cohort studies to rule out these alternative explanations. The Surgeon General's report linking smoking to health outcomes required decades of epidemiological reconstruction that would have been unnecessary with a proper prospective study.

The current debate over adolescent mental health and social media provides a direct parallel to the rollout of conversational AI. Smartphone adoption and social media use became ubiquitous among teenagers in the early 2010s. In the years that followed, researchers recorded a sharp increase in reports of adolescent anxiety and depression. Because the technology companies rolled out these platforms universally and rapidly, psychologists had no pre-registered cohort studies in place to track the psychological baseline of teenagers before they acquired smartphones.

The result is a highly contested scientific literature based almost entirely on observational data. Researchers like Jonathan Haidt argue that the rapid transition to a phone-based childhood directly caused the mental health crisis. Critics, including psychologist Candice Odgers, argue that the observational data is too weak to support strong causal claims. Odgers points out that the correlation between social media use and depression is small, and that the data cannot rule out reverse causality. The debate remains unresolved precisely because the intervention occurred without a control group. Researchers are attempting to extract causal certainty from data that was never structured to provide it.

Conversational AI presents an even more complex measurement challenge than social media. The interaction is adaptive and highly personalized. A language model modifies its responses based on the user's input, meaning the exposure varies significantly from person to person. A user asking for coding assistance receives a different psychological intervention than a user asking for relationship advice. This variability makes it exceptionally difficult to standardize the exposure in retrospective surveys. Researchers cannot simply measure screen time; they must account for the semantic content and emotional depth of the interactions.

The speed of AI adoption has also outpaced previous technological shifts. While smartphone penetration took nearly a decade to reach global saturation, conversational AI reached hundreds of millions of users in a matter of months. This accelerated timeline means the critical window for capturing a clean baseline closed almost immediately after the public release of the models. Public health institutions and academic psychology departments operate on grant cycles that require years to fund and execute a prospective cohort study. The technology industry's deployment strategy systematically bypassed these institutional safeguards.

The tools required to measure the effects of such an intervention exist in epidemiology and public health, but they are not being applied. A rigorous approach to deploying a population-scale intervention requires cohort studies. Researchers would track a large, representative sample of individuals over time, taking detailed psychological and behavioral measurements before they ever interact with the AI systems. As individuals adopt the technology, the researchers would continue to measure them, comparing their trajectories against those who remain unexposed. This approach isolates the specific effects of the technology from background cultural trends.

When true randomized controlled trials are impossible, researchers rely on staggered rollouts. If a technology company rolls out a feature regionally over several years, the delayed regions serve as a temporary control group for the early adopters. This allows researchers to isolate the effect of the technology from background cultural trends. For conversational AI, the deployment was effectively instantaneous and global. The companies provided the tools to anyone with an internet connection simultaneously. This decision maximized market share while removing the opportunity to conduct natural experiments based on geography or access.

Even without a clean control group, researchers could establish pre-registered population monitoring. This involves defining specific psychological metrics, such as rates of social isolation and attention fragmentation, and setting thresholds for what constitutes a significant change. By registering these metrics and thresholds before the intervention scales, researchers commit to a standard of evidence. Currently, the metrics used to evaluate the impact of conversational AI are generated after the fact, often relying on self-reported surveys that are highly vulnerable to bias and shifting cultural definitions of mental health.

The lack of measurement creates an asymmetry in how society manages the technology. The benefits of conversational AI are highly visible and immediately measurable in economic terms. Companies track user engagement, task completion times, and productivity gains with high precision. The psychological effects remain unmeasured. When a system optimizes for immediate engagement while leaving long-term psychological effects unquantified, the resulting technology will naturally drift toward behaviors that maximize the former at the expense of the latter.

The absence of evidence regarding population-level effects is frequently misinterpreted as evidence of safety. Because hospitals are not reporting a sudden influx of patients suffering from acute AI-induced emergencies, observers assume the systems are harmless. This assumption ignores the history of population-scale exposures. The effects of leaded gasoline and tobacco appeared as broad, slow shifts in population averages that took decades to isolate from background noise. Acute, localized emergencies are the wrong metric for this kind of exposure. The harms, if they exist, will manifest as statistical shifts in emotional regulation and social cohesion across entire generations.

We are currently running a global experiment on human psychology. The intervention is active and the exposure is massive. The mechanisms of interaction have no historical precedent. Because we failed to establish a baseline, we cannot know what the population looked like before the exposure began. Because we failed to maintain a control group, we have no way to isolate the effects of the intervention from the general noise of cultural change. The absence of measurement provides no information about harm or benefit. It guarantees only that we will remain blind to the results of our own experiment. When the effects eventually become large enough to be undeniable, we will have lost the data required to understand how they began.
