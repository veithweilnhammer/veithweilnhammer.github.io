# Evidence

Thesis: Mass adoption of conversational AI is a population-scale psychological exposure that reached hundreds of millions of users before society created the baselines, control groups, and monitoring systems needed to detect its population-level effects.

1. ChatGPT alone reached population scale within a few years: by July 2025 it had about 700 million weekly active users and about 18 billion messages per week, roughly 2.5 billion messages per day. Source: Chatterji et al. (2025), “How People Use ChatGPT,” NBER Working Paper 34255. Link: https://www.nber.org/papers/w34255
2. ChatGPT use is largely embedded in ordinary life rather than confined to work or programming: the same NBER study reports that non-work messages rose above 70% of use, with practical guidance, seeking information, and writing as the largest categories. Source: Chatterji et al. (2025), “How People Use ChatGPT.” Link: https://www.nber.org/papers/w34255
3. Affective use exists at large scale even when it is a minority of use: Anthropic found 2.9% of Claude.ai conversations were affective and analyzed 131,484 affective conversations from an initial sample of about 4.5 million conversations. Source: Anthropic (2025), “How people use Claude for support, advice, and companionship.” Link: https://www.anthropic.com/research/how-people-use-claude-for-support-advice-and-companionship
4. Short experimental studies of chatbot affective use are beginning, but they do not amount to population monitoring: an MIT Media Lab/OpenAI study analyzed more than 4 million conversations, surveyed more than 4,000 users, and ran a 28-day RCT with close to 1,000 participants; very high usage correlated with self-reported dependence, while effects varied by user state and usage duration. Source: Phang et al. (2025), “Investigating Affective Use and Emotional Well-being on ChatGPT.” Link: https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2504.03888
5. Leaded gasoline illustrates delayed recognition of population harm: tetraethyl lead was introduced in the 1920s, Patterson documented widespread environmental contamination in 1965, and Needleman et al. linked children’s elevated dentine lead to psychological and classroom deficits in 1979. Sources: Patterson (1965), “Contaminated and Natural Lead Environments of Man,” DOI: https://doi.org/10.1080/00039896.1965.10664271; Needleman et al. (1979), “Deficits in psychologic and classroom performance of children with elevated dentine lead levels,” DOI: https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJM197903293001301
6. Tobacco illustrates the same lag between widespread exposure and accepted evidence: Doll and Hill’s 1950 case-control study linked smoking and lung cancer, and the U.S. Surgeon General’s 1964 report made the causal conclusion an official public-health position. Sources: Doll & Hill (1950), “Smoking and Carcinoma of the Lung: Preliminary Report,” DOI: https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.2.4682.739; U.S. Surgeon General (1964), “Smoking and Health.” Link: https://collections.nlm.nih.gov/catalog/nlm:nlmuid-101584932X814-img
7. The social-media-and-teen-mental-health debate shows how hard causal inference becomes after a technology is already ubiquitous: the National Academies concluded that some features can harm some young people while evidence does not support a single broad causal claim, and called for stronger causal research. Source: National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine (2024), “Social Media and Adolescent Health.” DOI: https://doi.org/10.17226/27396; NCBI Bookshelf preface: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK603426/
8. Odgers’s review of Haidt’s argument is a concise example of the live dispute: she argues that strong population-level claims about social media and teenage mental illness exceed the causal evidence currently available. Source: Odgers (2024), “The great rewiring: is social media really behind an epidemic of teenage mental illness?” Nature. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1038/d41586-024-00902-2
9. The relevant epidemiological toolkit is available: causal inference relies on comparison groups, longitudinal follow-up, confounding control, and designs such as natural experiments or target trials when randomization is impossible. Sources: Hernán & Robins (2020), “Causal Inference: What If.” Link: https://www.hsph.harvard.edu/miguel-hernan/causal-inference-book/; Hill (1965), “The Environment and Disease: Association or Causation?” Link: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC1898525/

Exact quotes considered:

- None used in the draft. Modern sources are paraphrased rather than quoted; public-domain quotes were unnecessary for the argument.

Do not overclaim:

- Do not claim conversational AI has already been shown to cause population-level psychological harm.
- Do not claim the lead, tobacco, or social-media cases are mechanistically identical to AI; they are examples of delayed detection after mass exposure.
- Do not treat all chatbot use as therapy or companionship. Most measured use is practical or informational.
- Do not imply that industry usage studies are useless. They are informative, but they are short, platform-bound, and insufficient for independent population inference.
- Do not imply that surveillance requires reading everyone’s private conversations. Cohorts, consented data donation, aggregate monitoring, and natural experiments can preserve privacy better than indiscriminate logging.

Flags:

- I could verify weekly active users and daily message volume for ChatGPT from the NBER working paper, but I did not find a primary source giving global daily active users across all conversational AI products. The draft therefore uses “hundreds of millions of weekly users” and “billions of daily messages” rather than a precise daily-user count.
- The claim that there is no global baseline or monitoring system is a negative institutional claim inferred from the absence of a public, pre-registered, independent monitoring program; it is difficult to verify exhaustively.
- Platform-internal studies may use proprietary classification methods and samples that independent researchers cannot fully audit.

# Draft

Conversational AI reached population scale before anyone built the measurement system needed to understand its psychological effects. By July 2025, one product, ChatGPT, had about 700 million weekly active users and handled roughly 18 billion messages per week. Most of those messages were no longer work messages. They were requests for information, writing help, practical guidance, decisions, explanations, and advice woven into ordinary life.

That scale changes the category of the problem. A chatbot conversation can alter what a person attends to, which explanation they accept, how they describe a conflict, whether they seek help, how long they stay with a task, or how reassured they feel after a worrying thought. Many conversations will be trivial. Many will be useful. Some may reduce distress, improve access to information, or help people make sense of a difficult choice. The important fact is that these systems now sit inside the psychological routines of a large share of the population, while their effects are being measured mainly through product analytics, small experiments, user surveys, and scattered case reports.

A public-health researcher would recognize this as an exposure. The exposure is irregular, because people use chatbots for different purposes and with different intensity. It is personalized, because the content changes with the user. It is also dynamic, because the models, interfaces, memory systems, safety rules, and voice modes change over time. Those features make conversational AI harder to study than a pill, a pollutant, or a tobacco product, but they do not remove the need for baseline measurement. They make baseline measurement more important.

The missing baseline matters because population effects are often slow, diffuse, and easy to misattribute. If anxiety, loneliness, attention, help-seeking, political belief, or trust in institutions shifts over the next five years, conversational AI will be only one candidate explanation among many. Economic stress, war, climate events, school disruption, social media, sleep, work patterns, and ordinary diagnostic drift will all be plausible causes. Once exposure becomes nearly universal, the clean comparison between exposed and unexposed people starts to disappear. Researchers can still compare heavier and lighter users, but that comparison is already confounded by why people choose different levels of use in the first place.

This is the basic reason control groups matter. Without a control group, a change after exposure can look meaningful even when it would have happened anyway. Without a baseline, the pre-existing trend is uncertain. Without repeated measurement, short-term relief can be mistaken for durable improvement, and a small accumulating cost can look like background noise. These problems apply equally to benefit and harm. A system that improves access to advice, reduces loneliness for some users, or helps people organize their lives may fail to receive credit if no one measures the right outcomes. A system that increases dependency, reinforces unusual beliefs, or fragments attention may remain defensible for too long if no one can separate its effect from the rest of life.

The history of public health is partly a history of learning this lesson late. Leaded gasoline entered mass use in the 1920s because it improved engine performance. The neurological danger of lead was already known in other contexts, but the population-level consequences of dispersing lead through exhaust were harder to establish. Clair Patterson’s 1965 work showed that industrial lead had contaminated the human environment far beyond natural levels. Herbert Needleman’s 1979 study then linked elevated lead in children’s teeth to lower psychological and classroom performance, helping shift attention from dramatic poisoning to lower-level developmental harm. By the time the evidence became politically effective, generations of children had already been exposed.

Tobacco followed a different mechanism but a similar evidential pattern. Cigarette smoking became widespread long before the causal link with lung cancer became an official public-health fact. Doll and Hill’s 1950 study was one of the key epidemiological steps, and the 1964 U.S. Surgeon General’s report consolidated the conclusion for policy. Several features produced the delay: lung cancer has long latency, smoking behavior is socially patterned, and commercial interests contested the evidence. The link became hard to deny only after investigators compared smokers and non-smokers, measured dose-response patterns, followed cohorts, and built a converging case from different designs.

The current debate about social media and adolescent mental health is a closer warning because it concerns behavior, development, and everyday technology rather than a chemical exposure. Jonathan Haidt has argued that smartphone and social-media adoption played a major role in the rise of adolescent mental-health problems. Critics such as Candice Odgers argue that the strongest versions of that claim exceed the causal evidence. The National Academies’ 2024 report took a measured position: some features of social media can harm some young people, some uses can help, and the evidence does not support a single broad causal story. That conclusion is scientifically cautious, but it is also an indictment of the measurement environment. After years of near-universal adoption, society is still arguing about causal effects with incomplete data.

Conversational AI risks repeating that pattern under more difficult conditions. Social media at least left public traces: posts, likes, follows, feeds, screen time, advertising categories, and platform-level metrics. Chatbot use is more private and more semantically intimate. The relevant dose includes minutes of use, topic, emotional state, response style, degree of agreement or challenge, use of memory, age, and the role the chatbot occupies in that person’s life. Two users may both spend twenty minutes with a chatbot each evening while receiving very different psychological inputs.

Early studies are useful, but they cannot carry the whole burden. Anthropic reported that 2.9% of Claude.ai conversations were affective, meaning that users were seeking support, advice, coaching, counseling, companionship, or related forms of personal engagement. An MIT Media Lab and OpenAI study analyzed millions of conversations, surveyed thousands of users, and ran a 28-day randomized trial with close to 1,000 participants. It found a nuanced pattern: very high usage correlated with self-reported dependence, while the effects of voice interaction varied with the user’s initial state and total usage. These studies are exactly the sort of early work the field needs. They are also short, platform-specific, partly internal to the companies deploying the products, and far too small to answer population questions.

The harder work would look more like epidemiology than product research. Large prospective cohorts could measure mental health, sleep, attention, loneliness, social support, belief confidence, help-seeking, and daily functioning before or near the start of chatbot adoption, then follow people over time as their use changes. Participants could donate limited, privacy-preserving summaries of chatbot use: frequency, duration, topic categories, modality, model version, and broad interaction features, without exposing raw private conversations to researchers. The goal would be to study patterns across groups, not to inspect individual lives.

The minimum useful design would measure the same people repeatedly, before their exposure patterns are interpreted.

Natural experiments would also matter. Model features roll out at different times across countries, age groups, subscription tiers, schools, workplaces, and platforms. Voice modes, memory, companion features, safety changes, outages, and regulatory restrictions create staggered exposure patterns. If researchers know about these changes in advance, they can pre-register analyses comparing groups whose access changes at different times. That kind of design will never be as clean as a randomized drug trial, but it can help separate the effect of the technology from the background trend.

Population monitoring should also be pre-registered. Before a new class of highly personal AI features reaches hundreds of millions of people, independent researchers and public agencies could define a small set of outcomes worth tracking: self-reported distress, loneliness, sleep, school functioning, work functioning, crisis-service use, dependency-like use, and perceived social support. The measures should be boring, repeated, and stable. A monitoring system that changes its outcome definition after every news cycle will reproduce the same confusion it was meant to solve.

Privacy is the obvious objection, and it is serious. A society that responds to chatbot risk by normalizing central inspection of private conversations would create a different psychological hazard. The better answer is data minimization, consented cohorts, independent governance, secure research environments, aggregate reporting, and designs that exploit rollout timing rather than content surveillance. Public health often works with sensitive data. It does not follow that all sensitive data should be collected, or that companies should become the only institutions allowed to study the consequences of products they operate.

The honest caveat is that measurement failure tells us nothing about direction. Conversational AI may produce large benefits in some groups. It may help people learn, write, rehearse conversations, manage bureaucracy, translate information, or obtain support when human help is unavailable. It may also create costs that are concentrated in vulnerable users or hidden in small changes to attention, dependency, belief formation, or social behavior. The present evidence cannot support a confident population-level claim in either direction.

That uncertainty is the problem. We are allowing conversational AI to become a routine part of cognition, advice, emotional regulation, and social life while leaving the population question to be reconstructed later from incomplete traces. When a technology changes slowly enough to become ordinary, the absence of measurement is easily mistaken for the absence of an effect. The more responsible position is less dramatic and more demanding: treat conversational AI as a mass psychological exposure, build the cohorts and natural experiments now, and accept that the first duty is to make the effect detectable before arguing about its size.
