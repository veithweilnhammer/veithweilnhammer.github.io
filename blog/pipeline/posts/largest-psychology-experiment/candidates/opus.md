# Evidence

**Thesis (one sentence):** The mass adoption of conversational AI is a
population-scale intervention on human psychology that was deployed with no
baseline measurement, no control group, and no monitoring system designed to
detect population-level effects, so we are unusually badly equipped to notice
either harm or benefit until it is already far advanced.

## Key claims and sources

1. **ChatGPT reached roughly 700 million weekly active users within about three
   years of launch, and adoption kept climbing after that.** OpenAI said in early
   August 2025 that ChatGPT was on track for 700 million weekly active users,
   about four times the prior year. ChatGPT launched on 30 November 2022.
   - TechCrunch, "OpenAI says ChatGPT is on track to reach 700M weekly users"
     (4 Aug 2025). https://techcrunch.com/2025/08/04/openai-says-chatgpt-is-on-track-to-reach-700m-weekly-users/
   - CNBC, "OpenAI's ChatGPT to hit 700 million weekly users, up 4x from last
     year" (4 Aug 2025). https://www.cnbc.com/2025/08/04/openai-chatgpt-700-million-users.html
   - Later trade estimates put weekly users around 800 million by late 2025;
     these are secondary/analyst figures and should be treated as approximate
     (see Flags).

2. **Existing measurement of psychological effects is tiny relative to the
   exposure.** The largest controlled study to date, an MIT Media Lab / OpenAI
   randomized controlled trial, followed just under 1,000 participants for four
   weeks. Its scale (hundreds of people, one month) illustrates how far short
   current measurement falls of a population of hundreds of millions using the
   tool for years.
   - Fang et al., "How AI and Human Behaviors Shape Psychosocial Effects of
     Extended Chatbot Use: A Longitudinal Randomized Controlled Study" (2025),
     arXiv:2503.17473. https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.17473
   - Phang, Fang, et al., "Investigating Affective Use and Emotional Well-being
     on ChatGPT" (OpenAI / MIT Media Lab, 2025).
     https://cdn.openai.com/papers/15987609-5f71-433c-9972-e91131f399a1/openai-affective-use-study.pdf

3. **Leaded gasoline: a population-scale neurotoxic exposure recognized decades
   after it began.** Tetraethyl lead was added to gasoline from the 1920s; Clair
   Patterson showed in the 1960s that atmospheric lead was far above natural
   background; Herbert Needleman's 1979 study linked childhood lead to lower IQ
   and worse classroom performance; the US did not finish banning leaded
   automotive gasoline until 1996.
   - Needleman HL et al., "Deficits in Psychologic and Classroom Performance of
     Children with Elevated Dentine Lead Levels," N Engl J Med 300:689–695
     (1979). doi:10.1056/NEJM197903293001301 (verified via Crossref).
   - Patterson CC, "Contaminated and natural lead environments of man," Arch
     Environ Health 11:344–360 (1965). doi:10.1080/00039896.1965.10664234

4. **A 2022 estimate quantifies the delayed cost: about half the US population
   alive in 2015 had been exposed to harmful childhood lead levels, for an
   estimated 824 million cumulative lost IQ points.** This is the scale of a
   population effect that ran for decades before it was measured and acted on.
   - McFarland MJ, Hauer ME, Reuben A, "Half of US population exposed to adverse
     lead levels in early childhood," PNAS 119(11):e2118631119 (2022).
     doi:10.1073/pnas.2118631119 (verified via Crossref).

5. **Tobacco: a lag of over a decade between clear evidence and official
   acknowledgment, and longer to action.** Doll and Hill published a strong
   statistical link between smoking and lung cancer in 1950; the US Surgeon
   General's report acknowledging causation came in 1964.
   - Doll R, Hill AB, "Smoking and carcinoma of the lung," BMJ 2:739–748 (1950).
     doi:10.1136/bmj.2.4682.739
   - Surgeon General's Advisory Committee, "Smoking and Health" (1964), US Public
     Health Service. https://profiles.nlm.nih.gov/spotlight/nn/catalog/nlm:nlmuid-101584932X202-doc

6. **Social media and adolescent mental health is the live, still-contested
   case of a population-scale exposure whose effects remain disputed even though
   people are actively looking.** Haidt argues for a causal role; Odgers and
   others argue the correlational evidence is weak and effect sizes small. Both
   sides agree adolescent distress rose after the early 2010s.
   - Odgers CL, "The great rewiring: is social media really behind an epidemic of
     teenage mental illness?" Nature 628:29–30 (2024).
     doi:10.1038/d41586-024-00902-2 (verified via Crossref).
   - Haidt J, *The Anxious Generation* (2024) — book; paraphrase only.

7. **Basic epidemiological method requires a baseline, a comparison group, and a
   monitoring plan set up before or during an exposure.** Cohort studies,
   natural experiments, staggered rollouts, and pre-registered outcome tracking
   are the standard tools for detecting diffuse population effects and managing
   confounding. (Textbook epidemiology; no single citation needed.)

## Exact quotes considered

- I am not relying on any direct quotation of modern copyrighted text (Haidt,
  Odgers, the OpenAI/MIT papers) — all such material is paraphrased.
- The 1964 Surgeon General report (US government, public domain) contains the
  conclusion that cigarette smoking is causally related to lung cancer in men.
  I paraphrase rather than quote, because I could not verify the exact wording
  letter-for-letter against a primary scan within this task (see Flags).

## Do not overclaim

- Do not claim conversational AI is harming people at the population level. The
  argument is about the absence of measurement, which cuts both ways: we also
  cannot demonstrate population-level benefit.
- Do not present the 800-million user figure as an official OpenAI number; the
  verified official figure is ~700 million weekly (Aug 2025).
- Do not imply lead, tobacco, and AI are equivalent in mechanism or certainty of
  harm. They are used only as precedents for *delayed detection*, not as proof
  that AI will follow the same path.
- Do not claim no one is studying AI's psychological effects. Small studies
  exist; the point is that nothing at population scale, with a baseline and
  controls, was set up.
- Do not resolve the social-media debate. Present it as genuinely unsettled.

## Flags

- Post-2025 user figures (~800M weekly, ~1B monthly) come from analyst/trade
  aggregators, not OpenAI; treat as approximate. The 700M weekly figure is the
  reliable, sourced one.
- The exact wording of the 1964 Surgeon General conclusion was not verified
  character-for-character here; the draft paraphrases it.
- "Largest psychology experiment in history" is rhetorical framing, not a
  literal ranking; the draft treats it as an analogy, not a measured claim.
- Session-duration and daily-active figures vary widely by source and
  methodology; the draft avoids leaning on them.

# Draft

In early August 2025, OpenAI said that ChatGPT was on track to reach 700 million
weekly active users, roughly four times the number a year earlier. The product
had launched in November 2022. In under three years, a way of talking to a
computer that almost no one had tried became something that a substantial
fraction of everyone online now does every week: asking for advice, drafting
messages, working through decisions, arguing, confiding. Whatever else that is,
it is an exposure. A large share of humanity has changed how it thinks out loud,
seeks reassurance, and processes information, and the change happened faster than
any comparable shift in how people use their minds.

Calling it an intervention is meant literally. A conversational system that a
person consults many times a week sits in the path of several processes that
psychology has studied for a century. It shapes mood, because people bring
distress to it and receive responses that soothe, escalate, or reframe. It shapes
belief, because it answers factual and moral questions with fluent confidence and
people update on what it says. It shapes attention, because it offers an immediate
place to direct any half-formed thought. And it shapes social life, because time
spent in conversation with a model is time not spent in conversation with a
person, or is a rehearsal that changes how the conversation with a person then
goes. Each of these is a plausible channel through which daily use could move a
psychological outcome up or down, and each is measurable in principle. The MIT
Media Lab study found that the small fraction of participants who used the tool
most heavily reported different outcomes from lighter users, which is exactly the
kind of dose-response pattern that would matter at scale. It is also exactly the
kind of pattern that a four-week study of a thousand people cannot pin down across
a population of hundreds of millions using the tool for years.

When a new exposure on this scale reaches this many people this quickly,
epidemiology has a standard way of finding out what it does. You measure the
population before the exposure arrives, so you have a baseline. You compare people
who are exposed with similar people who are not, so you have a control group. You
decide in advance which outcomes to track and you watch them over time, so that a
slow or diffuse change cannot hide inside the noise. None of this was done for
conversational AI. There was no pre-exposure measurement of the things it might
plausibly affect — how people reason, how much they trust their own judgment, how
they handle boredom or loneliness, how children learn to write. There is no group
of comparable people held back as a control, because the tool spread to everyone
at once. And there is no agreed set of population-level outcomes that anyone is
tracking on purpose to see whether they move.

The result is that we have run something close to the largest uncontrolled
intervention on human psychology ever attempted, and we built almost no way to
observe its effects. The best controlled evidence available is a randomized trial
run by the MIT Media Lab with OpenAI, which followed just under a thousand people
for four weeks. That is careful work, and it is the right kind of study. Set it
against the exposure it is trying to understand — hundreds of millions of people,
several years, no upper limit on how central the tool becomes to a given life —
and the mismatch in scale is the whole point. We are measuring a river with a
teaspoon.

Missing controls are not a bookkeeping problem that can be patched later. They
change what is knowable at all. If cognition, mood, or social behavior shifts
gradually across a whole population, and every person is exposed, there is nothing
to compare the shift against. A change in how teenagers write, or how often adults
sit with a hard feeling instead of typing it into a chat window, will show up
alongside a dozen other things happening in the same years — a pandemic's
aftermath, economic strain, other technologies. Without a baseline and a
comparison group established in advance, any effect can be attributed to something
else, and confounding cannot be untangled after the fact. The honest position is
that we have arranged things so that we could not tell.

The problem compounds the longer adoption runs. In the first months after a tool
appears, exposed and unexposed people still resemble each other, and someone who
had captured the population at that moment would have had a usable comparison. As
adoption approaches everyone, the unexposed group shrinks and stops being
comparable — the people who still avoid conversational AI after years of ubiquity
differ systematically from those who adopted it, in age, occupation, and
temperament, which is precisely the kind of self-selection that ruins a
retrospective comparison. The window in which a clean measurement is cheap is
open early and closes quietly. For conversational AI, much of that window has
already passed without anyone using it.

This pattern of running an exposure for years before measuring it is not new, and
its history is not reassuring. Tetraethyl lead was added to gasoline starting in
the 1920s and burned in engines for the rest of the century. The geochemist Clair
Patterson showed in the 1960s that lead in the atmosphere and in human bodies was
far above any natural level. The pediatrician Herbert Needleman published evidence
in 1979 that children with higher lead exposure had lower IQ scores and worse
classroom performance, and his work, along with Patterson's, was resisted by the
lead industry for years. The United States did not finish removing lead from
automotive gasoline until 1996, more than seventy years after the exposure began.
A 2022 study estimated that about half the US population alive in 2015 had been
exposed to harmful lead levels as young children, costing an estimated 824 million
IQ points across the population. The harm was real, large, and measurable — once
someone finally measured it, decades in.

Tobacco followed a similar shape. Doll and Hill published a strong statistical
link between smoking and lung cancer in 1950, yet the US Surgeon General's report
acknowledging that smoking causes lung cancer did not appear until 1964, and
serious regulation took longer still. In both cases the delay had two sources.
Part of it was industry pressure to keep the question open. But part of it was
structural: the exposure was already universal or nearly so, the harm was slow and
statistical rather than immediate and obvious, and the measurement systems needed
to see it clearly had to be built after the fact, against resistance, from
incomplete records.

The closest live example is the argument over social media and adolescent mental
health, and it is instructive precisely because it remains unresolved. Jonathan
Haidt argues that the spread of smartphones and image-based social platforms after
about 2012 is a major cause of rising anxiety and depression in teenagers,
especially girls. Candice Odgers and other researchers argue that the
correlational evidence is weak, the effect sizes are small, and the rise in
distress may owe more to other causes. Both sides agree that adolescent mental
health worsened after the early 2010s. What they cannot settle, more than a decade
later and with many people actively studying it, is whether the exposure caused
the change. That disagreement persists even though social media is far more
visible and better documented than most exposures. If a decade of attention still
leaves the causal question open for social media, the situation for conversational
AI is worse, because for AI almost no one set up the measurements at all.

There are things that could still be done, and most of them are ordinary science.
Cohort studies could enroll people now and follow their reasoning, mood, work, and
relationships over years, distinguishing heavy users from light ones with matched
comparisons. Natural experiments already exist and are being wasted: access to
particular tools varies by country, by workplace policy, by age restriction, and
by the timing of feature rollouts, and each of those differences is a chance to
compare otherwise similar groups. Staggered rollouts, common in other fields as
stepped-wedge designs, could deliberately vary who gets access when, turning a
product launch into an evaluable trial. Pre-registered population monitoring could
name in advance the outcomes worth watching and track them on fixed schedules, so
that a real signal would not have to be reconstructed later from arguments. None
of this requires slowing the technology down. It requires deciding that the
effects are worth knowing and building the instruments to see them while the
exposure is underway.

The caveat matters, and it runs in both directions. The absence of measurement is
not evidence of harm. It is entirely possible that conversational AI is, on
balance, good for how people think and feel — that it lowers the barrier to
working through a problem, gives isolated people a patient interlocutor, and helps
more than it costs. The trouble is that we are as unequipped to detect that
benefit as we are to detect a harm. A genuine improvement in how a hundred million
people reason or cope would be just as invisible, for the same reasons, as a
genuine decline. We have settled into an arrangement in which the effects of a
global exposure cannot be measured, and we reached it by default, without anyone
deciding that this is how it should be.

That is the part worth sitting with. The exposure is already global. The tools to
study it are standard and available. What is missing is the decision to use them
while there is still a population that remembers what came before, and while the
differences between exposed and unexposed groups have not yet been erased by
universal adoption. Lead and tobacco were eventually measured because someone
built the studies, absorbed the resistance, and waited years for the data. We can
run those studies at the start of an exposure instead of the end of one. Whether
we do is not a technical question. It is a choice about whether we would rather
know.
