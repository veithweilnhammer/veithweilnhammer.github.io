# Brief: We Are Running the Largest Psychology Experiment in History With No Control Group

## Slug
largest-psychology-experiment

## Working title
We Are Running the Largest Psychology Experiment in History With No Control Group

## One-line angle (also the Substack subtitle / SEO hook)
Hundreds of millions of people now talk to conversational AI daily, and we took no baseline and built no way to detect what it is doing to us at the population level.

## Single argument (thesis)
Mass adoption of conversational AI is a population-scale intervention on human psychology that was deployed with no baseline measurement, no control group, and no plan to detect population-level effects, so we are unusually unequipped to notice harm — or benefit — until it is far advanced.

## Concrete anchor (optional — a real example to ground the argument)
The history of delayed-harm detection: leaded gasoline, tobacco, and more recently the debate over social media and adolescent mental health — each a case where a population-scale exposure ran for years or decades before its effects were measured and acknowledged.

## What this post must cover
- The exposure is real and huge: conversational AI went from niche to hundreds of millions of daily users in a couple of years, functioning as an intervention on mood, belief, attention, and social life.
- The measurement gap: there was no pre-exposure baseline, no control group, and no monitoring system designed to detect population-level psychological effects.
- Why this makes effects hard to see: without controls, slow or diffuse changes are easily attributed to other causes or missed entirely, and confounding is unmanageable after the fact.
- The historical pattern of delayed detection: leaded gasoline, tobacco, and the still-contested social-media-and-teens debate as cases of long lags between exposure and recognized harm.
- What could be done: cohort studies, natural experiments, staggered rollouts, and pre-registered population monitoring — the epidemiology we are not doing.
- The honest caveat: absence of measurement is not evidence of harm; the point is that we have chosen not to be able to find out either way.
