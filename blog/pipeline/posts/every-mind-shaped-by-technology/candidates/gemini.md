# Evidence

1. **Thesis:** Every human mind has been shaped by the tools of its era, and the recurring panic that a new technology will damage cognition shows a consistent pattern: the effects are real but reliably different from, and usually smaller than, what the alarmists forecast.
2. **The oldest instance:** In Plato’s *Phaedrus*, Socrates recounts the myth of Theuth and Thamus, where Thamus warns that the invention of writing will create forgetfulness and offer only the semblance of wisdom. (Plato, *Phaedrus*, trans. Benjamin Jowett, 274c-275b).
3. **The printing press pattern:** The proliferation of printed books generated widespread anxiety among early modern scholars about information overload, prompting the invention of new reference tools and reading strategies rather than cognitive collapse. (Blair, Ann. 2010. *Too Much to Know: Managing Scholarly Information before the Modern Age*. Yale University Press).
4. **The modern case:** The "Google effect" demonstrates that humans remember where to find information rather than the information itself when they expect it to be externally stored, reflecting an expansion of transactive memory. (Sparrow, B., Liu, J., & Wegner, D. M. 2011. "Google Effects on Memory: Cognitive Consequences of Having Information at Our Fingertips." *Science*, 333(6043), 776-778. DOI: 10.1126/science.1207745).

# Draft

In Plato’s *Phaedrus*, Socrates recounts an Egyptian myth concerning the god Theuth, who presents his invention of writing to King Thamus. The setting is critical: Theuth approaches the king to offer his arts to the Egyptian people, claiming that letters will make them wiser and improve their memories. The king decisively rejects the invention on the grounds that it will degrade human cognition. Thamus warns that writing will "create forgetfulness in the learners' souls, because they will not use their memories; they will trust to the external written characters and not remember of themselves." He argues that the new technology provides an aid to reminiscence rather than true memory, yielding "only the semblance of truth" and leaving students with the appearance of wisdom without the reality. Socrates extends the critique by observing that a written text preserves a solemn silence when questioned, incapable of defending itself or adapting its answers to the specific needs of the reader. The fear that a new technology will permanently damage human cognition and weaken our internal capacities is as old as the recorded word. This exact concern reliably accompanies every major transition in how humans store and process information.

This historical concern reappears today regarding artificial intelligence. Critics suggest that offloading reasoning, drafting, and synthesis to large language models will atrophy our fundamental cognitive capabilities, leaving a generation incapable of deep thought or original analysis. The argument mirrors Thamus’s warning that relying on an external system will erode the internal capacity it replaces. Every human mind has been shaped by the tools of its era, and the recurring panic over new technology shows a consistent pattern. The cognitive effects of a new medium are real, but they are reliably different from, and usually smaller than, what the alarmists forecast. Technologies genuinely reshape cognition, but the changes consistently arrive as reorganizations of how we think and trade-offs in our mental strategies rather than the wholesale intellectual decline that critics predict.

Evaluating these changes requires recognizing that there is no tool-free baseline of human cognition to protect. Human thinking has always been co-constituted with the available tools, starting with spoken language itself. The development of language fundamentally altered how humans categorize the world, store information, and coordinate action. Subsequent technologies, from written script and mathematical notation to the printing press and mechanical calculation, further restructured the cognitive environment. The mind adapts to the environment it operates within, optimizing its resources to exploit the affordances of the tools at hand. When the environment changes to include a new tool, cognitive strategies shift to make use of it. The baseline natural mind that critics often seek to defend is already the product of thousands of years of technological integration. We think with our tools, and our tools dictate which cognitive muscles we exercise.

The transition from oral to written culture demonstrates the reality of these cognitive trade-offs. Ancient scholars and bards maintained vast oral memories because their environment required the internal storage of cultural, historical, and practical knowledge. Homeric poetry, for instance, relied on rhythmic formulas and specific metrical structures specifically because those features made massive epics memorizable without a physical text. Writing made those specific feats of internal memorization unnecessary by moving the storage of information into the external environment. The human brain’s memory practices changed in response to the availability of the written word. The loss of extensive oral recitation capabilities was a real cost, paid in exchange for the capacity to transmit complex, precise information across generations and geographies without degradation. Writing reduced the specific type of memory practice that Thamus valued, but it enabled forms of cumulative knowledge and complex textual analysis that an exclusively oral culture could not support. The technology allowed thinkers to construct arguments that exceeded the capacity of human working memory, fundamentally expanding the scope of intellectual work.

The invention of the printing press provoked an intense round of similar anxiety. In the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, scholars warned that an unmanageable abundance of books would overwhelm the mind and destroy the practice of careful, deep reading. The historian Ann Blair has documented how early modern academics complained bitterly about information overload, using the phrase "multitude of books" to describe a crisis of excessive text. They feared that the sheer volume of available information would lead to superficial engagement and intellectual confusion. The expectation was that the human mind would buckle under the weight of too much information, losing the ability to synthesize or retain coherent bodies of knowledge. Scholars warned that the core intellectual virtue of deep, repetitive reading of foundational texts was being replaced by a shallow, frantic consumption of new material.

Scholars responded to the volume of text by inventing new methods to manage the excess. They developed indices, encyclopedias, bibliographies, and the practice of keeping commonplace books. The commonplace book became a standard tool for educated readers: a personal notebook organized by thematic headings, where readers transcribed specific quotes and facts from the multitude of books they encountered. Reading practices shifted from the intensive study of a few texts to the strategic extraction of information from many texts. Academics learned to skim, to consult reference works, and to organize excerpts under their own headings for future retrieval. The mind developed new strategies to navigate the changed environment. The printing press did change how people read, moving them away from the deep memorization of a few core texts, but it simultaneously created the modern practices of reference reading and cross-textual synthesis.

Recent technological transitions provide direct measurements of these strategic shifts. In 2011, the psychologists Betsy Sparrow, Jenny Liu, and Daniel Wegner tested how search engines affect memory. They conducted a series of experiments investigating the cognitive consequences of having information at our fingertips. In one key experiment, participants typed newly learned trivia statements into a computer. Half the participants were told the computer would save what they typed, and the other half were told the information would be erased. The researchers found that participants who expected to have future access to the information had significantly lower rates of recall for the statements themselves. However, these same participants showed enhanced recall for exactly which folder the information was saved in. This phenomenon, known as the Google effect, demonstrates that humans remember where to find information rather than the information itself when they expect it to be externally stored.

To confirm that search engines were changing baseline cognitive priorities, Sparrow and colleagues also used a modified Stroop task to measure reaction times to computer-related words after participants were asked difficult trivia questions. The participants showed slower reaction times to words related to search engines after encountering hard questions, indicating that the concepts of search and external retrieval were highly activated in their minds when faced with an unknown fact. The results demonstrate a real, measurable cognitive change, representing a strategic reallocation of effort. The brain prioritizes the location of the data over the content when the environment guarantees the content remains accessible.

This behavior aligns tightly with the theory of transactive memory, which Daniel Wegner originally proposed in the 1980s to describe how humans divide memory tasks within groups. People naturally offload the storage of specific facts to their partners or colleagues based on expertise. In a long-term relationship, one partner might remember the details of social engagements while the other remembers the mechanics of household finances. The internet functions as an expansive, highly accessible digital transactive memory partner. The Google effect shows human cognition doing what it has always done: treating the external environment as a collaborative partner in memory storage.

We can apply this historical and experimental pattern to artificial intelligence. Large language models represent another shift in the cognitive environment, moving beyond the external storage of facts to the external generation of synthesis and pattern recognition. When an individual asks a model to draft a document, summarize a dataset, or generate a baseline argument, they are treating the system as a cognitive partner. People will reliably offload certain types of routine drafting, summarization, and preliminary analysis to these systems. Expecting real cognitive change is reasonable, given the historical record of human adaptation to new tools. The capacities that individuals practice daily will shift, just as the capacity for oral recitation shifted with the advent of writing and the capacity for internal fact retention shifted with the advent of search engines.

Both extreme positions on artificial intelligence fail to capture the historical dynamic. Assuming that artificial intelligence will destroy our capacity for thought overlooks the long history of successful cognitive adaptation to external tools. Assuming that the technology will leave our fundamental thinking unchanged overlooks the reality that offloading cognitive tasks reliably alters our internal practices. Evaluating the impact requires identifying which specific capacities we are offloading and what new skills are replacing them. If search engines prioritized the skill of locating information over retaining it, artificial intelligence will likely prioritize the skill of verifying and directing synthesis over generating the initial draft. The cognitive effort shifts from the blank page to the evaluation of the generated output.

Technology consistently exacts a cost in exchange for a new capacity. Recognizing that trade-off allows us to ask exactly what we are giving up and what we are gaining. Writing diminished oral memory but made civilization possible. Print diminished intensive, repetitive reading but created the infrastructure of modern scholarship. Search engines diminished internal fact retention but provided immediate access to global information. The human mind will reorganize itself around artificial intelligence, just as it reorganized itself around the written word, the printed book, and the search engine.
