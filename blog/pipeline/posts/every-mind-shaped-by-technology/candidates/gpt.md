# Evidence

Thesis: Every human mind is shaped by the cognitive tools around it, and the long history of panic about new media shows a repeated pattern: the changes are real, specific, and often less like decline than a reallocation of memory, attention, and judgment.

1. Plato records an early version of technological anxiety about cognition: in *Phaedrus*, Socrates recounts Thamus warning that writing will weaken memory and produce the appearance of wisdom. Source: Plato, c. 370 BCE, *Phaedrus* 274c-275b, trans. Benjamin Jowett. Link: https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedrus.html
2. The idea that cognition can extend into tools has a modern philosophical formulation in the extended-mind thesis, where external artifacts can play functional roles in thought. Source: Andy Clark and David Chalmers, 1998, “The Extended Mind.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1093/analys/58.1.7
3. Cognitive offloading is an established psychological concept: people use physical actions and external devices to reduce the information-processing demands of tasks. Source: Evan F. Risko and Sam J. Gilbert, 2016, “Cognitive Offloading.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tics.2016.07.002
4. The printing press intensified early-modern concerns about excess information and produced reading, excerpting, indexing, and note-taking strategies to manage overload. Source: Ann Blair, 2003, “Reading Strategies for Coping with Information Overload ca. 1550-1700.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1353/jhi.2003.0003
5. Blair’s book-length study argues that information overload and information-management tools long predate digital media. Source: Ann Blair, 2010, *Too Much to Know: Managing Scholarly Information before the Modern Age*. Link: https://yalebooks.yale.edu/book/9780300165395/too-much-to-know/
6. Transactive memory describes systems in which people remember both information and who or what can retrieve it. Source: Daniel M. Wegner, 1985, “A Computer Network Model of Human Transactive Memory.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1521/soco.1984.2.2.140
7. The “Google effect” study found that people expecting future access to information showed lower recall for the information itself and better recall for where to access it. Source: Betsy Sparrow, Jenny Liu, and Daniel M. Wegner, 2011, “Google Effects on Memory: Cognitive Consequences of Having Information at Our Fingertips.” Link: https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1207745 and PubMed abstract https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21764755/
8. A recent meta-analytic review reports that Google-effect findings vary with cognitive load, device, prior internet use, knowledge base, and region, which supports a moderated effect rather than a single uniform cognitive decline story. Source: Chen Gong and Yang Yang, 2024, “Google Effects on Memory: A Meta-Analytical Review of the Media Effects of Intensive Internet Search Behavior.” Link: https://doi.org/10.3389/fpubh.2024.1332030
9. Medieval and early-modern memory cultures show that writing coexisted with elaborate trained memory practices, so literacy changed the use of memory rather than simply eliminating it. Source: Mary Carruthers, 2008, *The Book of Memory: A Study of Memory in Medieval Culture*, 2nd ed. Link: https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9781107051126

## Exact quotes that may be used

- Plato, *Phaedrus*, trans. Jowett: “for this discovery of yours will create forgetfulness in the learners' souls, because they will not use their memories; they will trust to the external written characters and not remember of themselves.” Verified at https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedrus.html
- Plato, *Phaedrus*, trans. Jowett: “you give your disciples not truth, but only the semblance of truth; they will be hearers of many things and will have learned nothing; they will appear to be omniscient and will generally know nothing.” Verified at https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedrus.html

## Do not overclaim

- Do not present Plato’s passage as a prediction about all literacy; it is a dialogue passage, a myth within an argument, and it also treats writing as useful for reminders.
- Do not say writing destroyed memory. Literate cultures developed new memory systems, reference practices, and scholarly techniques.
- Do not say the printing press caused shallow reading in a simple way. It increased volume and created management problems, while also enabling new forms of scholarship.
- Do not treat the Google effect as proof of general memory decline. The finding concerns expectations of future access and recall strategy.
- Do not infer that AI will be harmless because earlier panics were overstated. Earlier technologies caused real cognitive and institutional changes.
- Do not infer that every cognitive offload is beneficial. Offloading frees capacity in some contexts and weakens practice in others.
- Do not use the extended-mind thesis as settled proof that tools literally are parts of the mind; use it as a useful way to describe dependence on external cognitive resources.

- I verified Plato’s wording against the MIT Classics public-domain Jowett text. Other translations differ, so quote wording should stay tied to that translation.

# Draft

The worry that artificial intelligence will weaken human thought has a very old structure. A new tool arrives, it makes some mental act easier to outsource, and the outsourcing is described as damage. The object of concern changes from writing to print, from print to search engines, and now from search engines to generative AI, but the underlying fear is stable: a capacity that once had to be exercised internally will move into the environment, and the person will become less able, less attentive, or less wise.

Plato gives the cleanest early version of this worry in *Phaedrus*. Socrates tells a story about Theuth, the Egyptian inventor of letters, presenting writing to King Thamus as a discovery that will make people wiser and improve their memories. Thamus rejects the claim. Writing, he says, will “create forgetfulness in the learners' souls,” because people will trust “external written characters” instead of remembering for themselves. It will also give students “the semblance of truth,” so that they seem to know many things while understanding little.

The passage still works because the worry is recognizable. Replace writing with a search engine or a language model and the charge barely needs updating. The tool supplies an answer, the user avoids the hard work of recall or reasoning, and a fluent surface begins to substitute for internal competence. The historical distance is useful because writing now looks like the foundation of learned culture, which makes the early fear instructive. Plato’s passage therefore forces a better question: how can a technology be cognitively dangerous in one respect and cognitively productive in another?

The answer begins with a baseline problem. A tool-free human mind is a fiction. Human beings speak, count, draw, write, measure, diagram, list, file, search, and calculate with artifacts around them. Even ordinary thinking depends on arrangements outside the skull: a shopping list, a calendar, a map, a notebook, a formula written on a board, a phone reminder, a saved message. Modern psychologists call part of this cognitive offloading, meaning the use of action or external structure to reduce the mental demands of a task. Philosophers have made a stronger version of the point, arguing that some tools can function as parts of an extended cognitive system when they reliably support memory and reasoning.

The consequence is modest and important. The human mind is usually a mind-in-an-environment, and technologies change the environment in which thought operates. A calendar changes what a person has to remember. A written proof changes what a mathematician can inspect at once. A spreadsheet changes the scale at which a business can reason about costs. A search engine changes whether remembering a fact matters less than remembering how to retrieve it. Once cognition is understood this way, the central question shifts from whether a tool shapes the mind to which parts of thinking it reshapes, under which conditions, and for whose benefit.

Writing is the best example because Thamus was partly right. Writing did reduce the need to hold some material in memory. A contract, a law, a poem, or an argument could be stabilized outside the body. That external stability changed what people needed to memorize and how they could check what had been said. It also made new kinds of thought possible. A written text can be reread, compared with another text, annotated, copied, indexed, corrected, and transmitted across time. These operations are hard to build on speech alone because speech disappears unless someone remembers and repeats it.

Writing reorganized memory. Literate cultures still trained memory intensely. Medieval scholars, lawyers, monks, and physicians used writing alongside memorization, excerpting, ordering, and retrieval systems. A manuscript or book changed the practical meaning of knowing. In one setting, knowing meant reciting a passage accurately. In another, it meant knowing where a passage was, how it related to other passages, and how to bring it to bear on a question. Memory moved toward a partnership between internal recall and external record.

The printing press produced a similar pattern at a larger scale. More books meant more access, but also more selection, filtering, and overload. Early-modern scholars complained about abundance and built tools for managing it: bibliographies, indexes, commonplace books, encyclopedias, notebooks, catalogues, and systems for excerpting. Ann Blair’s work on early-modern information management is useful because it corrects the assumption that overload began with the internet. People who lived through the expansion of print already faced a recognizable version of the modern problem: the world contained more potentially relevant text than one person could read, remember, or judge unaided.

Again, the alarm captured something real while leaving most of the change undescribed. Print did create bad books, cheap controversy, distraction, and a larger burden of selection. It also changed science, religion, administration, education, and political life by making texts more available and more reproducible. The cognitive effect was therefore uneven. Some practices of attention and memory became less central, while other practices became indispensable: comparing editions, scanning tables, using indexes, keeping notes, and judging sources at a distance from the original speaker. The problem of too much information became part of learned culture, along with the tools for managing it.

The “Google effect” gives a modern experimental version of the same pattern. In 2011, Betsy Sparrow, Jenny Liu, and Daniel Wegner published studies showing that people who expected information to remain available later were less likely to remember the information itself and more likely to remember where to find it. The paper framed the internet as a form of transactive memory. In the original social version of that idea, a couple, team, or group distributes knowledge across members: one person remembers the tax details, another remembers the route, another remembers who to ask. Search engines made a nonhuman system available for a similar role.

The careful interpretation keeps the finding narrow. The Google effect shows that people adjust memory strategy when reliable access changes the task. If a fact can be retrieved quickly, the useful thing to store may be the path to the fact, the search term, the source, or the category of place where the answer lives. That strategy can be efficient. It can also fail when access disappears, when the retrieved answer is wrong, or when the user never builds enough internal knowledge to judge what has been retrieved. The cost is therefore specific: less practice in storing some content internally, more dependence on retrieval systems, and more need for source judgment.

AI is the current case for this older pattern. Generative systems differ from writing, print, and search because they can produce fluent language, synthesize sources, draft arguments, write code, tutor, summarize, and simulate dialogue. They store information and also participate in the production of the answer itself. That makes the cognitive offload deeper. A search engine can help someone find the page that explains a concept. A language model can produce the explanation, adjust it to the user, answer follow-up questions, and generate the paragraph that makes it sound understood.

The risk follows from the same mechanism that makes the tool useful. If AI handles first drafts, routine explanations, calculations, summaries, translations, and code patterns, users can spend more time on judgment, planning, taste, verification, and problem selection. The same offload can also reduce practice in the very skills being delegated. A student who always asks for a summary may read less patiently. A clinician who always asks for a differential diagnosis may notice fewer weak signals unaided. A programmer who always accepts generated code may lose contact with the structure of the system. These are empirical questions about particular tasks and users, rather than a single verdict on AI as such.

The older cases also warn against a comforting response. Plato’s overstated fear of writing gives little reason to dismiss every later fear. Writing really did change memory. Print really did change reading and authority. Search really did change recall strategy. The alarmists often miss the shape of the change, and the defenders often understate that a change occurred. The useful position asks for specificity.

Specificity starts by naming the capacity being offloaded. Is the tool carrying rote memory, arithmetic, navigation, drafting, social initiation, diagnosis, self-reflection, or moral judgment? These capacities have different developmental roles. Offloading a phone number has a different cost from offloading the ability to form an argument. Offloading spell-checking has a different cost from offloading the decision about whether a claim is true. Some skills are valuable mainly because they support a result; others are valuable because the practice builds the person who can judge the result.

The next question is whether the offload preserves supervision. A calculator is safer when the user has enough number sense to notice an impossible answer. A search engine is safer when the user has enough background knowledge to judge a source. AI is safer when the user can evaluate the structure, evidence, and purpose of the output. The danger grows when the tool produces answers in domains where the user lacks the internal model needed to detect failure. Fluency makes this problem harder because well-formed language can hide weak reasoning.

The final question is institutional. Cognitive technologies rarely act on isolated individuals only. Schools decide what students may outsource. Workplaces decide whether speed matters more than understanding. Platforms decide which forms of dependence are profitable. A society can adopt a tool in ways that preserve skill, or in ways that quietly remove the occasions for practicing it. Writing supported memory cultures when education still trained memory. Search supported knowledge when users learned how to evaluate sources. AI will support thought where people remain responsible for questions, standards, and verification.

The long record from Plato to Google supports a clear lesson: the first panic usually mistakes reorganization for simple decline. Human cognition has always been partly built out of tools, and each powerful tool changes the division of labor between memory, attention, judgment, and the surrounding world. AI belongs in that history, with one added pressure: it can imitate the finished products of thinking more convincingly than earlier tools. The task is to decide, deliberately and case by case, which parts of thought are worth outsourcing, which parts must stay practiced, and which new skills are needed to supervise the machines now thinking with us.
