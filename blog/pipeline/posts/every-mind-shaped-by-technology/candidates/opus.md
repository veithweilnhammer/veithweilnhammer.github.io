# Evidence

**Thesis (one sentence):** Every human mind has been built with the tools of its
era, so there is no tool-free "natural" cognition to lose; the recurring fear
that a new technology will wreck thinking — from Plato on writing to the "Google
effect" on memory — follows a stable pattern in which the changes are real but
consistently narrower, and usually smaller, than what the alarmists predict.

## Key claims and sources

1. **In Plato's *Phaedrus*, Socrates tells the Egyptian myth of Theuth and
   Thamus, in which the king rejects writing as a cause of forgetfulness and a
   giver of the mere appearance of wisdom.** This is the oldest recorded version
   of the "new technology will damage cognition" worry.
   Source: Plato, *Phaedrus*, 274e–275b (Benjamin Jowett translation).
   Link: https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/1636/pg1636.txt (Project Gutenberg
   #1636); Greek/English at Perseus:
   https://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus:text:1999.01.0174:text=Phaedrus:section=275a

2. **Writing genuinely did change memory practices**, shifting cultures from
   reliance on trained oral memory toward external storage — a real
   reorganization of cognition, consistent with (not identical to) Thamus's
   worry. Source: Walter J. Ong, *Orality and Literacy: The Technologizing of the
   Word* (1982). Link (publisher):
   https://www.routledge.com/Orality-and-Literacy-30th-Anniversary-Edition/Ong-Hartley/p/book/9780415538374

3. **The printing press provoked early-modern anxieties about information
   overload, distraction, and shallow reading**, together with new coping tools
   (indexes, reference works, commonplace books). Source: Ann M. Blair, *Too Much
   to Know: Managing Scholarly Information before the Modern Age* (Yale University
   Press, 2010). Link:
   https://yalebooks.yale.edu/book/9780300165395/too-much-to-know/

4. **Complaints about "too much to read" predate print and go back to
   antiquity.** Seneca, writing in the first century CE, warned that "the
   abundance of books is a distraction" (*distringit librorum multitudo*).
   Source: Seneca, *Epistulae Morales ad Lucilium*, Letter 2. Link (Latin/English,
   public domain):
   https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Moral_letters_to_Lucilius/Letter_2

5. **The "Google effect": across four studies, people who expected continued
   access to information recalled the information itself less well and recalled
   where to find it better.** Source: Betsy Sparrow, Jenny Liu, Daniel M. Wegner,
   "Google Effects on Memory: Cognitive Consequences of Having Information at Our
   Fingertips," *Science* 333(6043):776–778 (2011).
   DOI: https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1207745
   Abstract (PubMed): https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21764755/

6. **The effect is best read as cognitive offloading / transactive memory — a
   change in strategy — not a general decline in memory ability.** Same source
   (Sparrow et al., 2011); the transactive-memory framing traces to Daniel
   Wegner, "Transactive Memory: A Contemporary Analysis of the Group Mind" (1987).

## Exact quotes (public domain; transcribed and verified against the linked source)

- Thamus to Theuth (Plato, *Phaedrus* 275a, Jowett trans., Gutenberg #1636):

## Do not overclaim

- Plato/Socrates did not "ban" or literally refuse to write; the *Phaedrus* is
  itself a written text. The point is a recorded argument, not a policy.
- The myth is set in Egypt and attributed to Thamus; it is Socrates reporting a
  tale, and scholars debate how far it represents Plato's own settled view.
- The "Google effect" is a shift in encoding strategy under specific
  expectations, not proof that the internet is "making us stupid" or lowering IQ.
- Writing "changing memory" does not mean human memory capacity shrank; trained
  oral memory declined as a practice while literate memory tools expanded.
- "Digital amnesia" is a marketing term (a 2015 security-company survey), not an
  established clinical or cognitive diagnosis.

## Flags (could not fully verify, or sources disagree)

- **Google-effect robustness.** A large preregistered Social Sciences Replication
  Project (Camerer et al., *Nature Human Behaviour*, 2018) attempted Sparrow et
  al.'s Experiment 1 (the computer-priming / modified-Stroop result) and did not
  replicate it. Other components have mixed support. I therefore treat the effect
  as real in direction but modest and contested, and I say so in the draft. DOI:
  https://doi.org/10.1038/s41562-018-0399-z
- **Barnaby Rich (1613) "multiplicity of books" quote.** Widely cited (including
  via Blair) but the exact wording and precise source vary across secondary
  citations; I paraphrase the sentiment rather than quote it verbatim.
- **Gessner "confusa et nociva librorum multitudo."** Attributed to Conrad
  Gessner's concern about a "confusing and harmful abundance of books"; I could
  not verify the exact Latin against a primary page image, so I reference Blair's
  account of the period generally rather than quote Gessner.
- **Seneca translation.** The Latin is secure; the English "distraction" is a
  standard but not unique rendering. I flag it as a translation.

# Draft

Worry about artificial intelligence has settled on a specific fear: that handing
our reading, writing, and remembering to machines will hollow out the mental
skills those tasks used to build. Students will let a chatbot draft their essays
and never learn to think in paragraphs. Professionals will stop holding facts in
their heads because a model will always supply them. The concern is serious and
worth taking seriously. It is also very old. The earliest written version of it
that survives is roughly 2,400 years old, and it is aimed at writing itself.

In Plato's *Phaedrus*, Socrates tells a story about the Egyptian god Theuth, who
invents many arts and brings them to the king, Thamus, hoping they will spread.
When Theuth presents writing as a cure for poor memory, Thamus refuses the
praise. Writing, he says, "will create forgetfulness in the learners' souls,
because they will not use their memories; they will trust to the external written
characters and not remember of themselves." It offers "an aid not to memory, but
to reminiscence," and gives students "not truth, but only the semblance of
truth." They will "appear to be omniscient and will generally know nothing." Read
today, the complaint is uncannily specific: a new external medium will let people
retrieve knowledge without internalizing it, and leave them looking wise while
actually knowing less.

The striking thing about Thamus's argument is that part of it was correct. Before
widespread literacy, cultures that transmitted law, genealogy, and epic by word
of mouth developed formidable oral memory, supported by rhythm, formula, and
repetition. As writing spread, that trained oral memory became less necessary and
less cultivated. Walter Ong, who studied the transition from oral to literate
cultures, described writing as a technology that restructures thought, not merely
records it. So Thamus was right that writing would move memory outside the body
and change how people used the memory they kept. He was wrong about almost
everything that mattered after that. Writing did not produce a population of
forgetful pretenders. It enabled cumulative knowledge, allowed ideas to travel
across centuries and languages, and made possible the philosophy, science, and
literacy that Thamus's critics have relied on ever since — including Plato, who
made the argument by writing it down.

This is the pattern worth naming. Every human mind has been shaped by the tools
available to it, which means there is no tool-free baseline mind that a new
technology corrupts. Speech, writing, number systems, and printed books each
reorganized how people remember, reason, and pay attention. When a further tool
arrives, the fear that it will damage cognition tends to be partly accurate about
the mechanism and badly wrong about the scale and direction of the outcome. The
predicted collapse does not come. What comes instead is a reorganization: some
mental work moves outward onto the tool, and the capacities freed up get spent
elsewhere.

The printing press supplies the second clear instance. The worry it raised was
not memory but volume. Once books could be produced in quantity, scholars found
themselves facing more text than any person could read, and they said so in terms
that sound modern. The complaint predates print, in fact. Seneca, in the first
century, had already grumbled that "the abundance of books is a distraction" and
advised sticking to a few good authors rather than drifting among many. After
Gutenberg, that unease became widespread. Ann Blair, a historian who studied how
early-modern scholars coped with the flood of print, documents a period convinced
it was drowning in books — too many to read, too much of it repetitive or
shallow, attention scattered across a swelling pile. Writers described the sheer
number of books as a kind of affliction on the age.

What happened next was again a reorganization rather than a decline. The response
to information overload was to build tools for managing it: alphabetical indexes,
tables of contents, bibliographies, encyclopedias, and the commonplace book, in
which readers copied and sorted passages for later retrieval. These are
technologies of selection and search. They let readers navigate more material
than they could hold in memory, which is precisely the skill a literate,
book-rich culture needs. The abundance that felt like a disease became the
ordinary condition of educated life, and the coping tools became invisible
infrastructure. The fear was pointing at something real — attention is finite,
and more text does compete for it — but the outcome was a new set of mental
habits, not a ruined generation.

It is worth being precise about what those habits replaced. A scholar in a
manuscript culture read a small number of texts slowly and often knew long
passages by heart. A scholar in a print culture read more texts, more quickly,
and relied on the index to return to a passage rather than on memory to hold it.
The second scholar has traded depth of recall for breadth of coverage, and the
trade is legible: something was given up, and something specific was gained. The
same coping tools that made print manageable also changed what counted as
knowing a subject, from having it in memory to knowing where in the literature it
lived and how to get back to it. That is the quiet signature of a technology
reshaping cognition, and it is easy to miss because the winners of the trade
rarely mourn what they no longer need to do.

The modern version of Thamus's specific worry, about memory, has actually been
measured. In 2011, Betsy Sparrow, Jenny Liu, and Daniel Wegner ran a set of
experiments on how the expectation of internet access changes what people
remember. Across four studies, they found that when people believed a fact would
remain available to look up later, they recalled the fact itself less well, and
recalled where to find it better. People who typed statements into a computer and
were told the file would be saved tended to remember the folder rather than the
content. The authors framed this as transactive memory: the internet functions as
an external store, and we remember the path to information instead of the
information. The finding entered public conversation as the "Google effect," and
often as proof that search engines are eroding memory.

The careful reading is narrower, and it matters for the argument. What the studies
show is a change in strategy, not a loss of capacity. People offload storage to a
reliable external source and reallocate effort to knowing how to reach it — the
same move a scholar makes when she remembers which reference book holds an answer
rather than memorizing the answer. That is cognition adapting to its
environment, which is what cognition has always done with writing, indexes, and
address books. The evidence also asks for caution on its own terms. A large
preregistered replication effort in 2018 tried to reproduce one of the study's
central experiments, the one showing that hard questions prime thoughts of
computers, and did not succeed. The direction of the offloading effect has held
up better than that particular result, but the episode is a useful reminder that
even the well-known demonstrations here are more modest and less settled than the
headlines suggest.

Put the three cases side by side and the shape is consistent. Writing did move
memory outside the body, and trained oral memory faded, but literacy expanded
what minds could do rather than shrinking it. Print did overwhelm readers with
volume, and attention did fragment, but the culture answered with search tools
and grew accustomed to abundance. Digital search does shift some remembering from
what to where, but this is offloading of the kind humans have practiced for
millennia, not a general decay. In each case the alarm identified a genuine
mechanism — externalized memory, divided attention, offloaded recall — and then
overshot on the consequences, predicting decline where the record shows
reorganization and trade-off.

None of this justifies complacency about AI, and the historical pattern does not
promise that the outcome will be benign. It sets the terms for a better question.
The useful thing to ask about a new cognitive tool is not whether it will "rot our
brains," a prediction the long record keeps failing to confirm, and not whether it
"changes nothing," which no serious observer believes. The useful question is
specific: which capacity is being moved onto the tool, what do we gain by moving
it, and what is the cost of the skill we stop practicing. Writing cost us trained
oral memory and bought cumulative knowledge, a trade almost everyone would take.
Search costs some factual recall and buys fast access to far more than any mind
could hold, a trade whose downsides show up mainly when the connection fails or
the source is wrong. Large language models are now positioned to absorb not just
storage and retrieval but drafting, summarizing, and stretches of reasoning
themselves, which reach closer to the core of what we mean by thinking than any
earlier tool. That raises the stakes of the trade and makes the accounting harder,
because the skill being offloaded is no longer memory for a fact but the act of
working a problem through.

So the honest expectation is that AI will change how we think, in real and
measurable ways, exactly as writing and print and search did. Some mental work
will move onto the machines, and some skills built by doing that work ourselves
will weaken from disuse. Whether that is a loss or a gain will not be decided by
the technology in general but by which specific capacities we choose to keep
exercising and which we are willing to let go. Thamus was watching this happen at
the very beginning, and he named the mechanism correctly while misjudging the
result. The task now is to keep the sharper half of his insight — that
externalizing a mental skill really does change it — without repeating his
mistake of assuming the change can only be for the worse.
