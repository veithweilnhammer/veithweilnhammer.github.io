## 1. Style-guide violations

The draft has excellent factual grounding but repeatedly falls into the "Hard bans" from the style guide—particularly punchy setup sentences, "not X, it is Y" constructions, evocative image-lists, and parallel "trade" rhythms. 

*   **"Not X, it is Y" / Negation definitions:**
    *   *Quote:* "Writing did not produce a population of forgetful pretenders. It let knowledge accumulate..."
    *   *Rewrite:* "Instead of producing a population of forgetful pretenders, writing let knowledge accumulate..."
    *   *Quote:* "The question then is never whether a tool shapes the mind, because every tool does. It is which parts of thinking it reshapes, under what conditions, and at what cost."
    *   *Rewrite:* "Since every tool shapes the mind, the relevant question is which parts of thinking a new technology reshapes, under what conditions, and at what cost."
    *   *Quote:* "Instead of asking whether AI will rot our brains, a prediction the long record keeps failing to confirm, or whether it changes nothing, which no serious observer believes, the productive question is specific..."
    *   *Rewrite:* "The productive question to ask about AI is which specific capacity is being moved onto the tool, what we gain by moving it, and what skill we lose by no longer practicing it."

*   **Evocative image-lists / Triadic lists:**
    *   *Quote:* "...a shopping list, a calendar, a map, a formula on a board, a saved message."
    *   *Rewrite:* "...tools like calendars or written lists."
    *   *Quote:* "...too many to read, too much of it repetitive or shallow, attention scattered across a swelling pile."
    *   *Rewrite:* "...a period where scholars struggled to manage the sudden volume of available text."
    *   *Quote:* "...a student who always asks for a summary may read less patiently, a clinician who always asks for a differential may notice fewer weak signals unaided, a programmer who always accepts generated code may lose contact with the structure of the system."
    *   *Rewrite:* "...relying on AI for these tasks could cause a student to read less patiently or a programmer to lose contact with their system's structure."

*   **Punchy setup/announcement sentences:**
    *   *Quote:* "The useful thing about the distance is that we already know how the story turned out."
    *   *Rewrite:* "We know how this transition played out over the subsequent centuries."
    *   *Quote:* "What happened instead was reorganization." 
    *   *Rewrite:* "Readers responded to this overload by reorganizing their habits and building new management tools..."
    *   *Quote:* "It helps to be precise about what those habits replaced."
    *   *Rewrite:* (Cut the sentence entirely and lead with the historical comparison).
    *   *Quote:* "The modern version of Thamus's worry about memory has actually been measured."
    *   *Rewrite:* "Psychologists have attempted to measure the modern version of Thamus's worry by studying how the internet affects memory."

*   **Antithesis / Parallel "trade" constructions:**
    *   *Quote:* "A scholar in a manuscript culture read a small number of texts slowly and often knew long passages by heart. A scholar in a print culture read more texts more quickly and relied on an index..."
    *   *Rewrite:* "Before the printing press, scholars read a small number of texts slowly and memorized long passages. After print became common, scholars read widely and relied on indexes..."
    *   *Quote:* "Writing cost trained oral memory and bought cumulative knowledge, a trade almost everyone would take. Print cost intensive rereading and built the infrastructure of modern scholarship. Search cost some factual recall and bought fast access to more than any mind could hold."
    *   *Rewrite:* "Just as writing expanded knowledge while reducing the need for oral memory, and print enabled modern scholarship while reducing intensive rereading, AI will demand its own trade-offs."
