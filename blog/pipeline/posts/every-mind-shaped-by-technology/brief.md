# Brief: Every Mind That Ever Lived Was Shaped by a Technology

## Slug

## Working title
Every Mind That Ever Lived Was Shaped by a Technology

## One-line angle (also the Substack subtitle / SEO hook)
The fear that AI will reshape how we think repeats a worry as old as writing itself — and the long record shows the effects are real but rarely the ones the alarmists predict.

## Single argument (thesis)
Every human mind has been shaped by the tools of its era, and the recurring panic that a new technology will damage cognition — from Plato on writing to the "Google effect" on memory — shows a consistent pattern: the effects are real but reliably different from, and usually smaller than, what the alarmists forecast.

## Concrete anchor (optional — a real example to ground the argument)
Plato's *Phaedrus*, where Socrates tells the myth of Thamus rejecting writing because it will "implant forgetfulness" and give the appearance of wisdom without its reality — an argument strikingly parallel to today's fears about AI.

## What this post must cover
- The framing claim: human cognition has always been co-constituted with tools (speech, writing, print, calculation), so there is no tool-free baseline "natural" mind to lose.
- The oldest instance: in Plato's *Phaedrus*, Socrates warns that writing will weaken memory and offer only the semblance of understanding — a specific, recorded version of today's worry.
- The pattern repeats: the printing press provoked fears of information overload and shallow reading; each new medium drew similar alarms.
- The modern case, handled carefully: the "Google effect" studies suggesting people remember where to find information rather than the information itself — a real, measurable change, but a shift in strategy more than a decline.
- The consistent lesson: technologies genuinely reshape cognition (writing did change memory practices), but the changes are usually reorganizations and trade-offs, not the wholesale decline predicted.
- Applying it to AI without complacency: expect real cognitive change, be skeptical of both the "it will rot our brains" and "it changes nothing" extremes, and ask which specific capacities are being offloaded and at what cost.
