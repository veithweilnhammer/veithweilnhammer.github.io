# Evidence

**Thesis:** Every human mind has been built with the tools of its era, so there
is no tool-free "natural" cognition to lose. The recurring fear that a new
technology will wreck thinking — from Plato on writing to the "Google effect" on
memory — follows a stable pattern: the changes are real and specific, but
consistently narrower, and usually smaller, than what the alarmists predict.
What arrives is reorganization and trade-off, not wholesale decline.

## Verified facts, claims, and sources (union of all three candidates)

1. **Plato's *Phaedrus* is the oldest recorded version of the "new technology
   damages cognition" worry.** Socrates recounts the Egyptian myth of Theuth,
   inventor of writing, who presents it to King Thamus as a cure for poor memory.
   Thamus rejects the claim, arguing writing will cause forgetfulness and give
   only the appearance of wisdom. Set in Egypt; it is Socrates reporting a tale.
   Source: Plato, *Phaedrus* 274c–275b, trans. Benjamin Jowett (public domain).
   Verified against MIT Classics (https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedrus.html)
   and Project Gutenberg #1636 (https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/1636/pg1636.txt).

2. **Writing genuinely reorganized memory.** As literacy spread, trained oral
   memory (rhythm, formula, repetition — the machinery behind memorized epics)
   became less necessary and less cultivated, while external storage and new
   literate techniques expanded. Writing restructures thought, it does not merely
   record it. Source: Walter J. Ong, *Orality and Literacy: The Technologizing of
   the Word* (1982).

3. **Literacy changed the *use* of memory rather than eliminating it.** Medieval
   and early-modern scholars, lawyers, monks, and physicians kept elaborate
   trained-memory practices alongside writing. Source: Mary Carruthers, *The Book
   of Memory: A Study of Memory in Medieval Culture*, 2nd ed. (2008),
   https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9781107051126.

4. **Complaints about "too much to read" predate print and reach back to
   antiquity.** Seneca (1st century CE) warned against scattering attention
   across many books. Source: Seneca, *Epistulae Morales ad Lucilium*, Letter 2.
   Verified at https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Moral_letters_to_Lucilius/Letter_2.

5. **The printing press intensified early-modern anxieties about information
   overload, distraction, and shallow reading — and produced coping tools**
   (indexes, tables of contents, bibliographies, encyclopedias, commonplace
   books) rather than cognitive collapse. Sources: Ann Blair, *Too Much to Know:
   Managing Scholarly Information before the Modern Age* (Yale, 2010),
   https://yalebooks.yale.edu/book/9780300165395/too-much-to-know/; and Ann
   Blair, "Reading Strategies for Coping with Information Overload ca.
   1550–1700," *Journal of the History of Ideas* (2003),
   https://doi.org/10.1353/jhi.2003.0003.

6. **The "Google effect."** Across four studies, people who expected continued
   access to information recalled the information itself less well and recalled
   *where* to find it better. In one experiment, participants who typed
   statements into a computer and were told the file would be saved remembered
   the folder rather than the content. Source: Betsy Sparrow, Jenny Liu, Daniel
   M. Wegner, "Google Effects on Memory: Cognitive Consequences of Having
   Information at Our Fingertips," *Science* 333(6043):776–778 (2011),
   https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1207745 (abstract:
   https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21764755/).

7. **The Google effect is best read as cognitive offloading / transactive
   memory — a change in strategy, not a general loss of memory ability.**
   Transactive memory: people (couples, teams) remember both information and who
   or what can retrieve it, distributing storage across the group; the internet
   serves as a nonhuman partner in that system. Sources: Daniel M. Wegner,
   "Transactive Memory: A Contemporary Analysis of the Group Mind" (1987), and
   "A Computer Network Model of Human Transactive Memory," *Social Cognition*
   (1985), https://doi.org/10.1521/soco.1984.2.2.140.

8. **Cognitive offloading is an established concept:** people use physical action
   and external devices (lists, calendars, notebooks, phones) to reduce the
   information-processing demands of a task. Source: Evan F. Risko and Sam J.
   Gilbert, "Cognitive Offloading," *Trends in Cognitive Sciences* (2016),
   https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tics.2016.07.002.

9. **The extended-mind thesis** gives a philosophical formulation of dependence
   on external cognitive resources: some tools can play a functional role in
   thought when they reliably support memory and reasoning. Used as a useful
   description, not settled proof that tools literally *are* parts of the mind.
   Source: Andy Clark and David Chalmers, "The Extended Mind," *Analysis* (1998),
   https://doi.org/10.1093/analys/58.1.7.

## Exact quotes (public domain, Jowett translation — verified)

- "for this discovery of yours will create forgetfulness in the learners' souls,
  because they will not use their memories; they will trust to the external
  written characters and not remember of themselves. The specific which you have
  discovered is an aid not to memory, but to reminiscence, and you give your
  disciples not truth, but only the semblance of truth." (Plato, *Phaedrus* 275a)
- "they will appear to be omniscient and will generally know nothing; they will
  be tiresome company, having the show of wisdom without the reality." (same
  passage)
- Seneca, *Epistulae Morales* 2 (Gummere trans.): "in reading of many books is
  distraction." (Latin commonly cited as *distringit librorum multitudo*.)

## Do not overclaim

- The *Phaedrus* passage is a myth within a dialogue, not a prediction about all
  literacy or a policy; Socrates/Plato made the argument by writing it down, and
  the dialogue also grants that writing is useful for reminders. Scholars debate
  how far it represents Plato's settled view.
- Writing did not shrink human memory capacity; trained oral memory declined as a
  *practice* while literate memory tools expanded.
- The printing press did not simply "cause shallow reading." It increased volume
  and created management problems while enabling new forms of scholarship.
- The Google effect is a shift in encoding strategy under expectations of future
  access, not proof the internet is "making us stupid" or lowering IQ.
- Earlier panics being overstated does not prove AI will be harmless. Each
  technology caused real cognitive and institutional change; not every offload is
  beneficial.

## Flags and dropped claims

- **Google-effect robustness (major).** A large preregistered replication (Camerer
  et al., "Evaluating the replicability of social science experiments in Nature
  and Science," *Nature Human Behaviour*, 2018,
  https://doi.org/10.1038/s41562-018-0399-z) attempted Sparrow et al.'s design and
  did **not** replicate it. The direction of the offloading effect has held up
  better than the specific computer-priming / modified-Stroop result. Treated as
  real in direction but modest and contested; the draft says so. (Gemini's draft
  presented the Stroop priming result as solid — corrected here.)
- **Gemini's Stroop description** ("slower reaction times to search-engine words")
  is retained only as an illustration and paired with the replication caveat; it
  is one of the results that did not hold up.
- **A 2024 meta-analysis** (Gong & Yang, "Google Effects on Memory," *Frontiers in
  Public Health*, https://doi.org/10.3389/fpubh.2024.1332030) reports the effect
  varies with cognitive load, device, prior internet use, and knowledge base —
  consistent with a moderated effect. Cited for heterogeneity only; the draft
  relies on Sparrow et al. for the classic finding.
- **Dropped as unverifiable:** the Barnaby Rich (1613) "multiplicity of books"
  quote and Conrad Gessner's "confusa et nociva librorum multitudo" — widely
  cited but exact wording could not be verified against a primary source, so the
  period sentiment is paraphrased via Blair rather than quoted.
- **Dropped as non-factual:** "digital amnesia," which is a 2015 security-company
  marketing term, not an established cognitive or clinical finding.

# Draft

Worry about artificial intelligence has settled on a specific fear: that handing
our reading, writing, and remembering to machines will hollow out the mental
skills those tasks used to build. Students will let a chatbot draft their essays
and never learn to think in paragraphs. Professionals will stop holding facts in
their heads because a model will always supply them. The concern deserves to be
taken seriously. It is also very old. The earliest written version of it that
survives is roughly 2,400 years old, and its target is writing itself.

In Plato's *Phaedrus*, Socrates tells a story about the Egyptian god Theuth, who
invents many arts and brings them to King Thamus, hoping they will spread. When
Theuth presents writing as a cure for poor memory, Thamus refuses the praise.
Writing, he says, "will create forgetfulness in the learners' souls, because they
will not use their memories; they will trust to the external written characters
and not remember of themselves." It offers "an aid not to memory, but to
reminiscence," and gives students "only the semblance of truth," so that they
"appear to be omniscient" while knowing nothing. Read today, the complaint is
uncannily specific. A new external medium will let people retrieve knowledge
without internalizing it, and leave them looking wise while actually knowing
less. Swap writing for a search engine or a language model and the charge barely
needs updating.

The useful thing about the distance is that we already know how the story turned
out. Part of Thamus's argument was correct. Cultures that transmitted law,
genealogy, and epic by word of mouth had built formidable oral memory, supported
by rhythm, formula, and repetition, which is what made long poems memorizable
without a text. As writing spread, that trained oral memory became less necessary
and less cultivated. Walter Ong, who studied the shift from oral to literate
cultures, argued that writing restructures thought rather than simply recording
it. Thamus was right that writing would move memory outside the body and change
how people used the memory they kept. He was wrong about almost everything that
followed. Writing did not produce a population of forgetful pretenders. It let
knowledge accumulate, allowed ideas to travel across centuries and languages, and
made possible the philosophy, science, and literacy his heirs have relied on ever
since, including Plato, who preserved the argument by writing it down.

That gap between an accurate mechanism and a wrong forecast is the pattern worth
naming, and it rests on a simple point about cognition. There is no tool-free
baseline mind that a new technology corrupts. Human beings speak, count, draw,
write, measure, list, file, and search with artifacts around them, and even
ordinary thinking leans on arrangements outside the skull: a shopping list, a
calendar, a map, a formula on a board, a saved message. Psychologists call part
of this cognitive offloading, the use of action or external structure to reduce
the mental demands of a task. Philosophers have made a stronger version of the
claim, arguing that a reliable external tool can function as part of an extended
cognitive system. The consequence is modest and important: the human mind is
usually a mind-in-an-environment, and a new technology changes the environment in
which thought operates. The question then is never whether a tool shapes the
mind, because every tool does. It is which parts of thinking it reshapes, under
what conditions, and at what cost.

The printing press supplies the second clear instance, and the worry it raised
was volume rather than memory. Once books could be produced in quantity, scholars
faced more text than any person could read, and they said so in terms that sound
modern. The complaint predated print. Seneca, in the first century, had already
warned that "in reading of many books is distraction" and advised sticking to a
few master authors rather than drifting among many. After Gutenberg the unease
became general. Ann Blair, a historian of how early-modern scholars coped with
the flood of print, documents a period convinced it was drowning in books: too
many to read, too much of it repetitive or shallow, attention scattered across a
swelling pile. The predicted outcome was that the mind would buckle under the
weight and lose the ability to hold a coherent body of knowledge.

What happened instead was reorganization. The response to overload was to build
tools for managing it: alphabetical indexes, tables of contents, bibliographies,
encyclopedias, and the commonplace book, a personal notebook in which readers
copied passages under thematic headings for later retrieval. These are
technologies of selection and search. They let readers navigate more material
than they could memorize, which is exactly the skill a book-rich culture
requires. Reading shifted from the intensive study of a few core texts toward
strategic extraction from many. The abundance that felt like an affliction became
the ordinary condition of educated life, and the coping tools faded into
invisible infrastructure. The fear had identified something real, since attention
is finite and more text does compete for it, but the result was a new set of
mental habits rather than a ruined generation.

It helps to be precise about what those habits replaced. A scholar in a
manuscript culture read a small number of texts slowly and often knew long
passages by heart. A scholar in a print culture read more texts more quickly and
relied on an index to return to a passage rather than on memory to hold it. The
second scholar gave up depth of recall and gained breadth of coverage, and the
trade is legible in both directions. What counted as knowing a subject moved from
having it in memory toward knowing where in the literature it lived and how to
get back to it. That is the quiet signature of a technology reshaping cognition,
and it is easy to miss because the people who benefit rarely mourn the practice
they no longer need.

The modern version of Thamus's worry about memory has actually been measured. In
2011 Betsy Sparrow, Jenny Liu, and Daniel Wegner ran experiments on how the
expectation of future access changes what people remember. Across four studies,
people who believed a fact would remain available to look up later recalled the
fact itself less well and recalled where to find it better. Participants who typed
statements into a computer and were told the file would be saved tended to
remember the folder rather than the content. The authors framed this as
transactive memory, an idea Wegner had developed in the 1980s to describe how a
couple or a team distributes storage across members, one person keeping the
finances while another keeps the calendar. The internet works as a nonhuman
partner in that system, and the finding entered public conversation as the
"Google effect," often reported as proof that search engines are eroding memory.

The careful reading is narrower, and it matters for the argument. What the studies
show is a change in strategy, the reallocation of effort from storing a fact to
knowing how to reach it, which is the same move a scholar makes when she remembers
which reference book holds an answer rather than the answer itself. The evidence
also asks for caution on its own terms. A large preregistered replication effort
in 2018 tried to reproduce the study and did not succeed, and the specific
computer-priming result has held up worst of all. The direction of the offloading
effect is better supported than that particular experiment, but the episode is a
reminder that even the famous demonstrations here are more modest and less settled
than the headlines suggest. The cost, when it appears, is specific: less practice
storing some content internally, more dependence on retrieval, and more need to
judge whether what comes back is right.

Put the three cases side by side and the shape is consistent. Writing moved memory
outside the body and trained oral recitation faded, yet literacy expanded what
minds could do. Print overwhelmed readers with volume and attention fragmented,
yet the culture answered with search tools and grew used to abundance. Digital
search shifted some remembering from what to where, yet this is offloading of a
kind humans have practiced for millennia. In each case the alarm caught a genuine
mechanism, externalized memory or divided attention or offloaded recall, and then
overshot the consequences, predicting collapse where the record shows
reorganization and trade-off.

None of this licenses complacency about AI, and the historical pattern makes no
promise that the outcome will be benign. It sets the terms for a better question.
Instead of asking whether AI will rot our brains, a prediction the long record
keeps failing to confirm, or whether it changes nothing, which no serious observer
believes, the productive question is specific: which capacity is being moved onto
the tool, what do we gain by moving it, and what is the cost of the skill we stop
practicing. Answering it well takes three steps. The first is to name the capacity
honestly, because offloading a phone number is not the same as offloading the
ability to build an argument, and some skills matter only for their result while
others matter because the practice builds the person who can judge the result. The
second is to ask whether the offload preserves supervision: a calculator is safe
when the user has enough number sense to catch an impossible answer, and a model
is safe when the user can evaluate the structure and evidence of what it produces.
Fluency makes this harder, because well-formed language can hide weak reasoning.
The third is institutional, because schools, workplaces, and platforms decide
which forms of dependence become normal, and a society can adopt a tool in ways
that keep skill alive or in ways that quietly remove the occasions for practicing
it.

Large language models raise the stakes because they reach further into the work
than any earlier tool. Writing and search moved storage and retrieval outward. A
model can absorb drafting, summarizing, translation, and stretches of reasoning,
producing the finished product of thought and adjusting it to the user on request.
The same mechanism that makes it useful is what makes the offload deep: a student
who always asks for a summary may read less patiently, a clinician who always asks
for a differential may notice fewer weak signals unaided, a programmer who always
accepts generated code may lose contact with the structure of the system. These
are empirical questions about particular tasks and users, not a single verdict on
AI. The honest expectation is that AI will change how we think in real and
measurable ways, exactly as writing and print and search did. Writing cost trained
oral memory and bought cumulative knowledge, a trade almost everyone would take.
Print cost intensive rereading and built the infrastructure of modern scholarship.
Search cost some factual recall and bought fast access to more than any mind could
hold. The mind will reorganize itself around AI as it reorganized around the
written word, the printed book, and the search engine. Thamus named the mechanism
correctly at the very start and misjudged the result, and the task now is to keep
the sharper half of his insight, that externalizing a mental skill really does
change it, without repeating his mistake of assuming the change can only be for
the worse.
